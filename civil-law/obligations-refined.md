# Civil Law — Named Contracts (NCC Book IV)

## Primary Sources

**New Civil Code (R.A. 386), Book IV**: Named contracts occupy Arts. 1458–2084, beginning after the general law of obligations and contracts ends at Art. 1422. This module begins at Art. 1458 (Contract of Sale) and proceeds through the named contracts authorized for Phase 9 coverage. The general law of obligations (Arts. 1156–1304) and the general law of contracts (Arts. 1305–1422) — consent, object, cause, validity classification, extinguishment, novation, and damages theory — belong to `core/obligations-contracts.md` and are not restated here.

**Presidential Decree No. 1529 (Property Registration Decree)**: Governs registration requirements for sales of registered real property — the Torrens dimension of the double-sale rule. Load `property-land-law/torrens-system.md` for registration effects.

**Batas Pambansa Blg. 22 (Bouncing Checks Law)**: Intersects with the payment mechanics in sales and loan contracts.

**Republic Act 6552 (Maceda Law)**: Protects buyers of real property on installment payments from arbitrary cancellation by sellers. Applies specifically to residential real property sold on installment.

## Contract of Sale (Arts. 1458–1637)

### Essential Elements and Formation

**Sale defined (Art. 1458)**: One of the contracting parties obligates himself to transfer the ownership of and to deliver a determinate thing, and the other to pay therefor a price certain in money or its equivalent. Three essential elements: consent of the parties; determinate subject matter; price certain in money or its equivalent.

**Perfection (Art. 1475)**: The contract of sale is perfected at the moment there is a meeting of minds upon the thing which is the object of the contract and upon the price. From that moment, the parties may reciprocally demand performance — but the parties may provide that ownership shall not pass until payment, delivery, or another condition.

**Price requirements (Art. 1469)**: The price must be certain or ascertainable. A price fixed by reference to a specific external standard (published market price, appraisal by a named third person) is sufficiently certain. Price left entirely to one party's sole will renders the contract void — illusory consideration.

### Transfer of Ownership and Delivery

**Tradition (Art. 1477)**: Ownership passes to the buyer upon delivery of the thing, unless it was expressly agreed that ownership shall not pass until full payment or another condition is fulfilled. The agreement to delay ownership passage is valid between parties but does not bind third parties without registration.

**Constructive delivery (Art. 1498)**: When the sale is made through a public instrument, execution of the instrument is equivalent to delivery of the thing described in it, provided no contrary intention appears and no impediment to physical possession exists. *Constructive delivery does not occur when the seller does not have possession at the time of the instrument's execution or when a third party is in possession and refuses to yield.*

**Symbolic delivery (Art. 1498 second paragraph)**: Delivery of keys or other symbols representing dominion over the thing sold operates as delivery. Applies to warehoused goods, vehicles, and similar situations where the "thing" is accessed through a symbol.

**Delivery to carrier (Art. 1523)**: When the seller is authorized to send the goods, delivery to a carrier is delivery to the buyer unless the contrary is indicated by the terms of the contract or the circumstances.

### The Emptio Rei Speratae and Emptio Spei

**Emptio rei speratae** (sale of expected thing — Art. 1461): If the object of the sale is a thing which does not yet exist (future thing) but is expected to come into existence, the contract is conditional — if the thing does not come into existence, the contract is void for lack of subject matter.

**Emptio spei** (sale of hope or chance — Art. 1461 second paragraph): The buyer purchases the hope itself, not the thing. Even if the thing does not come into existence, the contract is valid and the price must be paid. The distinction is in what was sold — the hope, not the expected thing.

### Double Sale (Art. 1544)

When the same real property has been sold to different buyers, ownership passes to:
- First buyer to register in good faith — if the property is registered (Torrens)
- First buyer to possess in good faith — if registration was not made
- First buyer by oldest title in good faith — if neither registered nor possessing

**Good faith is paramount**: A buyer with prior knowledge of an earlier sale is in bad faith. Good faith requires absence of notice — actual or constructive — of the prior sale or claim. Registration in the property registry creates constructive notice to the whole world.

*Cruz v. Cabana* and related double-sale cases: SC has consistently held that Art. 1544 applies only to sales by the same vendor; if different portions were sold by different titles, Art. 1544 does not govern. The rule also requires that the competing buyers both purchased from the same seller — double sale by different sellers (e.g., one buying from the registered owner and another from the heir) is governed by the general rules of ownership, not Art. 1544.

### Warranty Against Eviction (Arts. 1548–1560)

**Nature**: The vendor warrants that the buyer shall not be disturbed in the legal and peaceful possession of the thing sold by reason of prior title or act imputable to the seller. Eviction is the loss of the thing sold to a third person by virtue of a final judgment based on a right prior to the sale or an act of the vendor.

**Requisites for warranty to activate**: (1) buyer is deprived of the thing sold in whole or in part; (2) deprivation is by virtue of a final judgment; (3) judgment is based on a right existing prior to the sale or act imputable to the seller; (4) buyer notifies the seller of the eviction action in due time (so the seller may defend).

**Effect of eviction**: The buyer may demand from the seller: the value of the thing at the time of eviction; income or fruits if the buyer has been required to return them; costs of the eviction action; expenses of the contract; damages, if eviction is due to vendor's bad faith.

**Waiver of warranty**: Parties may waive the warranty by express agreement. Waiver in good faith (no knowledge of risk) — buyer's only remedy is return of price without damages. Waiver with knowledge of risk and assumption — buyer waives everything; no remedy even if eviction occurs.

### Warranty Against Hidden Defects (Arts. 1561–1581)

**Scope**: The seller is bound to warrant against hidden defects — defects existing at time of sale, not apparent on ordinary examination, that render the thing unfit for its intended use or that materially diminish its usefulness such that the buyer would not have purchased at the price agreed or would have given a lower price.

**Three conditions for warranty to apply**: (1) defect is hidden — the buyer could not discover it with ordinary examination; (2) defect existed at the time of the sale — defects arising after sale are not covered; (3) defect is material — the thing is unfit for its use or its value is significantly diminished.

**Buyer's remedies (redhibition or quanti minoris)**:
- *Acción redhibitoria* — rescind the contract and return the thing; seller returns price plus expenses
- *Acción quanti minoris* — reduce the price proportionally

If the vendor was aware of the defect and did not disclose it: buyer may also claim damages in addition to rescission or price reduction.

**Prescriptive period (Art. 1571)**: Six months from delivery of the thing sold, for acción redhibitoria or quanti minoris based on hidden defects. Courts have strictly applied this six-month rule; filing more than six months after delivery is barred.

### Pacto de Retro vs. Equitable Mortgage (Arts. 1601–1616)

**Pacto de retro** (conventional redemption): The vendor reserves the right to repurchase the thing sold within an agreed period. Ownership genuinely passes to the vendee; the vendor's right is purely an option to buy back. The agreed period may not exceed 10 years (Art. 1606).

**Equitable mortgage**: A transaction that appears to be a sale but was intended by both parties as a security for a loan. The law presumes equitable mortgage (not pacto de retro) when any of the following circumstances exist (Art. 1602):
- Price of the "sale" is unusually inadequate
- Vendor remains in possession as lessee or otherwise
- Seller paid taxes after the alleged sale
- Purchaser retains part of the purchase price (as a reserve)
- Vendor bound to pay interest on the price
- Vendee's right to be substituted as mortgagee in case vendor pays

**Resolution of doubt**: Art. 1603 — in case of doubt, the contract shall be construed as an equitable mortgage rather than a pacto de retro. The law favors retention of ownership by the vendor against forfeiture.

**R.A. 6552 (Maceda Law) layover**: On residential property sold on installment, the Maceda Law overrides the general pacto de retro/cancellation rules when the buyer has paid at least two years of installments — the buyer has a 60-day grace period and a right of refund proportionate to payments made before the contract is cancelled.

## Barter (Arts. 1638–1641)

**Nature**: Exchange of one thing for another. No money changes hands (if money is involved, classification depends on whether the portion in money is less than the value of the thing given — if the thing given is worth more, barter rules apply; if money is greater, sale rules apply).

**Warranty rules**: Barter is governed by the same warranty rules as sale — if the property received has a hidden defect, the receiving party may rescind the exchange and claim the thing back, or claim proportional reduction of the value of the thing he gave.

**Double barter with eviction**: If one party was evicted from the property received, that party may recover the thing given in exchange (in barter, there is no "price" to return — the remedy is to reclaim what was exchanged).

## Lease (Arts. 1642–1688)

### Definition and Essential Elements

**Lease defined (Art. 1642)**: The lessor binds himself to give the lessee the enjoyment or use of a thing for a price certain, and for a period which may be definite or indefinite. Three elements: the thing (object of the lease); price (rent); period.

**Lease vs. sale distinguished**: In a lease, ownership is not transferred; the lessee acquires only temporary use and enjoyment. The contract is onerous and commutative — the lessor gives use, the lessee gives rent.

### Obligations of the Lessor (Art. 1654)

**To deliver the thing**: The lessor must deliver the thing leased in a condition fit for its intended use — not merely habitable but suitable for the use contemplated by the contract.

**To maintain the thing**: The lessor must make all repairs necessary to keep the thing suitable for its intended use throughout the period of the lease. *Necessary repairs are at the lessor's expense; ordinary wear-and-tear repairs are at the lessee's expense unless the contract provides otherwise.*

**Peaceful possession**: The lessor must maintain the lessee in peaceful and adequate enjoyment of the leased property for the entire duration of the lease. If a third party disturbs the lessee's possession through a claim of right (as opposed to through a mere act), the lessor must defend the lessee.

### Obligations of the Lessee (Art. 1657)

**To pay the rent**: At the time and place stipulated. If not stipulated, rent is due at the end of each month for urban leases and at the end of each agricultural year for rural leases.

**To use the thing as a diligent family man**: The lessee must use the thing as a prudent person would, for the purpose stipulated or inferable from custom.

**To return the thing**: Upon expiration of the lease, the lessee must return the thing in the same condition it was received, subject to normal wear and tear. Damage attributable to the lessee's fault must be compensated.

### Urban and Rural Lease Distinctions

**Urban leases**: Buildings, apartments, houses, and commercial establishments. The parties generally have broader freedom to fix terms. Unlawful detainer (ejectment) governs recovery of possession — the MTCC/MTC has exclusive jurisdiction, and the one-year period for filing runs from demand to vacate.

**Rural leases** (agricultural leases): The agricultural tenancy laws and the CARL framework overlay the Civil Code provisions. Load `property-land-law/agrarian-reform.md` for agricultural lease regulation under R.A. 3844 and RA 6657.

### Sublease and Assignment (Arts. 1650–1651)

**Sublease**: The lessee may sublease unless expressly prohibited in the contract. In a sublease, the lessee remains bound to the original lessor and also becomes the lessor to the sublessee. The sublessee is not in privity with the original lessor except where the sublessee receives from the sublessee-lessee rents not yet due to the original lessor (Art. 1652).

**Assignment of lease**: Lessee assigns the *entire* lease to a third party. Assignment requires the lessor's consent unless the contract expressly permits it. In assignment, the assignee steps into the lessee's position; the original lessee may be released from liability if the lessor consents to the substitution.

### Unlawful Detainer — Civil Law Trigger

When the lessee refuses to vacate upon termination of the lease or demand to vacate, the remedy is unlawful detainer under Rule 70 of the Rules of Court, filed in the MTC/MTCC within one year of the last demand. Route to `core/remedial-law.md` for the procedure; the substantive right to eject arises from the lease contract.

## Mutuum and Commodatum (Arts. 1933–1961)

### The Two Loan Contracts Distinguished

**Commodatum (Arts. 1935–1952)**: The bailor delivers a non-consumable thing to the borrower (bailee) for the latter's use for a period of time, after which the borrower must return the *identical thing*. Commodatum is *essentially gratuitous* — the bailor does not receive any compensation. If any compensation is received, the contract becomes a lease or another contract depending on the facts.

**Mutuum (Arts. 1953–1961)**: The lender delivers money or other fungible things to the borrower, who acquires ownership and must return the same amount of the same kind and quality. Mutuum is a contract of loan where ownership passes — the *identical thing* need not be returned, only an equivalent quantity and quality. Mutuum may be with or without interest; *there is no legal interest unless expressly stipulated in writing* (Art. 1956).

### Commodatum — Obligations and Risk

**Bailee's obligations**: Use the thing only for the purpose agreed upon and in conformity with its nature; return the thing at the end of the period; bear ordinary expenses for use and preservation.

**Risk of loss in commodatum (Art. 1942)**: The bailee is liable for the loss of the thing even by fortuitous event in four situations: (1) bailee delays return; (2) bailee devotes it to a different purpose; (3) thing could have been saved by substituting his own property; (4) bailee lent it to a third person without the bailor's authority.

**Bailee is not liable for ordinary wear and tear** attributable to normal use consistent with the purpose of the loan.

### Mutuum — Interest Rules

**No interest without stipulation (Art. 1956)**: Interest shall not be due unless it has been expressly stipulated in writing. An oral agreement to pay interest is unenforceable for lack of written form.

**Legal rate of interest**: When interest is due but the parties did not stipulate a rate, or when the stipulated rate is voided, the current BSP-prescribed legal rate applies (currently 6% per annum per BSP Circular 799, subject to SC resolutions on interest). *Nacar v. Gallery Frames* (G.R. No. 189871, 2013): SC fixed 6% per annum as the uniform legal rate for money judgments from 2013 forward; verify whether a later SC resolution has modified this as of knowledge cutoff.

**Usurious rates**: Courts may reduce unconscionable interest rates under Art. 1229 (reduction of penal clauses). The Usury Law ceiling was effectively lifted by Central Bank Circular 905 — there is technically no maximum rate — but courts have voided stipulations for being contrary to morals and public policy under Art. 1306 when rates are grossly unconscionable.

## Deposit (Arts. 1962–2009)

### Voluntary Deposit (Arts. 1962–1995)

**Nature**: The depositor delivers a thing to the depositary for safekeeping, with the obligation to return it upon demand or when the agreed period expires. Deposit is primarily for the depositor's benefit — the depositary receives the thing as a custodian, not as a borrower.

**Key obligations of the depositary**: Keep the thing safely; not use it without the depositor's permission (unless use is necessary to preserve it); return the identical thing on demand.

**Depositary's liability for loss**: Liable for loss or deterioration due to fraud or negligence. Standard of care: that of a good father of a family (Art. 1972), elevated to extraordinary care if the depositary receives compensation.

**Deposit in a bank (Art. 1980)**: Fixed, savings, and current deposits with a bank or similar institution are governed by the provisions on mutuum, not voluntary deposit — because the bank acquires ownership and is obligated to return the equivalent sum, not the identical bills. The distinction has practical significance: bank deposits are loan relationships, not bailment relationships; the depositor is a creditor of the bank.

### Necessary Deposit (Arts. 1996–2004)

**Nature**: Deposit made under compelling circumstances — calamity, fire, earthquake, flood — or by travellers in hotels and inns. The depositor has no free choice of the depositary; necessity forces the entrust of property.

**Hotel and innkeeper liability (Art. 1998)**: Hotels and inn keepers are responsible for the safekeeping of goods deposited by travellers, even if no specific receipt is issued, as long as the traveller notified the hotel of the presence of the goods and followed the hotel's regulations for safekeeping. Liability is that of a depositary.

**Limit on hotel liability (Art. 1998)**: Hotels are not responsible for: (a) damage to goods brought in without the hotel's knowledge; (b) damage caused by force majeure; (c) loss or injury caused by the traveller's own guests, visitors, or servants.

### Judicial Deposit (Arts. 2005–2009)

**Nature**: Ordered by a court for the safekeeping of property subject to litigation — a sequestration or receivership. The judicial depositary is not a private party acting voluntarily; they are an officer of the court bound by court order.

**Duties of judicial depositary (Art. 2007)**: Take care of the property with the diligence of a good father of a family; follow court instructions; return it to the person designated by the court.

## Agency (Arts. 1868–1932)

### Nature and Constitution

**Definition (Art. 1868)**: By the contract of agency, a person (the agent) binds himself to render some service or do something in representation or on behalf of another (the principal), with the consent or authority of the latter.

**Agency vs. mere service contract**: In agency, the agent acts on behalf of the principal and the principal is bound by the agent's acts within the scope of authority. In a simple service contract, the contractor acts on their own behalf to produce a result.

**Express and implied agency**: Agency may be express (oral or written) or implied from the acts and conduct of the parties. Courts look to the totality of circumstances to determine whether an agency relationship exists.

### Extent of Authority — General vs. Special Power of Attorney

**General agency (Art. 1877)**: An agent constituted in general terms is authorized only to perform acts of administration — ordinary acts needed to preserve and administer the principal's estate, and those acts which the agent is accustomed to perform in the ordinary course of the principal's affairs.

**Special power of attorney (SPA — Arts. 1877–1878)**: Acts that go beyond administration require an SPA. Art. 1878 enumerates acts requiring an SPA — the list includes: (a) make gifts; (b) enter into contracts by which ownership is conveyed; (c) create or extinguish real rights; (d) borrow money; (e) lease real property for more than one year; (f) bind the principal as a guarantor or surety; (g) waive an obligation gratuitously; (h) enter into any contract by which the agent acquires property on behalf of the principal; and others. The enumeration in Art. 1878 is not exhaustive — courts apply the principle that any act of strict ownership requires an SPA.

**Formal requirements**: Under Art. 1171, an SPA to enter into a contract that requires a public instrument must itself be in a public instrument. An SPA to sell real property must be notarized.

### Ostensible Authority and Protection of Third Parties

**Ostensible authority** (apparent authority): When the principal's conduct leads a third party to reasonably believe that the agent has authority, the principal is bound by the agent's acts even though actual authority was absent or limited. The basis is estoppel — the principal who created the appearance of authority cannot deny it against a third party who relied on it in good faith.

*Safeguards for third parties*: (a) the principal expressly or impliedly granted authority; (b) or, the principal's conduct reasonably led a third party to believe the agent was authorized, and the third party acted in good faith without negligence.

**Agency after revocation — third-party protection (Art. 1922)**: A third party who had no notice of revocation of the agent's authority and who in good faith dealt with the agent is protected — the principal is bound by acts performed after revocation but before the third party received notice.

### Undisclosed Principal

When an agent acts in his own name but on behalf of an undisclosed principal, the agent is personally liable to the third party — as if the agent were the contracting party. The principal, however, may directly enforce the contract against the third party, and the third party may sue the principal upon discovering the principal's identity.

**Art. 1883**: If the agent acts in his own name, the principal has no right of action against the third party, and the third party has no right of action against the principal — unless the third party knew of the principal and the contract operated in the principal's interest.

### Obligations of the Agent

**Duty to follow instructions (Art. 1887)**: Execute the agency in conformity with the principal's instructions; in the absence of instructions, act as a prudent manager would under the circumstances.

**Duty of loyalty (Art. 1889)**: The agent must not acquire for himself, in the execution of the agency, what the principal directed him to acquire. Self-dealing is prohibited unless the principal consents.

**Duty to account (Art. 1891)**: Every agent is bound to render an account of transactions to the principal and to deliver to the principal all money and property received on the principal's account. No setoff against the principal's property is permitted unless the principal owes money to the agent.

### Obligations of the Principal

**To reimburse and indemnify (Arts. 1912–1913)**: The principal must reimburse the agent for expenses necessarily incurred in the execution of the agency — including expenses reasonably incurred in good faith even if the transaction was not completed. The principal must indemnify the agent for damages suffered in the execution, provided the agent was not at fault.

**Liability for the agent's acts (Art. 1910)**: The principal is bound by acts of the agent within the scope of authority as if the principal performed them directly. The principal is also bound by unauthorized acts of the agent if the principal ratifies those acts expressly or by implication.

### Irrevocable Agency

Agency is in principle revocable at the principal's will (Art. 1920). Exceptions — agency is irrevocable when:
- The agency was constituted in the common interest of both the principal and the agent
- The agency was constituted in the interest of a third person who accepted the stipulation (third-party beneficiary)
- The agency was expressly stipulated to be irrevocable

Even in an irrevocable agency, revocation in violation of the agreement gives rise to damages — but it is effective as against third parties who have notice.

### Extinguishment of Agency (Art. 1919)

Agency is extinguished by: (1) revocation; (2) withdrawal of the agent; (3) death, civil interdiction, insanity, or insolvency of the principal or agent; (4) dissolution of the firm or corporation which entrusted or accepted the agency; (5) accomplishment of the object or expiration of the period.

**Ratification (Art. 1901)**: An unauthorized act by an agent that is ratified by the principal has the same effect as an act originally authorized — ratification retroacts to the date of the agent's act.

## Suretyship and Guaranty (Arts. 2047–2084)

### The Core Distinction

**Guaranty (Art. 2047, first paragraph)**: The guarantor promises to pay or perform for the debtor if the debtor fails — but the guarantor's liability is *secondary and subsidiary*. The creditor must first exhaust the principal debtor's assets before going after the guarantor. This is the **benefit of excussion** (beneficio de excusión, Art. 2058).

**Suretyship (Art. 2047, second paragraph)**: A person who binds himself *solidarily* with the principal debtor is a **surety**, not a guarantor. The surety's liability is primary and direct — the creditor may demand full payment from the surety without first proceeding against the principal debtor.

### Benefit of Excussion (Art. 2058)

**Available only to a guarantor**, not to a surety. To invoke the benefit, the guarantor must: (1) set it up against the creditor; (2) point out to the creditor the property of the debtor within Philippine territory sufficient to cover the debt; (3) advance to the creditor the amount needed to enforce against that property.

**When benefit of excussion is unavailable (Art. 2059)**: (a) guarantor expressly renounced it; (b) guarantor is bound solidarily (surety); (c) debtor is insolvent; (d) principal debtor has absconded; (e) it cannot be enforced against the principal debtor within the Philippines.

### Defenses Available to the Guarantor

**Defenses of the debtor**: The guarantor may avail himself of all defenses pertaining to the principal debtor — except those purely personal to the debtor (e.g., the debtor's minority). If the principal obligation is void, the guaranty is void.

**Personal defenses**: The guarantor may avail of their own defenses — that the guaranty contract was procured by fraud; that the guaranty was not in writing (Art. 2055 requires guaranty to be in writing); that the guaranteed amount has been paid; that prescription has run on the guaranty.

**Reduction of guaranty (Art. 2055 second paragraph)**: A guaranty is not presumed — it must not extend beyond what is expressly stated. In case of doubt, a guaranty must be construed in favor of the guarantor (strict construction against extension of the guaranty obligation).

### Subrogation Rights of the Paying Guarantor (Art. 2067)

The guarantor who pays the debt is subrogated to the rights of the creditor against the principal debtor — including the creditor's accessory rights such as mortgage or pledge. The paying guarantor therefore steps into the creditor's shoes.

**Guarantor's right of reimbursement (Art. 2066)**: The guarantor who pays for the debtor has the right to recover from the debtor: (a) the amount paid; (b) interest thereon from the time the guarantor notified the debtor of the payment; (c) expenses incurred after notifying the debtor; (d) damages, if applicable.

## Key Jurisprudence — Named Contracts

*Heirs of Arturo Garcia v. Municipality of Iba* (double sale): Good faith buyer who registers first prevails over prior unregistered buyer who had actual notice — the registration rule under Art. 1544 is clear.

*Spouses Raymundo v. Bandong* (pacto de retro vs. equitable mortgage): SC applied Art. 1603 presumption in favor of equitable mortgage where the vendor remained in possession after the "sale" and continued to pay the land taxes.

*Fiestan v. CA* (G.R. No. 81552, 1991): In the absence of express authorization in the SPA, an agent's sale of real property is unauthorized — courts will not imply authority to convey ownership from a general agency.

*National Power Corp. v. Philipp Brothers Oceanic Inc.*: Agency authority strictly construed; the principal is not bound by unauthorized acts unless he ratified them or clothed the agent with apparent authority.

*Mobil Philippines Inc. v. Court of Appeals*: A depositary for hire is held to a higher standard of care; loss or damage while in the depositary's custody is presumptive negligence absent proof of fortuitous event.

*The Insular Life Assurance Co. v. Toyota Bel-Air*: Benefit of excussion invocation requires the guarantor to point to specific, non-exempt property sufficient to satisfy the debt — a general statement that "the debtor has assets" is insufficient.

## Anti-Hallucination Guardrails — Named Contracts

Never allow partnership (Arts. 1767–1867) to enter this module: all partnership questions route to `commercial-law/partnership-law.md`. The boundary is Art. 1766 (end of general contract) and Art. 1767 (start of partnership) — partnership doctrine is fully outside this module's scope.

Never allow credit transactions (Arts. 2085–2141: pledge, real mortgage, chattel mortgage, antichresis) to enter this module: they are unbuilt and unrouted as of Phase 9. Flag such queries for a future credit-transactions module.

Never confuse guaranty with suretyship: the benefit of excussion belongs exclusively to the guarantor — a surety is solidarily liable and has no benefit of excussion. When a person binds himself solidarily with the debtor, the characterization as "guarantor" in the document is not controlling — the solidary obligation makes him a surety.

Never apply the six-month prescriptive period for hidden defects to any action other than the acción redhibitoria and quanti minoris: contractual breach actions and warranty against eviction follow the 10-year written-contract prescriptive period.

Never omit the good-faith requirement in double sale under Art. 1544: priority of registration is not absolute — a bad-faith first registrant loses to a prior unregistered purchaser who was in good faith. Registration does not cure bad faith.

Never treat the Maceda Law (R.A. 6552) as universally applicable: it covers only residential real property sold on installment. Commercial property, industrial property, and properties not sold on installment do not fall within Maceda Law coverage.

The bank deposit rule (Art. 1980) is consistently mischaracterized: bank deposits are mutuum, not deposit. The depositor-bank relationship is creditor-debtor, not depositor-depositary. Banks own the deposited funds and owe a debt for the equivalent amount.
