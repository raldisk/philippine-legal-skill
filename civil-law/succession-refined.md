## Preterition (Art. 854)

### Concept and Scope

**Preterition** is the total omission of a compulsory heir in the direct line from the testator's will — the heir is not expressly excluded and is not named at all, neither as an heir nor as a legatee/devisee. Art. 854 distinguishes preterition from disinheritance: disinheritance is an express act done on legal grounds; preterition is a silence that operates by law regardless of the testator's intent.

**Who may be preterited**: Only compulsory heirs in the direct line — legitimate children and descendants, legitimate parents and ascendants. The surviving spouse is a compulsory heir but is *not* in the direct line; omission of the spouse from a will does not constitute preterition and does not trigger Art. 854. Illegitimate children are not subject to preterition under the traditional view; their omission is treated differently (they may claim their legitime by collation or reduction, but the Art. 854 annulment mechanism does not apply).

### Legal Effect of Preterition

**Annulment of the institution of heirs**: Preterition of one compulsory heir in the direct line annuls the *institution of heirs* entirely — not merely reduces it proportionally. This is not a reduction of testamentary dispositions; it is a complete nullification of the designation of heirs in the will.

The **legacies and devises**, however, remain valid insofar as they do not impair the legitime of the preterited heir. The will does not become entirely void — only the institution of heirs portion is annulled.

*Practical consequence*: If a testator names Person A as the sole heir and makes three specific legacies, and then it is discovered that a legitimate child was preterited, the naming of A as heir is annulled, but the three legacies survive subject to reduction if they impair the preterited heir's legitime. The preterited heir receives their legitime. A, no longer an instituted heir, takes nothing except what the testator's legacies may have designated — and A was not named as a legatee, so A inherits nothing. The estate therefore passes intestate to the extent not covered by valid legacies.

**What preterition does not do**: It does not automatically void the entire will. It does not disinherit anyone. It does not cancel specific legacies that do not impair the legitime. Courts have consistently held that Art. 854's effect is surgical — it removes the institution of heirs while preserving valid legacies and devises.

### Preterition vs. Disinheritance

| Criterion | Preterition (Art. 854) | Disinheritance (Arts. 915–921) |
|---|---|---|
| Act required | No act — silence | Express act in will |
| Legal grounds required | None — omission suffices | Specific grounds required |
| Effect on institution | Institution annulled | Institution valid; heir excluded |
| Effect on legacies | Legacies survive subject to legitime | Legacies survive subject to legitime |
| Heir's remedy | Automatic — claims by operation of Art. 854 | Petition in probate if grounds are defective |
| Reconciliation (Art. 922) | Not applicable — no prior exclusion | Subsequent reconciliation restores heir's right |

## Accretion in Succession (Arts. 1015–1033)

### When Accretion Operates

**Accretion** is the right by which, when two or more persons are called to the same inheritance, legacy, or devise, and one of them cannot or does not want to accept his share, that share is added (accretes) to the shares of the co-heirs, co-legatees, or co-devisees.

**Three triggering events (Art. 1016)**:
First, predecease — the called heir, legatee, or devisee died before the testator, making acceptance impossible. Second, incapacity — the called person is excluded by law (Art. 1032 unworthiness or disqualification) or lacked capacity at the time of the testator's death. Third, renunciation — the called person repudiates the inheritance, legacy, or devise without designating another recipient.

**Essential condition (Art. 1017)**: For accretion to operate in testamentary succession, the co-heirs, co-legatees, or co-devisees must have been called to the *same portion* of the estate. If they were called to separate and independently designated portions, there is no community of portion — accretion does not apply, and the defaulting share either goes by substitution (if the testator provided one), by representation (if applicable), or intestate.

### Proportion and Order of Accretion

**Proportion (Art. 1019)**: Accretion is in proportion to the shares of the co-heirs or co-legatees in the common portion. If A and B were each called to one-half of a legacy and A renounces, B receives the entire legacy.

**Priority of right of representation over accretion**: The right of representation (in intestate succession) or the substitution designated by the testator (in testamentary succession) operates first. Accretion fills the gap only when no representative and no substitute exists.

**Accretion in intestate succession (Art. 1021)**: If in the share of an intestate heir one of the co-heirs does not wish to or cannot accept, his portion shall accrete to the other co-heirs. The same proportionality rule applies.

### Obligations Attaching with Accreted Share (Art. 1020)

The heir who benefits by accretion takes the accreted share with all its charges and obligations — the same charges and obligations that attached to the share of the heir who could not or would not accept. Accretion is not gratuitous in the sense of being charge-free; it is share-plus-charges.

## Legacies and Devises in Depth (Arts. 900–920, 950–954)

### Classification

**Legacy**: Testamentary disposition of personal property (chattel, money, credits). **Devise**: Testamentary disposition of real property. The distinction matters because NCC rules on delivery, reduction, and loss differ between the two.

**Specific vs. generic legacy**: A specific legacy identifies a particular item ("I give my Rolex to A"). A generic legacy designates a type ("I give one million pesos to B"). A generic legacy of money does not give the legatee a right to any specific money in the estate — the executor must set aside the amount, and the legatee has a credit against the estate.

**Legacy of credit (Art. 935)**: A legacy of a credit owed to the testator releases only the debt of the debtor as it stood at the time of the legacy's execution — any credit that arose after the will was made is not covered unless the will expressly extends the legacy to future credits.

**Legacy of remission (Art. 936)**: The testator may remit the debt a third person owes — the effect is that the debtor is freed from the obligation. The legacy of remission does not require the debtor's consent; it is unilateral and effective from the testator's death.

**Legacy of education and support (Art. 1082)**: A legacy for education lasts until the legatee obtains a professional degree or until the legatee reaches the age established by the testator. If no age is established, the legacy lasts until a professional degree is obtained. The support legacy is distinct — it lasts during the legatee's life unless a shorter term is fixed by the testator.

### General Legacy vs. Specific Legacy — Loss Before Death

**Loss of specific legacy (Art. 950)**: If the specific thing bequeathed is entirely lost during the testator's lifetime, and the loss is attributable to the testator's own act or default, the legatee has no remedy. If it is lost before the testator's death through no fault of the testator, the legacy is extinguished — but if the thing existed at the time of the will's execution and was only lost afterward, the legatee may claim the insurance proceeds or indemnity, if any.

### Order of Reduction When Estate Is Insufficient

When the estate is insufficient to pay all legitimes and satisfy all testamentary dispositions, the reduction follows this order (Arts. 950–954):

First, legacies and devises are reduced. Among legacies, the reduction follows this sequence: legacies of remuneratory character (compensation for services) and legacies for a sum of money are reduced before legacies of specific things. Legacies charged specifically on a particular property of the estate are the *last* to be reduced among legacies.

Second, after all legacies and devises have been reduced to zero if necessary, the institution of heirs is reduced proportionally.

Third, if even after full reduction of legacies and the institution, the legitime is still impaired, the compulsory heir may directly attack the testamentary disposition as inofficious and demand reduction or return.

## Repudiation of Inheritance (Arts. 1040–1057)

### Who May Renounce

**Legal capacity required (Art. 1043)**: Only persons with capacity to alienate property may renounce an inheritance. A minor or an incompetent cannot renounce without court approval and without compliance with formalities required for the alienation of property.

**Form of renunciation (Art. 1052)**: Repudiation of an inheritance must be done in a public or authentic instrument. Oral repudiation has no legal effect. If it is an inheritance by will, the repudiation may be made in a public instrument or by petition presented to the probate court.

**No partial repudiation (Art. 1051)**: Repudiation must be unconditional and total for the portion being renounced. A person cannot accept the inheritance subject to a condition, nor can they accept a part and renounce the rest — unless there are two separate titles, such as an inheritance by testate succession and another by intestate succession, in which case the heir may accept one and repudiate the other.

### Effects of Repudiation

**On the repudiating heir (Art. 1040)**: The renouncing heir is treated as if they had never been called to the succession. Their repudiation retroacts to the moment of the testator's death for all legal purposes.

**On accretion**: After repudiation, accretion operates in favor of the co-heirs called to the same portion, in proportion to their respective shares.

**On the children of the repudiating heir (Art. 1042)**: The children of the repudiating heir do *not* acquire the right of representation in testamentary succession if their parent repudiates. Representation requires predecease or incapacity — voluntary repudiation excludes representation. The repudiating heir's children may inherit from the testator in their own right only if they are themselves called — by will or by intestate succession independently.

### Effect on Creditors of the Repudiating Heir (Art. 1052)

If the heir repudiates an inheritance to the prejudice of his own creditors, the creditors may petition the court to authorize the heir to accept the inheritance on behalf of the creditors to the extent of the debt. This is the *acción pauliana* applied to repudiation — a creditor remedy when the debtor-heir acts in bad faith by renouncing a windfall to defeat creditor claims.

## Collation: Mechanics and Scope (Arts. 1061–1077)

### Who Is Obliged to Collate (Arts. 1061–1062)

**Compulsory heirs who concur with other compulsory heirs** must bring into the mass of the estate all property and rights received from the decedent by gratuitous title during the decedent's lifetime. Collation is mandatory for compulsory heirs in the direct line — legitimate children, descendants, legitimate parents, and ascendants — when they concur with each other in the succession.

**Voluntary heirs and legatees are not obliged to collate** unless the testator expressly imposes the obligation in the will. Without such express imposition, collation applies only to compulsory heirs.

**Grandchildren representing their parents (Art. 1077)**: Grandchildren who inherit by right of representation must collate what their parent would have been obliged to collate had the parent inherited.

### What Is Subject to Collation (Art. 1061)

**All gratuitous transfers** from the decedent to the collating heir during the decedent's lifetime are subject to collation: outright donations, transfers of property without adequate consideration, and any other disposition that enriched the heir at the decedent's expense.

### What Is Exempt from Collation (Arts. 1067–1071)

**Support in the ordinary sense** — regular support given by the decedent to the heir for sustenance, education, and living expenses during the decedent's lifetime — is not subject to collation. The rationale is that support is a legal obligation, not a gratuitous transfer.

**Medical and funeral expenses** of the heir paid by the decedent are not subject to collation.

**Education expenses** (Art. 1068): Expenses for the professional education of the heir must be collated unless the testator expressly exempts them. The education is not free from collation merely because it occurred during the heir's minority — what matters is whether the testator manifested an intent to charge or exempt. Where no intent appears, the default is that professional education expenses *are* collatable if they exceed what the other heirs received in comparable form.

**Property insurance proceeds** received by an heir from policies maintained by the decedent are not collatable unless the decedent paid the premiums during the period of gratuitous maintenance — a contested edge that requires case-by-case analysis.

**Donations propter nuptias** (Art. 1069): Donations made to a child by reason of their marriage *are* collatable unless the testator/donor declared otherwise.

### Computation of Collation

The computation follows this sequence:

Step 1 — Determine the net estate (gross estate less debts and charges). This is the divisible estate.

Step 2 — Add back all collatable donations: the fictitious mass for computing legitimes is the net estate *plus* all inter vivos donations subject to collation, regardless of whether those properties still physically exist.

Step 3 — Compute the legitime of each compulsory heir based on the fictitious mass.

Step 4 — Credit each heir's collatable donations against their legitime share. If the donation received already equals or exceeds the heir's legitime, the heir receives nothing more from the estate and keeps the donated property (subject to reduction if the donation impairs the legitime of others).

Step 5 — If the donation exceeded the heir's legitime and free portion combined, the excess must be returned to the estate (reduction of inofficious donation — Art. 771).

*Numerical example*: Net estate = ₱2,000,000. Testator made one donation of ₱500,000 to Child A during life. There are two legitimate children (A and B) and no surviving spouse. Fictitious mass = ₱2,500,000. Legitime = ½ = ₱1,250,000 → each child's legitime = ₱625,000. Child A has already received ₱500,000 by donation. A is charged ₱500,000 against A's ₱625,000 legitime share — A receives ₱125,000 more from the estate. Child B receives ₱625,000 from the estate. Free portion of ₱1,250,000 is distributed per will or intestate.

## Partition of the Estate

### Extrajudicial Settlement under Rule 74

**Conditions for extrajudicial settlement**: All three conditions must concur — (1) the decedent left no will; (2) the decedent left no debts; (3) all heirs are of legal age or minors are represented by judicial guardians or legal representatives. If any condition is absent, judicial settlement is required.

**Form**: The heirs shall execute a public instrument (notarized deed) of extrajudicial settlement. This instrument must:
- Identify all heirs
- Inventory the estate
- Specify each heir's share
- State the fact that the decedent left no will and no debts

**Publication requirement**: The deed or the fact of extrajudicial settlement must be published in a newspaper of general circulation in the province once a week for three consecutive weeks. Publication protects creditors and unknown heirs.

**Two-year lien (Rule 74, §4)**: Notwithstanding the extrajudicial settlement, the estate property distributed to the heirs remains liable for two years after the date of distribution for any claim by creditors who did not participate in the settlement. The two-year lien runs from the date of publication of the notice or, if none was published, from the date of the instrument. *Heirs who received the property are personally liable proportionally to the value of the property received.*

**Affidavit of self-adjudication**: Where there is only one heir, the heir may adjudicate the entire estate to himself by executing an affidavit before a notary public, also subject to the publication requirement and the two-year lien.

### Judicial Partition (Rule 90)

**When judicial partition is required**: (1) the decedent left a will; (2) the estate has outstanding debts; (3) not all heirs agree on extrajudicial partition; (4) any heir is a minor without a guardian who has authority to bind the minor; (5) a third party claims title to estate property.

**Jurisdiction**: The Regional Trial Court of the province where the decedent was a resident at the time of death (for residents) or where the estate is located (for non-residents).

**Project of partition (Rule 90, §1)**: After payment of all debts and charges, the executor or administrator submits a project of partition — a proposed division of the remaining estate among the heirs — to the court for approval. The court may approve it without a hearing if no heir objects. If objections are filed, the court holds a hearing.

**Effect of court-approved partition**: Upon approval and registration, title passes to each heir as to their designated portion. Court-approved partition is binding on all parties and their successors in interest.

### Rescission of Partition (Arts. 1097–1104)

**Rescission for preterition (Art. 1097)**: If a compulsory heir was omitted from the partition, the partition may be rescinded at the omitted heir's instance. The omitted heir may demand partition again, with full inclusion of all affected parties.

**Rescission for lesion (Art. 1098)**: A partition may be rescinded when any one of the co-heirs received a share worth less than one-fourth of what it should have been, provided the lesion is not the result of alienation or encumbrance made after partition. The aggrieved heir must choose between rescission and supplementary compensation.

**Limit on rescission for lesion (Art. 1099)**: The action for rescission on the ground of lesion prescribes in two years from the time the partition was made. If the partition is rescinded, the parties must return to each other what they have received; a new partition correcting the lesion follows.

## Reduction of Inofficious Testamentary Dispositions (Arts. 911–914)

### When Inofficiousness Arises

A testamentary disposition is inofficious when it impairs the legitime — when the testator gave more through a will, donation, or combination thereof than the free portion allows, leaving insufficient estate to satisfy the compulsory heirs' legitimes.

### Order of Reduction

**Step 1 — Legacies and devises are reduced first (Art. 911)**:

Among the legacies and devises, proportional reduction applies unless the testator expressly specified that certain legacies are preferred. The order is:

First, legacies or devises of money or personal property (general legacies) are reduced proportionally. Second, legacies or devises of specific things — the specific legacy is reduced last among general legacies, except for legacies specifically charged on a particular property (those are last of all).

**Step 2 — Institution of heirs is reduced after legacies (Art. 912)**: If full reduction of all legacies and devises is insufficient to satisfy the legitime, the institution of heirs is next reduced. The institution is reduced proportionally among all instituted heirs unless the testator has established priority.

**Step 3 — Inter vivos donations are attacked last (Art. 771 cross-reference)**: If the estate and the testamentary dispositions combined are still insufficient after full reduction, the compulsory heir may attack inter vivos donations in reverse chronological order — the most recent donation is reduced first, then progressively earlier ones. This is the action for reduction of inofficious donations; it is subject to its own prescriptive period.

### Fideicommissary Substitution in Full (Arts. 869–870)

**Definition (Art. 869)**: Fideicommissary substitution is a testamentary arrangement where the testator designates a first heir (the fiduciary) who is obligated to preserve the inheritance and transmit it, at a specified time or event, to a second heir (the fideicommissary).

**Requisites**:
- Two successive heirs
- Both must be alive at the time of the testator's death (Art. 863)
- The fiduciary must be related to and not separated by more than one degree from the fideicommissary (Art. 863, second paragraph)
- The obligation to preserve and transmit must be imposed on the first heir

**The one-degree limit**: The second heir (fideicommissary) must be within one degree of relationship from the first heir (fiduciary). This does not mean one degree from the testator — it means the fiduciary and the fideicommissary must be parent and child, or siblings. A fideicommissary more than one degree removed from the fiduciary renders the substitution void as to the second designation, though the first heir's interest survives.

**Rights of the fiduciary**: The fiduciary inherits and enjoys the property during their lifetime (or for the period fixed). They may receive fruits and use the property. They *may not* alienate or encumber the fideicommissary property in a manner that would defeat transmission — the fiduciary holds as a trustee in function, though the civil law form is not technically a trust.

**Rights of the fideicommissary**: The fideicommissary has a present right from the time of the testator's death, even though enjoyment is deferred. The fideicommissary may alienate or transmit this future right (subject to the condition that they survive the termination event). If the fideicommissary predeceases the fiduciary and the testator did not provide for this contingency, the designation of the fideicommissary fails; the property remains with the fiduciary's estate subject to residual disposition.

**Comparison with trust**: The fideicommissary substitution is a succession mechanism, not a trust under the Civil Code. The Civil Code does not have a trust chapter analogous to Anglo-American trust law. For trust-like arrangements in Philippine law, load `commercial-law/banking-finance.md` for trust entities, or note the incomplete codification of trust law in the NCC Arts. 1440–1457.

## Key Jurisprudence — Succession Mechanics

*Aznar v. Duncan* (G.R. No. L-24365, 1968): Preterition under Art. 854 annuls the institution of heirs but does not void the will in its entirety — legacies and devises survive unless they impair the legitime.

*Viuda de Villanueva v. Juico* (applied): Collation is mandatory for compulsory heirs; the obligation to collate runs from the time the donation was made and is assessed against the fictitious mass at death.

*Vda. de Kilayko v. Tengco* (G.R. No. 45425, 1989): SC applied the one-degree limitation on fideicommissary substitution strictly; substitution designating a grandchild as fideicommissary through an intervening heir was void as to the fideicommissary designation.

*Santos v. Santos* and related partition cases: Courts have consistently required that extrajudicial settlement instruments comply strictly with Rule 74 — publication is mandatory, and the two-year lien is a statutory right that cannot be waived by the heirs among themselves to the prejudice of creditors.

*Heirs of Lasam v. Umengan* (G.R. No. 168156, 2007): Partition of estate may be binding even as to heirs who did not sign when they received their shares and had actual notice of the partition — constructive ratification by acceptance of benefits.

*Diancin v. Bishop of Jaro*: Legacy of education — SC held the legacy covers only the professional education contemplated by the testator; the legatee's post-graduate studies beyond the degree specified do not extend the legacy unless the will expressly provides.

## Anti-Hallucination Guardrails — Succession Mechanics

Never confuse preterition with disinheritance: preterition operates by silence; disinheritance requires an express act on specific legal grounds. Preterition annuls the institution of heirs; it does not void the will.

Never apply the Art. 854 preterition mechanism to the surviving spouse: the spouse is a compulsory heir but not in the direct line; spouse omission from a will is not preterition.

Never omit the one-degree limit in fideicommissary substitution (Art. 863): the fiduciary and fideicommissary must be within one degree of each other — parent-child or siblings. A grandchild as fideicommissary (two degrees from the fiduciary parent) violates this limit.

Never conflate collation (bringing donations back into the fictitious mass) with reduction of inofficious donations (returning excess to the estate): collation is an accounting mechanism; reduction compels actual return of property when the donation impairs the legitime.

Never allow the two-year Rule 74 lien to be erased by private agreement among heirs: it is a statutory protection for creditors and runs from publication or partition date regardless of heir-side arrangements.

Never apply accretion when the testator provided a substitute: substitution operates before accretion. Accretion fills gaps only where no substitute was designated and no right of representation applies.

The order of reduction in Art. 911–912 is mandatory: legacies before institution of heirs. A court cannot reverse this order to spare preferred heirs at the expense of other legatees without authority from the testator's own priority designation in the will.
