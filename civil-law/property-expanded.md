## Property of Public Dominion and Patrimonial Property (Arts. 420–425)

### The Classification and Its Consequences

**Property of public dominion (Art. 420)**: Property belonging to the State and devoted to public use or to the service of the national government. Three categories:
- Property for public use: roads, canals, rivers, torrents, ports and bridges constructed by the State, banks, shores, roadsteads, and others of similar character
- Property for public service: property intended for the service of the national government — government buildings, armories, forts
- Property for the development of national wealth: national parks, forest reserves, mineral lands, and analogous properties

**Core incidents of public dominion**:
- **Inalienable**: Property of public dominion cannot be alienated or encumbered without specific legislative authority. The State cannot validly convey public dominion property to private parties except through proper conversion to patrimonial property.
- **Imprescriptible**: Acquisitive prescription does not run against the State for property of public dominion regardless of how long a private party has been in possession (Art. 1113). Only when property has been converted to patrimonial status can prescription begin to run.
- **Exempt from levy and execution**: Property of public dominion cannot be levied upon, attached, or subjected to the satisfaction of money judgments.

**Patrimonial property of the State (Art. 421)**: Property of the State that is not of the character described in Art. 420 — i.e., not devoted to public use, public service, or national wealth development. Patrimonial property may be acquired and alienated like property of private persons, subject to statutory requirements.

**Conversion from public dominion to patrimonial (Art. 422)**: The conversion requires either: (a) express withdrawal from public use or public service by a positive act of Congress or the competent authority; or (b) the property has ceased to be devoted to public use or public service in fact and there is a clear governmental intent to treat it as patrimonial. Mere non-use or abandonment is insufficient — formal declaration is required before prescription can run.

*Heirs of Malabanan v. Republic* (G.R. No. 179987, 2009): SC En Banc held that alienable and disposable lands of the public domain that have been in open, continuous, exclusive, and notorious possession since June 12, 1945, qualify for registration under PD 1529 §14(1) — but the lands must first have been officially classified as alienable and disposable by the DENR before the registration may proceed. Public domain land not yet classified as alienable and disposable remains outside the reach of prescription.

### Shorelines, Riverbanks, and Adjacent Private Land

**Shores and coastlines (Art. 420)**: The seashore, including the littoral zone, is property of public dominion and cannot be subject to private appropriation. Reclaimed land may be reclassified as alienable and disposable if properly alienated by the national government — the reclassification must be explicit.

**Riverbanks**: The banks of rivers are also property of public dominion to the extent of the river's natural bed. Alluvion (accretion by river deposits) attaches to riparian private land; but the river's natural bed itself remains public.

## Hidden Treasure (Arts. 438–439)

### Definition and Discovery Rules

**Hidden treasure (Art. 439)**: Money, jewelry, precious stones, or other movables substantially concealed or buried of which no one is the legitimate owner. Three conditions: (1) the thing must be hidden or buried; (2) it must consist of money, jewelry, precious objects, or other movables; (3) no legitimate owner exists or is ascertainable.

**Discovery by the owner of the land (Art. 438)**: If the owner of the land discovers hidden treasure on their own property, it belongs to them entirely.

**Discovery by a stranger (Art. 438, second paragraph)**: If hidden treasure is discovered by chance on another's property, the finder receives one-half and the landowner receives the other half — provided the discovery was by chance (accidental). A finder who deliberately searched for the treasure without the landowner's permission is not entitled to any share; the landowner takes it all.

**"By chance" — the controlling condition**: The chance-discovery requirement means the finder was not engaged in a deliberate search. A laborer accidentally uncovering buried treasure while digging a well or plowing fields qualifies. An explorer who systematically excavates to find treasure does not — their claim to one-half depends on the landowner's authorization.

**State ownership exception**: When the thing found is of historical or scientific value, the State has a claim under the National Cultural Heritage Act and related laws — the finder-landowner split in Art. 438 yields to special laws for culturally significant discoveries.

## Modes of Acquiring Ownership (Art. 712)

### The Full Enumeration

Art. 712 states: Ownership is acquired by occupation, law, donation, succession, prescription, tradition in consequence of certain contracts, and intellectual creation. The NCC thus recognizes seven modes.

**Occupation (Arts. 713–720)**: Acquisition of ownership over things that have no owner (res nullius) or that have been abandoned. Fish in rivers, game in forests, and wild animals are acquirable by occupation subject to fishing and wildlife regulations.

**Law (Art. 712)**: Ownership arising directly from legal rules — e.g., accession (Art. 440), where the landowner acquires what is incorporated into the land; the presumption of ownership (Art. 433).

**Donation (Arts. 725–773)**: A gratuitous conveyance of present ownership from the donor to the donee, with acceptance. Donation of real property must be in a public instrument and the acceptance must also be in a public instrument. Donation of personal property worth more than ₱5,000 must be in writing. For immovables, non-compliance with form renders the donation void (not merely voidable).

*Donations inter vivos* and *mortis causa* distinguished: donations inter vivos take effect during the donor's lifetime and are irrevocable (except for causes in Arts. 760–764); donations mortis causa are revocable and governed by the formalities of wills — if not executed as a will, they are void.

**Succession**: Covered in depth in `civil-law/succession-refined.md` and `special-laws/succession-inheritance.md`.

**Tradition**: Delivery in consequence of a valid contract of transfer — sale, barter, or other translative contract. Ownership does not pass by mere agreement; tradition (delivery, whether actual or constructive) is required under Art. 1477.

**Intellectual creation**: Route to the applicable intellectual-property module — `intellectual-property/copyright.md` for literary and artistic works, `intellectual-property/patents.md` for inventions, `intellectual-property/trademarks.md` for marks. The NCC provides no substantive intellectual property doctrine beyond the recognition of intellectual creation as a mode; the IP Code governs entirely.

**Prescription**: Covered immediately below in depth.

## Acquisitive Prescription (Arts. 1106–1155)

### Nature and Policy

Acquisitive prescription (usucapion) is the acquisition of ownership of real or personal property through the uninterrupted possession of the property in the concept of owner, openly, peacefully, and continuously for the period required by law, coupled with good faith and just title (ordinary) or without those additional requirements (extraordinary).

The policy rationale is twofold: to reward possessors who put property to productive use and who rely on their long possession, and to extinguish stale claims that disrupt established possession and social stability.

### Requirements for Both Kinds of Prescription

**Possession in concept of owner (Art. 1118)**: The possessor must hold as an owner — not as a lessee, agent, usufructuary, or depositary. Possession in concept of holder (permissive or by tolerance) does not generate prescription regardless of duration. *The animus domini — the intent to own — must accompany the physical possession.*

**Continuous possession (Art. 1120)**: Possession must be uninterrupted. The NCC treats juridical interruption (filing of suit) and natural interruption (loss of possession for more than one year) as breaking the running period. A possessor who recovers lost possession within one year is treated as never having lost it.

**Public and adverse possession (Art. 1118)**: The possession must be open (visible to those who might have an interest in contesting), peaceful (without force), and notorious (such that the true owner had or should have had notice).

### Ordinary Acquisitive Prescription (Arts. 1117–1132)

**Requirements**: (1) possession in concept of owner; (2) good faith; (3) just title; (4) uninterrupted possession for the required period.

**Good faith (Art. 526)**: The possessor believes that the person from whom the thing was received was the owner and had the right to convey it. Good faith is presumed; the party asserting bad faith bears the burden of proof. Good faith must exist at the *inception* of possession — a possessor who learns of the defect before the prescription period is completed cannot invoke ordinary prescription thereafter.

**Just title (Art. 1129)**: A title which, while sufficient to transfer ownership if it had come from the legitimate owner, actually came from a person who was not the owner or who lacked the power to transfer. Just title must be proved — it is not presumed. Examples: a deed of sale from a non-owner; a donation from an unauthorized donor; a deed of partition that included property not belonging to the estate.

**Period for immovable property (Art. 1134)**: Ten years of uninterrupted possession with good faith and just title.

**Period for movable property (Art. 1132)**: Four years with good faith and just title.

### Extraordinary Acquisitive Prescription (Arts. 1137–1138)

**Requirements**: (1) possession in concept of owner; (2) uninterrupted possession for the required period. No good faith or just title required.

**Period for immovable property (Art. 1137)**: **Thirty years** of uninterrupted possession, regardless of whether the possessor had good faith or just title. Adverse possession for 30 years ripens into ownership even against the registered title holder — subject to the Torrens limitation for registered land.

**Period for movable property (Art. 1132 second paragraph)**: Eight years without good faith and just title.

### Prescription and Registered (Torrens) Land

**The PD 1529 limitation on prescription against registered land**: Section 47 of PD 1529 provides that no title to registered land in derogation of the title of the registered owner shall be acquired by prescription or adverse possession. This means that extraordinary prescription does not run against Torrens-registered land in ordinary circumstances.

**The §14(2) PD 1529 exception**: Land that has been acquired by the State as alienable and disposable but not yet registered in a private name may be subject to prescription under §14(2) PD 1529 — which uses the NCC's 30-year prescription period. This provision bridges NCC prescription and Torrens registration: a person who has possessed alienable and disposable land for 30 years in the manner required by law may apply for original registration.

*Heirs of Malabanan v. Republic* (2009): Clarified that §14(1) PD 1529 (since June 12, 1945 possession) and §14(2) PD 1529 (30-year NCC prescription) operate on different conditions. §14(1) applicants need not prove 30-year possession from 1945; §14(2) applicants need 30 years of possession of land already classified as alienable and disposable.

### Tacking of Possession (Art. 1138)

A possessor may add their possession to that of their predecessor to complete the period required for prescription, provided the possessions are continuous and the current possessor can prove the transfer of possession — whether by succession (inheritance), purchase, or other mode of acquisition.

**Requirement for tacking**: The present possessor must have lawfully succeeded the prior possessor. A squatter who displaces a prior occupant by force cannot tack the displaced person's possession.

### Interruption of Prescription (Arts. 1120–1124)

**Natural interruption (Art. 1121)**: Possession is lost for more than one year — the entire period of prescription is interrupted and must begin again from zero.

**Civil interruption (Art. 1123)**: Filing of an action against the possessor by the person whose title is being extinguished. The interruption lasts from the service of summons. If the action is dismissed, the interruption is deemed not to have occurred from the time of the dismissal — *but the prescriptive period begins running again as of the dismissal*, it is not wiped out entirely.

## Nuisance (Arts. 694–707)

### Definition and Classification

**Nuisance (Art. 694)**: Any act, omission, establishment, business, condition of property, or anything else which: (1) injures or endangers the health or safety of others; (2) annoys or offends the senses; (3) shocks, defies, or disregards decency or morality; (4) obstructs or interferes with the free passage of any public highway, street, or body of water; (5) hinders or impairs the use of property.

**Public nuisance (Art. 695)**: Affects an entire community or neighborhood, or a considerable number of persons, although the extent of the annoyance may be unequal. Examples: illegal establishments in residential zones, slaughterhouses near residential areas, establishments generating excessive noise or noxious fumes.

**Private nuisance**: Affects only one person or a small number of persons in the enjoyment of private rights not common to the public. Examples: a neighbor's activity that disrupts only the adjacent property owner.

**Nuisance per se vs. nuisance per accidens**:
- *Nuisance per se*: Always a nuisance regardless of location or circumstances — e.g., a den of vice, an illegal slaughterhouse; may be summarily abated without prior court approval.
- *Nuisance per accidens*: A lawful use that becomes a nuisance because of the manner, circumstances, or location — e.g., a piggery lawfully established but later surrounded by residential development. Abatement requires a court finding; summary abatement is not allowed.

### Abatement of Nuisance

**Judicial abatement (Art. 700)**: The district health officer may bring an action in court to abate a nuisance. Private individuals may also bring an action for abatement and damages.

**Extrajudicial abatement (Art. 704)**: Permitted only for nuisances per se that are injurious to public health or safety and require immediate action. A municipality may summarily abate a nuisance per se using reasonable force. *Private individuals may extrajudicially abate a private nuisance only if it is not a nuisance per accidens, if the abatement is done without breach of peace, and if the means are no more than reasonably necessary.*

**Prescription for nuisance action (Art. 701)**: If private nuisance — five years from the date the cause of action arose. If public nuisance — the action does not prescribe in the ordinary sense because the public interest is continuous.

## Full Easement Typology (NCC Arts. 619–697, Beyond Right of Way)

`property-land-law/real-property-law.md` covers the easement of right of way (Art. 649) at practitioner depth. This module covers the full statutory range of NCC easements.

### Classification Framework (Arts. 619–614)

**Continuous vs. discontinuous**: Continuous easements — use may be continuous without human act at each instance (e.g., drainage of waters, light and view). Discontinuous easements — require an act of man at each use (e.g., right of way, well access).

**Apparent vs. non-apparent**: Apparent easements — indicated by visible signs or exterior markings (roads, drains, channels). Non-apparent — no external indication of their existence.

**The significance**: Apparent continuous easements may be acquired by prescription (20 years — Art. 620). Non-apparent or discontinuous easements cannot be acquired by prescription — they require a title.

### Easement of Light and View (Arts. 667–673)

**Easement acquired by prescription (Art. 668)**: When the owner of a building has opened windows or apertures on the wall of a neighbor's estate and has enjoyed the servitude for 20 years without interruption, the neighbor cannot build on their own estate to obstruct the windows, unless the wall is at the required legal distance.

**Legal minimum distances (Arts. 670–672)**: Windows or balconies facing directly onto a neighbor's property must maintain a minimum distance of 2 meters from the property line. Oblique or side views require 60 centimeters from the nearest edge of the window to the property line.

**Protest to interrupt prescription (Art. 668)**: The owner of the servient estate (the neighbor) can prevent the acquisition of the easement by protesting — notarially or judicially — within 20 years. Protest interrupts the prescriptive period for the easement.

### Easement of Drainage of Waters (Arts. 637–648)

**Lower estate's obligation (Art. 637)**: Lower estates are obliged to receive the natural flow of waters descending from higher estates. The owner of the lower estate cannot erect works that would obstruct this natural flow — doing so makes the lower estate owner liable for damages.

**Works for drainage**: The owner of the dominant estate may construct works necessary to convey water onto the lower estate, but only to the extent necessary and with the least damage to the lower estate. The right is limited to natural flow — the dominant estate owner may not artificially increase the volume of water flowing onto the lower estate.

**Rain water and rooftops (Art. 674)**: Rooftops must be designed so that rainwater falls on the owner's own land, not onto the neighbor's land, except where a specific easement for this purpose has been established.

### Easement of Party Wall (Arts. 658–666)

**Party wall (Art. 658)**: A wall built on the boundary between two adjoining properties for the common benefit of both owners. Presumed a party wall unless the contrary is established by title, external signs, or other evidence.

**Rights of co-owners**: Each adjoining owner owns the wall jointly. Either owner may raise the party wall at their own expense, provided the increased height or depth does not compromise structural integrity.

**Obligation to contribute**: Both owners must contribute proportionally to the cost of maintenance and repair, unless one waives their right to use the increased portion. If one owner adds a story to the party wall for their exclusive use, that owner pays the entire cost of the additional work.

**Signs of exclusive ownership (Art. 660)**: Presumption of party wall is rebutted when there is an indication that only one estate uses the wall — e.g., the wall is entirely on one estate's land; or the wall is bonded into only one structure.

### Easement of Intermediate Distances (Arts. 679–687)

**Planting trees and shrubs (Art. 679)**: Whoever plants trees near a neighbor's fence or wall must maintain a distance of at least 2 meters (fruit trees) or 50 centimeters (shrubs and small trees) from the neighbor's property line.

**Underground works (Art. 681)**: Trees whose roots penetrate a neighbor's property may be cut by the neighbor up to the property line without the tree owner's consent. Branches overhanging onto the neighbor's land may be cut.

**Construction near public highways (Art. 686)**: Constructions near public roads or highways must comply with special laws and local ordinances — load `core/local-government-law.md` for relevant building codes.

### Voluntary Easements (Arts. 688–693)

The owner of any estate may establish easements over it in favor of any person or estate, provided the easements do not violate any law, public order, or public policy. Voluntary easements must be constituted by will or contract. They bind successors in title if annotated on the title.

**Extinguishment of easements (Art. 631)**: Merger of dominant and servient estates; non-use for 10 years (discontinuous easements — 10 years of non-use; continuous non-apparent easements — 10 years from impediment to use); expiration of right of the grantor; annulment, rescission, or cancellation of title constituting the easement; abandonment.

## Key Jurisprudence — Property Expanded

*Republic v. CA and Naguit* (G.R. No. 144057, 2005): SC held that open, continuous, exclusive, and notorious possession of alienable and disposable public land since June 12, 1945 qualifies for original registration — the land need not have been privately owned before 1945; the requirement is the character of possession.

*Heirs of Malabanan v. Republic* (G.R. No. 179987, 2009): En Banc harmonization — §14(1) PD 1529 applies to possession since June 12, 1945 of land already classified as A&D; §14(2) applies to NCC 30-year prescription of A&D land classified as such before the period of possession began.

*NPC v. Ibrahim* (G.R. No. 168732, 2007): Land used for public service (power transmission lines) over privately owned land requires just compensation — NPC's presence on private land without expropriation constitutes taking.

*Ramos v. Herrera*: Easement of light and view acquired by prescription — SC required the dominant estate owner to prove 20 years of open, uninterrupted enjoyment of light and view, with no protest from the servient estate.

*Republic v. Alconaba* (G.R. No. 155012, 2004): Land that remains forest land (classified as timberland) even if actually occupied by private parties is inalienable public domain — possession does not ripen into ownership; no prescription runs.

*Pecson v. Court of Appeals* (G.R. No. 115814, 1995): Builder in good faith under Art. 448 — court clarified that the option belongs to the landowner and that the landowner must exercise the option; failure to exercise leaves the builder entitled to reimbursement.

## Anti-Hallucination Guardrails — Property Expanded

Never allow this module to restate what is already in `real-property-law.md`: classification of property, builder in good faith (Art. 448), co-ownership, usufruct, right-of-way easement, and Condominium Act coverage are in that module. This module stratifies on top; it does not repeat.

Never state that prescription runs against the State for public dominion property: public dominion land is imprescriptible under Art. 1113. Only patrimonial state property is subject to prescription — and even then, only after formal reclassification.

Never apply 30-year ordinary prescription to personal/movable property: extraordinary prescription for movables is 8 years under Art. 1132.

Never conflate just title with a true title: just title in ordinary prescription is a title that *would be* sufficient to transfer ownership if given by the true owner — but it came from a person who was not the owner. Just title is a defective title, not a valid one.

Never treat PD 1529 §47's prohibition against prescription over registered land as absolute without mentioning the §14(2) exception: persons who have possessed alienable disposable land for 30 years prior to registration may still apply for original title.

Never characterize nuisance per accidens as subject to summary extrajudicial abatement: only nuisance per se may be summarily abated. Nuisance per accidens requires judicial determination before abatement.

Never state that the easement of light and view is acquired after 10 years: Art. 620 requires 20 years for apparent continuous easements. The 10-year period applies to ordinary prescription for real property (with just title and good faith) — it does not apply to easement acquisition.
