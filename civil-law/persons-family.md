# Civil Law — Persons and Family (NCC Book I)

## Primary Sources

**New Civil Code (R.A. 386), Book I (Arts. 1–413)**: The foundational law on persons, civil status, domicile, juridical persons, name, absence, and civil register. The Family Code (E.O. 209), effective August 3, 1988, physically displaced the NCC's marriage, parental authority, support, filiation, and family home provisions. This module governs only the undisplaced residue.

**Republic Act 9048 (2001)**: Authorizes city and municipal civil registrars and consul generals to correct clerical or typographical errors in civil registry documents and to allow change of first name or nickname without a court order. Administrative remedy.

**Republic Act 10172 (2012)**: Amended R.A. 9048 to extend administrative correction to the day and month in the date of birth, and to the sex entry when patently typographical. Does not authorize change of sex based on gender identity.

**Republic Act 9255 (2004)**: Allows an illegitimate child to use the father's surname if the father voluntarily recognizes the child in the record of birth, a public document, or a private handwritten instrument signed by the father.

**Rule 108, Rules of Court**: Judicial proceeding for cancellation or correction of entries in the civil register. Governs substantial corrections — filiation, civil status, year of birth, legitimacy — not covered by R.A. 9048/10172.

**Republic Act 6809 (1989)**: Lowered the age of majority from 21 to 18 years. Age of majority is 18 for all legal purposes including capacity to contract and termination of parental authority.

## Juridical Capacity and Legal Capacity (Arts. 37–47)

### The Two Capacities Distinguished (Art. 37)

**Juridical capacity** is the fitness to be the subject of legal relations. It attaches to every natural person and is extinguished only by death. It cannot be restricted, waived, or transferred. A person with no legal capacity whatsoever still has juridical capacity — the distinction means they may hold rights and have obligations imposed on them by law, even if they cannot exercise those rights or undertake obligations by their own act.

**Legal capacity** is the power to do acts with legal effect — to acquire rights, assume obligations, and enter into transactions that bind. Legal capacity may be restricted (minority, insanity) or may have specific gaps (a person judicially declared prodigal lacks legal capacity over property but not over status matters).

The distinction governs remedies: a contract entered into by a person without the requisite legal capacity is **voidable** under Art. 1390(1), not void, unless a specific statute makes it void. Only a court of competent jurisdiction may declare the contract annulled at the instance of the incapacitated party or their guardian.

### Civil Personality (Arts. 40–43)

**Birth and civil personality (Art. 40)**: Civil personality begins at birth. The moment of complete delivery from the mother's womb — not conception — is the standard rule.

**Conceived child (Art. 41 — the conditional birth rule)**: A conceived but unborn child is considered born for all purposes favorable to it, subject to the condition that it be born later with the requirements of Art. 41. If the foetus had an intra-uterine life of at least seven months, it need only be born alive to enjoy civil personality retroactively from conception. If the intra-uterine life was less than seven months, it must survive at least 24 hours after complete delivery before civil personality attaches. The 24-hour rule is strict — if the under-seven-month foetus dies before 24 hours post-delivery, no civil personality arises, and no succession rights, insurance rights, or other favorable legal effects accrue.

*Practical consequence*: In estate planning and insurance, a conceived but unborn grandchild may be a beneficiary if the child satisfies Art. 41 — the favorable purpose condition is met when the right vests at birth, not when the right is exercised.

**Death (Art. 42)**: Civil personality extinguishes at death. The legal consequences of death — on property, contracts, obligations, and succession — are governed by the NCC's succession provisions (Book III), by contract, and by applicable special laws.

**Survivorship (Art. 43)**: When two or more persons who are called to succeed each other die and it is uncertain which of them died first, no presumption of survivorship applies — the law presumes they all died at the same time. This rule prevents any of them from succeeding to the other's property in an ambiguous case. Survivorship presumptions from foreign law (e.g., the common law younger-survives-older presumption) are not part of Philippine law.

### Juridical Persons (Arts. 44–47)

**Art. 44** recognizes three categories of juridical persons:

First, the State and its political subdivisions — the national government, provinces, cities, municipalities, and barangays. The State acts through instrumentalities and agencies, each with their own charter. Liability of the State in contract and in tort is governed by the State Immunity Doctrine under constitutional law; consult `core/constitutional-law.md` and `core/political-law.md`.

Second, corporations, institutions, and entities created for public interest or purpose by law — government-owned and controlled corporations (GOCCs), state universities, constitutional commissions as entities, and public instrumentalities. These are governed by their charters and the Administrative Code.

Third, corporations, partnerships, and associations for private interest — entities to which the law grants juridical personality separate and distinct from the shareholders, partners, or members. The separate-juridical-personality rule is foundational: debts and liabilities of the corporation are not the debts and liabilities of the stockholders, and vice versa. This module covers the NCC concept of juridical personality. For the corporate form in detail, load `commercial-law/corporation-law.md`. For the partnership form in detail, load `commercial-law/partnership-law.md`.

**Capacity of juridical persons (Art. 46)**: A juridical person may acquire and possess property of all kinds, incur obligations, and bring civil or criminal actions in conformity with the laws and regulations of its organization. The capacity is defined and limited by the charter or organizing instrument — a corporation can do only what its articles of incorporation authorize.

**Domicile of juridical persons**: The domicile of a corporation is its principal place of business as stated in its articles of incorporation, or as actually established if the AoI and actual operations differ. For jurisdictional and venue purposes, Philippine courts have treated the principal office as the domicile. Foreign juridical persons doing business in the Philippines are resident for jurisdictional purposes in the place where they have appointed a registered agent.

## Civil Status (Arts. 48–49)

**Civil status** is the set of characteristics that define a person's standing in the community for legal purposes: whether one is a minor or of full age, single or married, widowed or divorced, legitimate or illegitimate, a citizen or an alien. Civil status is determined by birth, recognition, legitimation, adoption, marriage, legal separation, annulment, declaration of nullity, and death.

**Erga omnes effect (Art. 49)**: Judicial decisions on civil status bind not just the parties to the proceeding but the entire world. A final judgment declaring a marriage void from the beginning alters the civil status of both parties as against all persons — not just inter partes. This is why the Solicitor General must be notified in nullity/annulment petitions and why civil registry entries are corrected pursuant to such judgments.

## Domicile (Arts. 50–57)

### Core Rules

**Single domicile rule (Art. 50)**: A person can have only one domicile at any given time. Domicile is the place of habitual residence — the place where the person intends to remain indefinitely and to which, when absent, the person intends to return.

**Domicile vs. residence**: Residence is the place of actual physical presence, which may be temporary. Domicile requires both the fact of residence and the animus manendi (intention to remain). A person may have multiple residences simultaneously but only one domicile.

**Change of domicile (Art. 51)**: Requires two concurrent elements: (1) actual removal or change of physical residence, and (2) bona fide intention to abandon the former domicile and make the new place the habitual residence. If either element is absent, the former domicile is retained. The absence of the animus manendi — even after years of physical presence elsewhere — means domicile has not changed.

### Domicile of Specific Persons

**Minors**: A minor's domicile follows that of the parent(s) exercising parental authority. Upon reaching 18 (R.A. 6809), the person acquires the capacity to establish an independent domicile.

**Persons under guardianship**: The ward's domicile follows that of the guardian. Change of the ward's domicile without court approval may be attacked.

**Juridical persons**: Domicile is the principal place of business — see above.

### Domicile in Election Law

Election law applies a domicile-of-choice doctrine requiring proof of physical presence and animus manendi with animus non revertendi (intent not to return). *Coquilla v. COMELEC*, *Romualdez-Marcos v. COMELEC*, and subsequent cases apply domicile rules to residence requirements for electoral candidacy. For election domicile doctrine, load `special-laws/election-law.md`.

## Name (Arts. 376–380 and R.A. 9048/10172)

### The General Rule: Judicial Authority Required (Art. 376)

No person may change their name or surname without judicial authority, except as expressly provided by statute. The rule protects civil registry integrity and prevents fraud through name changes. Courts apply strict scrutiny: there must be a compelling reason.

**Grounds recognized by courts for judicial name change (Rule 103)**:
The name is ridiculous, dishonorable, or extremely difficult to pronounce; the name has been honestly acquired and is the name by which the petitioner has been known since childhood; the change avoids confusion; the change is authorized by other valid grounds established in jurisprudence.

### Administrative Correction (R.A. 9048 and R.A. 10172)

**R.A. 9048 scope**: Clerical or typographical errors in any entry in the civil register — including first name and nickname — may be corrected without a court order. The civil registrar or consul general has authority. Clerical error means an obvious mistake committed in the performance of clerical work in writing, copying, transcribing, or typing an entry that is harmless and innocuous and visible to the eyes.

**R.A. 10172 extension**: The administrative remedy extends to: (a) the day and month (not year) of birth when the error is patently typographical; and (b) sex designation when it is patently clear that the entry is a typographical error. R.A. 10172 does not authorize change of sex designation based on psychological, sociological, or surgical grounds.

**Boundary between R.A. 9048 and Rule 108**: If the correction would change civil status, nationality, age (year of birth), or would affect filiation or legitimacy, R.A. 9048/10172 does not apply. Rule 108 judicial proceedings are mandatory. The test: does the correction affect legal status or create controversy? If yes — court. If patently typographical and harmless — administrative.

### Illegitimate Children's Surname (R.A. 9255)

An illegitimate child who has been acknowledged by the father may use the father's surname. Acknowledgment must be expressed — in the record of birth, a public document, or a private handwritten instrument signed by the father. The father cannot be compelled to recognize an illegitimate child for purposes of surname use. Recognition is a voluntary act.

**Effect of recognition on succession**: R.A. 9255 governs surname only — it does not automatically affect the child's successional rights. Successional rights of illegitimate children are governed by NCC Book III and the Family Code. Route to `special-laws/succession-inheritance.md`.

## Absence (Arts. 381–396)

### Stage One — Provisional Measures (Arts. 381–383)

When a person disappears from their domicile and their whereabouts are unknown, the court may, at the instance of any interested party, appoint an administrator of the absent person's property. There is no minimum period required for provisional measures — the mere fact of disappearance and unknown whereabouts is sufficient.

**Priority in appointment** (Art. 383): The spouse present is preferred as administrator; if there is no spouse, or the spouse is legally incapacitated, the nearest relative who is of age; if there is none, a disinterested person.

**Duration and powers of administrator**: The administrator manages the property, pays debts, and preserves the estate. The administrator does not have the power to alienate real property without court approval.

### Stage Two — Declaration of Absence (Arts. 384–389)

**When declarable**: After two years have elapsed without any news of the absentee, or since the last news (Art. 384), the declaration of absence may be applied for. If the absentee left an administrator, the period extends to five years before the declaration may be sought.

**Legal effects of declaration**: The administration of the absentee's property may be transferred to the surviving spouse, the heirs named in the will, the nearest relatives, or a disinterested person. The absentee's succession remains suspended — the estate does not open merely upon declaration of absence; the presumption of death rules (below) govern succession.

### Presumption of Death (Arts. 390–392)

**General presumption (Art. 390)**: After an absence of **seven years**, a person is presumed dead for all purposes, except for those of succession.

**For succession purposes (Art. 391)**: **Ten years** of absence are required before the presumption of death applies to allow succession to the absentee's estate.

**Special shortened periods (Art. 391)**: If the absentee was on board a vessel lost at sea or an aeroplane that is missing, or was in the armed forces and participated in war — **four years** of absence from the event suffice.

**Dangerous illness (Art. 392)**: If the absentee was in a place where a dangerous illness prevailed — **two years** from the last news or from the date of the illness.

**Reappearance and its effects**: If the absentee reappears or existence is proved after the presumption of death has been applied:
- Property: returned to the absentee in the condition it is in, subject to rights lawfully acquired by third parties in good faith during the absence.
- Succession: any distribution of the estate must be undone to the extent possible; third-party transactions in good faith are protected.
- Remarriage: the reappearance of the absent spouse does not automatically void the second marriage. Under Family Code Art. 42, the second marriage subsists until the proper affidavit of reappearance is recorded. Route to `family-relations.md` for the Family Code reappearance procedure.

## Civil Register (Arts. 407–413, R.A. 9048, R.A. 10172, Rule 108)

### Registrable Events (Art. 407)

The following must be entered in the civil register: births; marriages; deaths; legal separations; annulments of marriage; judgments declaring marriages void; legitimations; adoptions; acknowledgments of natural children; naturalization and changes of citizenship; loss or recovery of citizenship; civil interdiction; judicial determination of filiation; voluntary emancipation; changes of name.

### Evidentiary Value (Arts. 409–411)

**Public document status (Art. 410)**: The civil register books and all documents related thereto are public documents. They are prima facie evidence of the facts recorded. A civil registry certificate of birth is prima facie proof of filiation, civil status, and age.

**Rebuttal**: The prima facie presumption is rebuttable. The party asserting a different fact must present clear and convincing evidence to overcome the civil registry entry. *Hearsay that was relied upon in making the original entry does not improve the entry's evidentiary quality.*

### Correction of Civil Registry Entries

**Administrative correction — R.A. 9048 (as amended by R.A. 10172)**:
Covers: first name or nickname (when the change is to avoid confusion, the name is ridiculous or difficult to pronounce, or the change is in keeping with known identity); day and month of birth (typographical); sex designation (patently typographical).

Process: petition filed with the local civil registrar of the city/municipality where the entry is recorded. Notice posted. Civil Registrar decides. Appeal to civil registration authority, then to the courts.

**Does NOT cover**: Year of birth; surname; change of civil status; filiation; legitimacy; nationality; any entry whose correction would create a controversy.

**Judicial correction — Rule 108**:
Required for: changes in surname; correction of year of birth; changes in civil status (e.g., from illegitimate to legitimate); changes in filiation; cancellation of any entry.

Process: Special proceeding in the RTC of the city/municipality where the civil register is kept. Petition must implead the local civil registrar and all persons who have or may claim interest. The Solicitor General may be required to appear.

*Republic v. Remman Enterprises, Inc.* and related cases: courts will not allow Rule 108 to be used as a collateral attack on a final judgment, nor to create new rights through the mere correction of a civil registry entry.

## Key Jurisprudence — Persons and Civil Register

*Silverio v. Republic* (G.R. No. 174689, 2007): Petition for change of first name and sex entry following sex reassignment surgery denied. SC held Philippine law determines sex biologically at birth; no statute authorizes change of sex entry based on post-birth surgical or identity-based grounds. **Silverio remains controlling** as of knowledge cutoff; the R.A. 10172 administrative correction covers typographical errors only, not gender identity corrections.

*Republic v. Cagandahan* (G.R. No. 166676, 2008): SC En Banc allowed change of name and sex entry for a petitioner with congenital adrenal hyperplasia (intersex condition), applying the principle that the dominant biological reality controls. *Cagandahan* creates a narrow exception for biological conditions that were incorrectly recorded at birth — not for post-birth transitions. Confidence: [90] that *Cagandahan* and *Silverio* remain the controlling poles of the doctrine.

*Republic v. Albios* (G.R. No. 198780, 2013): Marriage contracted primarily for immigration purposes held valid. The motive of the parties does not affect the validity of consent — consent was genuine in the sense that the parties intended to be married and intended the legal consequences. The distinction between motive and consent is critical.

*Romualdez-Marcos v. COMELEC* (G.R. No. 119976, 1995): Domicile of origin retains force and is not easily lost; physical presence alone in another place does not establish domicile of choice without the animus. Detailed analysis of domicile in the electoral context.

*Ugdoracion Jr. v. COMELEC* and related absence declaration cases: Declaration of absence under Arts. 384–389 is a special proceeding; courts strictly require the statutory period of disappearance before granting declaration.

*Republic v. Court of Appeals and Capote* (G.R. No. 157043, 2006): Change of name under Rule 103; SC reiterated that compelling reason is required; the embarrassment or confusion standard is fact-specific.

## Anti-Hallucination Guardrails — Persons and Family (NCC Book I)

Never state that the Family Code superseded all of NCC Book I — it superseded only the marriage, parental authority, support, filiation, and related provisions (Arts. 52–87, 109–175, 194–208, 209–233, and related NCC articles). Arts. 37–57 (juridical capacity, legal capacity, civil personality, juridical persons, domicile) and Arts. 376–413 (name, absence, civil register) remain operative NCC law.

Never conflate juridical capacity with legal capacity: juridical capacity is inherent, cannot be restricted, and is extinguished only by death; legal capacity can be restricted by age, mental condition, or court order, and its restriction renders transactions voidable, not void.

Never apply Art. 41's conditional birth rule without the viability component: the 24-hour survival requirement is strictly limited to foetuses with intra-uterine life of less than seven months. Seven-months-or-more foetuses require only live birth.

Never apply *Silverio* as the only authority on sex marker changes in the civil register: *Cagandahan* (intersex biological condition) is a recognized exception. Both co-exist and govern different factual grounds.

Never substitute R.A. 9048 administrative correction for Rule 108 judicial proceedings when the correction would affect civil status, filiation, legitimacy, or nationality — the classification of the change (typographical vs. substantial) is the controlling question, and courts have consistently required judicial proceedings for any correction with legal status consequences.

Never state that Art. 43 creates a presumption that the younger person survived: it creates no survivorship presumption in either direction. The rule is simultaneous death.

Never omit the pre-Family Code edge on family home (NCC Arts. 152–162 vs. FC Arts. 152–162) when the date of constitution is factually relevant in estate and execution proceedings.
