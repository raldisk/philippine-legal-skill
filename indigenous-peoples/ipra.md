## Policy Basis and Constitutional Foundation
**Article XII, §2, 1987 Constitution** vests ownership of all lands of the public domain, waters, minerals, coal, petroleum, and natural resources in the State under the **Regalian Doctrine (Jura Regalia)**. IPRA carves out ancestral domains from this framework — they are *not* public domain lands; they are the private collective property of indigenous cultural communities/indigenous peoples (ICCs/IPs), held under a *native title* concept that predates and is independent of Spanish and American colonization.

**Native Title** — the *Cariño* doctrine (*Cariño v. Insular Government*, 41 Phil. 935 (1909), decided by the U.S. Supreme Court): Chief Justice Holmes recognized that where indigenous peoples have occupied land from time immemorial, that occupation itself constitutes a legal title — no Spanish or American grant is required because the land was never ceded to the colonial sovereign; it was continuously held by the community. IPRA codifies and extends this doctrine.

**Cruz v. Secretary of Environment and Natural Resources**, G.R. No. 135385 (Dec. 6, 2000): The SC, divided 7–7, failed to reach a majority to declare IPRA unconstitutional. The equal-division non-resolution effectively upheld IPRA's constitutionality. The four opinions are extensively cited in bar examinations for their competing analyses of the Regalian Doctrine vs. native title. The operative result is that IPRA remains valid law.

## Definitions (§3, IPRA)

**Indigenous Cultural Communities/Indigenous Peoples (ICCs/IPs)** — Groups of people or homogeneous societies identified by self-ascription and ascription by others, who have continuously lived as organized community on communally bounded and defined territory, and who have, under claims of ownership since time immemorial, occupied, possessed, and utilized such territories, sharing common bonds of language, customs, traditions, and other distinctive cultural traits, or who have, through resistance to political, social, and cultural inroads of colonization, non-indigenous religions and cultures, become historically differentiated from the majority of Filipinos.

**Ancestral Domains** — all areas belonging to ICCs/IPs held under a claim of ownership, occupied or possessed by ICCs/IPs themselves or through their ancestors, communally or individually since time immemorial, continuously to the present, *except when interrupted by war, force majeure, or displacement by force, deceit, stealth, or as a consequence of government projects or any other voluntary dealings entered into by government and private individuals/corporations*. Includes: lands, inland waters, coastal areas, natural resources, forests, pasture lands, burial grounds, worship areas, bodies of water, mineral and other natural resources.

**Ancestral Lands** — lands occupied, possessed, and utilized by individuals, families and clans *within* ancestral domains who are members of the ICCs/IPs since time immemorial. Distinguished from ancestral domains in that ancestral lands may be privately held by ICC/IP members as individuals or families, whereas ancestral domains are the collective territory of the community.

**NCIP** — National Commission on Indigenous Peoples, the primary government agency under the Office of the President, that formulates and implements policies for the recognition, promotion, and protection of the rights of ICCs/IPs.

## Four Bundles of Rights (IPRA §§4–8, Title II)

### 1. Rights to Ancestral Domains (§§4–8)
- **Right to own and develop ancestral domains** — ICCs/IPs have the right to own, develop, control, and use their ancestral domains under their own customary laws and traditions.
- **Right to manage and conserve** — the right to manage and conserve natural resources within the ancestral domain for their future generations.
- **Right to benefit sharing** — the right to an equitable share in benefits accruing from the development of their ancestral domains.
- **Right to receive royalties** — as provided by law (e.g., 1% gross output from mineral extraction under R.A. 7942 §16).
- **Right to claim, reclaim, and rehabilitate** — including the right to claim portions of ancestral domains declared as national parks, forests, watersheds, or reservations, subject to existing certifications.
- **Right to regulate entry** of migrants/settlers in the ancestral domain.
- **Right to maintain and protect** the ecology of the ancestral domain.

### 2. Right to Self-Governance and Empowerment (§§13–20)
- ICCs/IPs have the right to use their own commonly accepted justice systems, conflict resolution institutions, peace-building processes, and other customary laws.
- The State recognizes the right of ICCs/IPs to self-governance — to maintain their indigenous political structures, determine their own membership, and manage their affairs according to traditional laws.
- **Recognition of customary law**: Courts and administrative bodies must consider customary laws and practices when interpreting rights within ancestral domains.

### 3. Social Justice and Human Rights (§§21–28)
- Priority rights of ICCs/IPs in the use and enjoyment of natural resources within ancestral domains.
- Right to equal protection of the law without discrimination.
- Right to education using their own language and teaching methodologies.
- Right to indigenous schools, curricula, and teaching approaches.
- Right against forced relocations from ancestral domains (except for reasons of national defense, upon payment of just compensation).

### 4. Cultural Integrity (§§29–37)
- Right to practice and revitalize customs, traditions, spirituality, and cultural expressions.
- Right to receive and control traditional knowledge.
- Right to maintain and protect cultural sites, sacred grounds, burial sites, and heritage resources.
- Right to indigenous intellectual property.
- Protection of indigenous designs, ceremonies, and forms of artistic expression from appropriation.

## Free, Prior and Informed Consent (FPIC) — Full Framework (§§59, 65; NCIP Administrative Order No. 03-12)

### What is FPIC?
FPIC is the right of ICCs/IPs to give or withhold consent to any activity affecting their ancestral domains or their collective rights, *prior to* the commencement of that activity. It is not a mere consultation — it is a consent requirement; the activity may not proceed over the objection of the ICC/IP.

**Free** — consent given without coercion, intimidation, manipulation, or undue inducement.
**Prior** — consent sought and obtained *before* the commencement of any activity (not simultaneously, and not after the fact).
**Informed** — the ICC/IP has been provided, in a manner they understand, with full information about the nature, scope, purpose, duration, location, and potential impacts of the proposed activity.

### Activities Requiring FPIC (§59)
- **Research, bioprospecting, or study** of biological and genetic resources within ancestral domains.
- **Displacement and relocation** of ICCs/IPs.
- **Exploration, development, exploitation** of natural resources within ancestral domains (mining, energy, water, forestry).
- **Construction** of infrastructure projects within ancestral domains.
- Any government or private activity that may affect the rights, interests, or welfare of the ICC/IP communities within the ancestral domain.

### FPIC Process
1. **Applicant files with NCIP** — mandatory pre-application validation of ancestral domain coverage.
2. **NCIP issues Certification of Ancestral Domain** — confirms whether proposed area is within ancestral domain.
3. **NCIP conducts community assembly** — using the ICC/IP's traditional decision-making processes; all affected community members must participate.
4. **Informed deliberations** — the applicant presents the proposed activity; Q&A; community discussion using the IP's own language.
5. **Consensus-based decision** — following the community's customary decision-making process.
6. **Result**: FPIC granted (with conditions/MOA), FPIC modified, or FPIC withheld (the process ends; activity may not proceed).
7. **Certification Precondition (CP)** — NCIP issues a CP to the proponent confirming FPIC was obtained; government agencies (DENR, mines office, ECC-issuing body) require the CP before processing permits.

**FPIC cannot be waived by the NCIP on behalf of the ICC/IP** — the right belongs to the community; only the community can grant or withhold it.

**Absence of FPIC** = ground for cancellation or suspension of the relevant permit, license, or agreement. Agencies that issue permits without a CP from NCIP may be liable for grave abuse of discretion; the permit itself may be void.

## CADT and CALT — Titling System

### Certificate of Ancestral Domain Title (CADT) (§§11–12)
- **Issued by**: NCIP.
- **Covers**: The entire ancestral domain of the ICC/IP community (collective title; the land is *communally* owned).
- **Legal character**: Recognizes the native title of the ICC/IP over their ancestral domain; *not* a Torrens title under P.D. 1529 and is *not* registered with the Land Registration Authority.
- **Alienability**: Ancestral domains are **inalienable** — they cannot be sold, transferred, or otherwise disposed of except to members of the same ICC/IP community in accordance with their customary laws. They cannot be foreclosed, mortgaged, or subjected to levy or execution.
- **Delineation process**: Conducted by NCIP; boundary surveys; ethnographic studies; community ancestral domain management plan; publication; public notice; resolution of overlapping claims.

### Certificate of Ancestral Land Title (CALT) (§§11–12)
- **Covers**: Individual or family parcels *within* the ancestral domain, held by specific ICC/IP members under the community's customary laws.
- **Legal character**: Also native title, not Torrens.
- **More susceptible to individual transfer** within the community per customary law, but still subject to the ancestral domain's overall inalienability to non-members.

### CADT/CALT vs. Torrens Title
| Feature | CADT/CALT | Torrens Title (P.D. 1529) |
|---|---|---|
| Issuing body | NCIP | Land Registration Authority |
| Legal basis | Native title / IPRA | Public land alienation / court registration |
| Alienability | Inalienable to non-members | Freely transferable |
| Coverage | Collective (CADT); individual within community (CALT) | Individual parcel |
| Forum for disputes | NCIP (in the first instance for ancestral domain disputes) | RTC (Land Registration Court) |
| Interaction | Ancestral domains are NOT public domain — not covered by P.D. 1529 at all | Does not override CADT |

## NCIP Jurisdiction and the Exhaustion Doctrine (§66)
The NCIP has jurisdiction over all claims and disputes involving rights of ICCs/IPs. The NCIP's jurisdiction is not exclusive — after NCIP proceedings, appeal lies to the Court of Appeals. However, parties must **first exhaust remedies before the NCIP** before resort to courts, where the dispute falls within NCIP's statutory jurisdiction (ancestral domain boundaries, FPIC disputes, ICC/IP membership disputes, customary law issues).

**NCIP Regional Hearing Officer (RHO)** → **NCIP En Banc** → **Court of Appeals** → **Supreme Court**.

## IPRA and the Regalian Doctrine — Conflict Resolution
The Regalian Doctrine holds that all natural resources belong to the State. IPRA effectively carves out ancestral domains from the public domain — they are not State property; they belong to the ICC/IP community under native title. This creates apparent tension that the IPRA resolves by:
1. Declaring ancestral domains private property of the ICC/IP *prior to* and *independent of* any Spanish/American grant.
2. Providing that no classification of lands as forest reserve, national park, or mineral reserve may override CADT rights without FPIC.
3. Allowing the State to exercise eminent domain within ancestral domains only for national interest purposes and with full FPIC consultation and compensation.

The *Cruz v. DENR* deadlock (2000) means this tension remains legally unresolved at the SC level, but IPRA operates as valid law; agencies and courts apply it.

## IPRA and Mining Interface (Summary — Full Treatment in mining-law.md)
Mining in ancestral domains requires: FPIC from the ICC/IP (NCIP-certified CP); Mining rights granted by DENR-MGB; government and royalty shares to ICC/IP. R.A. 7942 §16 mandates 1% gross output royalty to the ICC/IP. The Certification Precondition from NCIP is a prerequisite to any mining permit or FTAA within an ancestral domain. *See `environmental-law/mining-law.md` §68–76 for detailed mining-FPIC interface; this module does not duplicate that content.*

## Anti-Hallucination Guardrails — IPRA
Ancestral domains are **not public domain land** — they are the private collective property of the ICC/IP under native title; do not apply the Regalian Doctrine to extinguish IPRA rights. *Cruz v. Secretary of DENR* did **not** declare IPRA unconstitutional — the SC tied 7–7 (neither majority to sustain nor to annul); IPRA remains valid law. FPIC is a **consent requirement**, not merely a consultation — the activity may not proceed if FPIC is withheld; never state that government may override FPIC by proceeding over objection. The Certification Precondition (CP) from NCIP is issued **to the project proponent** after FPIC is obtained — it is not a CP issued to the government agency; the permitting agency requires the CP as a condition before issuing its own permit. CADT and CALT are **not Torrens titles** and are not registered with the LRA — do not apply P.D. 1529 principles (Torrens system indefeasibility, prescription, assurance fund) to CADT/CALT disputes. The 1% royalty under R.A. 7942 §16 is **gross output** from mining within ancestral domains — it is separate from and in addition to government taxes and fees; never conflate it with income taxes or royalty taxes payable to the State. NCIP jurisdiction over ancestral domain disputes does not automatically exclude courts — after NCIP proceedings are exhausted, the CA has appellate jurisdiction; do not state courts have no role.
