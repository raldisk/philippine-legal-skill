# Legal Research Module

## Authority Hierarchy — Definitive Version

Always identify the strongest available authority before proceeding to weaker sources. Never substitute a secondary source for an available primary source.

### Tier 1 — Mandatory Primary Authority
- 1987 Constitution (supreme)
- Prior constitutions (1935, 1973) where historically operative
- Ratified and in-force treaties
- Statutes enacted by Congress (Republic Acts, Commonwealth Acts, Batas Pambansa)

### Tier 2 — Binding Judicial Authority
- Supreme Court En Banc decisions (binding on all lower courts and parties)
- Supreme Court Division decisions (binding; subject to En Banc modification or reversal)
- Note: the most recent En Banc decision on a doctrinal point controls over earlier decisions, even if the earlier decision is more frequently cited

### Tier 3 — Authoritative Interpretive Material
- Constitutional Commission (1986) deliberations and records — interpretive weight, not binding
- Constitutional Convention records (1934, 1971) — interpretive weight
- COMELEC resolutions within COMELEC's jurisdiction
- COA circulars and decisions within COA's jurisdiction
- CSC resolutions within CSC's jurisdiction
- DOJ opinions — persuasive, not binding on courts

### Tier 4 — Subordinate Authority
- Executive Orders, Administrative Orders, Proclamations — subordinate to statutes
- IATF and inter-agency issuances — subordinate to enabling authority
- Administrative regulations of line agencies

### Tier 5 — Persuasive Authority Only
- US Supreme Court decisions (especially pre-1935, given shared doctrinal roots)
- Spanish colonial-era decisions (historical/civil law context)
- Commonwealth parliamentary practice
- Decisions of international tribunals (ICC, ICJ, PCA) — binding internationally; domestically persuasive unless transformed by statute

### Tier 6 — Secondary Sources
- Philippine legal textbooks (Bernas, Cruz, Nachura, Agpalo, etc.)
- Law review articles and legal commentaries
- Bar exam suggested answers
- IBP opinions

### NOT LEGAL AUTHORITY — Never cite as such
- News articles, regardless of publication
- Speeches and press statements of officials
- Social media posts
- Political party platforms
- Opinions of non-legal experts on legal questions

## Source Verification Protocol

Before relying on any case:

**Step 1 — Verify GR/AM/AC number**: Confirm via the SC E-Library (elibrary.judiciary.gov.ph). Never cite a GR number from memory without high confidence it is accurate. If uncertain: cite only case name, parties, and year; note "GR number not confirmed."

**Step 2 — Verify holding vs. dicta**: Read the dispositive portion. The holding is what the case decided; dicta is explanatory language not necessary to the decision. Only the holding has precedential weight.

**Step 3 — Check for subsequent modification**: Has a later en banc decision modified, limited, or overruled this case? This is the most common research error — citing superseded doctrine.

**Step 4 — Identify the decision level**: En banc vs. division. An en banc decision on the same point controls over a division decision.

**Step 5 — Identify the constitutional text**: If the case was decided under a different constitutional provision (e.g., 1935 or 1973), assess whether the 1987 counterpart is materially identical before applying the case.

## Primary Research Sources

**SC E-Library** (elibrary.judiciary.gov.ph): Full text of Supreme Court decisions from 1901 onward. Primary source for case verification.

**Official Gazette** (officialgazette.gov.ph): Laws, executive issuances, official proclamations.

**Congress.gov.ph**: Full text of bills, committee reports, session journals.

**Senate.gov.ph**: Senate records, deliberations, committee reports.

**COMELEC** (comelec.gov.ph): Resolutions, rules, decisions.

**COA** (coa.gov.ph): Circulars, decisions.

**DOJ** (doj.gov.ph): Department circulars, opinions.

## AHAC Reasoning Structure

For every legal issue, structure internal reasoning as:

**Authority**: What provision, statute, or case governs?

**Holding**: What does the authority establish? (For cases: what was decided, not what was said)

**Application**: How does the holding apply to the facts of the present issue?

**Conclusion**: What result follows?

This structure forces identification of authority before conclusion. Never invert — never start from a desired conclusion and search for supporting authority.

## Research Workflow — Eight Phases ⚠️ REQUIRED

**Phase 1 — Issue Identification**: State the precise legal question(s) in terms of legal rights and duties, not in political terms. "Whether the President's proclamation of martial law in Mindanao satisfied the factual basis requirement of Article VII, §18" — not "whether martial law was justified."

**Phase 2 — Constitutional Analysis**: Identify all relevant constitutional provisions. Check Article III (bill of rights), Article VI (legislature), Article VII (executive), Article VIII (judiciary), Article IX (constitutional commissions), Article X (local government), Article XI (accountability), and any transitory provisions.

**Phase 3 — Statutory Analysis**: Identify governing statutes. Confirm that the statute is currently in force (not repealed or amended into irrelevance). Identify the specific provision(s).

**Phase 4 — Regulatory Analysis**: Identify implementing regulations, if any. Confirm they are consistent with the enabling statute (ultra vires check).

**Phase 5 — Jurisprudence Analysis**: Identify controlling en banc decisions. Identify division decisions if no en banc decision exists. Check for doctrinal evolution — is the case still good law?

**Phase 6 — Conflicting Authority**: Identify any conflicting precedents. Explain how the conflict is resolved (later-in-time, higher authority, more specific provision).

**Phase 7 — Doctrine Application**: Apply the AHAC structure. State the strongest argument for each side. Do not strawman.

**Phase 8 — Confidence Assessment**: Assign a score per the confidence scale (see crisis-frameworks.md). State the basis for the score.

## Common Research Errors — Anti-Patterns

**Citing superseded doctrine**: Citing an older case without checking whether it has been modified or overruled. Always check doctrinal evolution.

**Holding-dicta confusion**: Treating explanatory language in a decision as the case's holding. Only the dispositive portion and the ratio decidendi (reasoning necessary to the decision) carry precedential weight.

**Division-for-en-banc substitution**: Citing a division decision when an en banc decision on the same issue exists. En banc always controls.

**Secondary-for-primary substitution**: Citing a textbook's characterization of a case instead of the case itself. The textbook may be wrong, outdated, or may paraphrase inaccurately.

**Political-statement-as-authority**: Treating a Senator's floor speech, a PCOO statement, or a presidential spokesperson's press release as a statement of law. These are political facts, not legal authority.

**Fabricated authority**: Inventing GR numbers, holdings, or quotations to fill gaps. This is the single most serious error. When authority cannot be confirmed, say so.

**Constitutional-provision mismatch**: Applying a doctrine built on the 1935 Constitution to a 1987 provision without verifying that the provisions are materially identical.
