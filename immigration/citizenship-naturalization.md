# Citizenship and Naturalization

## Primary Sources
**1987 Constitution (Art. IV)** — defines Philippine citizenship; modes of acquisition. **Commonwealth Act 473 (Revised Naturalization Law)** — judicial naturalization for resident aliens. **R.A. 9139 (Administrative Naturalization Law of 2000)** — administrative naturalization for certain long-term resident aliens. **R.A. 9225 (Citizenship Retention and Re-Acquisition Act, 2003)** — reacquisition of Philippine citizenship by natural-born Filipinos.

---

## Citizenship — Constitutional Framework
**Citizens by birth (Art. IV §1)**:
1. Those whose fathers or mothers are citizens (jus sanguinis — blood-based, gender-neutral under 1987 Constitution).
2. Those born before January 17, 1973 of Filipino mothers who elected Philippine citizenship upon reaching age of majority.
3. Those who are naturalized as provided by law.

**No jus soli**: The Philippines does not follow jus soli (citizenship by birthplace) — being born in the Philippines to alien parents does not confer citizenship.

---

## Judicial Naturalization (C.A. 473)
**Requirements**: Resident in Philippines for ≥ 10 years (reduced to 5 years for certain favored nationalities/categories); own real estate or known lucrative trade; able to speak Filipino or any principal dialect; enrolled children in Philippine schools; good moral character; not disqualified.

**Disqualifications**: Polygamists; convicted of crimes involving moral turpitude; persons opposing organized government; advocates of violence; who are mentally alienated; nationals of countries at war with Philippines.

**Procedure**: Petition filed with RTC; published in Official Gazette; hearing; decision; appeal period (2 years for government to contest); oath-taking; certificate of naturalization.

---

## Administrative Naturalization (R.A. 9139)
**Eligibility**: Born in Philippines; resident for ≥ 30 years; no disqualifying factors; files application with the Committee on Naturalization (chaired by Solicitor General). Simpler and faster than judicial naturalization; for aliens born in Philippines with long residence.

---

## Dual Citizenship — R.A. 9225
**Natural-born Filipinos**: Those who lost Philippine citizenship through naturalization in a foreign country may **reacquire or retain** Philippine citizenship by taking an Oath of Allegiance before a Philippine consular officer or civil registrar.

**Effects**: Full rights of Philippine citizenship restored including right to own land, vote, and hold public office (subject to other laws; e.g., public office requires renouncing foreign citizenship and establishing residency).

---

## Loss of Citizenship
**Voluntary acts (C.A. 63)**: Naturalization in a foreign country; express renunciation; subscribing to oath of allegiance to foreign state; rendering military service in a foreign country; serving in government of foreign country.

**Recovery**: Under R.A. 9225 for natural-born Filipinos; judicial naturalization or administrative naturalization for others.

---

## Anti-Hallucination Guardrails — Citizenship and Naturalization
The Philippines follows jus sanguinis — birth in Philippine territory to alien parents does not confer citizenship. The 1987 Constitution made citizenship acquisition gender-neutral — the requirement of Filipino father (under prior constitutions) no longer applies for children born from 1987. R.A. 9225 applies only to **natural-born** Filipinos — naturalized Filipinos who lost citizenship through naturalization abroad cannot reacquire under R.A. 9225 but may re-naturalize under C.A. 473. Dual citizenship under R.A. 9225 does not automatically restore all rights — election participation requires actual residency and non-use of foreign passport; public office requires an additional renunciation step. Never state that naturalization is merely an executive act — judicial naturalization under C.A. 473 is a judicial proceeding subject to appeal.
---

## Citizenship — Deep Dive

**Jus sanguinis application**: Under the 1987 Constitution, Philippine citizenship by birth flows from either parent — father OR mother. This eliminated the pre-1987 rule that only a Filipino father automatically conferred citizenship; Filipino mothers previously could only transmit citizenship by election. **Note**: Persons born before January 17, 1973 of Filipino mothers must have elected Philippine citizenship upon reaching majority to be citizens.

**Election of citizenship (Art. IV §1[2])**: Must be performed upon reaching the age of majority (18); formal written election filed with the civil registry; must be followed by acts of allegiance (in practice: registration as voter, obtaining Philippine passport). *The Supreme Court has liberally construed "timely" election to include reasonable periods after majority — verify specific cases.*

---

## Naturalization Process — Judicial (C.A. 473)

**Step-by-step procedure**:
1. File petition in RTC of the province/city where applicant has resided for at least 1 year immediately prior to filing.
2. BI and DOJ must be served with copies.
3. Publication in Official Gazette and newspaper of general circulation for 3 consecutive weeks before the hearing.
4. Hearing — applicant testifies; character witnesses (minimum 2 Filipino citizens) testify.
5. Decision — RTC decision on the merits.
6. Appeal period — 30 days for government to appeal; 2-year probationary period before oath.
7. Oath of allegiance — after 2 years and satisfactory compliance; certificate of naturalization issued.

**Two-year probationary period**: Between RTC decision and oath-taking, the applicant must: (a) not have departed from the Philippines; (b) dedicated himself to a lawful calling or profession; (c) not have been convicted of any offense or violated any government promulgated rules; (d) not committed any act prejudicial to the interests of the country.

**Denaturalization (§18 C.A. 473)**: Government may file petition to cancel certificate of naturalization within 5 years of issuance on grounds of: fraud in obtaining naturalization; violation of conditions of naturalization; registration in a foreign consulate; subscribing to oath of allegiance to foreign state; deserting from military service in time of war.

---

## R.A. 9225 — Dual Citizenship in Detail

**Who qualifies**: Natural-born Filipinos — those who were citizens at birth under the 1935, 1973, or 1987 Constitutions. Does NOT include naturalized Filipino citizens who lost citizenship by acquiring foreign citizenship — they must re-naturalize.

**Process for reacquisition**:
1. File application at Philippine Consulate or Embassy (if abroad) or BI (if in Philippines).
2. Execute Oath of Allegiance to the Republic of the Philippines.
3. Submit proof of natural-born Philippine citizenship (birth certificate, old Philippine passport, CENOMAR, etc.).
4. Identification Certificate issued by consulate/BI.

**Derivative citizenship for minor children**: Unmarried children under 18 of persons who reacquire citizenship under R.A. 9225 are deemed citizens of the Philippines — no separate application needed if born after the parent lost citizenship.

**Voting rights**: Dual citizens may register as overseas voters under R.A. 9189 (Overseas Absentee Voting Act); must comply with OAV registration requirements; may vote in Philippine national and local elections from abroad.

---

## Public Office and R.A. 9225

**Constitutional restrictions**: Public officers must be Philippine citizens; natural-born citizenship required for President, VP, Senators, Representatives, SC Justices, and other constitutional offices.

**Renunciation for public office**: Under Valles v. COMELEC and subsequent jurisprudence — a dual citizen who is a natural-born Filipino must renounce foreign citizenship and execute an Oath of Renunciation of Foreign Citizenship before assuming any elected or appointed public office. Failure to renounce: disqualification.

**Solo renunciation**: Renouncing foreign citizenship under R.A. 9225 for purposes of public office must be unequivocal and clear; applying for a foreign passport after renouncing is deemed retraction.

---

## Foundlings and Stateless Persons

**Foundlings**: Under the 2014 COMELEC resolution and SC ruling in *Poe-Llamanzares v. COMELEC* (GR 221697, 2016): Foundlings born in the Philippines are presumed to be natural-born Philippine citizens absent evidence to the contrary. R.A. 11767 (Foundling Recognition and Protection Act, 2022) codifies this presumption.

**Stateless persons**: Persons with no recognized citizenship anywhere. Philippines is a party to the 1954 Convention Relating to the Status of Stateless Persons; UNHCR coordinates identification and protection. Stateless persons in the Philippines may apply for regularization or naturalization; subject to BI and DOJ procedures.

---

## Recognition of Foreign Citizenship

**Rules on recognition**: Filipino who acquires foreign citizenship without going through R.A. 9225 reacquisition — remains a Filipino for Philippine law purposes (dual citizenship problem); but foreign state may also claim the person as their national. Philippine courts apply the principle of "dominant and effective nationality" in private international law conflicts.

**Conflict of laws in succession**: Filipino who becomes a US citizen and dies in the Philippines — estate governed by Philippine law (Art. 16 NCC: real property in Philippines governed by Philippine law; personal property by national law of decedent — which is contested in dual citizenship cases). Philippine courts tend to apply Philippine law to estate administration proceedings conducted within Philippine jurisdiction.

---

## Refugees and Asylum Seekers

**Philippine refugee law**: R.A. 8072 (pending as of knowledge cutoff, but executive policy through EO and DOJ circulars governs); Philippines is party to the 1951 Refugee Convention and 1967 Protocol. UNHCR Philippines conducts refugee status determination in coordination with DOJ.

**Asylum procedure**: Application filed with DOJ-Refugee and Stateless Persons Protection Unit; interview; status determination; recognition grants Convention Travel Document (in lieu of passport). Recognized refugees entitled to work in the Philippines; may not be deported to countries where they face persecution (non-refoulement principle).

**Non-refoulement**: Absolute principle — Philippines cannot return a person to a country where they face a real risk of persecution, torture, or other serious harm. Non-refoulement applies even to persons not formally recognized as refugees.

## Key Cases — Citizenship and Naturalization

**Poe-Llamanzares v. COMELEC** (GR 221697, 2016): Grace Poe as foundling — SC held she is a natural-born Filipino citizen based on presumption; she reacquired Filipino citizenship under R.A. 9225; she established residency sufficient to run for President. Key ruling on foundlings and reacquisition of citizenship.

**Valles v. COMELEC** (GR 137000, 2000): Dual citizenship from birth (not through voluntary acquisition of foreign citizenship) is not a disqualification for running for public office; only voluntarily acquired foreign citizenship requires renunciation for public office.

**David v. Agbay** (GR 199113, 2015): A naturalized foreign citizen who reacquired Philippine citizenship under R.A. 9225 is considered a natural-born Filipino for purposes of land ownership upon reacquisition; retroactive effect of reacquisition is limited — land ownership rights attach upon reacquisition, not retroactively.

## Political Rights of Citizens

**Voting (R.A. 8189, R.A. 9189)**: Philippine citizens 18 years or older may register and vote. OFWs and dual citizens abroad may vote via overseas absentee voting (OAV) for national positions (President, VP, Senators, Party-list). OAV registration through COMELEC; voting at embassies/consulates or by personal delivery of ballot.

**Holding public office**: Natural-born citizenship required for enumerated positions. Dual citizens must renounce foreign citizenship before assuming public office. Naturalized citizens are not natural-born — ineligible for positions requiring natural-born status.

**Running for elected office**: Citizenship, residency, and age requirements must all be satisfied. COMELEC determines qualifications; dual citizenship issues frequently litigated (see *Valles*, *Reyes v. COMELEC*).

## Loss and Reacquisition — Practical Applications

**Reacquisition under R.A. 9225 for US citizens of Filipino origin**: Many Filipinos who naturalized in the US use R.A. 9225 to restore Philippine citizenship; allows ownership of land; avoids FIRB approval for certain investments; allows shorter processing times at BI; allows use of Philippine passport. Process: Oath of Allegiance at Philippine Consulate in the US; Identification Certificate issued; annotated birth certificate from PSA.

**Effect on property**: Upon reacquisition under R.A. 9225, former Filipino may acquire residential property up to constitutional limits (1,000 sqm urban; 1 hectare rural) as provided in R.A. 8179. Former Filipino reacquiring citizenship may also purchase land without the FIRB limits applicable to foreign nationals.

**Professional practice**: Natural-born Filipinos who reacquire citizenship under R.A. 9225 may practice their profession in the Philippines subject to PRC requirements. Some PRC-regulated professions require Philippine citizenship as a prerequisite — reacquisition under R.A. 9225 restores this eligibility.

---

## Citizenship of Corporations

**Filipino equity requirement for corporations**: Filipinos owning 60%+ of voting stock — corporation is Filipino for purposes of constitutional nationality requirements. R.A. 9225 reacquirees who are dual citizens count as Filipinos for equity purposes.

**Aliens in corporations**: Foreign nationals holding shares in Philippine corporations must comply with FIA limits. Shares held by alien natural persons plus shares held by alien entities — aggregated for foreign equity computation.

**Corporate citizenship of listed companies**: *Gamboa v. Teves* (GR 176579, 2012): "Capital" in Art. XII §11 (public utilities) means the voting shares only — Filipinos must own 60% of voting shares, not merely 60% of all shares. PLDT-related ruling that changed computation methodology.

## Anti-Hallucination Guardrails — Consolidated Citizenship
Jus sanguinis is the Philippine rule — birth in the Philippines to alien parents does not confer citizenship; never confuse with US jus soli. R.A. 9225 applies ONLY to natural-born Filipinos — those who were Filipino at birth and subsequently lost citizenship; NOT to naturalized Filipinos who later acquired foreign nationality. Election of citizenship (for persons born before 1973 of Filipino mothers) must be done upon reaching majority — courts have allowed reasonable delay but the election must be followed by affirmative acts of allegiance. Dual citizens who want to run for public office MUST execute an Oath of Renunciation of Foreign Citizenship — holding dual citizenship alone is not a bar to running but taking office without renunciation IS. Derivative citizenship of minor children under R.A. 9225 is automatic — no separate application required; extends to unmarried children under 18.

---

## Philippine Passport and Travel Documents

**Philippine passport**: Issued by DFA to Philippine citizens; evidence of citizenship; 5-year validity for minors under 18; 10-year validity for adults 18+. Holders of R.A. 9225 Identification Certificates may apply for a Philippine passport using that IC as basis.

**Machine-readable passports**: Philippines issues e-passports with biometric chip; required for visa-free entry to some countries (e.g., Schengen area under conditions). Lost Philippine passport abroad — report to nearest Philippine Embassy or Consulate; emergency travel document for return.

**Convention Travel Document**: Issued to recognized refugees by the Philippines — allows travel without a national passport; Philippines honors 1951 Refugee Convention obligations.
