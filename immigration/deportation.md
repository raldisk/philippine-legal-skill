# Deportation

## Primary Sources
**Commonwealth Act 613 §§37–40** — grounds, procedure, and effects of deportation. **Bureau of Immigration** — files complaints and executes deportation orders. **Board of Commissioners (BOC)** — hears and decides deportation cases quasi-judicially.

---

## Grounds for Deportation (§37 CA 613)
Aliens may be deported for:
- Entering the Philippines by fraud or misrepresentation.
- Entering without inspection or admission.
- Being convicted of crimes involving moral turpitude (CIMT) within 5 years of entry.
- Convicted of two or more offenses at any time after entry.
- Being an economic saboteur.
- Violating the terms of a non-immigrant visa (e.g., unauthorized employment).
- Prolonged overstaying.
- Becoming a public charge within 5 years of entry due to causes arising before entry.
- Engaging in subversive activities.

**CIMT**: Not defined in CA 613; courts and BI apply a definition similar to US jurisprudence — crimes inherently base, vile, or depraved, contrary to the accepted rules of morality.

---

## Deportation Procedure
1. Filing of deportation complaint with the BI Commissioner.
2. **Summary deportation**: BI Commissioner may summarily deport aliens who entered illegally or overstayed — no hearing required for these clear violations.
3. **Regular deportation proceedings**: For contested cases — BOC conducts hearing; alien given opportunity to be heard.
4. **Warrant of arrest**: BI may issue a warrant of arrest against an alien against whom a deportation charge has been filed, pending hearing.
5. **Deportation order**: BOC issues deportation order on finding of grounds.
6. **Appeal**: To the Secretary of Justice; then to the courts (CA, then SC) on certiorari for grave abuse of discretion.

---

## Effects of Deportation
**Blacklisting**: Deported alien placed on the immigration blacklist; bars readmission without specific clearance.

**Bail**: Alien arrested on deportation charges may be released on bail pending resolution.

**Constitutional due process**: An alien who has established domicile in the Philippines is entitled to notice and hearing before deportation. *Qua Chee Gan v. Deportation Board* (GR L-10280): domiciled aliens have due process rights. *Commissioner of Immigration v. Fernandez* [verify GR]: BI has inherent power to deport but subject to due process.

---

## Anti-Hallucination Guardrails — Deportation
Summary deportation applies only to clear cases of illegal entry and overstay — it does not apply to aliens claiming immigrant status or lawful residence; those cases require regular proceedings. Deportation is an administrative proceeding, not criminal — the standard of proof is preponderance of evidence, not proof beyond reasonable doubt. A deported alien may seek judicial review of the deportation order by filing a petition for certiorari — courts review for grave abuse of discretion, not de novo. Blacklisting is distinct from deportation — an alien may be blacklisted without a deportation order and vice versa. CIMT as a deportation ground applies to convictions for crimes committed within 5 years of entry; later convictions for two or more offenses have a different time frame.
---

## Crimes Involving Moral Turpitude (CIMT) — Analysis

**Definition**: No statutory definition; courts and BI adopt a case-by-case analysis. Generally: an act involving fraud, dishonesty, or grave moral depravity. Philippine courts have followed US immigration law precedents: theft, fraud, perjury, robbery — CIMT. Traffic violations, simple possession of drugs (contested) — not CIMT in some formulations.

**Conviction requirement**: CIMT as a deportation ground requires actual conviction by a court of competent jurisdiction; arrest, indictment, or charge alone insufficient. Conviction abroad: treated equivalently if in a country with fair judicial process.

**5-year rule**: Conviction for CIMT within 5 years of entry — deportation ground. Conviction for CIMT more than 5 years after entry: not CIMT deportation ground under §37, BUT conviction for TWO OR MORE offenses is a separate deportation ground with no time limit.

## Immigration Holding Areas and Detention

**BI Warden Facility**: Aliens under deportation proceedings or pending exclusion at the border may be detained at the Bureau of Immigration Warden Facility (BIWF) in Bicutan, Taguig, or at other designated holding facilities.

**Limits on detention**: *Mejoff v. Director of Prisons* (GR L-4254): Indefinite detention pending deportation is unconstitutional; habeas corpus lies for unreasonably prolonged detention. Post-*Mejoff*: BI must act within reasonable time; alien may be released on bail or recognizance pending deportation.

**Bail for immigration purposes**: BI may fix bail for aliens arrested on deportation charges; bail amount at BI Commissioner's discretion; bail may be in cash, surety bond, or property bond. Release on bail does not affect the deportation proceeding — it merely allows the alien to remain at liberty pending resolution.

**Humanitarian considerations**: Aliens with serious illness, pregnant aliens, elderly aliens, and unaccompanied minors — BI has protocols for humane treatment; UNHCR monitors for asylum seekers in detention; DSWD coordinates for minor detainees.

---

## Voluntary Departure

**Definition**: Alien who is the subject of deportation proceedings may voluntarily agree to leave the Philippines before a final deportation order; BI may grant voluntary departure subject to conditions (posting of departure bond; surrender of travel documents; confirmed booking).

**Advantages**: Avoids formal deportation order; may avoid blacklisting or reduce the period of inadmissibility; voluntary departure at own expense.

**Disadvantages**: If the alien fails to depart by the agreed date — the voluntary departure privilege is revoked; formal deportation order issued; blacklisted.

---

## Deportation of Spouses and Dependents

**Derivative deportation**: If the principal alien is deported, dependents holding derivative visas may also be subject to deportation proceedings. Dependents must have independent grounds to remain in the Philippines (e.g., §13[a] as spouse of a Filipino citizen) or must also depart.

**Child deportation considerations**: Alien child with a Filipino parent — courts have held that the best interests of the child must be considered; deportation proceedings must consider the child's ties to the Philippines; SC has allowed alien children to remain where deportation would be a disproportionate hardship.

---

## Special Deportation Cases — Blacklist and Persona Non Grata

**Blacklist**: BI maintains an immigration blacklist of deported aliens, persons with prior immigration violations, and security risks. Entry by a blacklisted alien is refused at the border. Blacklisting is an administrative act; may be lifted by BI Commissioner upon application showing changed circumstances.

**Persona Non Grata (PNG) declaration**: The Philippine government (through DFA) may declare a foreign national PNG — requiring immediate departure; usually for diplomats or foreign officials whose presence is contrary to the interests of the Philippines. DFA coordinates with BI for implementation.

**Expulsion of diplomats**: Under Vienna Convention on Diplomatic Relations, host state may declare a diplomat persona non grata at any time without explanation; the sending state must recall them or their diplomatic status ends.

---

## Immigration Violations — Administrative vs. Criminal

**Administrative violations**: Overstaying; failure to report; unauthorized employment; violation of visa conditions — handled by BI as administrative proceedings; fine, extension, or deportation order.

**Criminal violations (CA 613 §39–45)**: Persons who assist, harbor, or conceal illegal aliens — criminal liability; fine and imprisonment. Aliens who fraudulently obtain visas — criminal liability separate from deportation proceedings.

**Syndicated immigration fraud**: Large-scale fraudulent visa operations; human trafficking for immigration fraud — prosecuted under Anti-Trafficking in Persons Act (R.A. 9208 as amended by R.A. 10364/11862) and CA 613; BI and NBI coordinate.

---

## Post-Deportation Effects

**Period of inadmissibility**: Deported aliens generally cannot return to the Philippines without prior clearance from the BI Commissioner; no automatic re-entry right. Period varies: some orders impose permanent bar; others impose a specified number of years.

**Clearance for return**: Former deportee may apply for BI Commissioner's clearance; grounds for clearance: compassionate grounds (Filipino family); economic contribution; passage of substantial time; changed circumstances. Clearance application heard and decided by BI; appealable to DOJ.

**Effect on property**: Deportation does not automatically forfeit property of the alien. Property owned in accordance with law remains subject to the alien's ownership. However, if the alien was not entitled to own the property under constitutional limits — forfeiture may be ordered separately in a proper proceeding.

## Key Cases — Deportation

**Board of Commissioners v. Dela Rosa** (GR 95122, 1991): Children of Chinese nationals — BI's determination of citizenship questions in deportation proceedings is subject to judicial review; BI cannot unilaterally deprive persons of citizenship claims without proper hearing.

**Domingo v. Scheer** (GR 154745, 2004): Due process for aliens — alien domiciled in the Philippines who has obtained legal status has stronger due process rights than a new entrant; notice and hearing must be genuine, not perfunctory.

**Harvey v. Santiago** (GR 82544, 1988): American national charged with pedophilia deported; SC upheld deportation; BI's power to deport aliens who violate Philippine laws is broad; conviction in Philippine court is not a prerequisite for deportation where grounds are clear.

**Qua Chee Gan v. Deportation Board** (GR L-10280, 1958): Alien domiciliary has constitutional right to liberty; deportation proceeding must afford notice and meaningful opportunity to be heard; arrest pending deportation must be based on sufficient grounds; alien entitled to judicial review.

## Summary Reference — Deportation Grounds and Procedures

| Ground | Time Limit from Entry | Procedure |
|---|---|---|
| Illegal entry / entry by fraud | No time limit | Summary or regular proceedings |
| Overstaying | No time limit | Summary for clear cases |
| CIMT conviction | Within 5 years of entry | Regular BOC proceedings |
| Two or more offenses | Any time after entry | Regular BOC proceedings |
| Unauthorized employment | No time limit | Regular or summary |
| Economic sabotage | No time limit | Regular BOC proceedings |
| Subversive activities | No time limit | Regular BOC proceedings |

**Due process requirement**: Summary deportation limited to illegal entry and clear overstay. All other grounds require regular BOC proceedings with notice, hearing, and opportunity to respond.

## Anti-Trafficking vs. Deportation

**Trafficking victims**: Aliens who are victims of trafficking may not be deported without proper determination of their status. IACAT (Inter-Agency Council Against Trafficking) protocol requires identification of potential trafficking victims before any deportation action; BI coordinates with DSWD and DOJ.

**Non-punishment principle**: Trafficking victims who entered the Philippines illegally or violated immigration laws as a consequence of being trafficked — BI must not punish them for immigration violations attributable to their trafficking situation.

**Witness protection**: Alien trafficking victim who agrees to testify against their traffickers may be granted a special stay of deportation and work authorization pending the criminal proceedings; DOJ-IACAT coordinates.

---

## Immigration Enforcement Coordination

**BI and NBI**: Joint operations against illegal aliens and immigration fraud syndicates; NBI-Immigration Fraud Division coordinates with BI; criminal cases for immigration fraud filed in regular courts.

**BI and PDEA**: Coordination for alien drug offenders — alien convicted of drug offenses subject to deportation; PDEA may request BI hold on departure for persons under drug investigation.

**BI and SEC**: Foreign nationals operating businesses in violation of FIA restrictions — SEC coordinates with BI for investigation; SEC violation may lead to BI proceedings.

**Interpol coordination**: BI checks arriving aliens against Interpol notices; red notices result in arrest and coordination with NBI and relevant foreign authority; purple/yellow notices used for information sharing.

---

## Administrative Regulation of Aliens — Recent Developments

**Foreign ownership of PCMs (Professional Cable Operators)**: DICT and NTC coordinate with BI when foreign-owned cable operators attempt to operate beyond permitted equity levels; immigration enforcement used as supplementary tool.

**POGO (Philippine Offshore Gaming Operators) enforcement**: Following POGO abolition by EO (verify current date and EO number), BI conducted massive deportation operations against undocumented POGO workers; thousands of Chinese nationals deported in 2024; illustrates BI's capacity for large-scale enforcement.

**Alien registration compliance sweeps**: BI periodically conducts alien registration compliance checks in commercial establishments; aliens without ACR I-Card — fined and given compliance period; repeated non-compliance — deportation proceedings.

## Anti-Hallucination Guardrails — Consolidated Deportation
BI is an administrative agency — its proceedings are quasi-judicial but not criminal courts; the standard of proof is substantial evidence (administrative), not proof beyond reasonable doubt. A deportation order is enforced by the BI, not by police or sheriffs — coordinate with BI for enforcement of orders. The blacklist is separate from the deportation order — an alien may be blacklisted without a deportation order (e.g., security concern without finding of deportable ground). Non-refoulement applies even if the alien has no legal status — BI cannot deport to a country where the alien faces a real risk of persecution or torture. Trafficking victims have special protections — never advise deportation of an alien without first checking whether they may be a trafficking victim; improper deportation of a trafficking victim exposes BI officials to liability.

## BI Commissioner — Administrative Orders
**Memorandum Circulars**: BI Commissioner issues MCs updating visa schedules, extending grace periods, announcing enforcement priorities. Practitioners must monitor BI MCs — visa rules change frequently without legislative action.

**Commissioner's Discretion**: CA 613 grants broad discretion; courts review for grave abuse only. Judicial review is available via certiorari — but courts will not substitute their judgment for the Commissioner's on immigration policy unless there is a clear constitutional violation or grave abuse.

**Transparency and good governance**: DOJ and BI subject to FOI (R.A. 11032 and EO 2, s. 2016); immigration records may be requested subject to privacy and security limitations; alien registration data — semi-protected; criminal records — restricted.

**Deportation and human rights**: Philippines is party to the ICCPR, CAT (Convention Against Torture), and CRC. Under CAT Art. 3, the Philippines cannot expel, return, or extradite a person to a state where there are substantial grounds for believing they would be in danger of torture. This non-refoulement obligation runs parallel to the Refugee Convention obligation and applies regardless of whether the person has committed serious crimes. BI must consider CAT obligations before executing a deportation order to a country with a documented record of torture.
