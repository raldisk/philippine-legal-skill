# Alien Admission

## Primary Sources
**Commonwealth Act 613 (Philippine Immigration Act of 1940)** — primary statute; governs admission, exclusion, and deportation of aliens. **Bureau of Immigration (BI)** — under the DOJ; administers the Immigration Act. **BOC (Board of Commissioners)** — collegial body of the BI with quasi-judicial functions.

---

## Visa Classification — Non-Immigrants (§9 CA 613)
| Visa | Holder |
|---|---|
| 9(a) | Temporary visitor for pleasure or business (tourist) — most common |
| 9(b) | Non-resident alien in transit |
| 9(c) | Non-resident alien crewman |
| 9(d) | Treaty trader/investor (E-visa equivalent) |
| 9(e) | Accredited foreign government official |
| 9(f) | Student visa — must enroll at CHED/DepEd accredited school |
| 9(g) | Pre-arranged employee — working visa; must have AEP from DOLE |
| 9(h) | Treaties/special arrangements |

**Tourist visa (9[a])**: 30-day initial stay for most nationalities; extendable. Maximum stay limits vary by nationality. Overstaying is an administrative violation.

**Balikbayan privilege**: Former Filipino citizens and their families may enter and stay up to 1 year without visa under Balikbayan Program.

---

## Immigrant Visas (§13)
**§13(a)**: Alien spouse/children of Philippine citizen.

**§13(g)**: Former Filipino citizen (natural-born) who lost citizenship through naturalization in a foreign country — Dual Citizenship under R.A. 9225 may restore Philippine citizenship.

**SRRV (Special Resident Retiree's Visa)**: Under the Philippine Retirement Authority (PRA); for retirees meeting age and deposit requirements.

---

## Alien Employment
**Alien Employment Permit (AEP)**: Required from the DOLE for aliens employed in the Philippines. Exceptions: members of diplomatic/consular missions; aliens employed by the Philippine government; officers of resident foreign corporations duly registered with SEC.

**9(g) visa**: Work visa contingent on securing AEP. BI issues 9(g) upon proof of AEP.

---

## Exclusion and Denial of Admission
**Excludable aliens (§29 CA 613)**: Mentally defective persons; persons with certain contagious diseases; persons convicted of crimes involving moral turpitude (CIMT); stowaways; persons likely to become public charges; aliens denied admission for prior violations.

**Due process**: Excluded aliens at the border have limited due process rights. Aliens lawfully admitted who have established residence have stronger due process rights in removal proceedings.

---

## Anti-Hallucination Guardrails — Alien Admission
The §9(g) working visa is contingent on a valid AEP from DOLE — a 9(g) alone does not authorize employment without the AEP; both are required. Tourist visa (9[a]) does not authorize employment — employment on a tourist visa is an immigration violation. SRRV is issued by PRA, not BI — these are separate agencies with separate instruments. The 9(f) student visa requires enrollment at a school accredited by BI/CHED/DepEd — change of school requires BI clearance. Balikbayan privilege applies to former Filipino citizens AND their families — family members retain the privilege even if they are foreign nationals.
---

## Visa Extension and Change of Status

**Extension of tourist visa (9[a])**: Filed with BI District or satellite office; period-by-period extension; fee per extension; maximum cumulative stay subject to BI Commissioner discretion and nationality-based bilateral agreements. Some nationalities receive 59-day initial stay; others 30 days; verify current BI circular.

**Change of admission status (CAS)**: Converting from one visa category to another without departing the Philippines. Common: tourist → 9(g) working visa; student → 9(g); tourist → immigrant §13(a) spouse visa. CAS requires new application with supporting documents; BI Commissioner must approve.

**Downgrading**: Immigrant visa holder who has lost the basis for immigrant status (e.g., spouse dies) may be downgraded to non-immigrant status; or may face deportation proceedings.

---

## Special Visas and Investor Programs

**Special Investor's Resident Visa (SIRV)**: For aliens who invest at least USD 75,000 in Philippine enterprises in priority areas; administered by BOI (FIRB); BI issues the visa upon BOI recommendation. Holder entitled to 1-year multiple entry; renewable; conditional on maintaining investment.

**Special Non-Immigrant Visa (47[a][2])**: Presidential prerogative; for aliens whose admission is beneficial to the country; commonly used for foreign nationals employed by PEZA/BOI-registered enterprises without going through standard 9(g) process.

**Quota immigrant visas**: Philippine law allocates annual quota of 50 immigrant visas per country; governed by §13 CA 613; quota visas available for certain professional and family categories not covered by §13(a)-(g) spouse/relative preference.

**9(d) Treaty Trader/Investor**: For nationals of countries with treaties of commerce/navigation with the Philippines; limited; must maintain active trade/investment. Not widely used.

---

## Alien Registration

**Alien Certificate of Registration Identity Card (ACR I-Card)**: Required for all aliens staying in the Philippines beyond 59 days. Issued by BI; contains biometric data; must be carried at all times. Renewal: annually for most non-immigrants; every 5 years for immigrants.

**Alien registration report**: Annual report required from aliens during January each year; verifies current status, address, occupation. Failure to register or report — administrative fine; may lead to deportation if willful.

**BI registration exemption**: Diplomatic and consular personnel; children under 14 (in most cases); short-term visitors within the visa-exempt period.

---

## Foreign National Employment Rules in Detail

**Alien Employment Permit (AEP) — detailed rules**: DOLE issues AEP; application filed with DOLE-Regional Office where employment is; employer and employee are co-applicants; requires proof of unavailability of qualified Filipino for the position (Labor Market Test — LMT). LMT exemptions: positions designated in the List of Positions Restricted to Filipinos under EO 92; intra-company transferees covered by bilateral agreements; aliens serving as Board member or President of domestic corporation; certain missionary, humanitarian, or religious workers.

**Permit to Work (PTW) — formerly employed under Special Work Permit (SWP)**: Short-term (3–6 month) work authorization from BI for aliens who will engage in limited employment activities not requiring AEP (e.g., consultants, short-term technical experts, performing artists). SWP is BI-issued; not DOLE-issued; separate from AEP.

**Offshore Gaming Operators (POGO) — now abolished**: Former POGO regime allowed offshore gaming workers under a special visa and permit regime; POGO was abolished by Executive Order in 2024 (verify current status); related immigration issues for formerly employed POGO workers ongoing.

---

## Balikbayan Program — Expanded Rules

**R.A. 6768 as amended**: Balikbayan privilege extended to: (1) Filipino citizens or their spouses and unmarried children under 18 who are not Filipino citizens; (2) natural-born Filipinos who have been naturalized abroad and their legal families.

**Period of stay**: 1 year without visa from date of entry. Cannot be extended further under the Balikbayan privilege — must convert to appropriate visa category for longer stay.

**Privilege conditions**: Entry must be for pleasure or visit — not for employment; employment on Balikbayan privilege = immigration violation.

**Multiple use**: Balikbayan privilege is per entry — not per year; multiple trips allowed but each triggers a fresh 1-year period. Loss of the privilege: if the alien engages in unauthorized employment or violates terms.

---

## Border Control and Inspection

**Primary inspection**: Immigration officer at port of entry — brief interview, biometric capture, document verification. Decision: admit; secondary inspection; or immediate exclusion.

**Secondary inspection**: Longer screening; document verification; background checks. Alien may be held in immigration holding area pending secondary inspection; usually resolved within 24–48 hours.

**Watchlist orders**: BI maintains a watchlist of persons under investigation; those on watchlist may be flagged at the border; may be held for secondary inspection; criminal court can issue HDO (Hold Departure Order).

**Hold Departure Order (HDO)**: Issued by RTC or DOJ; prevents departure from the Philippines; served on BI Commissioner; BI implements at all ports of exit. Distinguish from Watchlist Order (BI internal monitoring tool) and Lookout Bulletin (DOJ advisory only — no mandatory hold).

---

## International Treaties Affecting Alien Admission

**ASEAN Free Trade Agreement and AFAS (ASEAN Framework Agreement on Services)**: MNP (Mode 4 — Movement of Natural Persons) provisions govern entry of ASEAN service suppliers and intra-corporate transferees. Philippines bound by AFAS commitments to facilitate entry of certain ASEAN nationals. AEP streamlining for ASEAN workers in specific sectors.

**Bilateral labor agreements**: Philippines has MOUs/MOAs with multiple countries (Japan, South Korea, Qatar, Saudi Arabia, etc.) governing OFW deployment and protection. Incoming foreign workers under bilateral agreements may have streamlined visa/work permit processes.

## Key Cases — Alien Admission

**Chiong v. Fariñas** (GR 211293, 2018): BI's broad discretionary power to exclude aliens at the border; courts do not interfere with immigration officers' exercise of discretion unless there is grave abuse; due process at the border is limited compared to inland proceedings.

**Mejoff v. Director of Prisons** (GR L-4254, 1951): A stateless person (neither admitted nor deported to any country) detained indefinitely — SC held that prolonged detention pending deportation violates constitutional rights; ordered release on bail. Principle: even inadmissible aliens cannot be detained indefinitely without legal justification.

**Republic v. Ferrer** (GR L-2446): Naturalized citizens may not be deprived of citizenship arbitrarily; naturalization certificate is a judicial grant subject to revocation only for fraud; due process required in denaturalization.

## Immigration Bureau — Powers and Procedures

**Powers of the BI Commissioner**: Issue rules and regulations; grant or cancel visas; order arrest and detention of deportable aliens; issue blacklist and watchlist orders; order exclusion at the border; grant parole or bail; accept or reject applications for change of status; impose fines for immigration violations.

**Administrative fines**: BI may fine aliens for overstaying; for failing to register; for failure to carry ACR I-Card; for unauthorized change of employment. Fines payable before departure; failure to pay may result in hold at the port of exit.

**Exit clearance**: Some aliens require BI clearance before departure — e.g., aliens with pending BOC cases; those who owe fines; minors traveling with one parent (requiring consent of absent parent); OFWs returning abroad (OWWA clearance).

---

## Treatment of Asylum Seekers at the Border

**Non-refoulement at ports of entry**: Immigration officers must not return a person to a country where they face persecution. Where asylum claim is made at the border, immigration officer must refer to the DOJ Refugee and Stateless Persons Protection Unit rather than immediately excluding.

**Temporary Protection Status**: DOJ may grant temporary protection to persons who cannot be immediately removed but do not yet have refugee status; allows stay pending status determination.

**UNHCR Manila**: Coordinates with DOJ on refugee status determination; monitors treatment of asylum seekers; provides legal assistance through partner NGOs.

---

## BI Quasi-Judicial Functions

**Deportation proceedings**: BOC exercises quasi-judicial functions in deportation cases; rules of evidence applicable in administrative proceedings; substantial evidence standard; alien may be represented by counsel.

**Naturalization cases**: BI participates in judicial naturalization as a party; BI Commissioner or authorized representative appears before RTC; may appeal favorable judgments granted to applicants who do not meet requirements.

**Cancellation of admission**: BOC may cancel admission status and declare alien's stay unlawful; precursor to deportation; alien entitled to hearing.

**Judicial review**: BI/BOC decisions appealable to DOJ; then to CA (Rule 43) or via certiorari to CA/SC for grave abuse of discretion. BI decisions on immigration matters are administrative — courts give deference but review for due process violations and grave abuse.

---

## Anti-Hallucination Guardrails — Consolidated Alien Admission
BI Commissioner's powers are broad but subject to constitutional due process requirements — broad administrative discretion does not mean unfettered discretion. The 47(a)(2) special visa requires presidential/executive approval — it is not a standard visa available through normal BI processing. POGO visas are effectively abolished (verify current executive order status) — never advise clients that POGO work permits are available without checking the current legal status. Exit clearance requirements for minors — both parents' consent may be needed if traveling with only one; BI has specific rules for minors' travel documentation. The Balikbayan privilege is a right of entry for 1 year — it does not automatically convert to immigrant status and does not allow employment.

---

## Visa-Free Entry Arrangements
**Visa-free countries**: Citizens of most Western nations, ASEAN members, Japan, and South Korea may enter the Philippines visa-free for 30 or 59 days (depends on nationality). BI Memorandum Circular updates the list; verify current nationalities and periods. Visa-free does not mean permit-free — employment still requires AEP and appropriate visa conversion.

**Reciprocity principle**: Visa-free privilege extended to nationals of countries that extend the same privilege to Filipino citizens; diplomatic reciprocity governs. DFA and BI coordinate on visa policy adjustments.

**Extension of visa-free stay**: Must file for extension at BI before initial period expires; fee required; failure to file before expiry = overstaying. Initial extension for most nationalities: 29 days added to initial 30-day period = maximum 59 days before first formal extension required.
