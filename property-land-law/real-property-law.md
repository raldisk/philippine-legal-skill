# Real Property Law

## Primary Sources
**NCC Book II (Arts. 414–773)** — property, ownership, possession, usufruct, easements. **Condominium Act (R.A. 4726)**. **Urban Development and Housing Act (R.A. 7279)**. Load `property-land-law/torrens-system.md` for title registration; load `property-land-law/land-registration.md` for original registration procedure.

## Classification
**Immovable property (Art. 415)**: Land; buildings/roads/constructions adhering to soil; trees/plants/growing fruits attached to land; stationary machinery for industrial purposes; contracts for public works; servitudes and real rights over immovables.

**Movable property (Art. 416)**: All other property.

**Classification consequences**: Immovables require written deeds for transfer; subject to real estate mortgage (not chattel); governed by different prescription periods; real actions filed in RTC.

---

## Ownership — Rights and Limitations
**Rights (Art. 428)**: Enjoy and dispose without limitations other than those established by law. No deprivation without competent authority, public use/welfare, and just compensation.

**Accession**: Owner acquires ownership of everything naturally produced by or incorporated into the property.

**Types of accession**:
- **Alluvion (Art. 457)**: Gradual, imperceptible soil deposits by river current → belong to riparian owner.
- **Avulsion**: Identifiable portion of land detached and attached to another's land → original owner retains ownership for 2 years to remove it.
- **Accretion**: More general concept including alluvion.
- **Accession industrial (Art. 445 et seq.)**: Builder, planter, sower — rights depend on good/bad faith of both builder and landowner.

**Builder in good faith (Art. 448)**: Builder believes in good faith he owns the land. Landowner's option: (a) appropriate the building/planting by paying fair value; OR (b) compel the builder to buy the land (unless land value greatly exceeds building — then court may fix equitable coexistence). *Pleasantville Development Corp. v. CA*: builder must have honest belief in ownership or right to build.

**Builder in bad faith**: Landowner may demolish/remove at builder's expense, OR appropriate without compensation; builder liable for rents/damages.

---

## Co-ownership (Arts. 484–501)
Undivided shares in a thing. Each co-owner may deal with his share without consent of others. No obligation to remain in co-ownership — action for partition always available. A co-owner's act benefiting the thing without opposition may bind others.

**Prescription as against co-owners**: Requires a clear, unequivocal act of repudiation communicated to co-owners — ordinary use and possession is not repudiation.

---

## Possession (Arts. 522–561)
**Possession in concept of owner**: Necessary for acquisitive prescription to run.

**Possession in concept of holder** (lessee, usufructuary): Prescription does not run in holder's favor against the owner.

**Good faith presumed (Art. 527)**: Current possessor presumed to be in good faith; burden on one asserting bad faith.

**Effects of possession**: Good-faith possessor entitled to gathered fruits, reimbursement of necessary and useful expenses; bad-faith possessor must restore fruits and pay damages.

---

## Usufruct (Arts. 562–612)
Right to enjoy another's property while preserving its form and substance. Usufructuary: entitled to all fruits; bears ordinary repairs and taxes. Naked owner: retains ownership. Extinguished by: death of usufructuary (if constituted for life); expiration of term; merger; renunciation; total loss.

---

## Easements (Arts. 613–695)
**Defined**: Encumbrance on a servient estate for the benefit of a dominant estate. Continuous/discontinuous; apparent/non-apparent; positive/negative.

**Legal easement of right of way (Art. 649)**: Established when the estate is surrounded/enclosed with no adequate outlet. Requirements: enclosing estate; inadequacy of outlet; shortest and least prejudicial route; payment of just indemnity. *Costabella Corp. v. CA* [verify GR]: inadequacy of existing access is threshold requirement.

**Extinguishment**: 10-year non-use (discontinuous easements); use becomes impossible; merger of ownership in servient and dominant estates.

---

## Condominium Act (R.A. 4726)
**Unit ownership**: Each unit is a separately registered parcel with an undivided interest in common areas.

**Condominium corporation**: All unit owners are shareholders/members; manages and maintains common areas. Unit owners must remain members.

**Transfer restrictions**: AOC and declaration of restrictions may govern transferability; right of first refusal may be imposed.

---

## Anti-Hallucination Guardrails — Real Property Law
Art. 448 option belongs to the landowner, not the builder — the builder cannot compel the landowner to accept purchase of the building. Good faith of the builder is presumed; the party asserting bad faith bears the burden of proof. Alluvion requires gradual and imperceptible deposits — avulsion follows different ownership rules. Co-ownership is not terminated by possession alone — repudiation must be clear, unequivocal, and communicated. The right of way easement requires actual lack of adequate outlet — inconvenience or longer route is insufficient. Prescription does not run in favor of a holder against the owner — only against possession in the concept of owner.
**Condominium unit**: Owner holds exclusive ownership of unit plus undivided interest in common areas. Master deed and Declaration of Restrictions (DR) registered with Registry of Deeds — governing documents. Condominium Corporation (CC): composed of all unit owners; manages common areas; collects dues; governed by by-laws and CC rules.

**Condominium vs. PD 957 (Subdivision and Condominium Buyers' Protective Decree)**: PD 957 governs sales of residential subdivision lots and condominium units; seller must register with HLURB (now DHSUD — Dept. of Human Settlements and Urban Development); buyers protected against fraudulent sales; escrow requirements for uncompleted developments.

---

## Quieting of Title (Arts. 476–481)
**Action to quiet title**: Filed by owner or possessor with valid interest against adverse claim creating cloud on title. Requirements: (1) plaintiff has legal/equitable title; (2) defendant's claim constitutes cloud — an instrument or proceeding that appears valid but is invalid. Prescriptive period: if in possession — imprescriptible; if not in possession — 10 years from when the cloud arose.

**Action to recover ownership (Accion reivindicatoria)**: Based on full ownership; recovers both title and possession; prescribes in 30 years (ordinary property). Distinguished from accion publiciana (possession de jure; 10-year prescription) and accion interdictal (possession de facto; 1-year prescription via forcible entry/unlawful detainer).

---

## Special Rules — Urban Land Reform
**UDHA (R.A. 7279)**: Governs resettlement of informal settlers; expropriation for socialized housing. LGUs required to identify areas for socialized housing. Eviction of underprivileged and homeless citizens: strict procedural requirements; notice; relocation prior to eviction; night-time eviction prohibited.

**Socialized housing**: Defined price ceiling per HLURB (DHSUD); VAT-exempt if below threshold; beneficiaries must be underprivileged. PD 957 applies; DHSUD registration required.

---

## Compulsory Sale and Expropriation
**Expropriation (Rule 67, Rules of Court)**: Government may expropriate private property for public use upon payment of just compensation. Two stages: (1) condemnation (right to expropriate); (2) just compensation (amount). Just compensation: FMV at the time of taking — not at filing of complaint. *Republic v. Lim*: Taking occurs when government deprives owner of beneficial use.

**Right to repurchase (Art. 1616 NCC + jurisprudence)**: If property expropriated was not used for the public purpose it was condemned for — original owner may repurchase at original price plus interest in some cases (*Heirs of Moreno v. Mactan-Cebu International Airport Authority*).

## Anti-Hallucination Guardrails — Real Property Law
Alluvion goes to the riparian owner — this is natural accretion, not artificial fill; artificial fill belongs to the person who placed it. Builder in good faith rule under Art. 448 requires landowner to make an election — the landowner cannot be forced to buy the building or let the builder keep it indefinitely without paying rent; the court can fix terms of coexistence. Easement of right of way requires indemnity payment — it is not gratuitous; absence of payment offer = easement not yet constituted. Quieting of title action by a person in possession is imprescriptible — but prescription runs if plaintiff is not in possession. Never confuse condominium project registration (PD 957, DHSUD) with title registration (PD 1529, LRA/RD). Expropriation just compensation is at the time of taking, not the time of filing or payment.

## Modes of Acquiring Ownership (Art. 712)
**Occupation**: Acquisition of ownerless movables (res nullius). Real property cannot be acquired by occupation since all Philippine lands belong to the State.

**Intellectual creation**: Author/inventor acquires ownership of work upon creation; rights governed by IP Code.

**Law**: Inheritance by operation of intestacy; co-ownership by marriage.

**Donation**: Gratuitous transfer; onerous donations taxed as sale. Real property donations: public instrument requirement; acceptance in same instrument or separate public instrument communicated to donor during lifetime.

**Tradition**: Delivery of movable; for real property, tradition may be: actual (physical delivery); constructive (execution of deed = tradition); tradition longa manu (pointing out property); traditio brevi manu (buyer already in possession as tenant converts to owner); constitutum possessorium (seller remains in possession as tenant after sale).

**Succession**: Heir acquires from moment of death.

**Prescription**: Acquisitive (positive) prescription — ordinary (10 years with just title and good faith) or extraordinary (30 years without title or good faith) for real property. Extinctive prescription: cuts off unexercised rights.

## Special Property Concepts

**Condominium rights in detail**: Unit owner's rights: exclusive ownership of unit; exclusive use of parking slot, storage (if designated); undivided interest in common areas; voting rights in CC proportionate to interest. CC's right: collect dues and assessments; enforce master deed/DR; manage common areas; institute legal proceedings.

**Horizontal property (condominiums) vs. horizontal developments (subdivisions)**: Condominiums governed by R.A. 4726; subdivisions governed by PD 957. Both require DHSUD license to sell; both subject to PD 957 buyer protection provisions.

**Air rights and mineral rights**: Surface owner does not own minerals unless expressly granted by State; State owns all minerals (Art. XII §2 Constitution). Air rights: owner of surface owns to a reasonable height for use and enjoyment; air rights may be separately sold (e.g., air rights sales in urban development).

## Restrictions on Ownership

**Constitutional restrictions (Art. XII)**: Only Filipino citizens or corporations with 60% Filipino ownership may own private land; non-resident Filipinos — may own; former natural-born Filipino who became foreign citizen — may acquire limited land (§10 R.A. 8179 — up to 1,000 sqm urban/1 hectare rural); foreign nationals — cannot own land; may own buildings and improvements; may enter long-term leases.

**Anti-Dummy Law**: Prohibits foreigners from using Filipino names or citizens to circumvent ownership restrictions; criminal liability; void contracts.

**Special zones**: In special economic zones (PEZA), foreign investors may use long-term leases (up to 75 years with renewal) of land within the zone; cannot own land.

**Condominium exception**: Under R.A. 4726 §5, a foreign national may acquire condominium units in a building where alien ownership does not exceed 40% of the total units.

## Nuisance (Arts. 694–707)
**Public nuisance**: Affects community/considerable number of persons; may be abated by: public authorities; summary abatement by injured party (for urgent cases); legal action.

**Private nuisance**: Violates private rights of one or more persons; remedied by: civil action for abatement and/or damages; injunction.

**Attractive nuisance doctrine**: Property owner owes duty of care to child trespassers where artificial condition on property is likely to attract children who cannot appreciate the danger (*Jarco Marketing Corp. v. CA*, GR 129792: landowner liability where child injury results from failure to secure dangerous equipment/premises from foreseeable child trespassers).

**Noise, vibration, fumes**: Industrial operations causing nuisance → owner liable even without negligence if activity constitutes a nuisance (strict liability for abnormally dangerous activities in the neighborhood).

## Key Cases — Real Property Law

**Pleasantville Development Corp. v. CA** (GR 79688): Builder in good faith rule requires honest belief in right to build; belief based on erroneous interpretation of a lease is not sufficient for Art. 448 builder-in-good-faith protection — the builder must believe they own the land itself.

**Spouses Tecson v. Spouses Tecson** (GR 179720): Co-ownership does not allow one co-owner to claim adverse possession against other co-owners without first making known the repudiation of co-ownership; covert exclusive possession insufficient.

**Manila International Airport Authority v. CA** (GR 155650): Government instrumentalities with charters like MIAA are not government-owned corporations subject to the Torrens system for all purposes — but their real property is still owned by the Republic; tax exemptions apply.

**Philippine National Railways v. Asuncion** (GR L-49987): Accretion belongs to the riparian owner as a matter of law — no formal claim or registration needed for ownership to transfer, though registration is advisable for full protection against third parties.

## Anti-Hallucination Guardrails — Real Property Law Update
Foreign nationals cannot own Philippine land — period. The condominium exception allows foreign ownership of units up to 40% of total condominium building — this is a building-level limit, not unit-by-unit. Air rights may be separately transacted but the underlying land is still subject to ownership restrictions. Builder in good faith under Art. 448 gives the landowner the option (not the builder); the builder cannot compel the landowner to sell. Acquisitive prescription of private land: 10 years (ordinary, with just title and good faith); 30 years (extraordinary, without title or good faith). Public domain land CANNOT be acquired by prescription regardless of possession length. Alluvion belongs to riparian owner automatically — no registration needed for the ownership right to vest, though annotation on title is recommended.

## Prescription Periods — Real Property Actions

| Action | Prescriptive Period | Basis |
|---|---|---|
| Accion reivindicatoria | 30 years (real property ordinary) | NCC Art. 1141 |
| Accion publiciana | 10 years | NCC Art. 1142 |
| Accion interdictal (forcible entry/unlawful detainer) | 1 year from dispossession | Rule 70 ROC |
| Quieting of title (in possession) | Imprescriptible | NCC Art. 480 |
| Quieting of title (not in possession) | 10 years | NCC Art. 480 |
| Reconveyance (person in possession) | Imprescriptible | Jurisprudence |
| Reconveyance (not in possession) | 10 years from fraudulent registration | Jurisprudence |
| Cancellation of void title | Imprescriptible | Jurisprudence |

**Acquisitive prescription of real property**:
- Ordinary: 10 years, with just title + good faith.
- Extraordinary: 30 years, without just title or good faith.

**State's right to recover public land**: Imprescriptible — no amount of adverse possession can defeat the State's claim to public land.

**Torrens-registered land**: Cannot be acquired by adverse possession against the registered owner — Torrens registration defeats prescription. *Exception*: if registered owner's title is void, the action to recover by the true owner may be filed anytime against the fraudster but not against an IPV.

**Easement of light and view (Art. 667)**: Owner may not open windows or make openings in party walls unless with neighbor's consent; openings in the owner's own wall facing neighbor's property — easement of light and view acquired by prescription (10 years; must be non-apparent). Setback rules from boundaries: 2 meters for direct view; 60 cm for oblique view.

**Hidden treasure (Art. 438)**: Hidden and unknown deposit of money, jewelry, or objects; belongs to the finder and owner of the property in equal shares if finder is not the owner. If finder is a trespasser — entire treasure goes to the owner. Treasure found during excavation for building — split equally.

**Mortgage lien vs. ownership**: A real estate mortgage does not transfer ownership — mortgagor retains ownership and possession. Upon default: mortgagee's remedy is foreclosure (judicial or extrajudicial), not automatic ownership transfer. Pactum commissorium (Art. 2088 NCC) — automatic ownership transfer clause in mortgage is void; mortgagee must always foreclose through proper legal process.
