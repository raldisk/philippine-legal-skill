# Torrens System

## Primary Sources
**Property Registration Decree (P.D. 1529)** — governing statute; superseded Act 496 (Land Registration Act). **Land Registration Authority (LRA)** — administers under DOJ; issues LRA Circulars. **Registry of Deeds** — maintains register; issues OCTs and TCTs per province/city.

---

## Core Principles
**Indefeasibility**: A Torrens title is conclusive against the whole world. No court has jurisdiction to entertain claims affecting a registered title except in a **direct proceeding** — not a collateral attack. Once the decree of registration is final (after 1 year from entry), it cannot be reopened except by direct action based on actual fraud.

**Mirror principle**: The certificate of title reflects the true and current state of the title. A buyer in good faith need not look beyond the certificate.

**Curtain principle**: Registration draws a curtain over prior history; purchasers need not investigate prior transactions.

**Insurance principle**: The Assurance Fund compensates persons deprived of registered land through fraudulent registration who cannot recover from the wrongdoer.

---

## Original Certificate of Title (OCT)
Issued upon original registration proceedings. Contains: title number; names of registered owners; lot description; encumbrances. OCT is the root title; subsequent transfers generate TCTs.

---

## Purchaser in Good Faith and for Value
**Requirements**: (1) purchase was for value; (2) purchaser had no knowledge, actual or constructive, of defects in the seller's title at the time of purchase; (3) no red flags on the face of the title or accompanying circumstances.

**Registration as constructive notice (§52 P.D. 1529)**: Registration constitutes constructive notice from the time of registration. A registered encumbrance or adverse claim is deemed known to all subsequent purchasers.

**Limitations**: Good faith does not protect a buyer who purchases from a forger — a forged deed conveys no title regardless of registration. *Spouses Vilbar v. Opinion* [verify GR]: forged deed = void ab initio; buyer from forger acquires nothing even if innocent.

---

## Subsequent Registration and Transfers
**Transfer Certificate of Title (TCT)**: Issued upon transfer; prior OCT or TCT cancelled.

**Deed of conveyance**: Must be registered with the Registry of Deeds. As against third persons: deed takes effect only upon registration (§51 P.D. 1529).

**Priority of registration**: Earlier-registered instrument prevails over later-registered one. Registered instrument prevails over unregistered one — even if unregistered instrument is earlier in time.

**Involuntary dealings**: Attachment, execution levy, adverse claims, lis pendens — annotated on the title. Adverse claim: expires after 30 days unless renewed; constitutes constructive notice.

---

## Reconveyance and Annulment Actions
**Action for reconveyance**: Compels wrongful registrant to transfer title to true owner. Basis in implied or constructive trust. **Prescriptive period**: 10 years from registration (implied trust); imprescriptible if based on void contract and plaintiff is in actual possession.

**Action for annulment**: Direct attack on the title itself. Must be filed in the proper court (RTC acting as land registration court).

**Collateral attack prohibition**: A Torrens title cannot be attacked collaterally — challenge must be through a direct action for cancellation or reconveyance.

---

## Anti-Hallucination Guardrails — Torrens System
Torrens title is NOT absolutely indefeasible — it is vulnerable to a direct action for annulment based on actual fraud and to government claims for misclassified public land. The mirror principle protects buyers in good faith — it does NOT protect a buyer who knew of defects or bought from a forger. A forged deed transfers NO title regardless of subsequent registration; a buyer from a forger gets nothing even if registered. The 10-year reconveyance period runs from registration; it is imprescriptible if the plaintiff is in actual possession and the basis is a void (not merely voidable) contract. ## The Torrens System — Core Principles

**Indefeasibility (§44 P.D. 1529)**: Certificate of title once registered becomes indefeasible and incontrovertible after 1 year from entry of decree. No court may reopen a decree of registration after 1 year EXCEPT on grounds of fraud — and only against the defrauding party, not innocent purchasers.

**Mirror principle**: The certificate of title reflects all encumbrances, liens, and interests affecting the land — what is not in the title does not bind the registered owner or purchasers (with exceptions for overriding interests listed in §44).

**Curtain principle**: Purchaser need not look beyond the four corners of the title; if title is clean, purchaser may rely on it.

**Insurance principle**: Assurance Fund compensates persons who suffer loss due to defects in the Torrens system that cannot be recovered from the wrongdoer (§§95–97 P.D. 1529).

---

## Indefeasibility — Exceptions and Limitations

**Overriding interests (§44 P.D. 1529)**: The following bind even if not on the certificate: liens/claims/rights arising under law (e.g., unpaid taxes; laborers' wages under NLRC; mechanic's lien); public highway; navigable waterway; public utility easement; prior registered adverse claim (30-day life unless renewed).

**Fraudulent registration does not give good title**: Fraudster cannot acquire indefeasible title. BUT a subsequent bona fide purchaser for value FROM the fraudster acquires indefeasible title. This is the "shelter principle" — the innocent purchaser is sheltered by the law.

**Reconveyance action**: Remedy of person deprived of land by fraud. Against the fraudster: 10 years from issuance of title. Against innocent purchaser: action may not prosper — limited to damages.

---

## Innocent Purchaser for Value (IPV)

**Definition**: One who purchases land without notice of any defect or encumbrance on the title; pays fair price; good faith must exist both at the time of contract AND registration of the deed.

**Test of good faith**: Purchaser inspected the property and title; made necessary inquiries when circumstances put him on guard; relied reasonably on the clean title. *Spouses Yu v. Pacleb* (GR 172628): One cannot claim IPV status if there are circumstances visible on the ground (e.g., occupants other than the seller) that require further inquiry.

**Due diligence for IPV**: Visit the property; inquire of occupants; check if the seller is in possession; verify the title against the certified true copy at the Registry of Deeds; check for annotations.

---

## Adverse Claims (§70 P.D. 1529)
**When filed**: Person claiming interest in registered land adverse to the registered owner may file adverse claim. Annotation on title; effective for 30 days; renewable by filing a new adverse claim.

**Effect**: Binds subsequent purchasers with constructive notice during 30-day period. After 30 days without filing of action, annotation is ineffective — BUT some courts have held the annotation remains until cancelled.

**Cancellation**: Adverse claimant or registrant may petition RTC to cancel; notice and hearing required. If claimant wins an action within 30 days: court decision annotated; protects the claimant's interest.

---

## Lis Pendens (§76 P.D. 1529)
**Purpose**: Notice that the subject land is involved in litigation; binds all subsequent persons acquiring interest in the land.

**When cancellable**: Court dismisses the action with finality; upon court order; upon final determination of the case adverse to the notice filer.

**Effect**: Purchaser pendente lite takes subject to the final outcome of the litigation; cannot claim IPV defense.

---

## Action for Reconveyance vs. Action to Cancel Title

**Reconveyance**: Plaintiff seeks to compel defendant to reconvey the land (transfer title back to rightful owner). Used when defendant wrongfully obtained title. Prescriptive period: 10 years from fraudulent registration (if not in possession); imprescriptible if plaintiff is in actual possession (action is one to quiet title).

**Cancellation of title**: Action to nullify the title itself; filed when the certificate was void ab initio (e.g., title issued over inalienable land). Imprescriptible — the state may file at any time.

**Reversion**: Action by the Republic to recover land that was fraudulently registered when it should remain public domain; imprescriptible when brought by the State.

---

## Voluntary Dealings — Registration of Deeds

**Documents that must be registered**: Sale, donation, exchange, mortgage, lease for more than 1 year, any encumbrance affecting title — must be registered to bind third parties.

**Two-step for transfer**: (1) BIR clearance (pay CGT/DST; obtain eCAR); (2) present deed and eCAR at Registry of Deeds; pay registration fees; new TCT issued in buyer's name.

**Legal separation of ownership and possession**: Registered owner is the legally recognized owner even if someone else is in actual possession — unless the possessor's right is annotated on title or falls within overriding interests under §44.

---

## Anti-Hallucination Guardrails — Torrens System
Indefeasibility attaches after 1 year from entry of decree — not from issuance of OCT. Fraud prevents indefeasibility against the fraudster — but the innocent purchaser from the fraudster IS protected (shelter principle). The 30-day adverse claim period is NOT automatically renewed by silence — a new adverse claim must be filed. Lis pendens cancellation is by court order — not automatic upon case filing. Good faith must exist at both execution of contract AND registration — a purchaser who learns of a defect before registration cannot claim IPV. Possession adverse to the registered owner does not by itself constitute constructive notice to a subsequent buyer — buyer must physically inspect AND make inquiries. Reversion and cancellation actions by the State are imprescriptible — never cite prescription as a defense to a government reversion case.

---

## Registration of Mortgages

**Real Estate Mortgage (REM)**: Must be recorded with the Registry of Deeds; annotation on title. Mortgage is not valid against third parties without registration.

**Extrajudicial foreclosure (Act 3135)**: Mortgagee may foreclose by auction upon default; notice requirements: publication once a week for at least 3 consecutive weeks in newspaper of general circulation in province/city; posting in conspicuous places. After auction: sheriff's certificate registered; one-year redemption period (for individuals); for banks under R.A. 8791: shorter redemption period (3 months or before registration of sale, whichever is earlier).

**Equitable mortgage (Art. 1602 NCC)**: Contracts purporting to be sales with right to repurchase may be presumed equitable mortgages in certain circumstances. Parties' actual intent governs; inadequacy of price is a badge of equitable mortgage. *Reyes v. CA* (GR L-39432): If true intent is security for a loan, the sale is recharacterized as equitable mortgage regardless of the instrument used.

---

## Transfer Certificate of Title (TCT) System

**Derivative title**: TCT derives from the original certificate (OCT); registered owner holds title by virtue of the chain from the OCT to the current TCT.

**Lost or destroyed certificate**: Petition to court for reconstitution of title (R.A. 26); administrative reconstitution through LRA for those destroyed by calamity. Reconstituted title has same force as the original.

**Spurious titles**: Title that was never legitimately issued; no valid basis in any OCT chain; completely void; no rights can be derived from it even by innocent purchaser. *Republic v. Guerrero* (GR 133168): Spurious titles not protected even when held by innocent purchaser — protection under Torrens applies only to validly issued titles obtained through fraud, not to titles that were never validly issued at all.

---

## Cancellation of Titles

**When title may be cancelled**: Order of court in action for reconveyance (if defendant defrauded the true owner); in action to annul a void judgment; upon voluntary surrender by owner; upon transfer to new owner; upon expropriation.

**Procedure**: Petition to RTC having jurisdiction over the property; notice and hearing; court order directs the Registry of Deeds to cancel and issue new title.

---

## Caveats and Limitations on IPV Status

**Buyer of property in possession of another (not the seller)**: Must inquire from the possessor; failure to inquire = bad faith as a matter of law. *Spouses Sabitsana v. Muertegui* (GR 181359): Buyer who ignores visible possession of a third party is not an IPV.

**Buyer without paying full consideration**: IPV status requires payment of reasonable/fair price. *De Pedro v. Romasan Development Corp.*: Grossly inadequate price may indicate bad faith; but inadequacy alone is not conclusive.

**Heirs of deceased landowner**: Purchase from heirs of registered owner — need to verify: death certificate; proof of heirship; no pending estate proceedings; estate tax clearance. Failure to verify = bad faith.

---

## Key Cases — Torrens System

**Heirs of Pedro Clemeña y Zurbano v. Heirs of Irene B. Bien** (GR 155508): Where two certificates of title overlap, the earlier one prevails; the later title is void to the extent of the overlap.

**Spouses Recto v. Heirs of Marcos** (GR 188456): IPV status is a question of fact; buyer must prove good faith by preponderance of evidence; the burden is on the buyer, not the opposing party.

**Aboitiz and Co. v. Pepito** (GR L-25848): Title acquired by prescription before the Torrens system was established in the area — when the owner subsequently registers under Torrens, the registered title relates back to the original acquisition by prescription; the title is not weakened by belated registration.

---

## Anti-Hallucination Guardrails — Torrens System Update
Redemption period for individual mortgagors under Act 3135: 1 year from registration of certificate of sale. For banks: 3 months or before registration (whichever is earlier) under R.A. 8791 — NEVER apply the bank redemption period to non-bank mortgagees, or vice versa. Reconstituted title has same force as original — but courts scrutinize reconstitution more carefully because of historical fraud in reconstitution cases; always verify authenticity of reconstitution. Spurious titles = never validly issued = IPV defense fails regardless of who holds them. Possession by person other than seller must trigger inquiry — failure is bad faith as a matter of law. Do not apply equitable mortgage doctrine without checking all circumstances under Art. 1602 — the doctrine is remedial but requires factual findings.

## Summary Reference — Key Torrens Concepts

| Concept | Rule | Exception/Note |
|---|---|---|
| Indefeasibility | Attaches 1 year from entry of decree | Fraud by the registrant — action against fraudster allowed |
| Mirror principle | Title reflects all interests | Overriding interests under §44 PD 1529 not shown in title still bind |
| IPV protection | Cannot be defeated by prior fraud | Does not apply to void (spurious) titles |
| Adverse claim | Effective 30 days | Must be renewed; does not automatically lapse from records |
| Lis pendens | Binds all subsequent purchasers | Cancelled when action dismissed or decided |
| Reconveyance from fraudster | 10 years | If plaintiff in possession: imprescriptible |
| Reconveyance from IPV | Not available | Remedy against fraudster only; IPV keeps title |

**Registration fees — effects of non-payment**: If a validly registered deed is cancelled for non-payment of registration fees — registration is deemed incomplete; prior registered interest may prevail. Non-payment of fees does not automatically void a registered instrument but prevents completion.

**Escheat of unclaimed registered land**: Land registered in the name of deceased persons with no heirs — State may file escheat proceedings; proceeds go to national treasury; RTC jurisdiction.
