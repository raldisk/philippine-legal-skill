# Negotiable Instruments — Liabilities, Presentment, Dishonor, and Discharge

## Primary Sources
**Act No. 2031 (NIL), Title IV (Liabilities of Parties, §§60–69), Title V (Presentment for Payment, §§70–96), Title VI (Notice of Dishonor, §§89–118), and Title VII (Discharge, §§119–125)**. Builds on `negotiable-instruments/general-provisions.md` and `negotiable-instruments/negotiation-and-holders.md`. Citations use **§** for NIL sections.

---

## Liability of Maker (§60)
The maker, by making the instrument, **engages to pay it according to its tenor**, and admits the existence of the payee and the payee's then capacity to indorse. The maker is the party **primarily liable** — presentment for payment is not a condition precedent to the maker's liability (§70).

---

## Liability of Drawer (§61)
The drawer, by drawing the instrument, **engages that on due presentment the instrument will be accepted, or paid, or both**, according to its tenor, and that if it is dishonored and the necessary proceedings on dishonor are duly taken, the drawer will pay the amount to the holder, or to any subsequent indorser who is compelled to pay it. The drawer may insert in the instrument an express stipulation negating or limiting their own liability to the holder. The drawer is **secondarily liable** — liability is conditioned on due presentment, dishonor, and (generally) notice of dishonor.

---

## Liability of Acceptor (§62)
By accepting a bill, the acceptor **engages to pay it according to the tenor of the acceptance**, and admits (a) the existence of the drawer, the genuineness of the drawer's signature, and the drawer's capacity and authority to draw the instrument; and (b) the existence of the payee and the payee's then capacity to indorse. The acceptor becomes **primarily liable** upon acceptance, displacing the drawee's pre-acceptance non-liability (`negotiable-instruments/general-provisions.md`, §127).

---

## Indorser Liability
**Who is deemed an indorser (§63)**: A person who signs an instrument otherwise than as maker, drawer, or acceptor is deemed an indorser, unless they clearly indicate by appropriate words their intent to be bound in some other capacity (e.g., a surety or guarantor, which carries different liability incidents under the Civil Code rather than the NIL's indorser warranty regime).

**Irregular (accommodation) indorser (§64)**: One who indorses an instrument not payable to their order, before the instrument is delivered to the payee — a common device to add credit/security to an instrument. Such an indorser is liable to the payee and all subsequent parties; their relationship to the maker/drawer is, as between themselves, the same as that of an ordinary indorser to a maker/drawer (subject to the accommodation party's right of indemnity from the accommodated party, `negotiable-instruments/general-provisions.md`, §29).

**Liability of general indorser (§66)**: Every indorser who indorses without qualification **engages that, on due presentment, the instrument will be accepted or paid, or both**, as the case may be, according to its tenor; and that if dishonored, and the necessary proceedings on dishonor are duly taken, the indorser will pay the amount to the holder or a subsequent indorser compelled to pay it. The general indorser additionally **warrants to all subsequent holders in due course**: (a) that the instrument is genuine and in all respects what it purports to be; (b) that the indorser has good title to it; (c) that all prior parties had capacity to contract; and (d) that the instrument is, at the time of indorsement, valid and subsisting. These warranties are the basis on which a **collecting bank** — itself an indorser when it stamps and presents a deposited check to the drawee — is held liable on a forged prior indorsement even though the forgery was not the collecting bank's own doing.

**Order of liability among indorsers (§68)**: As respects one another, indorsers are liable prima facie in the order in which they indorse — but evidence is admissible to show that, as between or among themselves, they have agreed otherwise (e.g., co-accommodation indorsers agreeing to share liability rather than be liable in strict sequence).

## Landmark Case — Forged Indorsement, Collecting Bank vs. Drawee Bank
***Associated Bank v. Court of Appeals***, G.R. Nos. 107382 & 107612 (Jan. 31, 1996). Thirty government checks payable to a hospital were encashed through a forged payee indorsement at a collecting bank, which then presented them to the drawee bank (PNB) for clearing. Holdings of continuing doctrinal importance:
- A forged signature is **wholly inoperative** (§23) — the forged indorsement does not operate as the payee's indorsement, and no title passes through it.
- The **drawee bank** is under strict liability to pay only according to the drawer's order; paying on a forged indorsement is not payment "to the drawer's order," so the drawee bank generally **may not debit the drawer's account** and bears the initial loss.
- The drawee bank may, however, **shift the loss back** to the collecting bank, because the collecting bank — by indorsing and presenting the check — became an indorser and is bound by the §66 warranties, including the warranty of genuineness of all prior indorsements; the collecting bank cannot set up the forgery defense against the drawee bank it warranted to.
- This "loss-shifting chain" runs back toward the forger, but stops wherever a party can prove the next party up the chain was negligent in a way that **substantially contributed** to the loss (here, comparative negligence on the drawer's part — continuing to release checks to a retired, unauthorized collector — produced a 50/50 apportionment between the drawee bank and the drawer's principal).
- The drawee bank that discovers a forged indorsement must give the collecting bank **prompt notice**; unreasonable delay that deprives the collecting bank of the opportunity to recover from the forger **forfeits the drawee bank's right of reimbursement**.

This case is the principal authority cited throughout Philippine banking practice for allocating loss on forged-indorsement checks, and should be the first citation reached for whenever a query involves a forged check passing through bank collection channels.

## Presentment for Payment
**Not a condition to the primary debtor's liability (§70)**: Presentment for payment is not necessary to charge the person primarily liable (maker, acceptor) — though if the instrument is by its terms payable at a special place and the primary debtor was able and willing to pay there at maturity, this is equivalent to a tender of payment.

**Demand instruments (§71)**: Where payable on demand, presentment must be made within a reasonable time after issue (for a note) or after the last negotiation (for a bill), to charge the drawer, and within a reasonable time after indorsement, to charge the indorser. What is "reasonable" is a question of fact depending on the instrument's nature, trade usage, and the facts of the particular case.

**Sufficiency of presentment (§72)**: The instrument must be exhibited to the person from whom payment is demanded, presented at a reasonable hour on a business day, at a proper place, to the party liable or their authorized agent.

**Place of presentment (§73)**: Where the instrument specifies a place of payment, presentment must be made there; otherwise at the party's place of business or residence, or, if neither can be found, wherever the party may be found, or at their last known place of business/residence.

**Presentment excused (§88)** where, after the exercise of reasonable diligence, presentment cannot be made; where the drawee is a fictitious person; or where presentment is waived (expressly or impliedly, e.g., by a renewal agreement). **Presentment for payment dispensed with (§92)** in additional circumstances, including where the drawer has no right to expect or require the drawee/acceptor to pay, and where, after dishonor by non-acceptance, presentment for payment is unnecessary even though not originally excused.

**Effect of delay**: Failure to present within the required time **discharges secondarily liable parties only to the extent of any loss caused by the delay** — it does not extinguish the underlying debt against the primary obligor. This distinction (delay-based discharge being limited to actual loss, not automatic) is the conceptual core of the stale-check doctrine developed fully in `negotiable-instruments/checks-and-special-rules.md`.

## Dishonor and Notice of Dishonor
**Dishonor by non-payment (§93)**: An instrument is dishonored by non-payment when it is duly presented for payment and payment is refused or cannot be obtained, or presentment is excused and the instrument is overdue and unpaid.

**Right of recourse**: Upon dishonor, the holder has an immediate right of recourse against all parties secondarily liable (drawers, indorsers), subject to the notice-of-dishonor requirement below.

**Notice of dishonor — general rule**: Except where notice is dispensed with (§§111–112) or delay is excused (§113), notice of dishonor must be given to each indorser and to the drawer to hold them liable. Notice may be given by or on behalf of the holder, or by or on behalf of any party to the instrument who might be compelled to pay it and who, upon taking it up, would have a right to reimbursement from the party notified.

**Form and timing**: Notice may be in writing or merely oral, and may be given by personal delivery or through the mails; it must be given within a reasonable time after dishonor — generally, the next business day for parties in the same place, or by the next mail for parties in different places.

**Waiver of notice (§§109–110)**: May be express or implied from the indorser's/drawer's conduct (e.g., an express waiver written on the instrument binds all parties; a waiver written above an indorser's signature binds only that indorser).

**When notice need not be given**: To the drawer, where the drawer and drawee are the same person, where the drawee is a fictitious person or has no capacity to contract, where the drawer is the person to whom the instrument is presented for payment, or where the drawer has no right to expect or require that the drawee/acceptor will honor the instrument (§114). To an indorser, where the drawee is a fictitious person or person without capacity and the indorser was aware of this when they indorsed (§115).

## Discharge of the Instrument (§119)
A negotiable instrument is discharged by:
1. **Payment in due course** by or on behalf of the principal debtor.
2. **Payment in due course** by the party accommodated, where the instrument was made or accepted for accommodation.
3. **Intentional cancellation** by the holder.
4. **Any other act** which discharges a simple contract for the payment of money.
5. The **principal debtor becomes the holder** of the instrument at or after maturity, in their own right.

"Payment in due course" (§88, cross-referenced) means payment made at or after maturity to the holder, in good faith, and without notice that the holder's title is defective.

---

## Discharge of Parties Secondarily Liable (§120)
A party secondarily liable is discharged by:
1. Any act which discharges the instrument itself.
2. **Intentional cancellation** of that party's signature by the holder.
3. **Discharge of a prior party** (discharging an earlier indorser discharges all subsequent indorsers who would otherwise have a right of recourse against the discharged party).
4. A **valid tender of payment** by a prior party.
5. **Release of the principal debtor**, unless the holder's right of recourse against the party secondarily liable is expressly reserved.
6. Any **agreement binding the holder to extend the time of payment**, or to postpone the holder's right to enforce the instrument, unless made with the assent of the party secondarily liable or unless the right of recourse against that party is expressly reserved.

**Renunciation (§122)**: The holder may renounce their rights against any party, before, at, or after maturity — absolutely and unconditionally — but a renunciation does not affect the rights of a holder in due course without notice of it, and (unless the instrument is surrendered to the party primarily liable) must be in writing.

**Cancellation — unintentional, presumption (§123)**: A cancellation made unintentionally, by mistake, or without the holder's authority is inoperative. Where an instrument or signature appears to have been canceled, the burden of proof is on the party alleging the cancellation was unintentional, mistaken, or unauthorized.

## Material Alteration (§§124–125)
**Effect (§124)**: Where an instrument is **materially altered without the assent of all parties liable on it**, the instrument is **avoided** — except as against (a) a party who themselves made, authorized, or assented to the alteration, and (b) subsequent indorsers. A **holder in due course who was not party to the alteration**, however, may still enforce payment **according to the instrument's original tenor** — the alteration does not destroy the underlying obligation as to an innocent HDC, it merely caps recovery at the unaltered amount/terms.

**What constitutes a material alteration (§125)**: Any change that alters (a) the date; (b) the sum payable, either of principal or interest; (c) the time or place of payment; (d) the number or relations of the parties; (e) the medium or currency in which payment is to be made; or which (f) adds a place of payment where none is specified, or (g) makes any other change in the instrument that alters its effect in any respect.

**Interaction with §14 (unauthorized completion of blanks)**: Filling a blank instrument beyond the authority actually given is treated as a material alteration as against the party who did not authorize the excess — but, per `negotiable-instruments/general-provisions.md` §14, an HDC may still enforce the instrument as completed, since the HDC takes free of this personal-defense-flavored limitation. Note the asymmetry with ordinary §124–125 alteration: §14 protects the HDC for the *completed* amount; §124's general rule protects the HDC only for the *original* amount where the instrument was already complete and was then altered. The distinguishing question is always: was the instrument complete (then later altered) or incomplete (then later completed, possibly beyond authority)?

## Quick Reference — Primary vs. Secondary Liability
| Party | Liability Type | Presentment Required? | Notice of Dishonor Required? |
|---|---|---|---|
| Maker | Primary | No | No |
| Acceptor | Primary | No (acceptance already fixes liability) | No |
| Drawer | Secondary | Yes (presentment to drawee/acceptor) | Yes, generally |
| Indorser (general) | Secondary | Yes | Yes, generally |
| Indorser (qualified, "w/o recourse") | None on the payment-guaranty branch; §65 warranties only | N/A for the negated branch | N/A for the negated branch |
| Accommodation/irregular indorser | Secondary, same as ordinary indorser | Yes | Yes, generally |

**Negotiating without indorsement — agent or broker (§69)**: A person who, as a mere agent or broker, negotiates an instrument without indorsing it does **not** thereby incur an indorser's liability — they are presumed to be transferring only such title as their principal had, consistent with the §49 rule that transfer without indorsement operates as assignment, not negotiation proper.

## Boundary Notes
This module covers **who is liable, on what terms, the mechanics of enforcing that liability (presentment and notice), and how liability is discharged or an instrument avoided**. It does **not** cover: requisites of negotiability, instrument formation, or bills-of-exchange acceptance mechanics (→ `negotiable-instruments/general-provisions.md`); negotiation, indorsement typology, or HDC status (→ `negotiable-instruments/negotiation-and-holders.md`); or check-specific rules — stale checks, certification, crossed checks, and the BP 22 boundary (→ `negotiable-instruments/checks-and-special-rules.md`).

## Anti-Hallucination Guardrails — Liabilities, Presentment, Discharge
The drawee bank's strict-liability default (it bears the loss on a forged-indorsement check) is **not** the end of the analysis — it may shift the loss back through the collection chain via the §66 warranty chain; do not present *Associated Bank v. CA* as holding the drawee bank simply absorbs the loss. A "without recourse" qualified indorsement removes only the payment-guaranty branch of §66 liability — see `negotiable-instruments/negotiation-and-holders.md` for the surviving §65 warranties; do not state a qualified indorser has zero exposure. Failure to present an instrument on time discharges a secondarily liable party **only to the extent of loss actually caused by the delay** — it is not an automatic, unlimited discharge, and does not affect the primary obligor's underlying debt at all. Material alteration under §124 avoids the instrument as against non-consenting parties, but an innocent HDC may still recover according to the instrument's **original** tenor — never state that material alteration is a complete real defense that defeats even this limited HDC recovery. Do not confuse §14 (unauthorized completion of an originally incomplete instrument, HDC-protective for the completed amount) with §124–125 (alteration of an originally complete instrument, HDC-protective only for the original amount) — they reach different outcomes and are frequently tested against each other precisely because they look similar.
