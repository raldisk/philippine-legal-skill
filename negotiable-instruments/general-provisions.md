# Negotiable Instruments — General Provisions

## Primary Sources
**Act No. 2031 (February 3, 1911) — The Negotiable Instruments Law ("NIL")**, adopted from the U.S. Uniform Negotiable Instruments Law. Governs bills of exchange, promissory notes, and checks. Citations below use **§** for NIL sections. **Not to be confused with B.P. 129** (Judiciary Reorganization Act of 1980 — court jurisdiction; unrelated body of law).

*Note*: NIL is a special law and prevails over the Civil Code on matters it covers; the Civil Code applies suppletorily to matters NIL does not address (§196 — law merchant and existing legislation govern cases not provided for).

---

## Instruments Covered
**Bill of exchange (§126)**: Unconditional order in writing, signed by the drawer, addressed to the drawee, requiring the drawee to pay on demand or at a fixed/determinable future time a sum certain in money to order or bearer.

**Promissory note (§184)**: Unconditional promise in writing, signed by the maker, engaging to pay on demand or at a fixed/determinable future time a sum certain in money to order or bearer. A note drawn to the maker's own order is not complete until indorsed by the maker.

**Check (§185)**: A bill of exchange drawn on a bank, payable on demand. Provisions applicable to demand bills apply to checks except where NIL otherwise provides. Full treatment in `negotiable-instruments/checks-and-special-rules.md`.

## Requisites of Negotiability (§1)
An instrument is negotiable only if it meets **all** of the following:
1. **In writing and signed** by the maker or drawer.
2. **Unconditional promise or order** to pay — a promise/order coupled with a condition destroys negotiability; mere reference to the transaction giving rise to the instrument does not (§3).
3. **Sum certain in money** (§2) — remains certain even if payable with interest, by stated installments, with an acceleration clause on default, with exchange at a fixed or current rate, or with attorney's fees/collection costs upon default.
4. **Payable on demand or at a determinable future time** (§4) — fixed period after date/sight; a fixed or determinable future date; or a fixed period after a specified event **certain to happen**, though its timing is uncertain. An instrument payable upon a contingency is **not negotiable**, and the contingency's later occurrence does not cure the defect.
5. **Payable to order or to bearer** (§§8–9).
6. **Drawee named or reasonably indicated** with reasonable certainty, for bills (inapplicable to notes).

**Mnemonic used in PH bar reviewers**: WU-POA (Writing, Unconditional, Payable on demand/determinable future time, Order/bearer, Addressed with certainty to drawee).

## When Payable on Demand, to Order, or to Bearer
**Payable on demand (§7)**: (a) expressed to be payable on demand, at sight, or on presentation; or (b) no time for payment is expressed. An instrument issued, accepted, or indorsed after maturity is, as regards the person so issuing, accepting, or indorsing it, payable on demand.

**Payable to order (§8)**: Drawn payable to the order of a specified person, or to him or his order — the payee must be named or otherwise indicated with reasonable certainty. May be drawn payable to the order of (1) a payee who is not the maker, drawer, or drawee; (2) the drawer or maker; (3) the drawee; (4) two or more payees jointly; (5) one or some of several payees; or (6) the holder of an office for the time being.

**Payable to bearer (§9)**: (a) expressed to be so payable; (b) payable to a person named therein **or** bearer; (c) payable to the order of a fictitious or non-existing person, and such fact was known to the person making it so payable; (d) the name of the payee does not purport to be the name of any person; or (e) the only or last indorsement is an indorsement in blank. Category (c) — the **fictitious-payee rule** — is a frequently tested exception: if a maker/drawer knowingly names a fictitious payee, the instrument is bearer paper regardless of its face wording, because the maker/drawer cannot complain of a defect they themselves created.

## Provisions Not Affecting Negotiability (§5)
An instrument containing a promise/order to do any act **in addition to** payment of money is **not negotiable** — except where the additional provision merely:
(a) authorizes sale of collateral on default; (b) authorizes confession of judgment on default; (c) waives a benefit/protection law for the obligor's advantage; or (d) gives the holder an election among alternative undertakings. These do not destroy negotiability because they do not impose an *additional* payment obligation — they regulate remedies for the *same* obligation.

**Omissions not affecting validity (§6)**: An instrument is not invalid for being undated, for not specifying value given or that value has been given, for not specifying the place of drawing or payment, for bearing a seal, or for not specifying a particular kind of currency (default: legal tender of the place of payment).

## Date, Blanks, and Completion
**Undated instruments (§13)**: Holder may insert the true date of issue or acceptance — insertion of a wrong date is, as to a subsequent HDC, the true date, but as between immediate parties an erroneous date may raise a defense.

**Incomplete instruments — blanks (§14)**: Where an instrument is wanting in a material particular, the person in possession has *prima facie* authority to complete it by filling the blanks. To enforce against any person who became a party prior to completion, the blanks must be filled **strictly in accordance with the authority given and within a reasonable time**. Against a holder in due course, an instrument completed in this manner is valid and effective for all purposes **even if not filled strictly as authorized or within reasonable time** — the HDC's protection (`negotiable-instruments/negotiation-and-holders.md`, §52) cures the drawer's/maker's risk in delivering an incomplete instrument. Unauthorized completion beyond the agreed amount is treated as a **material alteration** as to the party who did not authorize it (`negotiable-instruments/liabilities-and-discharge.md`, §125).

**Undelivered incomplete instrument (§15)**: If never delivered, an incomplete instrument is not a valid contract even in the hands of an ordinary holder; valid delivery is presumed until the contrary is proved.

**Delivery (§16)**: Every contract on a negotiable instrument is incomplete and revocable until delivery for the purpose of giving effect to it. Delivery may be conditional or for a special purpose only, as between immediate parties or remote parties with notice — but as to a holder in due course, a valid delivery is conclusively presumed. A check delivered as collateral security for a loan, for example, is conditionally delivered as between drawer and the immediate payee; the condition does not bind a subsequent HDC.

## Ambiguous Instruments and Signatures
**Construction where ambiguous (§17)**: Where the instrument's terms are ambiguous, rules of construction apply — e.g., where there is a conflict between the written and figure amounts, the written sum controls; an undated instrument is deemed dated as of the time of issue; where signed in a representative capacity but the capacity is not clear, the signer's personal liability may attach unless the instrument shows otherwise.

**Trade or assumed name (§18)**: A person signing in a trade or assumed name is liable to the same extent as if signed in their own name.

**Agent's signature (§19–20)**: Binds the principal if the agent had authority, however the authority may be shown. An agent who signs without indicating the representative capacity, or who adds words merely describing themselves as agent without disclosing a principal, may be held personally liable depending on the form of signature.

**Procuration (§21)**: A signature "per procuration" operates as notice that the agent has only limited authority; the principal is bound only if the agent acted within the actual limits of that authority.

**Forged or unauthorized signature (§23)**: A forged signature, whether of the maker, drawer, acceptor, or an indorser, is **wholly inoperative** — no right to retain, give a discharge for, or enforce payment against any party can be acquired through it, unless the party against whom enforcement is sought is precluded from setting up the forgery or want of authority (e.g., by negligence facilitating the forgery, or by ratification). §23 avoids only the forged signature, not necessarily the whole instrument as against parties who signed validly. Full liability-allocation treatment (drawee vs. collecting bank, comparative negligence) is in `negotiable-instruments/liabilities-and-discharge.md`.

**Capacity — infant or corporation (§22)**: Indorsement or assignment by a corporation or infant passes the property in the instrument notwithstanding that, by reason of want of capacity, the corporation or infant may incur no liability on the instrument. The transferee still acquires a valid title as against everyone except the infant (whose own liability remains voidable under Civil Code minority rules) or a corporation acting ultra vires.

## Definitions Used Throughout the NIL Modules (§191)
**"Acceptance"** — completed by delivery or notification. **"Bearer"** — the person in possession of a bearer bill or note. **"Holder"** — the payee or indorsee of a bill or note who is in possession of it, or the bearer. **"Indorsement"** — completed by delivery. **"Instrument"** — a negotiable instrument under this Act. **"Issue"** — the first delivery of the instrument, complete in form, to a person who takes it as holder. **"Value"** — valuable consideration. **"Written"/"Writing"** — includes printed/print. These definitions control the use of these terms across all four `negotiable-instruments/` modules.

## Consideration
**Presumption of consideration (§24)**: Every negotiable instrument is *prima facie* presumed issued for valuable consideration, and every person whose signature appears thereon is presumed to have become a party for value.

**Value defined (§25)**: Any consideration sufficient to support a simple contract. An antecedent or pre-existing debt constitutes value, whether the instrument is payable on demand or at a future time.

**Holder for value (§26)**: Where value has at any time been given for the instrument, the holder is deemed a holder for value as to all parties who became parties prior to that time.

**Lien as holder for value (§27)**: A holder with a lien on the instrument, arising from contract or by implication of law, is a holder for value to the extent of the lien.

**Effect of want of consideration (§28)**: Absence or failure of consideration is a matter of defense as against any person not a holder in due course; partial failure is a defense pro tanto, whether the failure is an ascertained/liquidated amount or otherwise.

**Accommodation party (§29)**: One who signs as maker, drawer, acceptor, or indorser without receiving value, to lend their name to some other person. Liable on the instrument to a holder for value, even though the holder, at the time of taking, knew the signer to be an accommodation party only. As between the accommodation party and the accommodated party, the latter must indemnify the former.

## Bills of Exchange — Additional Form Rules
**Not an assignment of funds (§127)**: A bill of itself does not operate as an assignment of funds in the drawee's hands; the drawee is not liable on the bill unless and until it accepts.

**Multiple drawees (§128)**: A bill may be addressed to two or more drawees jointly, whether partners or not; but not to two or more drawees in succession (alternative or successive drawees are not permitted in the same instrument).

**Inland vs. foreign bills (§129)**: A bill is inland if it is, or on its face purports to be, both drawn and payable within the Philippines; otherwise it is foreign. Unless the contrary appears, the holder may treat it as inland.

**Bill treated as note (§130)**: Where the drawer and drawee are the same person, or where the drawee is a fictitious person or one without capacity to contract, the holder may treat the instrument, at their option, as either a bill of exchange or a promissory note.

## Acceptance of Bills of Exchange
**How made (§132)**: The drawee's signed engagement to honor the bill as presented; must not express that the drawee will perform the promise by any other means than payment of money.

**Holder entitled to acceptance on the bill's face (§133)**: A provision elsewhere does not satisfy the holder's right to have the acceptance written on the bill itself, except where the parties expressly agree otherwise.

**Acceptance by separate instrument (§134)**: Where acceptance is written on a paper other than the bill itself, it binds the acceptor only in favor of a person to whom it is shown and who, on the faith of it, receives the bill for value.

**Promise to accept (§135)**: An unconditional written promise to accept a bill before it is drawn is deemed an actual acceptance in favor of every person who, on the faith of the promise, receives the bill for value.

**Time to accept (§136)**: The drawee has 24 hours after presentment to decide whether to accept; a longer retention of the bill, beyond what is allowed the drawee for deliberation, is deemed a refusal to accept (dishonor by non-acceptance).

**Kinds of acceptance (§139)**: **General** — assents without qualification to the order as drawn. **Qualified** — varies the effect of the bill as drawn (e.g., conditional, partial as to amount, qualified as to time or place of payment, or accepted by some but not all drawees). A holder may refuse a qualified acceptance; if they take it without the drawer's/indorsers' express or implied assent, the drawer and indorsers are discharged from liability unless they subsequently assent.

---

## Presentment for Acceptance
**When required (§143)**: Necessary to fix the maturity of an instrument payable a fixed period after sight, or where the bill expressly stipulates presentment for acceptance, or where the bill is payable elsewhere than at the drawee's residence/place of business (subject to listed exceptions).

**Effect of failure to present (§144)**: Where presentment for acceptance is required and the holder fails to present within a reasonable time, the drawer and all indorsers are discharged.

**Where presentment excused (§148)**: Where the drawee is dead, has absconded, or is a fictitious person or one without capacity to contract; or after reasonable diligence presentment cannot be effected; or, although presentment has been irregular, acceptance has been refused for some other reason.

**Dishonor by non-acceptance (§149)** — and the consequent right of the holder to immediate recourse against the drawer and indorsers — is treated together with dishonor by non-payment in `negotiable-instruments/liabilities-and-discharge.md`, since the notice-of-dishonor mechanics are unified across both forms of dishonor under the NIL.

---

## Bills in a Set
**Nature (§178)**: A bill may be drawn in a set of parts, each numbered and referring to the others; the whole of the parts constitutes one bill.

**Negotiating different parts to different holders (§179–180)**: A holder who negotiates two or more parts of a set to different persons is liable on every such part, and every indorser subsequent to them is liable on the part they negotiate, as if the parts were separate bills.

**Acceptance and payment (§181–182)**: Acceptance may be written on and is effective only on one part; if the acceptor accepts more than one part and the accepted parts are negotiated to different holders in due course, the acceptor is liable on every such part as if it were a separate bill. Payment on any one part, if made in due course, discharges the whole bill — even though the part paid is not surrendered, provided the payer is not put on notice that some other part has been negotiated to a holder in due course who has not been paid.

**Discharge of one part (§183)**: Where one part is discharged by payment or otherwise, the whole bill is discharged, except as against a holder in due course of another part without notice of the payment or discharge.

*Practical note*: bills in a set are rare in ordinary Philippine commercial practice (mostly historical/maritime trade usage) but remain a recurring bar exam topic precisely because they are unfamiliar; the core testable point is that payment of one part discharges all, subject to the HDC-of-another-part exception.

## Boundary Notes
This module covers **form, requisites, and the underlying contract** of negotiable instruments. It does **not** cover: negotiation mechanics, indorsement types, or holder-in-due-course status (→ `negotiable-instruments/negotiation-and-holders.md`); liability allocation among parties, presentment for payment, dishonor, discharge, or material alteration (→ `negotiable-instruments/liabilities-and-discharge.md`); or check-specific rules including stale checks, certification, and crossed checks (→ `negotiable-instruments/checks-and-special-rules.md`). ## Anti-Hallucination Guardrails — NIL General Provisions
**Act No. 2031 is the Negotiable Instruments Law — never cite B.P. 129 for NIL doctrine; B.P. 129 is the Judiciary Reorganization Act of 1980 and has no bearing on negotiability, indorsement, or instrument liability.** A condition attached to a promise/order to pay destroys negotiability — do not confuse this with the §5 list of provisions that do *not* affect negotiability (collateral-sale authorization, confession-of-judgment clause, waiver of obligor protections, election clauses); only those four are safe-harbored. An instrument payable upon a contingency is never negotiable even if the contingency later occurs — the defect is not cured retroactively. §14 completion-of-blanks protection runs in favor of a holder in due course only; it does not validate unauthorized completion against the original maker/drawer or a non-HDC holder. Never state that delivery is irrelevant to an instrument's validity — an instrument is revocable and incomplete as a contract until delivery, subject to the HDC delivery presumption. Do not present the §24 presumption of consideration as irrebuttable — it is a presumption, not an irrebuttable rule, and yields to proof of want or failure of consideration as against non-HDC holders.
