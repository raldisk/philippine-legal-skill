# Negotiable Instruments — Negotiation and Holders

## Primary Sources
**Act No. 2031 (NIL), Title II (Negotiation, §§30–50) and Title III (Rights of the Holder, §§51–61)**. Builds on `negotiable-instruments/general-provisions.md` (form, requisites, signatures). Citations use **§** for NIL sections.

---

## What Constitutes Negotiation (§30)
Transfer of an instrument from one person to another in such a manner as to constitute the transferee the **holder**. Two modes, determined by the instrument's character:
- **Bearer instrument** → negotiated by **mere delivery** (no indorsement required, even if specially indorsed).
- **Order instrument** → negotiated by **indorsement of the holder, completed by delivery**.

**Transfer without indorsement (§49)**: Where the holder of an order instrument transfers it for value without indorsing, the transferee acquires only such title as the transferor had (i.e., the transfer operates as a mere **assignment**, governed by Civil Code assignment-of-credit rules, not as a negotiation) — plus the right to demand the transferor's indorsement. For HDC-status purposes, negotiation is deemed to occur only when the indorsement is actually later made, not at the date of the underlying transfer.

---

## Indorsement — Form and Requisites
**How made (§31)**: Written on the instrument itself or on a paper attached to it (**allonge**); the indorser's signature alone, without additional words, is sufficient.

**Must be of the entire instrument (§32)**: An indorsement purporting to transfer only part of the sum, or to two or more indorsees severally (not jointly), does not operate as a negotiation. (An indorsement to pay part to one and the residue to another is likewise not a valid negotiation of the whole.)

**Indorsement to two or more payees jointly (§41)**: All must indorse, unless the one indorsing has authority to indorse for the others.

**Misspelled or wrong name (§43)**: Indorsee may indorse using the name as it appears on the instrument, adding (if desired) their correct signature.

**Effect on instrument drawn payable to a person "as cashier" or similar office (§42)**: May be treated either as payable to the individual holding the office, or to the institution; an indorsement by either the institution or the individual holder is sufficient.

**Presumptions (§§45–47)**: A signature is presumed prima facie to be valid and genuine until the contrary is shown. An indorsement is presumed made before the instrument's maturity (timing presumption). An undated indorsement is presumed made at the place where the instrument is dated.

---

## Kinds of Indorsement (§§33–40)
**Special indorsement (§34)**: Specifies the indorsee to whom, or to whose order, the instrument is payable. Thereafter negotiable only by that indorsee's own indorsement.

**Blank indorsement (§34)**: Specifies no indorsee — the instrument becomes payable to bearer and may thereafter be negotiated by delivery alone, **unless and until specially indorsed by a subsequent holder** (§35: the holder may convert a blank indorsement into a special one by writing above the indorser's signature words making it payable to themselves or another).

**Restrictive indorsement (§36)**: (a) prohibits further negotiation (e.g., "Pay X only"); (b) constitutes the indorsee an agent of the indorser (e.g., "Pay X for collection," "Pay X for account of Y"); or (c) vests title in the indorsee in trust for, or to the use of, some other person. A restrictive indorsement does *not* by itself prevent further transfer or negotiation of the instrument — it does prevent the instrument's negotiation by stripping subsequent holders of HDC status as to the restriction (§37: the indorsee under a restrictive indorsement, and every subsequent indorsee, takes the instrument with only the title and rights the restrictive indorsement gives — they may not give a better title than they themselves have).

**Qualified indorsement (§38)**: "Without recourse" or words of similar import — constitutes the indorser a mere assignor of title, **negating** the §66 warranty of payment but **not** the warranties of genuineness, good title, capacity of prior parties, and the indorser's lack of knowledge of any fact that would impair the instrument's validity (these latter warranties survive a qualified indorsement under §65; full treatment in `negotiable-instruments/liabilities-and-discharge.md`).

**Conditional indorsement (§39)**: Indorsement subject to a condition. The condition is effective between indorser and indorsee, and as to other parties with notice, but a party who pays in due course before having notice of the condition's non-fulfillment is not affected by it; the conditional indorsement does not impair the instrument's negotiability.

**Indorsement of bearer paper (§40)**: A bearer instrument that is specially indorsed remains, despite the special indorsement, negotiable by delivery alone as to any holder — the special indorsement merely adds the indorser as a party who warranted and may be held liable, but does not convert bearer paper into order paper.

## Continuation of Negotiable Character
**Continuing negotiability (§47)**: An instrument negotiable in its origin continues negotiable until it has been (a) restrictively indorsed, or (b) discharged by payment or otherwise. See `negotiable-instruments/liabilities-and-discharge.md` for discharge mechanics.

**Striking out an indorsement (§48)**: The holder may, at any time, strike out any indorsement not necessary to their own title. The indorser whose indorsement is struck out, and all indorsers subsequent to that indorsement, are thereby relieved from liability on the instrument.

**Negotiation back to a prior party (§50)**: Where an instrument is negotiated back to a party who was previously liable on it, that party may reissue and further negotiate the instrument — but may not enforce payment against any intervening party to whom they were themselves personally liable (no double recovery through round-tripping the instrument).

**Right of holder to sue; payment discharges (§51)**: The holder of a negotiable instrument may sue on it in their own name, and payment to the holder in due course discharges the instrument.

---

## Holder in Due Course — Requisites (§52)
A holder takes free of personal defenses **only if all four** requisites are met:
1. The instrument is **complete and regular on its face**.
2. The holder **became the holder before the instrument was overdue**, and without notice that it had been previously dishonored, if such was the fact.
3. The holder **took it in good faith and for value**.
4. At the time the instrument was negotiated to the holder, the holder had **no notice of any infirmity in the instrument or defect in the title** of the person negotiating it.

**Demand instruments and staleness (§53)**: Where an instrument payable on demand is negotiated an unreasonably long time after its issue, the holder is **not** deemed a holder in due course — this is the statutory hook for the stale-instrument doctrine elaborated for checks specifically in `negotiable-instruments/checks-and-special-rules.md` (6-month banking-practice benchmark).

**Notice received before full payment (§54)**: If the transferee, before having paid the full agreed amount, receives notice of an infirmity or defect, they are an HDC only to the extent of the amount already paid.

**Defective title (§55)**: Title is defective when the negotiator obtained the instrument (or any signature on it) by fraud, duress, force and fear, or other unlawful means, or for an illegal consideration, or negotiated it in breach of faith or under circumstances amounting to fraud.

**Notice of defect (§56)**: Requires actual knowledge of the infirmity/defect, or knowledge of facts such that taking the instrument amounted to bad faith — mere suspicious circumstances that a prudent person would have investigated are generally **not**, by themselves, notice; failure to inquire is not notice unless the failure itself amounts to bad faith.

**Landmark case — speculative purchase defeats HDC status**: *Montinola v. Philippine National Bank*, 88 Phil. 178 (1951). A treasury warrant/check materially overdue was purchased at a steep discount; the buyer was held **not** a holder in due course because (a) under §52 the check was taken long after it was overdue, and (b) the buyer was not even a proper "holder" under the NIL's definition (§191) given the chain of title. The case is the standard citation for the proposition that buying an obviously overdue instrument at a deep discount is itself circumstantial evidence defeating HDC status.

## Rights of the Holder — HDC vs. Non-HDC
**Rights of an HDC (§57)**: Holds the instrument **free from any defect of title of prior parties**, and free from defenses available to prior parties among themselves; may enforce payment for the full amount against all parties liable.

**Non-HDC holder (§58)**: Holds the instrument subject to the same defenses as if it were non-negotiable — i.e., subject to both real and personal defenses, as if simply assigned. (A non-HDC who is not party to any fraud or illegality, but merely derives title through an HDC, "steps into the shoes" of that HDC under the **shelter rule** — they enjoy the rights of the HDC they derive from, even though they themselves do not independently satisfy §52, provided they were not party to the fraud/illegality.)

**Presumption and burden of proof (§59)**: Every holder is **deemed prima facie** to be a holder in due course. But once it is shown that the title of any person who negotiated the instrument was defective, the burden **shifts to the holder** to prove either (a) that they themselves are an HDC, or (b) that some person under whom they claim acquired title as an HDC (the shelter rule).

**HDC status, once acquired, is not retroactively divested**: The four §52 requisites are tested **at the moment of negotiation** to the holder. Notice of an infirmity received *after* the holder has already become an HDC does not strip that status — it only matters for purposes of §54 (notice before *full payment*, relevant where the price was payable in installments or on credit). A person who is already an HDC when they take the instrument keeps that status even if they learn of the defect the next day.

---

## Real vs. Personal Defenses
**Real (absolute) defenses** — good against everyone, including an HDC, because they attack the instrument's very existence/validity: forgery of a necessary signature (§23); want of capacity (e.g., minority where the law treats the obligation as void rather than voidable); material alteration (as against the original tenor only — `negotiable-instruments/liabilities-and-discharge.md`, §§124–125); duress amounting to a complete absence of consent (as opposed to ordinary fraud/duress, which is personal); and prescription/extinctive prescription of the action.

**Personal (equitable) defenses** — good only against a non-HDC, because they arise from the particular dealings between specific parties and do not run with the instrument: ordinary fraud in inducement, failure or want of consideration, filling up of blanks beyond authority (as against the party who authorized only a lesser completion), conditional delivery not yet fulfilled, payment before maturity, and ordinary duress/intimidation falling short of vitiating consent entirely.

**Practical test**: ask whether the defense is one that could be asserted against the **whole world** (real) or only against the **specific party** who dealt with the defendant (personal). A promissory-note maker cannot raise want-of-consideration against an HDC; they can always raise forgery of their own signature against anyone, HDC or not.

## Warranties Accompanying Negotiation (§65)
A person who negotiates an instrument **by mere delivery** (bearer paper) or **by qualified indorsement** warrants to all subsequent holders **with right of recourse only against immediate parties**:
1. That the instrument is genuine and is in all respects what it purports to be.
2. That the negotiator has a good title to it.
3. That all prior parties had capacity to contract.
4. That the negotiator has no knowledge of any fact that would impair the validity of the instrument or render it valueless.

These four warranties survive even a "without recourse" qualified indorsement — only the payment-guaranty obligation of an unqualified general indorser (§66, treated in `negotiable-instruments/liabilities-and-discharge.md`) is what "without recourse" negates. This is the recurring point of confusion this module exists to resolve: *qualified does not mean warranty-free*.

## Quick Reference — Indorsement Types
| Type | Effect | Negates Recourse? | Negates §65 Warranties? |
|---|---|---|---|
| Special | Names the indorsee; further negotiation needs that indorsee's signature | No | No |
| Blank | No indorsee named; converts to bearer paper | No | No |
| Restrictive | Limits further negotiation, or creates agency/trust | No (generally) | No |
| Qualified ("w/o recourse") | Indorser disclaims the payment guaranty only | **Yes** | **No** — §65 warranties survive |
| Conditional | Subject to a stated condition | No (between immediate parties only) | No |

## Worked Illustration — Real vs. Personal Defenses
A induces B by fraud to issue a promissory note in A's favor, then negotiates it to C, a holder in due course. B cannot raise the fraud-in-the-inducement defense against C — ordinary fraud is personal, not real. Contrast: if A had **forged** B's signature on the note rather than fraudulently inducing B to sign it themselves, B's signature is void as to everyone, including C, because forgery (§23) is a real defense attacking the existence of B's obligation altogether, not merely the quality of B's consent. The doctrinal line is whether B ever validly bound themself at all (real defense) versus whether B was induced into a contract that B did, in fact, execute (personal defense).

## Anti-Hallucination Guardrails — Negotiation and Holders
A **restrictive** indorsement does not, by itself, prevent further transfer of the instrument — it strips subsequent transferees of HDC protection as to the restriction; do not conflate this with an outright prohibition on transfer. A **qualified** ("without recourse") indorsement removes only the payment-guaranty warranty — the warranties of genuineness, good title, prior parties' capacity, and the indorser's own good faith survive and are NOT negated by "without recourse" language. Bearer paper that has been specially indorsed remains negotiable by delivery alone — a special indorsement on bearer paper adds a warranting party but does not convert the instrument into order paper. The §59 presumption of HDC status is rebuttable and shifts the burden to the holder only after defective title is shown — it is not a conclusive presumption. Do not state that mere suspicious circumstances automatically constitute notice of defect under §56 — actual knowledge, or knowledge amounting to bad faith, is required; negligence alone in failing to investigate is generally insufficient unless it rises to bad faith. Never cite *Montinola v. PNB* for a proposition other than the overdue-instrument/speculative-purchase HDC defeat — the case does not stand for general check-forgery liability allocation (see `negotiable-instruments/liabilities-and-discharge.md` for that line of authority).
