# Trademark Law

## Primary Sources
**IP Code (R.A. 8293) Part III (§§121–170)** — trademarks, service marks, trade names, collective marks. **IPOPHL** — registers and administers trademarks. **Philippines is a member of the Paris Convention and Madrid Protocol** — international trademark registration available.

---

## Nature and Registration
**Trademark defined (§121.1)**: Any visible sign capable of distinguishing the goods or services of an enterprise.

**Registration required**: Unlike copyright, trademark protection in the Philippines is **registration-based** — the registered owner has the exclusive right to use the mark. First-to-file system (§122) — the first to apply for registration has priority, subject to well-known marks exception.

**Requirements for registration**:
- Mark must be **distinctive** — fanciful, arbitrary, or suggestive marks are registrable; descriptive marks are not unless they have acquired distinctiveness (secondary meaning).
- Not deceptive, immoral, contrary to public order.
- Not confusingly similar to prior registered or pending marks, well-known marks, or trade names.
- Not purely functional.

**Grounds for refusal (§123)**: Identical or confusingly similar to prior registered mark; generic/descriptive/geographically descriptive mark without secondary meaning; deceptive marks; marks contrary to public order; names of persons without consent; coat of arms/flags/official emblems; well-known marks.

---

## Confusing Similarity Tests
**Dominancy test**: Focus on the dominant feature(s) of the competing marks — if the dominant portion of the marks is similar and likely to cause confusion, infringement exists. *McDonald's Corp. v. MacJoy Fastfood Corp.* (GR 166115): applied dominancy test; "MacJoy" and "McDo" found confusingly similar.

**Holistic/totality test**: Compare the marks in their entirety as a whole. Less favored than dominancy test in recent SC decisions but still applied.

**Related goods/services test**: Likelihood of confusion also depends on whether the goods or services are related — confusion may exist even for different classes if the goods are closely related.

---

## Rights of Trademark Owner
**Exclusive right**: Use the registered mark in connection with the goods/services for which it is registered.

**Assignment**: Trademark may be assigned with or without the goodwill of the business; but assignment of a mark for goods — the assigned mark may not be used for goods that are not substantially the same quality as those previously associated with the mark.

**License**: Recorded with IPOPHL to be effective against third parties.

**Well-known marks (§123.1[e])**: Even unregistered well-known marks are protected from registration of similar marks. IPOPHL adopts WIPO criteria. *Sehwani Inc. v. In-N-Out Burger* (GR 171053, 2008): well-known foreign mark protected in Philippines even without local registration.

---

## Infringement
**Trademark infringement (§155)**: Unauthorized use of a registered mark — same mark on same goods, or confusingly similar mark on same/related goods, without owner's consent.

**Unfair competition (§168)**: Passing off one's goods as those of another by any means — including unregistered marks, trade dress, and packaging that creates confusion. Load `intellectual-property/unfair-competition.md`.

**Criminal liability (§170)**: Imprisonment 2–5 years and fine ₱50,000–₱200,000 for trademark infringement and unfair competition.

---

## Duration and Maintenance
**Term**: 10 years from registration date, **renewable indefinitely** for 10-year periods.

**Use requirement**: Mark must be used in commerce within 3 years from registration; non-use for an uninterrupted period of 3 years grounds for cancellation, unless special circumstances excuse non-use.

---

## Anti-Hallucination Guardrails — Trademark Law
Philippine trademark protection is registration-based (first-to-file) — not first-to-use, unlike in the US. Well-known marks are an exception — they are protected even without local registration, but the mark must meet WIPO well-known mark criteria. Never apply copyright law's automatic protection to trademarks — registration is required. Descriptive marks are NOT inherently registrable — they require secondary meaning (acquired distinctiveness). The dominancy test is the primary SC test; never ignore it in favor of a purely visual holistic comparison. Trademark infringement requires likelihood of confusion — it does not require proof of actual confusion.

## Registration Procedure

**Filing**: Application filed with IPOPHL; must include reproduction of the mark, list of goods/services in specified classes (Nice Classification), applicant's name/address, nationality, and declaration of actual use or bona fide intent to use.

**Examination**: IPOPHL examines for absolute grounds (mark not registrable per §123 first set of reasons) and relative grounds (conflict with prior marks).

**Opposition period**: 30 days from publication of the mark; any person may oppose on grounds that the mark conflicts with their own rights. Opposition filed with the BLA.

**Certificate of Registration**: Issued after opposition period (if no opposition) or after favorable decision (if opposition was filed and overruled).

**Declaration of actual use (DAU)**: Must be filed: (1) within 3 years from registration date; (2) within 1 year from each subsequent renewal date. Failure to file DAU = cancellation of registration.

---

## Classification of Goods and Services (Nice Classification)

**45 classes**: 1–34 for goods; 35–45 for services. Applicant must specify the class(es) and goods/services within each class. Registration is class-specific — protection covers the designated goods/services.

**Class headings**: Not sufficient specification — applicant must identify specific goods/services within the class; broad class headings not accepted without specification.

**Multi-class applications**: Philippines allows multi-class trademark applications — one application, multiple classes, multiple fees.

---

## Well-Known Marks in Detail

**Criteria (IPOPHL Guidelines; Paris Convention Art. 6bis)**: Mark must be well-known in the Philippines even if not registered and even if the owner has no business in the Philippines. Factors: (1) degree of knowledge by the relevant sector of the public; (2) duration, extent, and geographic area of use; (3) duration, extent, and geographic area of promotion; (4) duration and geographic area of registration; (5) record of successful enforcement; (6) value associated with the mark.

**Protection for well-known unregistered marks**: Bar registration of confusingly similar marks (§123.1[e]); basis for cancellation of later registered marks; basis for opposition.

**Dilution of famous marks**: Marks that are famous (higher standard than well-known) are additionally protected from dilution — use of the mark in a context that blurs or tarnishes the distinctiveness of the famous mark, even for dissimilar goods/services.

---

## Geographic Indications and Appellations of Origin

**Geographic indications (§§166–168 IP Code)**: Signs identifying a product as originating in a country, region, or locality where the product's quality, reputation, or other characteristic is attributable to its geographic origin. Protected against misleading use.

**Philippine GIs**: Iloilo Biscocho; La Trinidad strawberries; Cordillera coffee; specific geographic product types. IPOPHL registers GIs. TRIPS Art. 22–24 applies for international protection.

**Balikatan vs. Denominaciones de Origen**: Philippine system distinguishes collective marks, GIs, and appellations of origin. Trade mark with geographic component may still be registered if it is not deceptive and is used as a trade mark rather than a GI.

---

## Domain Names and Trademarks

**Domain name disputes**: IPOPHL Arbitration Rules and ICANN UDRP govern domain name disputes. Trademark owner may file complaint under UDRP against cybersquatters — requires: (1) domain name identical or confusingly similar to trademark; (2) registrant has no legitimate interest; (3) domain registered and used in bad faith.

**Reverse domain name hijacking**: Abuse of UDRP proceedings to deprive legitimate registrant of their domain name — complaint dismissed and complainant found to have abused process.

---

## Anti-Counterfeiting Enforcement

**BOC border seizure**: Rights holder registers trademark with BOC (R.A. 10863, CMTA); BOC authorized to detain suspected counterfeit goods at the border; rights holder examines; confirms infringement; BOC destroys.

**EIAB (Enforcement and Intellectual Property Rights Division of IPOPHL)**: Coordinates with law enforcement; conducts raids in coordination with NBI or PNP; administrative adjudication of unfair competition cases.

**NBI and PNP IP units**: NBI Intellectual Property Crime Division; PNP anti-carnapping and anti-counterfeiting units. Criminal cases for trademark infringement and unfair competition — filed in designated IP courts (RTC).

---

## Cancellation of Trademark Registration

**Grounds**: (1) Mark was not registrable at time of registration; (2) mark has become generic; (3) mark has become deceptive; (4) non-use for 3 consecutive years; (5) registered in bad faith.

**Cancellation procedure**: Filed with BLA; inter partes; hearing; decision appealed to Director General; then CA via Rule 43; then SC.

**Generic marks**: If a trademark becomes the generic name for the goods it identifies (e.g., "elevator," "aspirin" in other jurisdictions), it may be cancelled. Rights holder must police against generic use to preserve distinctiveness.

---

## Key Cases — Trademark Law

**Sehwani Inc. v. In-N-Out Burger** (GR 171053, 2008): Well-known foreign mark is protected in the Philippines even without local registration or local business operations; trademark registration obtained in bad faith by local entity is void.

**McDonald's Corp. v. MacJoy Fastfood Corp.** (GR 166115, 2005): The dominancy test — the dominant feature of the mark ("Mac" or "Mc") governs the similarity analysis; similarity in dominant features establishes likelihood of confusion regardless of other differences.

**Berris Agricultural Co. v. Abyadang** (GR 183404, 2010): The dominancy test applied to agricultural goods marks; overall impression created by the mark on an ordinary purchaser of reasonable intelligence determines the likelihood of confusion.

**Birkenstock Orthopaedie GmbH v. Phil. Shoe Expo Marketing Corp.** (GR 194307, 2014): A trademark registration must be defended against cancellation if the registrant fails to file the Declaration of Actual Use (DAU) within the prescribed period; failure to file DAU results in automatic cancellation.

| Event | Deadline | Consequence of Failure |
|---|---|---|
| File Declaration of Actual Use (DAU 1) | Within 3 years from registration | Automatic cancellation |
| Renewal filing | Within 6 months before expiry (or within 6 months after with surcharge) | Mark expires |
| File DAU with each renewal | Within 1 year from each renewal date | Cancellation |
| Opposition period | 30 days from publication | No opposition possible after |
| Cancellation petition | Any time (for absolute grounds); 5 years from registration (for relative grounds) | Check applicable ground |

## Industrial Designs

**Industrial designs (§§112–119 IP Code)**: Ornamental or aesthetic aspects of a product — lines, colors, shapes, texture, patterns. Separate from trademark and patent but often overlapping for consumer products.

**Registration required**: Protection requires registration with IPOPHL; unregistered designs not protected under IP Code (may be protected as copyright for original artistic works).

**Term**: 5 years from filing; renewable twice (maximum 15 years total).

**Test for infringement**: An informed user who compares the registered design with the alleged infringement — if the overall impression is the same (same aesthetic effect), infringement exists.

---

## International Trademark Registration — Madrid Protocol

**Madrid Protocol**: International trademark registration system administered by WIPO; Philippines is a member. Applicant files with IPOPHL; WIPO transmits to designated member states; each state examines under own law.

**Advantages**: Single application for multiple jurisdictions; single renewal; centralized recording of changes. Disadvantages: refusal in one country does not affect others; central attack (cancellation of base application within 5 years) can cascade.

**Philippine national phase**: Once WIPO forwards application to IPOPHL, IPOPHL has 18 months to refuse on absolute grounds; if no refusal, protection attaches.

## Trademark in the Digital Age
**Metatags and SEO**: Using a competitor's trademark as a hidden keyword in website metadata — may constitute passing off or trademark infringement if it diverts internet users from the legitimate trademark owner's website. Relevance is whether the use is commercial and likely to cause confusion. **Search engine keyword advertising**: Bidding on competitor's trademark as a keyword for advertising — IPOPHL and courts analyzing this as confusion of source; outcome varies based on visible use in the advertisement. **Social media handles**: Registration of social media handles that infringe or dilute trademarks — actionable under §168 unfair competition; platform's own dispute resolution procedures also available alongside legal action.

## Cancellation vs. Opposition — Procedural Comparison

| Feature | Opposition | Cancellation |
|---|---|---|
| When filed | During publication period (30 days) | Post-registration (any time for absolute grounds; 5 years for relative) |
| Who may file | Any person | Any person |
| Forum | IPOPHL BLA | IPOPHL BLA |
| Effect if granted | Application refused | Registration cancelled |
| Appeal | Director General → CA (Rule 43) → SC | Same |

**Priority of filing — practical note**: Trademark registration does not automatically defeat a prior user with a well-known mark; however, for ordinary marks, first to file wins. The DAU requirement further ensures that registered marks are actually being used in commerce — paper registrations for warehousing purposes will not survive the DAU deadline.

**Trademark tacking**: A trademark owner may tack a new version of their mark onto an earlier version for priority purposes if the two marks are legal equivalents — the new mark must create the same, continuing commercial impression. Allows trademark owners to modernize marks without losing priority date. Applied narrowly: the marks must be virtually identical — minor stylistic updates; not substantive changes.

**Color as trademark**: A single color may serve as a trademark for a specific product if it has acquired secondary meaning through exclusive use (e.g., Owens-Corning pink insulation in US). In the Philippines, single color marks are registrable under §121 IP Code if they are distinctive; IPOPHL practice has been cautious; acquired distinctiveness through use must be demonstrated.
