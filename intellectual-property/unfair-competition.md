# Unfair Competition

## Primary Sources
**IP Code (R.A. 8293) §168** — unfair competition. **Revised Penal Code Art. 189** — prior unfair competition provision (RPC), now largely superseded by IP Code for commercial IP matters but still relevant for some categories. **Civil Code** — Art. 19 (abuse of rights); Art. 26 (dignity and privacy violations); applicable in some unfair competition contexts.

---

## Nature of Unfair Competition
Broader than trademark infringement — covers any act that tends to create confusion between one's goods/services and those of a competitor by any means, even if no registered mark is involved. The core wrong is **passing off** — representing one's goods as those of another.

**Distinction from trademark infringement**: Trademark infringement requires a registered mark; unfair competition protects unregistered marks, trade dress, packaging, and business goodwill from deceptive imitation.

---

## Elements (§168 IP Code)
1. Defendant gave their goods/services the general appearance of the goods/services of another having established goodwill.
2. Such appearance was adopted in a way calculated to deceive the public or defraud the complainant.
3. Defendant's goods/services were intended to be confused with those of the complainant.

**Trade dress**: The overall commercial image of a product — packaging, appearance, configuration — may be protected against unfair competition even without trademark registration if the trade dress has acquired secondary meaning and the copying creates a likelihood of confusion.

---

## Acts Constituting Unfair Competition
**Passing off** (§168.2): Giving one's goods the general appearance of another's goods; selling goods with a false designation of origin; representing services as those of another.

**False designation**: Using any false designation of origin, false or misleading description of fact, or false or misleading representation.

**Trade secrets misappropriation**: Use of another's trade secret — confidential business information with independent economic value — obtained by improper means (breach of confidence, espionage, theft). Load `core/obligations-contracts.md` for breach of confidence doctrine.

**False advertising**: Misrepresentation of the nature, characteristics, or qualities of goods/services in commercial advertising or promotion.

---

## Remedies
**Civil (IP Code §168.2)**: Injunction; damages; accounting of profits; destruction of infringing goods.

**Criminal (§170 IP Code)**: Imprisonment 2–5 years and fine ₱50,000–₱200,000 for both trademark infringement and unfair competition.

**RPC Art. 189**: Some specific unfair competition acts may still be prosecuted under the RPC; concurrent jurisdiction possible.

---

## Trade Secrets
**Protection**: No specific registration; protection arises from secrecy and commercial value. Owner must take reasonable steps to maintain secrecy.

**Misappropriation**: Actionable when confidential information obtained by breach of confidence or improper means and used to the detriment of the owner.

**Duration**: As long as the information remains confidential and has commercial value — potentially indefinite, unlike patent protection.

---

## Anti-Hallucination Guardrails — Unfair Competition
Unfair competition under §168 does not require a registered trademark — it protects against passing off even for unregistered marks/trade dress. The elements require actual likelihood of confusion, not merely similar appearance — the appearance must be calculated to deceive. Trade secret protection requires the owner to take reasonable steps to maintain secrecy — voluntary disclosure or failure to protect destroys the trade secret status. Never conflate unfair competition with trademark infringement in remedies analysis — the threshold and elements differ. The criminal penalties under §170 IP Code apply to both trademark infringement and unfair competition; never treat unfair competition as purely a civil matter. RPC Art. 189 may still apply to specific acts of unfair competition defined therein — check whether the IP Code or RPC provision is the more appropriate charge.

## Unfair Competition vs. Other IP Violations — Distinctions

| Basis | Trademark Infringement | Copyright Infringement | Unfair Competition |
|---|---|---|---|
| Registered IP required | Yes — registered mark | No — automatic copyright | No — goodwill/trade dress |
| Core wrong | Unauthorized use of mark | Copying protected expression | Passing off; deception |
| Confusion required | Likelihood of confusion | Not required for copyright | Deceptive appearance required |
| Criminal penalty | Yes — §170 IP Code | Yes — §217 IP Code | Yes — §170 IP Code |

## Trade Dress Protection

**Definition**: The total image of a product or service — overall appearance including size, shape, color combinations, texture, graphics, and even the design of the establishment.

**Registrability**: Trade dress may be registered as a trademark if it is distinctive and non-functional. Non-distinctive trade dress may acquire secondary meaning over time through exclusive use.

**Functionality doctrine**: Functional elements of trade dress cannot be protected — if the feature is essential to the use or purpose of the article, or if protection would put competitors at a significant non-reputation-related disadvantage, it is functional and unprotectable.

**Key Philippine application**: Restaurant/fast food trade dress; packaging of consumer goods; store layout and design. *Plaintiff must show*: (1) distinctiveness; (2) non-functionality; (3) likelihood of confusion from defendant's imitation.

---

## Passing Off — Technical Elements

**Classic passing off (tri-partite)**: (1) Goodwill of plaintiff; (2) Misrepresentation by defendant causing confusion; (3) Damage to plaintiff's goodwill. *Erven Warnink BV v. J. Townend & Sons* (English case cited in Philippine IP literature) — foundational analysis applied in Philippine passing off cases.

**Extended passing off**: Goods bearing false designation of a class or type of goods (geographic indications; designation of origin) — all producers in the class/region may sue.

**Reverse passing off**: Defendant puts own name on goods actually produced by the plaintiff — attributes plaintiff's goods to the defendant. Less common; still actionable.

**Instruments of fraud**: Counterfeit marks; false invoices; deceptive labels; misleading URLs. Seizure of instruments of passing off is an available remedy.

---

## Trade Secrets in Detail

**Elements for protection**:
1. **Information**: Formula, pattern, compilation, program, device, method, technique, or process.
2. **Independent economic value**: Derives value from being secret — others would gain competitive advantage from having it.
3. **Reasonable efforts to maintain secrecy**: Owner must actively protect — non-disclosure agreements (NDAs); access controls; employee training; confidential stamps on documents.

**Employee obligations**: Employees who learn trade secrets during employment owe a duty of confidence even after resignation; NDAs extend this obligation post-employment. Without NDA: implied duty of confidence for confidential information learned in the course of employment.

**Independent development**: Third party who independently develops the same information — not liable for misappropriation; independent development is a complete defense.

**Reverse engineering**: Generally lawful for products legitimately acquired in the market — reverse engineering of software and hardware is not misappropriation unless done by breach of contract.

---

## Cybersquatting and Domain Names as Unfair Competition

**Cybersquatting**: Registering, using, or trafficking in a domain name that is identical or confusingly similar to a distinctive mark, with bad faith intent to profit from the mark owner's goodwill.

**Philippine jurisdiction**: No specific cybersquatting statute; claims under IP Code §168 (unfair competition) and the Civil Code (Art. 19 abuse of rights; Art. 20 damages). ICANN UDRP used for domain name recovery without litigation.

**Social media impersonation**: Using another's name, trade name, or mark on social media platforms to pass off as the legitimate entity — actionable as unfair competition under §168 and potentially as cyber libel or identity theft under R.A. 10175.

---

## Competition Law — Relationship to Unfair Competition

**Philippine Competition Act (R.A. 10667)**: Prohibits anti-competitive agreements, abuse of dominant position, anti-competitive mergers. PCA and IP Code unfair competition cover different ground: IP Code unfair competition = passing off and consumer deception; PCA = market competition distortions.

**Abuse of IP rights as competition violation**: Excessive royalty demands; sham litigation to exclude competitors; misuse of IP rights to restrain trade — may violate PCA as abuse of dominant position. Philippine Competition Commission (PCC) investigates; separate proceeding from IP enforcement.

---

## IPOPHL Mediation and ADR

**Mandatory mediation**: IP cases filed with IPOPHL BLA are referred to mediation first; if unresolved, proceed to formal hearing. IPOPHL-accredited mediators handle IP disputes.

**Settlement**: IP disputes highly amenable to settlement — licensing agreements, market partitioning, brand differentiation may resolve without full adjudication. Settlement agreements registered with IPOPHL.

## Key Cases — Unfair Competition

**Del Monte Corp. v. CA** (GR L-78325, 1990): "Del Monte" catsup bottle trade dress — packaging configuration protected as trade dress; defendant's use of similar bottle shape with similar label arrangement constituted unfair competition regardless of trademark registration.

**Tanduay Distillers v. Ginebra San Miguel** (GR 164324, 2008): Unfair competition requires proof of actual passing off or deceptive intention; mere similarity of packaging without evidence of deceptive intent is insufficient.

**Coffee Partners Inc. v. San Francisco Coffee & Roastery** (GR 169504, 2010): Trade name "San Francisco Coffee" — confusingly similar to prior trade name; trade name unfair competition covers not just marks but the entire commercial identity; prior user of trade name protected even without trademark registration.

**Asia Pacific Resources International v. Paperone Inc.** (GR 176066, 2014): Unfair competition is broader than trademark infringement; acts that tend to deceive the public about the source of goods — even if marks are different — may constitute unfair competition.

---

## Comparative Advertising

**Definition**: Advertising that explicitly or implicitly identifies a competitor or competitor's product. Generally allowed in the Philippines if: (1) accurate and truthful; (2) comparative claims can be substantiated; (3) not misleading; (4) does not denigrate the competitor.

**Denigration vs. comparative**: Merely stating superior performance of one's own product is comparative and permitted. Falsely stating that a competitor's product is dangerous or defective — denigration; actionable under IP Code and civil law.

**Substantiation requirement**: Advertiser must be able to substantiate any comparative claim with reliable data at the time the claim is made — DTI/IPOPHL may require documentation.

## Anti-Hallucination Guardrails — Consolidated IP
Never state that unfair competition requires a registered trademark — it explicitly does not. Trade dress is protectable without trademark registration if it has acquired secondary meaning and is non-functional. Trade secret protection has no term limit — it lasts as long as the information remains confidential AND has commercial value. Reverse engineering of legitimately acquired products is generally lawful — it is not misappropriation of trade secrets. Copyright infringement and unfair competition may overlap (e.g., unauthorized reproduction AND passing off of packaging) — both claims may be filed simultaneously. The criminal penalties for unfair competition (§170 IP Code) are the same as trademark infringement — not lighter. Cybersquatting is not specifically codified in the IP Code but may be prosecuted as unfair competition under §168.

## Pharmaceutical Sector and Unfair Competition
**International Non-Proprietary Names (INNs)**: Generic names of pharmaceutical substances cannot be monopolized as trademarks; use of INN in packaging is not infringement. However, mimicking the trade dress (color scheme, capsule shape, packaging design) of an originator drug may constitute trade dress infringement or unfair competition. **Data exclusivity**: Under TRIPS and R.A. 9502 (Universally Accessible Cheaper and Quality Medicines Act), data submitted by an originator for drug registration is protected from unfair commercial use for a period; generic manufacturers may rely on it only after data exclusivity expires. Misuse of proprietary clinical trial data before expiry — actionable.

**Anti-Hallucination Guardrails — Final Update**: Comparative advertising that is accurate and substantiated is generally lawful in the Philippines — not per se unfair competition. Denigration (false negative claims about competitor) crosses into unfair competition and defamation. FRAND obligation for SEP holders is a competition law concept — patent holder in standard-setting context who refuses to offer FRAND terms faces PCC action, not IPOPHL action. Industrial designs are a separate IP right with a 5-year (renewable twice) term — never confuse with trademarks or patents.

## False Designation of Origin and Geographical Indications — Enforcement
**Criminal enforcement**: §169.1 IP Code — using false designation of origin; criminal penalty under §170 (imprisonment 2–5 years; fine ₱50,000–₱200,000). Filed in designated IP courts.

**GI enforcement**: Producers from the geographic area may collectively sue to prevent misuse of the GI by non-qualifying producers. IPOPHL-registered GIs create a collective right enforceable in IP courts.

**Import restrictions**: BOC authorized to detain goods with false designations of origin at the border (R.A. 10863, CMTA, §252); rights holder requests detention; determination within 10 working days. Forfeit and destruction upon confirmation of violation. Geographic indications and appellations of origin entitled to same border protection as trademarks.

**Competitor product disparagement**: False statements of fact about a competitor's products or business — actionable as unfair competition AND business defamation (Art. 354 NCC; Art. 33 for civil action independent of criminal). Element: (1) false statement of fact; (2) publication; (3) about the plaintiff's business or product; (4) plaintiff suffered damages. Truth is a complete defense — accurate comparative claims are lawful. Puffery (non-specific superiority claims like 'the best in the industry') is not actionable — only specific false factual claims.

**Non-compete clauses and unfair competition**: Employees bound by valid non-compete agreements who violate them and compete by misappropriating trade secrets or client information — subject to both contractual remedies and unfair competition claims. Philippine courts enforce reasonable non-compete clauses (limited in time, geography, and scope); overly broad restraints of trade are void as contrary to public policy (Art. 1306 NCC).

**Franchising and unfair competition**: Former franchisee who sets up a similar business using the franchisor's trade secrets, customer lists, operational know-how, and trade dress — classic unfair competition; may also constitute trade secret misappropriation and trademark infringement if the ex-franchisee uses the franchisor's marks. Franchise agreements typically include post-term non-compete provisions, non-disclosure obligations, and IP ownership clauses — all enforceable to the extent they are reasonable. **Key distinction**: Using general skills and experience developed during the franchise (personal expertise) is lawful; using the franchisor's confidential information and systems is not. Philippine courts apply a balancing test between the freedom to work and the protection of legitimate business interests.

**Summary**: Unfair competition is the broadest IP protection tool — requiring no registration, covering trade dress, trade secrets, passing off, false designation, and deceptive commercial practices. Its flexibility makes it the most useful cause of action when registered IP rights have not yet been established or are difficult to prove.

## Enforcement Coordination — Multi-Agency Approach
**NBI IP Division**: Conducts raids on counterfeit goods operations; arrests infringers; files cases in IP courts. Search warrant applications supported by test purchases and rights holder affidavits.
**PNP Anti-Cybercrime Group**: Handles online IP violations — piracy websites, social media counterfeit sales, digital streaming piracy. Coordinates with IPOPHL and rights holders for evidence gathering.
**IPOPHL Enforcement Bureau**: Coordinates inter-agency task forces (IPACT — IP Action Committee); facilitates raids; manages rights holder registrations for border enforcement; issues administrative enforcement orders.
**DTI Fair Trade Enforcement Bureau**: Handles consumer deception and unfair trade practices under Consumer Act (R.A. 7394); concurrent jurisdiction for false advertising and deceptive product presentations; may refer IP-specific issues to IPOPHL.
**Barangay enforcement**: Local government units (LGUs) may conduct inspection of business establishments for counterfeit goods under local ordinances; may accompany NBI/PNP on raids in their jurisdiction.
