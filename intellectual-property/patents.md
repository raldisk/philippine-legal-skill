# Patent Law

## Primary Sources
**IP Code (R.A. 8293) Part II (§§21–119)** — patents and utility models. **IPOPHL** — examines and grants patents. **Philippines is a member of the Patent Cooperation Treaty (PCT)** — international patent applications may designate the Philippines.

---

## Patentable Inventions (§21)
**Requirements**: (1) **novel** — not in prior art; (2) **inventive step** — not obvious to a person skilled in the art; (3) **industrially applicable** — capable of being produced or used in any industry.

**Non-patentable (§22)**: Discoveries; scientific theories and mathematical methods; plant varieties (governed by R.A. 9168); essentially biological processes for producing plants/animals; methods for treatment of the human/animal body by surgery, therapy, or diagnosis; animal breeds; aesthetic creations; mental acts, games, business methods; computer programs per se.

---

## Patent Application and Grant
**First-to-file system (§29)**: The first applicant to file for the same invention is entitled to the patent — priority of filing prevails over priority of invention (unlike first-to-invent systems).

**Priority right (§31)**: Under the Paris Convention, an applicant who has filed in a member country may claim priority from that date (12 months for invention patents; 6 months for utility models/industrial designs) when filing in the Philippines.

**Examination**: IPOPHL conducts formal and substantive examination. Applicant may be required to amend claims.

**Publication**: Application published 18 months from filing (or priority date). Once published, provisional protection attaches — applicant may seek reasonable royalties from unauthorized users.

**Grant**: IPOPHL issues patent grant; registered in the Patent Register.

---

## Rights Conferred
**Exclusive rights (§71)**: Make, use, offer for sale, sell, import patented product; use patented process; make/use/sell/import product derived directly from patented process — without authorization of patent owner.

**Term**: **20 years from filing date**; not renewable.

**Utility models**: Protect technical innovations that are new and industrially applicable but need not involve an inventive step. Term: **7 years from filing**; not renewable.

---

## Limitations and Exceptions
**Prior user right**: Person who was in good faith using the invention in the Philippines before the filing date retains the right to continue that use.

**Compulsory licensing (§93)**: IPOPHL may grant a compulsory license on grounds including: non-working of the patent; national emergency; anti-competitive use; government non-commercial use. Reasonable royalties are paid to the patent owner.

**Research and experimental exception**: Acts done for experimental or research purposes do not constitute infringement.

**Bolar exemption**: Acts done to satisfy regulatory requirements for approval to market a pharmaceutical or agricultural product do not constitute infringement.

---

## Patent Infringement (§76)
**Definition**: Any unauthorized act within the exclusive rights of the patent owner. Literal infringement: every element of the claim is present in the accused product/process. **Doctrine of equivalents**: product/process that does not literally infringe but performs substantially the same function in substantially the same way to achieve substantially the same result may infringe.

**Remedies**: Civil — injunction; damages; reasonable royalties. Criminal (§84): imprisonment 6 months–3 years and fine ₱100,000–₱300,000 for fraudulent patents; patent infringement itself is primarily a civil matter.

---

## Anti-Hallucination Guardrails — Patent Law
The Philippines follows a first-to-file system — priority of invention does not determine patent rights; filing date does. Computer programs per se are not patentable — but a technical invention that uses software as part of a technical solution may be patentable; the distinction is critical. The 20-year patent term runs from the **filing date**, not from the grant date. Utility models have a 7-year non-renewable term and do not require an inventive step — they are a separate and lower tier of protection from invention patents. Compulsory licensing is an exception requiring grounds — it is not freely available; IPOPHL must find the statutory grounds satisfied. The doctrine of equivalents applies to literal non-infringement cases where the accused product/process is substantially equivalent — never apply it without analysis.

## Patent Claims — Drafting and Interpretation

**Independent vs. dependent claims**: Independent claim stands on its own (broadest scope); dependent claim incorporates all limitations of an independent claim and adds additional limitations (narrower scope). Infringement of an independent claim = infringement of all dependent claims.

**Claim interpretation**: Claims construed based on the specification and prosecution history (file wrapper estoppel). Claim amendments made to distinguish prior art limit the scope of the claim — cannot be recovered by the doctrine of equivalents (*Festo* principle applied in Philippine jurisprudence by analogy).

**Means-plus-function claims**: A claim element expressed as a means for performing a specified function — covers the corresponding structure/material in the specification and equivalents thereof.

---

## Prior Art and Novelty

**Prior art (§23)**: Any prior art existing before the filing date (or priority date if claimed); includes: earlier filed applications; published documents worldwide; prior public use or disclosure anywhere in the world. The Philippines follows the absolute novelty standard — prior art anywhere in the world defeats novelty.

**Grace period (§25)**: If the applicant or their predecessor disclosed the invention within 12 months before the filing date AND the disclosure was made: (a) by or with the consent of the inventor; or (b) in violation of a confidentiality obligation — such disclosure does not constitute prior art. Other disclosures (third parties without consent, unilateral disclosure by a competitor) DO constitute prior art.

**Inventive step/non-obviousness**: Compared against what a person having ordinary skill in the relevant art would know; compared against prior art as a whole; combination of prior art references may negate inventive step if the combination would be obvious to a skilled person.

---

## Biotechnology, Pharmaceutical, and Chemical Patents

**Pharmaceutical patents**: Patentable if they meet novelty, inventive step, and industrial applicability. New use of a known substance may be patentable. Enantiomers of known compounds may be patentable if they have unexpected advantages.

**TRIPS and compulsory licensing for pharmaceuticals**: Compulsory licensing for pharmaceuticals under TRIPS Art. 31 and the Doha Declaration — Philippines may grant compulsory licenses for HIV/AIDS, TB, malaria, and other public health emergencies at lower royalties.

**Bioequivalence and Bolar exemption**: Generic manufacturers may conduct clinical trials and regulatory submissions for bioequivalent drugs before patent expiry — this is not infringement (§72.4 IP Code).

**Traditional knowledge and plants**: Plant varieties protected under R.A. 9168 (Plant Variety Protection Act), not under patent law. Traditional knowledge associated with plant varieties — IPOPHL registers but does not patent; protection is sui generis.

---

## Patent Term Extension

**No automatic extension under IP Code**: Philippines does not provide for patent term extension for regulatory delays (unlike in the US for pharmaceuticals). The 20-year term from filing is absolute.

**Adjustment of term**: Possible under bilateral or multilateral agreements but not yet implemented under current IPOPHL practice.

---

## Patent Licensing

**Voluntary licensing (§§85–92)**: Patent owner may license others to make, use, sell, or import the patented invention; license agreements must be recorded with IPOPHL. License restrictions on price, territory, or field of use must comply with anti-competitive rules.

**Compulsory licensing grounds (§93)**:
1. Patented invention not worked in the Philippines within 3 years from grant (or 4 years from filing, whichever is later).
2. Philippine trade/industry suffered from high price of patented article.
3. Patented invention is not available to public in sufficient quantity.
4. National emergency or other extreme urgency.
5. Anti-competitive use of the patent right.
6. Public non-commercial use by government.

**Compulsory license procedure**: Petition to IPOPHL Director General; notice to patent owner; hearing; grant of license on terms including reasonable royalties.

---

## Inter Partes Review Equivalent — Opposition and Cancellation

**Opposition (§§57–60)**: Filed within 3 months of publication of application; any person may oppose on patentability grounds; IPOPHL hears the opposition; decision by Director of Patents.

**Post-grant cancellation (§§61–70)**: Any person may petition to cancel a patent on grounds: (1) not patentable; (2) patentee not entitled to the patent; (3) description is insufficient. Filed with IPOPHL Bureau of Legal Affairs (BLA); inter partes proceedings.

**CTA jurisdiction**: Cancellation decisions appealed to Office of the Director General; then to Court of Appeals (Rule 43); then Supreme Court. IP cases involving registration decisions: CA via Rule 43; not CTA. Note: Money claims and infringement civil actions: designated IP courts (RTC).

---

## Employees and Employed Inventors

**Service inventions (§§100–102)**: If the inventor is employed specifically to invent (researcher, R&D engineer) — the employer owns the patent. If the inventor's normal duties do not include inventing but the invention was made using employer's resources — employer has the right to use the invention upon payment of fair remuneration. If the inventor is employed in a non-inventing capacity and makes the invention on own time — the inventor owns it.

**Right to remuneration**: Even where employer owns the service invention, the inventor-employee may be entitled to separate remuneration if the invention is commercially significant and the remuneration does not correspond to the economic value — this is negotiable and governed by employment contract.

---

## Key Cases — Patent Law

**Creser Precision Systems v. CA** (GR 118708, 1997): One who is not the patent holder has no standing to bring an infringement action in their own name; only the patent holder (or exclusive licensee with authority) may sue.

**Smith Kline Beckman Corp. v. CA** (GR 126627, 2003): The doctrine of equivalents in the Philippines — a device that does not literally read on a patent claim but performs substantially the same function in substantially the same way to achieve substantially the same result is an infringing equivalent. The scope of claims must be determined against the prior art to avoid over-broad equivalence.

**Del Rosario v. CA** (GR 115106, 1995): Independent creation is a defense to patent infringement claims based on the doctrine of equivalents only if the alleged infringer developed the device independently; this is distinguished from independent invention (which is not a defense once the patent is granted — first to file wins).

## Summary — Patent vs. Utility Model

| Feature | Invention Patent | Utility Model |
|---|---|---|
| Novelty required | Yes — absolute novelty | Yes |
| Inventive step required | Yes | No |
| Term | 20 years from filing | 7 years from filing |
| Renewable | No | No |
| Substantive examination | Full | Formal only (no substantive) |
| Compulsory licensing | Yes | Yes |

## International Patent Framework

**Paris Convention**: Philippines is a member; priority right for 12 months from first filing in any member country; national treatment for foreign applicants.

**Patent Cooperation Treaty (PCT)**: Philippines is a designated state; international applications enter national phase in the Philippines; 30-month deadline from priority date to enter Philippine national phase; IPOPHL examines based on PCT international search report.

**TRIPS obligations**: Philippines complies with TRIPS minimum patent protection standards; 20-year term; patentability for all fields of technology; adequate enforcement.

---

## Patent Exploitation and Anti-Competitive Conduct

**Refusal to license**: Patent holder's refusal to license is generally within their rights. Exception: where the patent is essential to access a technology standard (Standard Essential Patents — SEPs) — patent holder must offer FRAND (Fair, Reasonable, and Non-Discriminatory) terms.

**Tie-in clauses**: Requiring licensee to buy unpatented goods from the patent holder as a condition of the license — may violate competition law (PCC jurisdiction).

**Package licensing**: Requiring licensee to take licenses for multiple patents as a package — scrutinized for anti-competitive effect; may be justified by legitimate business reasons.

**Export restrictions**: Patent licensee prohibiting from exporting to certain territories — scrutinized; some restrictions are legitimate; per se prohibitions disfavored under competition law.

## Key Terms — Patent Law Glossary

**Claim**: Statement defining the scope of patent protection; the legal boundary of the right. **Specification**: Description of the invention enabling a skilled person to practice it. **Prior art**: All publicly available information before the filing date. **Prosecution history estoppel**: Amendments made during examination limit the scope of claims; cannot recover through doctrine of equivalents. **Injunction**: Primary remedy for patent infringement; court may also award damages. **Royalty**: Payment for use of patented invention under license.

**Laches in patent cases**: Patent holder who unreasonably delays filing infringement suit while the infringer develops a business in reliance on the delay may be barred by laches from recovering pre-suit damages, even if within the prescriptive period. Philippine courts apply equitable principles including laches — patent holders should enforce promptly upon discovering infringement. Prescriptive period for patent infringement: 4 years from the commission of the infringement (*Creser Precision*; civil prescriptive period under NCC). Criminal prosecution: 3 years from discovery, not to exceed 8 years from commission (RPC rules apply as supplementary).

**Patent Misuse**: A patent holder who extends the patent monopoly beyond its intended scope (e.g., requiring licensees to pay royalties on unpatented goods; tie-in sales of non-patented items) is guilty of patent misuse; renders the patent unenforceable until the misuse is purged; separate from antitrust/competition law violations. Philippine courts may apply this equitable doctrine.

**Declaratory judgment of invalidity**: In the Philippines, invalidity is raised as a defense in infringement proceedings or by filing a cancellation petition with IPOPHL BLA. Unlike some jurisdictions, there is no standalone declaratory judgment action specifically for patent invalidity — invalidity is a defense or a cancellation ground. A defendant in an infringement case who proves the patent is invalid defeats the infringement claim.
