# Copyright Law

## Primary Sources
**Intellectual Property Code (R.A. 8293) Parts II and IV** — copyright and related rights. **IPOPHL (Intellectual Property Office of the Philippines)** — administers IP laws. Copyright registration is **optional** and not required for protection.

---

## Nature of Copyright
**Automatic protection**: Copyright vests automatically in the author upon **creation of the work** — no registration, deposit, or formality required. Distinguished from patents and trademarks which require registration.

**Scope**: Copyright protects the **expression** of an idea — not the idea, procedure, method, system, concept, principle, or discovery itself. *Baker v. Selden* principle applied in Philippine context.

---

## Copyrightable Works (§172)
Original intellectual creations in the literary and artistic domain including: books, pamphlets, articles, and other writings; periodicals and newspapers; lectures, sermons, addresses, dissertations; letters; dramatic/dramatico-musical compositions; choreographic works; musical compositions with or without words; drawings/paintings/sculptures/engravings; architectural works; illustrations/maps/plans/sketches; pictorial illustrations/advertisements; computer programs; motion pictures/audio-visual works; photographic works; derivative works (translations, adaptations, arrangements, with consent of original author).

**Not subject to copyright (§175)**: Statutes, rules, regulations, and official documents of government; judicial decisions; government reports, decisions, orders; official texts translated by government authority; news of the day and press information as pure information.

---

## Rights of Copyright Owner
**Economic rights (§177)**: Reproduction; dramatization/translation/adaptation; first public distribution; rental of computer programs; public display; public performance; other communication to the public.

**Moral rights (§193)**: Attribution (right to be identified as author); right to alter/withdraw work; right to object to mutilation/distortion/modification. Moral rights are **inalienable** and cannot be transferred.

**Term of protection**: Generally, **lifetime of the author + 50 years** after death. Anonymous/pseudonymous works: 50 years from publication. Audio-visual works/collective works: 50 years from publication or making.

---

## Limitations and Fair Use (§185)
**Four-factor test (as in §185[a])**: (1) purpose and character of use (commercial vs. educational); (2) nature of copyrighted work; (3) amount and substantiality of portion used; (4) effect on market value.

Permitted without consent: personal/private use not for profit; commentary/criticism for teaching/news/scholarship if source credited; reproductions by libraries for preservation; reporting of current events; parody.

---

## Infringement and Remedies
**Infringement**: Unauthorized exercise of any exclusive right of the copyright owner.

**Criminal liability (§217)**: Imprisonment of 1–3 years for first offense; 3–6 years for second; 6–9 years for third. Fines ranging from ₱50,000 to ₱1.5M. Applies to both copies and aiding/abetting.

**Civil remedies**: Injunction; damages (actual or statutory); destruction of infringing copies; attorney's fees.

**Presumption of authorship**: Author presumed to own copyright; corporate author rules apply to works made for hire.

---

## Related Rights
**Performers' rights**: Live performances; recorded performances.

**Sound recording producers' rights**: Reproduction, distribution, rental of sound recordings.

**Broadcasting organizations' rights**: Re-broadcasting; fixation of broadcasts.

Term of related rights: 50 years from the performance, recording, or broadcast.

---

## Anti-Hallucination Guardrails — Copyright Law
Copyright protection is automatic upon creation — registration with IPOPHL is optional and provides evidentiary advantages but is NOT required for protection or enforcement. Copyright protects expression, not ideas — never state that ideas, concepts, or procedures can be copyrighted. Moral rights are inalienable and cannot be assigned or waived by contract. The four-factor fair use test must be applied holistically — no single factor is determinative. Government works (statutes, judicial decisions, official reports) are not protected by copyright — they are in the public domain. Never state that photographs are not copyrightable — they are, as long as they possess sufficient originality.
---

## Ownership of Copyright

**Author as first owner (§178)**: Initial copyright owner is the author. Exception: employer owns copyright in works created by an employee within the scope of employment (work made for hire); the employee retains moral rights.

**Joint works**: Copyright owned jointly by all authors; exploitation requires consent of all; each may separately enforce against infringers.

**Commissioned works**: Copyright vests in the commissioner if payment was made for a specific purpose — applies to portraits, photographs, and other works on commission. If no specific purpose agreed, copyright remains with the author.

**Anonymous and pseudonymous works**: Copyright owner is the person who made the work public, unless the author discloses identity.

**Works of government employees**: Works created in the performance of official functions — government owns the copyright; works created on personal time using personal resources — author retains copyright.

---

## Transfer and Licensing

**Assignment**: Economic rights (not moral rights) may be assigned in whole or in part. Assignment must be in writing to be valid. Presumption against full assignment — ambiguous instruments interpreted narrowly to favor the author.

**Exclusive license**: Licensee may exploit the work to the exclusion of the licensor and others; may sublicense if agreed; may sue for infringement in own name.

**Non-exclusive license**: Licensor may grant same rights to others; licensee cannot sublicense without consent.

**Reversion right**: Author may reclaim rights from publisher/producer after 25 years from assignment if the work has not been substantially exploited and the author gives 1 year's notice.

**Collective licensing**: Philippine Music Industry (FILSCAP) and Kapisanan ng mga Brodkaster ng Pilipinas handle licensing for musical works; payment of licensing fees is an affirmative defense in infringement.

---

## Computer Programs and Digital Works

**Computer programs**: Protected as literary works under §172.1(n) and §172.2. Protection covers both source code and object code; does not extend to the underlying algorithms or logic.

**Backup copy exception**: Lawful owner of a copy of a computer program may make a backup copy solely for archival purposes — this single copy is lawful; resale of backup copy is infringement.

**Software as a service (SaaS)**: Access to software online — the user does not own or license a copy; issues of license terms vs. IP Code provisions; contractual terms govern.

**Databases**: Compilations of data are protected as derivative works if they show creativity in selection, arrangement, or coordination. Data itself is not protected; only the creative arrangement.

---

## Broadcasting and Digital Transmission

**Communication to the public (§177.6)**: Covers any means of transmission to the public, including online streaming, webcasting, podcasting, and social media dissemination. Rights holders must authorize digital transmissions.

**Streaming vs. downloading**: Streaming — communication to the public right applies; downloading — reproduction right applies. Unauthorized streaming platforms infringe the communication right.

**Digital Millennium Copyright Act equivalent**: R.A. 10175 (Cybercrime Prevention Act) §5 imposes criminal liability for online copyright infringement (aiding, abetting) over and above the IP Code penalties. IPOPHL may order takedown of infringing content online.

**Notice and takedown**: IPOPHL Memorandum Circular on online infringement — rights holders may request takedown from intermediaries (ISPs, platforms) upon notice; intermediary liability limited if they comply promptly.

---

## Collective Works and Compilations

**Collective works**: Encyclopedia, anthology, newspaper — each contributor retains copyright in their contribution; editor/compiler has copyright in the selection and arrangement.

**Derivative works (§173)**: Translations, adaptations, arrangements, dramatizations — protected as original works; but require the consent of the original copyright owner to be created. Making an unauthorized derivative work is infringement of the original.

**Folklore and traditional cultural expressions**: Protected under special IPOPHL regulations; State holds rights on behalf of indigenous communities; commercialization requires prior consent.

---

## Enforcement — IPOPHL and Courts

**Administrative enforcement**: IPOPHL Bureau of Legal Affairs (BLA) has quasi-judicial jurisdiction over IP disputes. Complaints for infringement filed with BLA; BLA may issue search, seizure, and destruction (SSD) orders against infringing goods.

**Civil courts**: RTC designated as IP courts have original jurisdiction over IP civil and criminal cases. Preliminary injunction commonly granted upon showing of likelihood of success on the merits and irreparable injury.

**Border measures (§230 IP Code)**: Rights holder may request Bureau of Customs (BOC) to detain importation of goods suspected to be counterfeit or pirated; requires posting of bond; BOC may order destruction if infringement confirmed.

## Key Cases — Copyright Law

**Habana v. Robles** (GR 131522, 1999): Substantial copying — copying need not be word-for-word; if a substantial part of the protected expression is taken, infringement exists. Similarity analysis: plaintiff shows copying; defendant shows independent creation or fair use.

**ABS-CBN Broadcasting Corp. v. Philippine Multi-Media System** (GR 175769, 2009): Retransmission of broadcast without authorization is a violation of the broadcasting organization's rights; cable retransmission requires separate license from rights holders.

**Pearl and Dean v. Shoemart** (GR 148222, 2003): Slim box light-boxes — the court found that the plaintiff's technical drawings were copyrightable but the actual light box itself was not; functional objects are not protected by copyright per se; the artistic portions may be separately protected.

---

## International Copyright Framework

**Berne Convention**: Philippines is a member; automatic protection in all member countries without formality; national treatment principle — foreign works get same protection as domestic works. Minimum protection: life + 50 years; no formalities.

**TRIPS Agreement**: WTO member obligations; sets minimum copyright standards; enforcement obligations; dispute settlement. Philippines complied by amending IP Code (R.A. 8293).

**WIPO Copyright Treaty (WCT) and Performances/Phonograms Treaty (WPPT)**: Philippines is a member. WCT addresses digital transmission rights; anti-circumvention of technological protection measures (TPMs); rights management information.

**Anti-circumvention (§193.4 IP Code + §5 R.A. 10175)**: Circumventing technological protection measures (TPMs — encryption, DRM) that control access to or copying of protected works is itself an infringement — separate from copying the underlying work.

---

## Collective Management Organizations (CMOs)

**FILSCAP (Filipino Society of Composers, Authors and Publishers)**: CMO for musical works; licenses public performance rights (restaurants, bars, radio, TV, online); distributes collected royalties to member composers, authors, publishers.

**PARI (Philippine Association of the Recording Industry)**: CMO for sound recordings and the recording industry; licenses sound recordings for broadcast and performance; distributes to producers and performers.

**IPOPHL accreditation**: CMOs must be accredited by IPOPHL to exercise collective rights; multiple CMOs for the same class of works may exist; rights holders may designate their CMO.

---

## Moral Rights — Detailed Application

**Attribution (right of paternity)**: Author's name must appear in connection with public uses of the work. Omission of author credit = violation of moral rights even if economic rights were properly licensed.

**Right of integrity**: Author may object to modifications that prejudice their honor or reputation. Derogatory treatment = distortion, mutilation, or other modification that is prejudicial. Test: prejudice to honor or reputation — objective standard judged by peers in the field.

**Right of disclosure**: Author controls the right of first publication; may withhold work from the public indefinitely; once published with consent — subsequent publication and use subject to economic rights only.

**Right of withdrawal**: Author may withdraw the work from commerce upon payment of damages to the licensee/assignee. Narrow right; not freely exercised; requires payment of indemnification.

**Moral rights of heirs**: After the author's death, moral rights may be exercised by the heirs for the duration of the copyright term. Heirs may not waive moral rights; they are inalienable and perpetual as to attribution and integrity.
