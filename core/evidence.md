# Evidence Module

## Primary Sources

**Rules of Court, Rules 128–134 (Rules on Evidence)**: The primary procedural framework governing admissibility of evidence in Philippine courts.

**Rules on Electronic Evidence (A.M. No. 01-7-01-SC, effective July 17, 2001, as amended)**: Governs the admissibility, authentication, and use of electronic documents and electronic data messages.

**1987 Constitution, Article III, §§2–3**: Unreasonable searches and seizures; exclusionary rule — evidence obtained in violation of §2 is inadmissible for any purpose in any proceeding.

**Republic Act No. 4200 (Anti-Wiretapping Law)**: Unlawfully obtained recordings are inadmissible; criminal liability for violators.

## Fundamental Concepts

### Admissibility (Rule 128, §3)

Evidence is admissible when it is **relevant** (has any tendency to make the existence of any fact that is of consequence more or less probable) and **competent** (not excluded by the Constitution, applicable law, or the Rules of Court).

Admissibility and probative weight are distinct: a piece of evidence may be admitted but given little weight by the court.

### Burden of Proof vs. Burden of Evidence

**Burden of proof** (*onus probandi*): The duty of a party to present evidence on the facts in issue necessary to establish the party's claim or defense by the amount of evidence required by law. It does not shift during the trial.

**Burden of evidence**: The duty of a party to produce evidence sufficient to make a prima facie showing of a fact, to meet the evidence already introduced by the opposing party. It shifts as evidence is produced.

### Standards of Proof

| Standard | Application | Definition |
|---|---|---|
| Beyond reasonable doubt | Criminal cases — conviction | Moral certainty; excludes every reasonable hypothesis of innocence; not mathematical certainty |
| Clear and convincing evidence | Some civil actions (fraud, forgery, disinhertiance, lost documents) | More than preponderance; evidence that produces firm belief or conviction |
| Preponderance of evidence | Civil cases generally | The greater weight of credible evidence; which side's evidence is more credible and of greater probative value |
| Substantial evidence | Administrative proceedings | Such relevant evidence that a reasonable mind might accept as adequate to support a conclusion |

**In criminal cases**, the prosecution bears the burden of proving guilt beyond reasonable doubt throughout the trial. The accused enjoys the constitutional presumption of innocence (Art. III, §14(2)). The burden never shifts to the accused to prove innocence — the accused's evidence aims only to create reasonable doubt.

## Judicial Notice and Judicial Admissions

### Judicial Notice (Rule 129)

Courts may take judicial notice, without proof, of: matters of public knowledge; laws of the Philippines; official acts of the legislative, executive, and judicial departments; geographical conditions; laws of nature; measures of time; and similar matters. Courts do not take judicial notice of foreign laws — these must be pleaded and proved as facts.

**Discretionary judicial notice**: The court may take notice of matters which are of public knowledge, capable of unquestionable demonstration, or ought to be known to judges because of their judicial functions. The parties must be given the opportunity to present evidence to the contrary.

### Judicial Admissions (Rule 129, §4)

An admission made in the pleadings, or in the course of the trial, whether written or oral, does not require proof. Cannot be contradicted unless there is palpable mistake or that no such admission was made. Judicial admissions are binding on the party making them.

## Qualifications of Witnesses

**General rule**: All persons who can perceive and perceiving, can make known their perception to others, may be witnesses (Rule 130, §20). No person is disqualified by reason of age, opinion, religion, or other status — but may be subject to rules on credibility.

**Competency vs. credibility**: Competency addresses whether the witness may testify at all. Credibility concerns the weight to be given to the witness's testimony.

**Disqualifications**: (a) Mental incapacity or immaturity — those who cannot intelligently make themselves understood; (b) marriage (marital disqualification rule): during the marriage, spouses cannot testify for or against each other without the consent of the other, except in criminal cases of one against the other or their direct descendants or ascendants; (c) dead man's rule (Rule 130, §23): parties or assignors of parties in a case or persons in whose behalf a case is prosecuted cannot testify against the deceased or incapacitated as to any matter of fact occurring before the death or incapacity — applicable only in actions against the estate.

## Privileged Communications

Communications covered by privilege are excluded from evidence even though relevant and otherwise admissible.

**Attorney-client privilege (Rule 130, §24(b))**: An attorney cannot be examined as to any communication made to the attorney by the client in the course of professional employment, or the attorney's advice thereon. The privilege belongs to the client and may only be waived by the client. Does not apply to communications made to enable the commission of a crime or fraud.

**Marital privilege (Rule 130, §24(a))**: A spouse cannot be examined as to communications made between spouses during the marriage. Survives death of either spouse. Does not apply to crimes committed by one spouse against the other or their descendants.

**Physician-patient privilege (Rule 130, §24(c))**: A physician cannot be examined as to information acquired in attendance upon a patient in a professional capacity which was necessary to enable the physician to act. The privilege belongs to the patient.

**Priest-penitent privilege (Rule 130, §24(d))**: A minister of the gospel or priest cannot be examined as to any communication made to the priest in the priest's professional character, in the course of discipline enjoined by the church.

**State secrets privilege (Rule 130, §24(e))**: Public officials cannot be examined as to communications made to them in official confidence when the court finds that public interest in keeping such communication secret outweighs the interest in disclosure.

## Hearsay Rule and Exceptions

### The Hearsay Rule (Rule 130, §37)

Hearsay is a statement, other than one made by the declarant while testifying at trial or hearing, offered in evidence to prove the truth of the matter asserted. Hearsay is generally inadmissible. The rationale: the declarant was not under oath, cannot be cross-examined, and the trier of fact cannot observe the declarant's demeanor.

An out-of-court statement offered NOT for the truth of its content (e.g., to show the statement was made, or to show the declarant's state of mind) is NOT hearsay.

### Exceptions to the Hearsay Rule (Rule 130, §§38–47)

**Dying declaration (§38)**: A statement made by a person under consciousness of impending death, declaring the cause and surrounding circumstances of the death. Admissible only in a criminal case where the death is the subject of inquiry, and only if the declarant would have been a competent witness had they survived. *Consciousness of imminent death is an essential element — the declarant must have believed death was certain and imminent, not merely probable.*

**Declaration against interest (§39)**: Statements by a person now unavailable as a witness that were, at the time made, against the declarant's pecuniary or moral interest.

**Act or declaration about pedigree (§40)**: Family members' statements about family history when the declarant is unavailable and the relationship is established.

**Res gestae (§42)**: Spontaneous statements or statements accompanying an act made so closely connected with the event as to be part of it. The spontaneity eliminates the risk of fabrication. Two types: spontaneous exclamations (verbal acts) and verbal acts accompanying an equivocal act.

**Entries in the course of business (§43)**: Entries made in the regular course of business, at or near the time of the act, condition, or event, by a person with actual knowledge. Foundation must be laid through a custodian or qualified witness.

**Official records (§44)**: Entries in official records made in the performance of an official duty by an officer with special knowledge. Evidence of the facts therein stated.

**Learned treatises (§47)**: Statements in published treatises or periodicals established as reliable authority — usable only to examine expert witnesses; not independently admissible as substantive evidence.

## Documentary Evidence

### Original Document Rule (Rule 130, §3, formerly Best Evidence Rule)

When the subject of inquiry is the contents of a document, writing, recording, photograph, or other record, the original must be produced. Copies are admissible only under the exceptions.

**Original** includes: the document itself, any counterpart, a printout of electronically stored information if it accurately reflects the data.

**Secondary evidence** (copies, oral testimony about contents) is admissible when: the original was lost or destroyed without bad faith by the offeror; the original is in the possession or control of the adverse party who fails to produce it; the original consists of numerous accounts or documents which cannot be examined in court without great loss of time; the original is a public record.

### Authentication of Private Documents (Rule 132, §20)

Every private document must be authenticated before it may be received in evidence. The genuineness and due execution of the document must be proved by: the person who executed it; the person before whom it was acknowledged; any person who witnessed its execution; or indirect evidence (handwriting comparison).

### Public Documents (Rule 132, §23)

Public documents are self-authenticating — their due execution and genuineness are presumed. Include: written official acts of official bodies; public records; notarized documents.

### Parol Evidence Rule (Rule 130, §10)

When the terms of an agreement have been reduced to writing, the agreement is deemed to contain all the terms agreed upon, and there can be no evidence of such terms other than the written agreement itself. Exceptions: intrinsic ambiguity; failure to express the true intent due to mistake or imperfection; validity of the document is in question; terms between parties and strangers to the document.

*Parol evidence rule applies only to contracts between parties to the written document. Does not apply to receipts, promissory notes, or other documents that are not complete expressions of the parties' agreement.*

## Electronic Evidence

### Admissibility of Electronic Documents

Under the Rules on Electronic Evidence, an electronic document has the same legal effect as a written document if its integrity and reliability can be established.

**Functional equivalence**: An electronic document is the functional equivalent of a written document for authentication and reliability purposes.

**Electronic signature**: A method of signing an electronic document that identifies and authenticates the person signing and indicates the person's approval. May be by digital signature, biometric authentication, PIN, or other methods.

### Authentication of Electronic Evidence

An electronic document is authenticated by: evidence that the system producing it was reliable; evidence of the integrity of the information and communication technology system; testimony of a witness who executed the document; other corroborative evidence.

**Electronic data messages**: Include emails, SMS, chat messages, and similar transmissions. Admissible as evidence of their contents.

**Chain of custody for digital evidence**: Preservation of metadata, logs, and integrity hashes is critical. Alteration or tampering with metadata can render electronic evidence inadmissible.

## The Exclusionary Rule

**Constitutional basis (Art. III, §3(2))**: Any evidence obtained in violation of §2 (unreasonable searches and seizures) or §3(1) (privacy of communication) shall be inadmissible for any purpose in any proceeding. The fruit of the poisonous tree doctrine applies — evidence derived from an unconstitutional search is also excluded.

**Waiver**: The right against unreasonable searches and seizures may be waived. The accused must raise the issue of admissibility before trial or during trial before the evidence is offered.

**Exclusionary rule scope**: Applies only to government action. Evidence obtained by private individuals acting without government involvement is not excluded by Art. III — but may be subject to other rules (e.g., Anti-Wiretapping Act for private recordings).

## Chain of Custody in Drug Cases

In prosecutions under R.A. 9165 (Comprehensive Dangerous Drugs Act), chain of custody is a critical evidentiary doctrine because the *corpus delicti* (the drugs themselves) must be preserved and authenticated from seizure to presentation in court.

**Section 21, R.A. 9165 (as amended by R.A. 10640)**: The seized items must be immediately inventoried and photographed after seizure at the place of arrest or at the nearest police station, in the presence of: the accused or a representative or counsel; an elected public official; and a representative of the National Prosecution Service or media. All four signatories are required.

**Saving clause**: Non-compliance with §21 will not render the seizure and custody void if: (a) there is justifiable ground for non-compliance; and (b) the integrity and evidentiary value of the seized items are properly preserved. The prosecution bears the burden of explaining the justifiable grounds and proving that the integrity was maintained.

**Four links in the chain**: Seizure and marking; turnover to investigating officer; turnover to forensic chemist; submission to the court. Each transfer must be documented.

## Demurrer to Evidence

**Civil cases (Rule 33)**: After the plaintiff rests, the defendant may file a demurrer to evidence (motion to dismiss for insufficiency of evidence). If denied, the defendant may still present evidence. Under the 2019 Rules, leave of court is no longer required.

**Criminal cases (Rule 119, §23)**: After the prosecution rests, the accused may file a demurrer to evidence with or without leave of court. **Critical rule**: If filed without leave of court and denied, the accused waives the right to present evidence and the case is submitted for decision on the prosecution's evidence. If filed with leave and denied, the accused may still present evidence.

---

## Offer, Objection, and Ruling

Evidence must be formally offered before it can be considered. The court cannot consider evidence that has not been formally offered, even if it was attached to the pleadings.

**Offer of documentary evidence**: Made after all witnesses for the offering party have testified; done orally unless the court allows written offer.

**Offer of testimonial evidence**: Made at the time the witness is called to testify (oral offer of name, purpose, and substance).

**Objection**: Must be made as soon as the ground for objection becomes apparent. Failure to object constitutes waiver.

## Anti-Hallucination Guardrails — Evidence

Never conflate burden of proof (does not shift) with burden of evidence (does shift). Never state that hearsay is always inadmissible — there are over 15 recognized exceptions; always check the exception before concluding inadmissibility. The exclusionary rule under Art. III applies only to government actors — private citizen searches and seizures are not automatically covered. The dying declaration requires consciousness of imminent death — a statement made by someone who hoped to survive is not a dying declaration. In drug cases, non-compliance with §21 chain of custody does not automatically render evidence inadmissible if the saving clause is satisfied — analyze the justifiability of the deviation and preservation of integrity. The original document rule applies only when the contents of the document are in issue — not when the document is offered merely to prove its existence.
