# Local Government Law Module

## Constitutional Framework

**Article X, 1987 Constitution**: The fundamental charter for local government. Key provisions:

**§2**: Territorial and political subdivisions shall enjoy local autonomy.

**§3**: Congress shall enact a local government code.

**§5**: Each LGU shall have the power to create its own sources of revenues and levy taxes, fees, and charges subject to guidelines and limitations prescribed by Congress, consistent with the basic policy of local autonomy.

**§10**: No province, city, municipality, or barangay may be created, divided, merged, abolished, or substantially altered except in accordance with the criteria established in the local government code and subject to approval by a majority of votes cast in a plebiscite in the political units directly affected.

**§15–§21**: Autonomous regions. The 1987 Constitution provides for two autonomous regions: Muslim Mindanao and the Cordilleras.

## Local Government Code (R.A. 7160)

The LGC took effect January 1, 1992. It is the primary legislation implementing Article X.

**Devolution**: The LGC transferred national government functions (health, social welfare, agriculture, public works at the local level) to LGUs. *Pimentel v. Aguirre* (336 SCRA 201, 2000): The President may not reduce the IRA (Internal Revenue Allotment) unilaterally — this is a congressional appropriation matter and a constitutional entitlement of LGUs.

**Internal Revenue Allotment (IRA) / National Tax Allotment (NTA)**: LGUs are entitled to a share of national taxes. *Mandanas v. Ochoa* (G.R. No. 199802, 2021): landmark ruling that expanded the base for computing the IRA/NTA to include all national taxes (not only "internal revenue taxes"), significantly increasing LGU shares.

## LGU Powers

### General Welfare Clause (§16)

Every LGU shall exercise the powers expressly granted, those necessarily implied, and those necessary, appropriate, or incidental for its efficient and effective governance, and those essential to the promotion of the general welfare.

**Scope**: The general welfare clause is the residual source of local legislative power. However, it cannot override national statutes, constitutional limitations, or rights of persons. *Tatel v. Municipality of Virac* (207 SCRA 157, 1992): ordinances must not contravene the Constitution or any statute.

### Taxing Power (§§128–196)

LGUs may levy local taxes, fees, and charges subject to limitations in the LGC and the Constitution. National taxes may not be levied by LGUs. *Manila Electric Company v. Province of Laguna* (306 SCRA 750, 1999): MERALCO liable for local franchise tax despite national franchise.

**Limitations**: LGU taxes must not be unjust, excessive, oppressive, confiscatory, or contrary to declared national policy. Tax ordinances must comply with the procedure in the LGC.

### Police Power / Regulatory Power

LGUs exercise local police power through ordinances. *Social Justice Society v. Atienza* (G.R. No. 156052, 2007): Manila ordinance rezoning Pandacan oil terminals upheld as valid exercise of police power. But police power is subject to due process and cannot abridge constitutional rights.

### Eminent Domain (§19)

LGUs may exercise eminent domain through their legislative bodies for public use, upon payment of just compensation, subject to prior valid and definite offer. Approval of Sanggunian required.

## Local Legislative Bodies

**Sangguniang Panlalawigan** (provincial): Legislative body of the province.
**Sangguniang Panlungsod** (city): Legislative body of the city.
**Sangguniang Bayan** (municipal): Legislative body of the municipality.
**Sangguniang Barangay**: Legislative body of the barangay.

**Quorum and voting**: Majority of all members constitutes quorum. Majority of members present (with quorum) required for ordinance passage unless LGC specifies otherwise.

**Ordinance vs. resolution**: Ordinances are permanent; resolutions are temporary or declaratory. Ordinances require three readings; resolutions require one. *Laguna Lake Development Authority v. Court of Appeals* (231 SCRA 292, 1994): distinctions between ordinances and resolutions.

---

## Local Chief Executives — Powers and Limitations

**Executive powers**: Implementation of ordinances, supervision of officers, command of local police (subject to INP/PNP chain), representation of LGU.

**Veto power**: Local chief executive may veto ordinances within 15 days of receipt. Sanggunian may override by two-thirds vote.

**Disciplinary authority**: Local chief executives have disciplinary authority over subordinate local officials.

**Recall**: Local officials may be recalled by a majority of registered voters in the LGU. One recall per term; not within one year from assumption of office or one year before a regular election.

## Autonomous Regions

### Bangsamoro Autonomous Region in Muslim Mindanao (BARMM)

**Legal basis**: Republic Act 11054 (Bangsamoro Organic Law, 2018) as ratified by plebiscite February 6, 2019. The BARMM replaced the Autonomous Region in Muslim Mindanao (ARMM).

**Constitutional basis**: Article X, §15–§21. The Constitution permits Congress to create autonomous regions "taking into account their shared and common historical and cultural heritage, economic and social structure, and other relevant characteristics."

**Bangsamoro Government**: Parliamentary system with a Chief Minister. The Bangsamoro Parliament is the legislative body.

**Exclusive powers of BARMM**: Internal and autonomous affairs enumerated in the BOL. Reserved powers of national government include foreign affairs, national defense, monetary policy, certain criminal law.

**North Cotabato doctrine** (*Province of North Cotabato v. GRP*, 2008): the autonomous region cannot have an "associative relationship" with the Philippine state that resembles a state-within-a-state. The BOL was drafted to avoid the constitutional defects of the MOA-AD. Whether the BOL fully resolves those defects remains subject to legal scrutiny.

### Cordillera Autonomous Region

The 1987 Constitution created the framework but the organic act for the Cordillera was rejected in the ratification plebiscite. The Cordillera Administrative Region (CAR) exists as an administrative region, not an autonomous region.

## Inter-Local Government Relations

**League of Provinces, Cities, Municipalities**: Advisory bodies; no legislative authority over LGUs.

**Inter-LGU cooperation**: LGUs may enter into inter-LGU agreements for delivery of basic services and joint projects.

**National government supervision**: The President supervises LGUs to ensure acts are within the scope of their prescribed powers. Supervision is not control — the President cannot substitute judgment for the LGU's on matters within local autonomy. *Drilon v. Lim* (235 SCRA 135, 1994): distinction between supervision and control; the DILG Secretary cannot override local ordinances on policy grounds (supervision only), but may declare them invalid for being contrary to law.

**DILG oversight**: The Department of Interior and Local Government exercises supervisory functions over LGUs. DILG opinions are persuasive but not binding; local ordinances require court action to be declared invalid.
## Anti-Hallucination Guardrails — Local Government Law

The *Mandanas v. Ochoa* (2021) NTA computation expanded the base to include all national taxes — never apply the pre-*Mandanas* formula that limited the base to internal revenue taxes only. Never state that the President has control over LGUs — the President has *supervision* only; the distinction (*Drilon v. Lim*, 1994) is constitutional and controlling. Never apply LGC article numbers from memory without verification — the LGC is a lengthy statute and article numbers have been amended; cite provisions with the qualifier "verify against current codification." The Bangsamoro Organic Law (R.A. 11054) replaced ARMM with BARMM — never apply ARMM-era doctrine to the current BARMM regime without checking whether the BOL has changed the applicable rule. The *Province of North Cotabato* (2008) MOA-AD doctrine was decided on the specific language of that document; the BOL was drafted to address those constitutional defects, and applying the MOA-AD ruling wholesale to the BOL requires case-specific analysis.
