# Political Law Module

## Executive Power Analysis Framework

### Commander-in-Chief Powers — Three Distinct Powers

The 1987 Constitution, Article VII, Section 18 creates three distinct executive powers that differ in constitutional standard, duration, and reviewability.

**Calling-out power**: The President may call out the armed forces to prevent or suppress lawless violence, invasion, or rebellion. No prior congressional concurrence required. Factual basis is reviewable by the Supreme Court under expanded judicial review. *Lagman v. Medialdea* (G.R. No. 231658, 2017): calling-out power upheld; Court's role is to determine whether the President acted within constitutional limits, not to substitute its judgment for the President's.

**Suspension of the privilege of the writ of habeas corpus**: Requires actual invasion or rebellion and public safety requires it. Congress may revoke by majority vote. Supreme Court reviews sufficiency of factual basis. Duration: 60 days; extendable with Congressional approval.

**Martial law**: Same grounds as writ suspension. Same congressional and judicial review mechanisms. The Court in *Lagman* (2017) and subsequent martial law extension cases held: (a) Court reviews factual basis independently, not deferentially; (b) the standard is "probable cause" not guilt beyond reasonable doubt; (c) the Constitution does not require the President to await actual invasion or rebellion — an imminent danger suffices.

**Critical doctrine from Lagman series**: Courts may not substitute their assessment of the military and intelligence situation for the President's, but must determine whether the factual claims are not baseless. *Lagman v. Pimentel III* (G.R. No. 235935, 2018) and subsequent cases extended this framework.

### Executive Privilege

*Senate v. Ermita* (488 SCRA 1, 2006): Presidential communications privilege and deliberative process privilege are constitutionally grounded. However, neither is absolute. Court must balance executive privilege against legislative power to investigate.

*Neri v. Senate Committee on Accountability* (G.R. No. 180643, 2008): Three-part presidential communications privilege test: (1) the protected communication must relate to a quintessential and non-delegable presidential power; (2) it must be authored, solicited, and received by a close presidential adviser; (3) the presidential communications privilege remains a qualified privilege, subject to the Senate's demonstration of need.

*Senate v. Ermita* further established that department heads may not be required to appear before Congress for question hour without the President's consent (§22), but witnesses in §21 legislative inquiries have a different standard.

---

### Appointment Power

**Regular appointments**: Made during sessions, with Commission on Appointments confirmation where required (heads of executive departments, ambassadors, officers of the AFP from rank of colonel or naval captain, and other officers Congress vests with appointment power requiring confirmation).

**Ad interim appointments**: Made during recess, effective until disapproval by CA or adjournment of next session. *Matibag v. Benipayo* (380 SCRA 49, 2002): ad interim appointment is a permanent appointment, not temporary; COMELEC commissioners may be given ad interim appointments.

**Midnight appointments**: Section 15, Article VII prohibits appointments two months before a presidential election through inauguration day. *De Castro v. JBC* (615 SCRA 666, 2010): prohibition does not apply to the judiciary. Controversial ruling; minority view applied the prohibition broadly.

---

### Removal Power

The President's power to remove is inherent in the power to appoint for officers removable at the President's pleasure. Constitutional officers (COMELEC commissioners, Ombudsman, COA, CSC) are insulated from presidential removal except through impeachment. The Ombudsman may be removed only by impeachment. *Gonzales III v. Office of the President* (679 SCRA 614, 2012): Deputy Ombudsman may be removed by the President — not covered by the Ombudsman's insulated status.

## Justiciability Analysis Framework

### The Expanded Judicial Review Doctrine

Article VIII, Section 1 (1987): "Judicial power includes the duty of the courts of justice to settle actual controversies involving rights which are legally demandable and enforceable, and to determine whether or not there has been a grave abuse of discretion amounting to lack or excess of jurisdiction on the part of any branch or instrumentality of the Government."

This is the most significant innovation of the 1987 Constitution for judicial review. It effectively narrows the political question doctrine by giving courts jurisdiction whenever grave abuse of discretion is alleged.

### Standing

**Requirements**: (1) personal and substantial interest in the case; (2) injury in fact — actual or imminent harm; (3) injury traceable to the assailed act; (4) injury redressable by the relief sought.

**Relaxed standing**: *David v. Macapagal-Arroyo* (489 SCRA 160, 2006): in cases involving constitutional rights, transcendental importance, or when strong public interest exists, the Court may relax standing requirements.

**Taxpayer standing**: Available when public funds are illegally disbursed. *Belgica v. Ochoa* (2013): taxpayer standing recognized for PDAF challenge.

**Legislative standing**: Legislators have standing to challenge acts that impair their legislative prerogatives. *Ople v. Torres* (293 SCRA 141, 1998).

### Mootness and Exceptions

**General rule**: Courts do not decide moot questions.

**Exceptions** (*David v. Macapagal-Arroyo*): (1) grave constitutional violation; (2) exceptional character requiring definitive doctrine; (3) public interest demands resolution; (4) issue is capable of repetition yet evading review.

### Political Question Doctrine

**Pre-1987**: Broad political question doctrine protected many executive and legislative acts from judicial review.

**Post-1987**: Significantly narrowed. *Francisco v. House of Representatives* (2003): the expanded judicial review provision means courts may reach questions previously deemed political, if grave abuse of discretion is alleged. The Court no longer abstains merely because a question involves a political branch.

**Surviving political questions**: Those textually committed to another branch with no judicially manageable standards — e.g., the President's assessment of foreign policy, military strategy in ongoing operations.

### Facial Challenge, Overbreadth, and Void-for-Vagueness

**Facial challenge**: Attack on the law's constitutionality based solely on its text, without reference to specific facts.

**Overbreadth doctrine**: Applicable primarily in free speech cases. A law is overbroad if it sweeps protected conduct along with unprotected conduct. *Disini v. Secretary of Justice* (716 SCRA 237, 2014): portions of Cybercrime Prevention Act struck down on overbreadth.

**Void-for-vagueness**: A law is void if it fails to give persons of ordinary intelligence fair notice of what is prohibited and authorizes arbitrary enforcement. *Southern Hemisphere v. Anti-Terrorism Council* (632 SCRA 146, 2010): anti-terrorism provisions challenged; Court upheld but set parameters for interpretation.

**As-applied vs. facial**: The Court generally prefers as-applied challenges. Facial challenges in non-free speech cases are disfavored. *Romualdez v. Sandiganbayan* (435 SCRA 371, 2004).

## Separation of Powers and Checks and Balances

**Non-delegation doctrine**: Legislative power may not be delegated except to local governments and administrative bodies with sufficient standard. *Eastern Shipping Lines v. POEA* (166 SCRA 533, 1988): two-pronged test — completeness test (law must be complete when it leaves the legislature) and sufficient standard test (must provide adequate guidelines).

**Bicameral Conference Committee limits**: *Tolentino* (1994): conference committee may introduce new provisions but these must be germane. The enrolled bill doctrine — courts will not inquire into the internal proceedings of Congress to verify compliance with constitutional requirements, except as to quorum and voting.

**Executive legislation**: The President may not legislate through executive orders or administrative issuances. EOs must find basis in either constitutional or statutory authority. *Ople v. Torres* (1998): National Computerized Identification Reference System via AO struck down — no legislative basis, violated right to privacy.

---

## Emergency Powers

Article VI, Section 23(2): Congress may grant the President emergency powers in times of war or national emergency. Requirements: (1) there is a war or national emergency; (2) the grant is by law; (3) the law must define the purpose; (4) the law must specify the period; (5) subject to restrictions Congress may impose; (6) powers cease when declared by resolution.

Emergency powers differ from commander-in-chief powers. Emergency powers require affirmative congressional grant. Commander-in-chief powers are inherent in the Constitution.
## Anti-Hallucination Guardrails — Political Law

Never present the three-part *Neri* presidential communications privilege test as three equal elements — the third element (qualified privilege subject to override) is the balancing step after privilege is established, not a co-equal element alongside (1) and (2). Never state that *De Castro v. JBC* (2010) settled the midnight-appointment question as to all offices — the ruling is limited to the judiciary; its application to other offices remains contested and the decision was not unanimous. Never state that the calling-out power is unreviewable — *Lagman v. Medialdea* (2017) held the Court reviews factual basis of martial law and writ suspension; the calling-out power has a lower standard but *David v. Arroyo* (2006) established it is not immune from review for grave abuse. Never conflate the three commander-in-chief powers — calling-out, writ suspension, and martial law each have distinct constitutional standards, review mechanisms, and duration limits; treating them interchangeably is analytical error.
