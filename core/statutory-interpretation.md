# Statutory Interpretation Module

## Hierarchy of Interpretive Methods

No single interpretive canon is universally controlling. Apply methods in sequence, moving to the next only when the prior is inconclusive.

**Step 1 — Plain meaning (literal rule)**: If the statutory text is clear and unambiguous, apply it as written. *Verba legis non est recedendum* — from the words of the law, there should be no departure. Courts may not construe unambiguous statutes to reach a different result under the guise of "interpretation."

**Step 2 — Contextual reading**: Read the provision in light of the entire statute. A provision is not read in isolation; it must be construed as part of a coherent whole. *Ut res magis valeat quam pereat* — every part of the statute must be given effect.

**Step 3 — Legislative history**: When text is ambiguous, examine: (a) the bill's original version and amendments; (b) bicameral conference committee reports; (c) sponsorship speeches; (d) committee reports; (e) interpellations on the floor; (f) congressional deliberations. The enacted text, however, remains controlling — intent inferred from legislative history may not override clear statutory text.

**Step 4 — Purpose/teleological interpretation**: Construe the provision in light of the statute's evident purpose and the mischief it was designed to correct. *The spirit of the law controls over the letter when literal application produces absurd results.*

**Step 5 — Consequentialist check**: If a literal reading produces an absurd, unjust, or constitutionally suspect result, the Court may depart from the literal meaning to give effect to the evident legislative intent. *Reyes v. Court of Appeals* and related cases: interpretations leading to absurd results are rejected.

## Key Canons of Construction

**Expressio unius est exclusio alterius**: Expression of one thing implies exclusion of others. When a statute enumerates items, unlisted items are excluded. Apply with caution — only when the enumeration appears intended to be exhaustive.

**Ejusdem generis**: Where a general term follows specific terms, the general term is limited to things of the same class as the specific terms. Example: "motor vehicles, vessels, aircraft, and other conveyances" — "other conveyances" is limited to motorized transport.

**Noscitur a sociis**: A word is known by its associates. Ambiguous terms are interpreted in light of surrounding words and context.

**Reddendo singula singulis**: Where there are two subjects and two objects, each object is referred to its appropriate subject in the order they appear.

**Casus omissus**: A matter omitted from a statute cannot be supplied by the courts. If the legislature did not address a situation, the court cannot legislate by construction.

**Liberal vs. strict construction**: Penal statutes are strictly construed against the state and in favor of the accused. Tax statutes are strictly construed against the government and in favor of the taxpayer. Remedial statutes are liberally construed to effectuate their purpose.

## Statutory Conflict Resolution

**Lex specialis derogat legi generali**: Special law prevails over general law on the same subject. *The Local Government Code* as special law prevails over inconsistent provisions of general administrative law, subject to later-in-time analysis.

**Lex posterior derogat legi priori**: Later law prevails over earlier law of equal rank, on the same subject matter. The later-in-time rule applies to statutes of equal rank; it does not apply to statutes vs. the Constitution.

**Irreconcilable repugnancy**: If two statutes cannot be reconciled, the later one controls. Courts first attempt to harmonize statutes; repeal by implication is disfavored.

## Constitutional Avoidance

Where a statute is susceptible to two interpretations — one constitutional, one not — the Court will adopt the constitutional interpretation, provided it is a reasonable reading of the text. This doctrine does not authorize courts to rewrite legislation; it selects among plausible readings.

*Southern Hemisphere v. Anti-Terrorism Council* (2010): Human Security Act construed narrowly to avoid overbreadth; the Court adopted a limiting construction rather than striking down.

Limits: Constitutional avoidance cannot be used to give a statute a meaning clearly contrary to legislative intent. If the only defensible reading is unconstitutional, the statute is struck down.

## Administrative Interpretation

**Contemporaneous construction**: The interpretation given to a statute by the agency charged with its administration is entitled to great weight. *Commissioner of Internal Revenue v. CA* (301 SCRA 152, 1999) and numerous cases: BIR rulings on tax statutes receive judicial deference on technical tax matters.

**Limits on administrative interpretation**: Agency interpretation cannot override plain statutory text. An agency may not interpret a statute in a manner that exceeds its statutory authority or violates the Constitution.

**Long-continued practice**: Administrative practice spanning decades may evidence the correct interpretation of an ambiguous statute, particularly when Congress has not objected.

---

## Penal Statutes — Special Rules

Penal statutes are strictly construed against the state and in favor of the accused. When there is doubt, the doubt is resolved in favor of the accused. This does not mean penal statutes are inapplicable — it means courts do not extend them beyond their clear text by analogy or implication.

**Retroactivity**: Penal statutes may not be applied retroactively to punish acts not criminal at the time of commission (Article III, §22 — ex post facto clause). Exception: a penal statute may be given retroactive effect if it is favorable to the accused.

## Statutory Construction in Philippine Constitutional Law

When a statute is challenged as unconstitutional:

**Presumption of constitutionality**: Statutes enjoy a presumption of constitutionality. The challenger bears the burden of proving unconstitutionality beyond reasonable doubt. *Estrada v. Sandiganbayan* (369 SCRA 394, 2001): standard for voiding statutes.

**Partial invalidity**: If a portion of a statute is unconstitutional, the remainder stands if it is separable. Severability clause, if present, is given effect.

**Facial vs. as-applied challenge**: As-applied challenges attack a statute's application to specific facts; facial challenges attack the statute's text as unconstitutional in all cases. Courts prefer as-applied challenges except in free speech cases where overbreadth doctrine applies.

## Legislative History Framework

When statutory ambiguity exists, analyze legislative history in this sequence:

1. **Original bill text**: What did the sponsor intend?
2. **Committee amendments and reports**: What changes were made and why?
3. **Floor debates and interpellations**: What did legislators understand the provision to mean?
4. **Bicameral conference committee report**: What was the final reconciled intent?
5. **Enrolled bill**: The text as signed is the final authoritative statement.

**Critical limit**: Legislative history is an aid, not a substitute, for statutory text. Enacted text remains controlling. Courts may use legislative history to resolve ambiguity, but may not use it to override unambiguous language.
## Anti-Hallucination Guardrails — Statutory Interpretation

Never apply *casus omissus* as an absolute bar — courts may fill gaps where the omission is clearly unintentional and where strict application would defeat the statute's evident purpose; the doctrine has recognized exceptions. Never apply legislative history to override unambiguous statutory text — legislative history resolves ambiguity, not clear text. Never treat a law review article or textbook commentary as legislative history — legislative history means actual congressional materials (bills, committee reports, floor deliberations), not scholarly commentary about what the law should mean. Never apply the penal statute strict-construction rule to extend it beyond its proper scope — strict construction in favor of the accused means doubts are resolved for the accused, not that penal laws cannot apply to conduct clearly within their terms. The enrolled bill doctrine prevents courts from examining internal congressional proceedings except as to quorum and voting requirements; never suggest courts will invalidate a statute based on alleged procedural irregularities in its passage that are not apparent from the enrolled bill.
