# Legislative Procedure Module

## Senate Submodule

### Composition and Quorum

Twenty-four senators elected at-large, nationwide. Six-year terms, staggered so half the Senate is elected every three years. The Senate is a continuing body — senators who are not up for election in a given cycle remain seated.

**Quorum**: Majority of all members (13 of 24) constitutes a quorum to do business. *Avelino v. Cuenco* (83 Phil. 17, 1949): quorum is computed on the basis of all 24 senators, not merely those present or those who are not resigned/dead/incapacitated. A senator who walks out does not reduce the quorum base.

**Continuing body doctrine**: Because only half the Senate is replaced in each election, the Senate does not need to re-adopt its rules at the start of each Congress in the same mandatory way the House does. This has institutional implications for Senate President succession: the rules of the previous Congress may remain in effect until formally amended.

---

### Senate President: Election, Powers, and Removal

**Election**: Article VI, §16(1) provides each House shall choose, among its members, a Senate President and a House Speaker. No constitutional procedure is specified; the Senate's own rules govern.

**Standard procedure**: Election by majority vote of all members (13 votes). Secret ballot or viva voce depending on Senate rules in effect.

**Removal**: The Senate President holds office at the pleasure of the majority. A Senate President may be removed through a majority vote consistent with the Senate's rules. The Constitution does not enumerate specific grounds — removal is a political act subject to the Senate's internal governance. The critical question is whether the removal was done in conformity with the Senate's own rules (procedural validity) and whether there was a quorum.

**Judicial review of Senate President disputes**: Courts may review whether grave abuse of discretion attended the election or removal, per expanded judicial review. However, courts will not substitute their political judgment for the Senate's on questions of confidence and leadership.

**Key analytical framework for Senate President disputes**:
1. Was a quorum present? (Constitutional requirement — compute from all 24)
2. Was the vote consistent with the Senate's own rules? (Procedural validity)
3. Was the officer properly notified and given an opportunity to be heard? (Due process, if any)
4. Does the disputed action create a constitutional infirmity? (Constitutional validity)
5. Is the dispute justiciable under expanded judicial review? (Gravity of abuse)

### Legislative Investigations (Section 21)

Senate may conduct inquiries in aid of legislation. Requirements: (a) the inquiry must be in aid of legislation; (b) conducted in accordance with duly published rules of procedure; (c) the rights of persons appearing or affected must be respected.

*Standard Chartered Bank v. Senate Committee on Banks* (G.R. No. 167173, 2007): the Senate Committee may issue subpoenas in aid of legislation; bank secrecy laws do not automatically bar disclosure in legislative inquiries, but the Committee must respect evidentiary privileges.

*Neri v. Senate Committee* (2008): presidential communications privilege applies even in Senate §21 inquiries, but must be invoked properly with specificity. A blanket refusal to answer is not sufficient.

**Publication requirement**: The rules of procedure for legislative inquiries must be published. *Garcillano v. House of Representatives* (G.R. No. 170338, 2008): failure to publish rules is a jurisdictional defect that prevents valid subpoenas.

---

### Question Hour (Section 22)

Department heads may be required to appear before and be heard by either House on any matter pertaining to their departments. The President may prevent a department head from appearing. This provision differs from §21: department heads must appear under §21 unless executive privilege is invoked, but under §22, the President's consent is required.

---

### Confirmation Powers (Commission on Appointments)

Joint body of 12 senators and 12 House members. Confirms: heads of executive departments, ambassadors and other diplomatic representatives, officers of the AFP from the rank of colonel or naval captain, and other officers Congress may vest with confirmation requirement by law.

*Calderon v. Carale* (208 SCRA 254, 1992): Congress cannot by statute expand the CA confirmation requirement beyond the constitutional enumeration.

## House of Representatives Submodule

### Speakership and Leadership

The Speaker is elected by a majority of all House members. Removal follows the same majority-of-all-members standard. The House is not a continuing body — rules must be adopted at the start of each Congress.

**Speakership disputes**: Same analytical framework as Senate President disputes. Quorum, rule compliance, constitutional validity, justiciability.

**Turncoatism and party-switching**: The Anti-Party Switching Act (if enacted) would affect House dynamics. Analyze under applicable law at the time of the dispute.

---

### Impeachment Procedure (Article XI, §§2–3)

**Who may be impeached**: President, Vice-President, Members of the Supreme Court, Members of Constitutional Commissions, and the Ombudsman. Members of Congress and ordinary officials are not impeachable.

**Grounds**: culpable violation of the Constitution; treason; bribery; graft and corruption; other high crimes; betrayal of public trust.

**Step-by-step procedure**:

Step 1 — Initiation: A verified complaint is filed with the Secretary-General of the House by any member of the House, or by any citizen upon endorsement by a House member. A resolution of impeachment endorsed or filed by at least one-third of all House members also constitutes initiation. *Francisco*: initiation occurs upon filing of the complaint, not upon referral to the Committee on Justice.

Step 2 — Sufficiency in form and substance: The House Committee on Justice determines sufficiency in form (proper form, verified, signed) and sufficiency in substance (grounds are substantial in law). A negative finding may be elevated to the House plenary.

Step 3 — House action: If found sufficient in substance, the complaint is calendared for plenary deliberation within 60 session days. A one-third vote of all House members is required to pass the Articles of Impeachment.

Step 4 — Trial in Senate: Senate sits as impeachment court. Presiding officer: Senate President (or the Chief Justice if President is on trial). Two-thirds vote of all Senate members (16 of 24) required for conviction.

Step 5 — Judgment: Conviction results in removal from office and disqualification from holding any public office. The Senate may also impose an additional judgment of disqualification. Criminal liability is a separate matter.

**One-year bar**: *Francisco v. House of Representatives* (2003): only one impeachment proceeding may be initiated against the same officer within one year. The bar runs from the date of initiation (filing of verified complaint), not from the date of acquittal or conviction. If two complaints are filed on the same day, the bar applies to the second.

**Quo warranto as alternative**: *Republic v. Sereno* (2018): the Court allowed quo warranto as a separate remedy for lack of qualification, distinct from impeachment for grounds specified in the Constitution. This doctrine is highly contested. Assess with [60-79] confidence range until further en banc clarification.

### Contempt Power

Both Senate and House may punish members and non-members for disorderly behavior and contempt. Imprisonment for contempt of the Senate during a §21 investigation is limited to the session of Congress during which the contempt was committed. *Arnault v. Nazareno* (87 Phil. 29, 1950): Congress's contempt power is inherent.

## Legislative Process: Bicameral Procedure

**Three readings**: A bill must pass three separate readings on three separate days in each house. Printed copies must be distributed at least three days before the third reading except when the President certifies urgency.

**Bicameral Conference Committee**: When the two houses pass different versions, a BCC reconciles. *Tolentino v. Secretary of Finance* (1994): BCC may introduce new provisions if germane to the bill's subject matter. The enrolled bill doctrine prevents courts from examining internal congressional proceedings except as to quorum and voting.

**Veto override**: Two-thirds of all members of each house required to override a presidential veto.

**Enrolled bill doctrine**: The signing of a bill by the Speaker and Senate President and the certification that it was duly passed are conclusive as to its due enactment. Courts will not inquire into proceedings that preceded enrollment.
## Anti-Hallucination Guardrails — Legislative Procedure

Never invent specific Senate or House rule numbers — the internal rules are complex, frequently amended, and easily confused; cite only the constitutional provisions (Art. VI) and the doctrinal principles from jurisprudence. Never state that *Avelino v. Cuenco* was decided under the 1987 Constitution — it is a 1935 Constitution case applied by analogy to the 1987 Constitution. Never conflate the quorum-to-do-business threshold (majority of effective membership) with the Senate-President-election threshold (majority of "all the respective members" under Art. VI, §16[1]) — these are constitutionally distinct and the second threshold remains a contested open question. The 2015 Drilon session precedent is not a Supreme Court decision; it is Senate practice and has persuasive but not binding weight. Never state that a legislative investigation (§21) can compel department heads to appear without executive privilege analysis — *Neri* established that presidential communications privilege applies even in §21 inquiries if properly invoked.
