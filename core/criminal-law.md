# Criminal Law Module

## Primary Sources

**Revised Penal Code (Act No. 3815, as amended)**: The principal codification of Philippine criminal law. Divided into two books: Book I (general provisions — principles, circumstances affecting liability, penalties) and Book II (specific felonies and their penalties).

**Special Penal Laws**: Statutes outside the RPC that define and penalize specific offenses. See `special-penal-laws.md` for cybercrime (R.A. 10175), data privacy (R.A. 10173), AMLA (R.A. 9160 as amended), anti-graft (R.A. 3019), dangerous drugs (R.A. 9165), VAWC (R.A. 9262), child abuse (R.A. 7610), and others.

**1987 Constitution, Article III**: Bill of Rights — due process, unreasonable searches and seizures, rights of the accused, self-incrimination privilege, double jeopardy, ex post facto prohibition, bill of attainder prohibition.

**Rules of Court, Rules on Criminal Procedure**: Procedural framework for criminal prosecutions. Load `remedial-law.md` for procedural analysis.

## Felonies vs. Offenses — Fundamental Distinction

**Felonies** (Art. 3 RPC): Acts and omissions punishable by the RPC. Committed by means of deceit (*dolo*) or fault (*culpa*).

**Offenses**: Acts punishable by special penal laws. The RPC applies suppletorily to offenses under special laws unless the special law provides otherwise or its application would be inconsistent with the special law's nature (*People v. Simon*, G.R. No. 93028, 1994).

**Mala in se vs. mala prohibita**:
- *Mala in se*: Inherently evil; intent (*mens rea*) is an element. RPC felonies are generally mala in se.
- *Mala prohibita*: Wrong because prohibited by law; intent is generally not an element. Many special penal law offenses are mala prohibita. The act alone, if proven, suffices.
- Critical distinction: Good faith and lack of criminal intent are defenses in mala in se; they are generally not defenses in mala prohibita offenses.

## Elements of a Felony — Analytical Framework

For any RPC felony, the prosecution must prove three elements beyond reasonable doubt:

**1. That there is an act or omission** — a positive volitional act, or a failure to act when there is a legal duty to act. Mere thought is not punishable.

**2. That the act or omission is punishable by the RPC** — the act must fall within the description of a specific felony in Book II.

**3. That the act or omission is performed by means of dolo or culpa** (Art. 3):
- *Dolo (intentional felony)*: Freedom of action + intelligence + intent. All three must concur.
- *Culpa (culpable felony)*: Freedom + intelligence + negligence, imprudence, lack of foresight, or lack of skill. Intent is absent; criminal liability arises from the manner of execution.

**Intent vs. motive**: Intent is the purpose to use a particular means to effect a result; it is an element of dolo. Motive is the moving power that impels a person to act; it is generally not an element but may be relevant circumstantially.

## Circumstances Affecting Criminal Liability

### Justifying Circumstances (Art. 11) — No Criminal Liability, No Civil Liability

Act is lawful; no crime committed. Person is not criminally or civilly liable.

1. **Self-defense**: Unlawful aggression by the offended party; reasonable necessity of the means employed; lack of sufficient provocation on the part of the person defending himself. *Unlawful aggression is the indispensable element* — without it, self-defense fails.
2. **Defense of relatives**: Same elements; plus the relative defended is within the enumeration (spouse, ascendants, descendants, brothers/sisters, relatives by affinity within same degrees, relatives by consanguinity within fourth civil degree).
3. **Defense of strangers**: Same elements; plus the person defending is not induced by revenge, resentment, or other evil motive.
4. **State of necessity** (avoidance of greater evil): Evil sought to be avoided actually exists; injury feared is greater than injury done to avoid it; no other practical and less harmful means.
5. **Fulfillment of duty or lawful exercise of right or office**.
6. **Obedience to order of superior for some lawful purpose**.

### Exempting Circumstances (Art. 12) — No Criminal Liability; Civil Liability May Attach

Person is exempt from criminal liability but the underlying act may still give rise to civil liability (borne by the person who has charge of the exempt person).

1. **Imbecility or insanity** at the time of the act. Exception: the insane person acted during a lucid interval.
2. **Minority under 15 years**: Absolute exemption. R.A. 9344 (Juvenile Justice Act): minimum age of criminal responsibility is 15 years old. Those below 15 are exempt.
3. **Minority 15 to under 18 without discernment**: Exempt.
4. **Accident without fault or intention**: While performing a lawful act with due care, causes injury by mere accident.
5. **Compulsion by irresistible force**: Physical force so severe that the person had no other choice.
6. **Impulse of uncontrollable fear**: Fear of equal or greater injury.
7. **Insuperable cause**: Prevented by lawful or insuperable cause from performing an act required by law.

### Mitigating Circumstances (Art. 13) — Reduce Penalty

Incomplete justification or exemption; under 18 or over 70; no intention to commit so grave a wrong; sufficient provocation or threat; immediate vindication of a grave offense; passion or obfuscation; voluntary surrender; voluntary confession of guilt; physical defect; illness reducing will-power; analogous circumstances.

**Privileged mitigating** (Art. 68–69): Incomplete justifying/exempting circumstances when majority of the elements are present; reduce penalty by one or two degrees, not merely a period.

### Aggravating Circumstances (Art. 14) — Increase Penalty

Treachery (*alevosia*); abuse of superior strength; use of means weakening defense; evident premeditation; craft, fraud, disguise; advantage of public position; contempt of or with insult to public authority; disregard of rank, age, sex; dwelling; abuse of confidence or obvious ungratefulness; nighttime, uninhabited place, or band; recidivism; reiteracion; price, reward, or promise; inundation, fire, poison, explosion, stranding of vessel, derailment; evident premeditation; cruelty.

**Generic aggravating**: Can be offset by an ordinary mitigating circumstance.
**Qualifying aggravating**: Changes the nature and name of the crime (e.g., treachery converts homicide to murder). Cannot be offset by mitigating circumstances.
**Special aggravating** (under special laws or Rules): Cannot be offset by ordinary mitigating circumstances.

## Stages of Execution (Art. 6)

**Consummated**: All elements necessary for execution and accomplishment are present.

**Frustrated**: Offender performs all acts of execution which would produce the felony but the crime is not produced by reason of causes independent of the will of the perpetrator.

**Attempted**: Offender commences the commission by overt acts and does not perform all acts of execution by reason of some cause or accident other than his own spontaneous desistance.

**Desistance**: Not punishable if spontaneous before all acts of execution are performed; the prior overt acts may still be punishable as a separate felony if they themselves constitute an offense.

**Impossible crime (Art. 4(2))**: Act performed would be an offense against persons or property if not for inherent impossibility of its accomplishment or inadequate or ineffectual means. Punished with *arresto mayor* and a fine.

## Persons Liable (Art. 16–20)

**Principals**: By direct participation; by induction; by indispensable cooperation.

**Accomplices**: Those who, not being principals, cooperate in the execution of the offense by previous or simultaneous acts.

**Accessories**: Those who have knowledge of the commission of the crime and, without having participated as principals or accomplices, take part subsequent to its commission.

Accessories are exempt from liability for crimes committed by: their spouses, ascendants, descendants, legitimate/natural/adopted brothers/sisters, or relatives by affinity within same degrees — except accessories who profited themselves or assisted the offender to profit from the crime.

**Conspiracy (Art. 8)**: When two or more persons come to an agreement and decide to commit a felony. In conspiracy, the act of one is the act of all — all conspirators are liable as principals.

**Proposal (Art. 8)**: When a person who has decided to commit a felony proposes its execution to some other person or persons. Conspiracy and proposal are punishable only in cases where the law specifically provides.

## Penalties — Framework (Arts. 25–76)

**Classification**: Capital punishment (abolished by R.A. 9346 for crimes under the RPC and special laws, except for heinous crimes under certain conditions — verify current legislative status); afflictive penalties; correctional penalties; light penalties.

**Principal penalties**: Reclusion perpetua (20 years 1 day to 40 years); reclusion temporal (12 years 1 day to 20 years); prision mayor (6 years 1 day to 12 years); prision correccional (6 months 1 day to 6 years); arresto mayor (1 month 1 day to 6 months); arresto menor (1 day to 30 days); destierro; perpetual or temporary absolute disqualification; perpetual or temporary special disqualification; fine.

**Indeterminate Sentence Law (Act No. 4103)**: Applicable to offenses under the RPC and special laws where imprisonment exceeds one year. Court imposes a minimum (within the range of the penalty next lower to that prescribed) and maximum (within the range of the proper penalty). Does not apply to: death penalty offenses, habitual delinquents, persons convicted of certain specific offenses, parole violators.

**Three-fold rule (Art. 70)**: Maximum duration of prisoner's sentence shall not be more than three times the length of time corresponding to the most severe penalty; in no case shall it exceed 40 years.

## Extinction of Criminal Liability (Arts. 89–99)

Death of the convict (as to personal penalties); service of sentence; amnesty; absolute pardon; prescription of the crime and of the penalty; marriage of offended woman (limited offenses); express pardon by offended party (limited offenses).

**Prescription of crimes (Art. 90)**: Reclusion perpetua or death — 20 years; *reclusion temporal* — 20 years; *prision mayor* — 15 years; *prision correccional* — 10 years; *arresto mayor* — 5 years; libel and other similar offenses — 1 year; oral defamation and slander by deed — 6 months; light offenses — 2 months.

**Prescription of penalties (Art. 92)**: Runs from the day the convict evades sentence; stops upon apprehension.

## Selected Book II Felonies — Doctrinal Anchors

**Homicide (Art. 249)**: Killing of any person not falling under parricide, murder, or infanticide. Penalty: reclusion temporal.

**Murder (Art. 248)**: Homicide qualified by any of the qualifying circumstances: treachery; in consideration of price/reward/promise; by means of inundation/fire/poison/explosion/etc.; evident premeditation; cruelty. Penalty: reclusion perpetua to death.

*Key distinction*: Qualifying circumstances (treachery, evident premeditation, abuse of superior strength) must be alleged in the Information and proved beyond reasonable doubt to convict for murder. Failure to allege results in conviction for homicide only.

**Parricide (Art. 246)**: Killing of one's father, mother, child (whether legitimate or illegitimate), other legitimate ascendants, legitimate descendants, or spouse.

**Theft (Art. 308)**: Taking of personal property belonging to another, with intent to gain, without violence or intimidation and without consent. Distinguished from robbery (Art. 293): theft lacks violence/intimidation.

**Estafa (Art. 315)**: Swindling. Three modes: (1) with unfaithfulness or abuse of confidence; (2) by means of false pretenses or fraudulent acts; (3) through fraudulent means. Intent to defraud is an element. Estafa is often charged alongside civil action for the same transaction.

**Qualified theft (Art. 310)**: Theft committed by domestic servant, or with grave abuse of confidence, or when the value of property stolen is large. Penalty two degrees higher than ordinary theft.

**Libel (Art. 353)**: Public and malicious imputation of a crime, vice, or defect (real or imaginary) or any act, omission, condition, status, or circumstance tending to cause dishonor, discredit, or contempt of a natural or juridical person. Elements: (1) imputation of a discreditable act; (2) publication; (3) identity of person defamed; (4) malice. *Actual malice* required when the defamed person is a public officer acting in official capacity (*Vasquez v. Court of Appeals*, G.R. No. 118971, 1999). Cyber-libel under R.A. 10175 has a longer prescriptive period and higher penalty — load `special-penal-laws.md`.

**Bribery (Arts. 210–212)**: Direct bribery (Art. 210) — public officer agrees to perform an act constituting a crime or an unjust act; indirect bribery (Art. 211) — public officer accepts a gift by reason of office. Corruption of public official (Art. 212) — the giver's counterpart offense.

**Malversation (Art. 217)**: Accountable public officer appropriates, takes, misappropriates, or consents to the taking of public funds or property. Demand for accounting is the procedural trigger for a prima facie presumption of malversation if the officer cannot satisfactorily explain the deficiency.

## Anti-Hallucination Guardrails — Criminal Law

Never state that the death penalty is currently imposed and executed in the Philippines without verifying the current legislative status — R.A. 9346 (2006) abolished it for RPC and special law offenses; periodic legislative attempts to restore it must be checked against current law. Never conflate qualifying and generic aggravating circumstances — the procedural consequences differ fundamentally. Never state that intent is not an element of a mala in se offense — it always is. For special penal laws, always check whether the RPC applies suppletorily before importing RPC defenses.
