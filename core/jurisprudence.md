# Jurisprudence Module

## Using This Module

For each case: identify whether it is controlling (en banc, not overruled) or persuasive (division decision, older constitution, foreign authority). Distinguish the holding from dicta. Trace doctrinal evolution — an older case may be limited, distinguished, or superseded by later doctrine.

ANTI-HALLUCINATION: If a GR number cannot be confirmed, cite only the case name and year and note: *"GR number not confirmed — verify against SC E-Library."* Never invent holdings.

## Foundational Constitutional Cases

**Angara v. Electoral Commission** (63 Phil. 139, 1936): Established judicial review over all branches. The Court is the final interpreter of the Constitution. Electoral Commission held to have jurisdiction over election protests of members of the National Assembly, having been created by the Constitution itself with autonomous power.

**Avelino v. Cuenco** (83 Phil. 17, 1949): Senate President election dispute. Court initially declined jurisdiction as political question. On rehearing, held quorum means majority of all 24 senators, not majority of those present. Subsequent doctrine has limited the pure "political question" framing in intra-legislative disputes when constitutional rights are implicated. The case remains the baseline for Senate quorum computation.

**Javellana v. Executive Secretary** (50 SCRA 30, 1973): 1973 Constitution ratification challenge. Fragmented Court: no majority on validity of ratification, but majority held the Court would not restrain implementation. The political question doctrine at its broadest post-Commonwealth application.

**Lansang v. Garcia** (42 SCRA 448, 1971): Suspension of the writ of habeas corpus is judicially reviewable as to factual basis. Departs from earlier absolute executive discretion doctrine. Foundational for the Lagman analysis under 1987 Constitution.

## Separation of Powers / Institutional Power Cases

**Francisco v. House of Representatives** (415 SCRA 44, 2003): Foundational impeachment case. Defined "initiation" of impeachment proceedings as filing of the verified complaint or resolution, not referral to committee. The one-year bar runs from initiation. Expanded judicial review means even impeachment proceedings are subject to Court scrutiny for grave abuse of discretion.

**Senate v. Ermita** (488 SCRA 1, 2006): Executive privilege. E.O. 464 requiring presidential consent before executive officials appear before Congress struck down in part. Distinction between §21 (legislative inquiry) and §22 (question hour) upheld. Presidential communications privilege is qualified.

**Neri v. Senate Committee** (G.R. No. 180643, 2008): Presidential communications privilege shielded communications between President and NEDA Director-General regarding NBN-ZTE deal on three specific questions. Court upheld privilege as to those questions, held Senate's contempt in excess of jurisdiction. Controversial; minority held privilege was not properly invoked.

**Belgica v. Ochoa** (710 SCRA 1, 2013): PDAF (Priority Development Assistance Fund) declared unconstitutional. Post-enactment identification of projects by legislators violates separation of powers and non-delegation doctrine. Congress cannot exercise implementation functions belonging to the executive.

**Araullo v. Aquino** (728 SCRA 1, 2014): Disbursement Acceleration Program partially unconstitutional. Cross-border transfers violate appropriations law. Augmentation requires actual savings, not anticipated savings.

## Bill of Rights / Civil Liberties Cases

**Oposa v. Factoran** (224 SCRA 792, 1993): Intergenerational responsibility. Children have standing to sue for cancellation of timber licenses based on right to balanced ecology. Broadened standing doctrine for environmental cases. Basis for subsequent environmental jurisprudence and OPOSA doctrine.

**David v. Macapagal-Arroyo** (489 SCRA 160, 2006): Presidential Proclamation 1017 (state of national emergency) partially upheld, partially struck down. Court recognized expanded judicial review even for national emergency proclamations. Established exceptions to mootness doctrine. Upheld calling-out power but struck down enforcement of laws without legislative grant and warrantless arrests on vague "acts of terrorism."

**Disini v. Secretary of Justice** (716 SCRA 237, 2014): Cybercrime Prevention Act analysis. Online libel provision upheld as applied to original authors but struck down as applied to those who receive and react to content. Section on aiding and abetting online libel struck down as overbroad.

**Southern Hemisphere v. Anti-Terrorism Council** (632 SCRA 146, 2010): Human Security Act. Court dismissed facial challenges. Set doctrine that facial challenges to penal laws are disfavored outside free speech context. Upheld the law's constitutionality while requiring restrictive interpretation.

## Executive Power Cases

**Chavez v. Gonzales** (G.R. No. 168338, 2008): "Hello Garci" controversy. Prior restraint analysis. Warning by government officials to media not to broadcast constituted prior restraint. Content-based restrictions subject to strict scrutiny.

**De Castro v. JBC** (615 SCRA 666, 2010): Midnight appointment prohibition in Article VII, §15 does not apply to the Supreme Court because Article VIII, §4(1) mandates the Court be filled within 90 days of vacancy. Produced controversy but remains controlling precedent.

**Gonzales III v. Office of the President** (679 SCRA 614, 2012): Deputy Ombudsman and Special Prosecutor are not constitutionally insulated from presidential removal, unlike the Ombudsman. The Ombudsman's independence does not extend to deputies in the same absolute way.

## Ombudsman / Accountability Cases

**Republic v. Sereno** (G.R. No. 237428, 2018): Court upheld quo warranto as an available remedy to oust Chief Justice Sereno for failure to file SALN, holding this was a quo warranto issue (qualification), not an impeachment issue (culpable violation). Highly controversial 8-6 decision. Dissents argued quo warranto circumvents the impeachment mechanism for impeachable officers. This remains contested doctrine — assess confidence accordingly.

**Funa v. Ermita** (G.R. No. 184740, 2010) and subsequent Funa cases: Established that designation in an acting capacity does not apply to constitutional officers requiring Commission on Appointments confirmation. Consolidated doctrine on incompatible offices and hold-over capacity.

---

## Election Law Cases

**Comelec v. Silva** and related cases: Establish COMELEC's broad administrative and quasi-judicial jurisdiction over elections.

**Macalintal v. COMELEC** (405 SCRA 614, 2003): Overseas Absentee Voting Act constitutional analysis.

**Pimentel v. Joint Committee** (G.R. No. 163783, 2004): Congress sitting as canvassing body for presidential election has ministerial functions; cannot go beyond canvassing.

**Roque v. COMELEC** (G.R. No. 188456, 2010): Automated election system constitutionality upheld.

---

## International Law / Treaty Cases

**Bayan v. Zamora** (342 SCRA 449, 2000): VFA is a treaty within Article VII §21 meaning; upheld as valid. US forces' presence in the Philippines upheld under RP-US Mutual Defense Treaty and VFA framework.

**Saguisag v. Ochoa** (779 SCRA 241, 2016): EDCA is an executive agreement, not a treaty; does not require Senate concurrence. Within President's constitutional authority over foreign affairs and commander-in-chief powers. Strong dissent argued the EDCA's scope required Senate concurrence.

**Province of North Cotabato v. GRP** (568 SCRA 402, 2008): MOA-AD between GRP and MILF struck down. The associative relationship described between the Bangsamoro Juridical Entity and the Philippine state was inconsistent with the 1987 Constitution because it created a state within a state without prior constitutional amendment.

## Doctrinal Timeline: Political Question Doctrine

| Period | Doctrine | Key Case |
|---|---|---|
| 1936 | Judicial review established; scope unclear | *Angara* |
| 1949 | Senate internal matters: political question | *Avelino* |
| 1971 | Factual basis of writ suspension: reviewable | *Lansang* |
| 1973 | Ratification: political question at its broadest | *Javellana* |
| 2003 | Expanded judicial review narrows political question; impeachment reviewable | *Francisco* |
| 2006 | Emergency powers, national emergency proclamations reviewable | *David* |
| 2017 | Martial law: independent factual review | *Lagman* |
| 2018 | Quo warranto vs. impeachment: jurisdiction overlap created | *Sereno* |

**Current doctrine**: The political question doctrine survives primarily for questions textually committed to another branch with no judicially manageable standards. Virtually any constitutional question may be reviewed under the expanded judicial review standard of grave abuse of discretion.

## Controlling vs. Persuasive Authority Matrix

| Authority Type | Binding? | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| SC En Banc, not overruled | Yes | Binding on all courts |
| SC Division, not modified | Yes | Binding unless modified by En Banc |
| SC En Banc that supersedes earlier En Banc | Yes | Later controls |
| SC decisions on different constitutional text | Persuasive | 1935/1973 cases re similar provisions |
| US Supreme Court | Persuasive | Given shared doctrinal roots |
| Commonwealth/Spanish tradition | Persuasive | Historical context only |
| ConCom records | Interpretive aid | Not binding authority |
| Legal commentaries | Secondary | Never overrides primary authority |

## Private Law — Landmark Cases

*Note: This section covers controlling cases in obligations/contracts, family law, succession, labor, and criminal law. For extended doctrinal treatment, load the relevant substantive module. GR numbers marked [unconfirmed] require verification against SC E-Library.*

### Contracts and Quasi-Delicts

**Nikko Hotel Manila Garden v. Reyes** [unconfirmed GR — verify]: Abuse of rights doctrine (Art. 19 NCC) requires three elements: legal right, abuse, and damage. Not every exercise of a legal right is actionable; the manner of exercise must be contrary to morals, good customs, or public policy.

**Air France v. Carrascoso** (18 SCRA 155, 1966): Quasi-delict doctrine applied to breach of contract accompanied by bad faith. Bad faith in contractual performance converts the action into one also cognizable under tort principles; moral damages available.

### Family Law

**Santos v. Court of Appeals** (G.R. No. 112019, January 4, 1995): Established the three-element test for psychological incapacity under Art. 36 FC — gravity, juridical antecedence, incurability. Remained controlling doctrine until modified by *Tan-Andal*.

**Republic v. Court of Appeals and Molina** (G.R. No. 108763, February 13, 1997): Established eight operational guidelines for Art. 36 petitions. Effective standard from 1997 through 2021. *Significantly modified by Tan-Andal* — no longer the controlling test.

**Tan-Andal v. Andal** (G.R. No. 196359, May 11, 2021, En Banc): **Current controlling doctrine on Art. 36.** Psychological incapacity is a legal concept, not a medical diagnosis. Expert testimony helpful but not indispensable. Incurability is relative to the marriage, not absolute. *Molina* guidelines are useful tools, not mandatory prerequisites.

**Republic v. Manalo** (G.R. No. 221029, April 24, 2018, En Banc): Art. 26(2) FC — the right to remarry after a foreign spouse obtains a valid divorce extends to cases where the Filipino spouse obtained the divorce abroad. Overturned the prior rule limiting Art. 26(2) to divorces initiated by the foreign spouse.

### Labor Law

**Agabon v. NLRC** (G.R. No. 158693, November 17, 2004, En Banc): Established the rule that dismissal for just cause but without procedural due process is valid but entitles the employee to nominal damages (₱30,000). Overruled *Serrano v. NLRC* on this point.

**Jaka Food Processing Corporation v. Pacot** (G.R. No. 151378, March 28, 2005): Extended *Agabon* framework to authorized-cause dismissals — nominal damages of ₱50,000 for authorized cause dismissal without proper notice. *Note: SC may update these amounts.*

**St. Martin Funeral Home v. NLRC** (G.R. No. 130866, September 16, 1998): Certiorari over NLRC decisions must be filed with the Court of Appeals, not directly with the Supreme Court. Established the appellate route for NLRC review.

### Criminal Law

**People v. Simon** (G.R. No. 93028, July 29, 1994): RPC applies suppletorily to special penal law offenses unless the special law expressly provides otherwise or suppletory application is inconsistent with the law's nature. Foundational case for mala prohibita analysis.

**People v. Dulay** [unconfirmed GR — verify]: Established that the four-witness rule in drug cases is mandatory; non-compliance with §21 R.A. 9165 inventory requirements triggers analysis under the saving clause but does not automatically warrant acquittal if integrity and evidentiary value are preserved.

**Malto v. People** [unconfirmed GR — verify]: Cyber-libel prescriptive period — present the 1-year (Art. 90 RPC suppletory) and 12-year (Act 3326) interpretations and note SC's resolution of the dispute must be verified.

### Succession

**Acain v. Intermediate Appellate Court** (G.R. No. 72706, October 27, 1987): Preterition — total omission of a compulsory heir from the will produces the effect of annulling the institution of heirs (Art. 854 NCC). Distinguished preterition (total omission) from mere reduction of legitime.

*For Iron Curtain Rule (Art. 992) and legitime computation, load `succession-inheritance.md` — those provisions are codified law with minimal landmark-case doctrinal evolution in the SC.*

## Anti-Hallucination Guardrails — Jurisprudence (Private Law Addendum)

Never cite *Santos v. CA* as current controlling doctrine on psychological incapacity — it has been modified by *Tan-Andal* (2021) on the incurability standard. Never apply the *Molina* eight-guideline checklist as a mandatory requirement after *Tan-Andal*. Never state *Agabon* nominal damages amounts as fixed — they are SC-benchmarks subject to update; verify current amounts. For drug case chain-of-custody cases, only landmark doctrinal cases should be cited by GR number — individual drug case GR numbers are numerous and easily confused; load `special-penal-laws.md` for §21 doctrine. For private law cases generally, load the relevant substantive module first; this section provides doctrinal anchors, not comprehensive jurisprudence coverage.
