# Administrative Law Module

## Sources of Administrative Power

Administrative agencies derive power from three sources, in descending order of authority:

**Constitutional grants**: Certain agencies are constitutionally created (COMELEC, COA, CSC, Ombudsman, NLRC-related provisions) and derive primary power from the Constitution itself. Their enabling statutes fill procedural gaps but cannot diminish constitutional powers.

**Statutory delegation**: Most agencies exist by statute. The enabling act defines the agency's jurisdiction, powers, and limitations. An agency may not act beyond its enabling statute — *ultra vires* acts are void. *Eastern Shipping Lines v. POEA* (1988): valid delegation requires (a) completeness of delegation and (b) sufficient standard to guide agency discretion.

**Presidential authority**: Executive agencies under the President's control and supervision may receive power by executive order, administrative order, or memorandum circular, to the extent consistent with the agency's enabling statute.

## Classification of Administrative Functions

**Quasi-legislative (rule-making)**: Agency issuances with the force of law. Must comply with the Administrative Code publication requirement (R.A. 9184 and Administrative Code of 1987). Publication in the Official Gazette or newspaper of general circulation is required for effectiveness. *Tañada v. Tuvera* (146 SCRA 446, 1986): even internal rules of agencies affecting rights of persons must be published.

**Quasi-judicial (adjudicatory)**: Agency hearings and decisions on individual rights. Subject to *Ang Tibay* due process requirements (see crisis-frameworks.md). Must observe the right to notice, the right to be heard, the requirement that decision be based on evidence.

**Ministerial functions**: Acts where no discretion is involved; compliance with a mandatory duty. Mandamus lies to compel ministerial acts; mandamus does not lie to compel discretionary acts.

**Discretionary functions**: Acts requiring judgment and assessment. Courts review only for grave abuse of discretion, not for correctness of judgment on technical matters.

## Due Process in Administrative Proceedings

*Ang Tibay v. CIR* (69 Phil. 635, 1940) remains the foundational case. Its seven cardinal rights in administrative proceedings are binding doctrine:

1. Right to a hearing, including the right to present one's case and submit evidence
2. The tribunal must consider evidence presented before it
3. Decision must be based on evidence presented at the hearing (substantial evidence standard)
4. The tribunal must act on its own independent consideration of the law and facts; not delegate fact-finding to a subordinate whose report is not open to challenge
5. Decision must be rendered in such a manner that parties know the issues and reasons
6. Evidence must be substantial — such relevant evidence as a reasonable mind might accept as adequate to support a conclusion
7. Decision must be rendered on evidence presented — no denial of due process if the party was given an opportunity to be heard even if not actually heard

**Substantial evidence standard**: Lower than preponderance of evidence. "Such relevant evidence as a reasonable mind might accept as adequate to support a conclusion." *Montoya v. Escayo* and related cases.

**Notice and hearing**: Both components of procedural due process. Notice must be adequate to apprise the party of the nature of the charges and the proceeding. Hearing need not be a full trial — an opportunity to submit position papers satisfies the requirement in many administrative contexts.

## Exhaustion of Administrative Remedies

Before filing court action challenging an administrative decision, the party must exhaust all available administrative remedies. Failure to exhaust is a ground for dismissal of the court action (without prejudice).

**Rationale**: Agencies have primary jurisdiction and expertise over their subject matter. Courts will not interfere until the agency has completed its review process.

**Exceptions to exhaustion doctrine**:
- Pure question of law
- Urgency requiring immediate judicial relief
- Denial of due process at the administrative level
- Administrative action is patently illegal or ultra vires
- Issue is purely legal with no factual dispute
- Amount involved is small and administrative cost is excessive
- Agency action is confiscatory

---

## Doctrine of Primary Jurisdiction

Even when a court has jurisdiction, if the matter requires the technical expertise of an administrative agency, the court will defer to the agency's primary determination. *Industrial Enterprises v. CA* (184 SCRA 426, 1990): matters requiring special technical knowledge should first be decided by the agency with expertise.

Distinguish from exhaustion: primary jurisdiction applies even when no administrative remedy is pending; it is a deferral of judicial action to allow agency determination of technical issues.

---

## Judicial Review of Administrative Decisions

**Standard**: Grave abuse of discretion amounting to lack or excess of jurisdiction. Courts do not re-examine the merits of agency factual determinations if supported by substantial evidence. *NLRC decisions*: reviewed by CA via certiorari, then by SC via certiorari; SC does not re-weigh evidence.

**Scope of review**:
- Was the agency's jurisdiction validly invoked?
- Did the agency observe due process?
- Is the decision supported by substantial evidence?
- Did the agency commit grave abuse of discretion?
- Did the agency act within its enabling statute?

**What courts will NOT do**: Substitute their judgment for the agency's on matters within the agency's expertise; re-weigh conflicting evidence; reverse agency findings merely because the court would have weighed evidence differently.

## Office of the Ombudsman

**Constitutional basis**: Article XI, §§5–14. Ombudsman is an independent constitutional office, not under the executive.

**Jurisdiction**: All acts or omissions of public officials and employees of the government, GOCCs, state universities, and local government units.

**Powers**: Investigate, prosecute, and recommend disciplinary action against public officials. Has prosecutorial authority before the Sandiganbayan for officials with SG 27 and above.

**Administrative disciplinary vs. criminal jurisdiction**: Ombudsman may impose administrative sanctions (suspension, dismissal) in addition to filing criminal cases. Administrative findings do not require proof beyond reasonable doubt.

**Review**: Ombudsman decisions in administrative cases are reviewable by the Court of Appeals via Rule 43. Ombudsman decisions in criminal cases (resolutions on probable cause) are reviewable by the SC via certiorari.

**Independence**: *Gonzales III v. Office of the President* (2012): The President cannot remove the Ombudsman, who is removable only by impeachment. Deputy Ombudsman is removable by the President.

---

## Sandiganbayan

**Constitutional basis**: Article XI, §4. Created as a special anti-graft court.

**Jurisdiction**: Offenses committed by public officials in relation to their office, where the official holds a position with SG 27 and above, or where the offense charged has a penalty above prision correccional. P.D. 1606 as amended by R.A. 10660 governs.

**Key rule**: The offense must be committed "in relation to office" — not all crimes by public officials fall under Sandiganbayan jurisdiction. *Lacson v. Executive Secretary* (301 SCRA 298, 1999): discussed Sandiganbayan jurisdictional parameters.

---

## Government-Owned and Controlled Corporations (GOCCs)

GOCCs are subject to administrative law but have both private (corporate) and public (governmental) dimensions. Officers of GOCCs with original charters are public officers subject to the Ombudsman's jurisdiction. Officers of GOCCs incorporated under the Corporation Code occupy a hybrid position. *Quimpo v. Tanodbayan* and related cases: determined when GOCC officers are public officers for anti-graft purposes.

---

## Administrative Code of 1987 (E.O. 292)

The primary codification of administrative law. Covers organization of the national government, the civil service, the budget process, and the administrative procedure framework. Agencies must observe the Code's procedural requirements unless a specific statute provides otherwise.
## Anti-Hallucination Guardrails — Administrative Law

Never conflate the seven *Ang Tibay* cardinal rights with eight — the "exercise of independent judgment" is incorporated into right (4), not a separate right. Never state that Sandiganbayan jurisdiction requires SG 27+ for all offenses — verify the current jurisdictional thresholds under R.A. 10660 for the specific offense and position. Never treat agency interpretations as having the same binding force as statutes — administrative interpretations are entitled to great weight but courts may override them when clearly erroneous. Never state that the Ombudsman's deputies share the Ombudsman's removal insulation — *Gonzales III v. Office of the President* (2012) held deputies are removable by the President. The exhaustion doctrine has recognized exceptions; always check whether an exception applies before concluding that a court action was prematurely filed.
