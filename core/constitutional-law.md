# Constitutional Law Module

## Constitutional Tradition Overview

Three constitutions form the Philippine constitutional tradition. Doctrinal continuity exists across all three for some provisions; others represent clear breaks.

---

## 1935 Constitution

**Historical context**: Drafted by the 1934 Constitutional Convention under US Commonwealth governance. Patterned substantially on the US Constitution. Took effect November 15, 1935.

**Structure**: Unicameral National Assembly (later amended to bicameral Congress), executive branch, Supreme Court. Bill of Rights modeled on US Fifth and Fourteenth Amendments.

**Key doctrinal contributions**:
- Established judicial review in the Philippine constitutional order, formalized in *Angara v. Electoral Commission* (63 Phil. 139, 1936): the Court articulated that it is "the final arbiter in the interpretation of constitutional provisions."
- Electoral tribunal jurisdiction first established under this Constitution.
- The doctrine that the Constitution is the supreme law of the land traces to this text.

**Amendment history**: Amended in 1940 to restore bicameralism and extend presidential term to four years with one re-election.

**Relevance today**: Jurisprudence decided under the 1935 Constitution on separation of powers, judicial review, and bill of rights retains persuasive authority where equivalent 1987 provisions exist.

---

## 1973 Constitution

**Historical context**: Drafted by the 1971 Constitutional Convention. Ratification is doctrinally controversial. Proclaimed ratified by President Marcos via Proclamation No. 1102 (January 17, 1973) through citizen assemblies. *Javellana v. Executive Secretary* (G.R. No. L-36142, 1973): the Supreme Court was divided on the validity of ratification but a majority held the issue was a political question and courts would not interfere with implementation.

**Structure**: Modified parliamentary form. President as symbolic head of state; Prime Minister as head of government. Later amendments collapsed this structure and concentrated power in President Marcos.

**Key doctrinal contributions**:
- *Lansang v. Garcia* (42 SCRA 448, 1971, decided before but applied under 1973 framework): Court held it has authority to inquire into factual basis of suspension of the privilege of the writ of habeas corpus. This limited the "political question" scope in security matters.
- *Aquino Jr. v. Enrile* (59 SCRA 183, 1974): Military tribunal jurisdiction over civilians during martial law period.
- Established constitutional basis for the Batasan Pambansa (interim and regular).

**Amendment history**: Extensively amended through referenda and constitutional amendments, including 1976 and 1981 amendments.

**Relevance today**: 1973 Constitution jurisprudence is primarily of historical relevance. Where 1987 provisions supersede 1973 provisions, 1987 doctrine controls. For understanding institutional design choices made in the 1987 Constitutional Commission, 1973 period serves as the negative reference point.

---

## 1987 Constitution — Article-by-Article Analysis Framework

**Historical context**: Drafted by the 1986 Constitutional Commission (Con-Com), a body of 48 members appointed by President Corazon Aquino. Ratified by plebiscite February 2, 1987. Took effect February 11, 1987.

**Interpretive sources** (load in order of weight):
1. Text of the provision
2. Structural inference from constitutional design
3. Con-Com deliberations (Volumes I–V of Records of the Constitutional Commission)
4. Sponsorship speeches and interpellations
5. Ratification context
6. Subsequent Supreme Court doctrine

---

### Article I — National Territory

Defines the national territory including the Philippine archipelago, territorial sea, air space, subsoil, seabed, and other submarine areas. The phrase "other areas over which the Philippines has sovereignty or jurisdiction" is a deliberate expansion beyond strict UNCLOS definitions.

**WPS relevance**: Article I is the constitutional basis for Philippine sovereignty claims in the West Philippine Sea. However, Article I's scope does not automatically resolve UNCLOS entitlement questions. Load references/public-international-law.md for interaction analysis.

---

### Article II — Declaration of Principles and State Policies

Not self-executory as a rule. *Tanada v. Angara* (272 SCRA 18, 1997): Article II provisions "do not embody judicially enforceable constitutional rights but guidelines for legislation." However, they serve as aids in construing ambiguous provisions.

Key provisions for political-law analysis: Section 1 (democratic and republican state), Section 2 (incorporation of international law), Section 3 (civilian supremacy), Section 4 (prime duty to defend state), Section 7 (independent foreign policy), Section 8 (freedom from nuclear weapons).

**Section 2** — Adoption of generally accepted principles of international law as part of the law of the land: this is the incorporation doctrine. Distinguish from transformation doctrine (requiring legislative act). Load references/public-international-law.md.

---

### Article III — Bill of Rights

Self-executory. Constitutes judicially enforceable rights against state action. Key provisions:

**Section 1 (Due Process and Equal Protection)**: Substantive and procedural due process. *Ynot v. IAC* (148 SCRA 659, 1987): substantive due process requires sufficient standard, not arbitrary deprivation. Equal protection allows reasonable classification.

**Section 2 (Search and Seizure)**: Warrant requirement with judicial authorization. Exclusionary rule in Section 3(2).

**Section 4 (Freedom of Expression)**: *Chavez v. Gonzales* (G.R. No. 168338, 2008): prior restraint analysis; overbreadth doctrine; content-based vs. content-neutral distinction. *SWS v. COMELEC* (357 SCRA 496, 2001): election surveys as protected expression.

**Section 12–14 (Rights of the Accused)**: *Miranda* rights constitutional basis; right to counsel; right to bail; *Enrile v. Sandiganbayan* (G.R. No. 213847, 2015): bail on humanitarian grounds even in capital offenses.

**Section 17 (Self-Incrimination)**: Privilege applies to testimonial compulsion, not to physical evidence.

---

### Article VI — Legislative Department

**Composition**: Senate (24 members, nationwide, six-year terms, staggered); House of Representatives (district and party-list members, three-year terms).

**Quorum**: Majority of each House constitutes a quorum to do business. *Avelino v. Cuenco* (83 Phil. 17, 1949): Court initially declined jurisdiction on Senate President election dispute as political question, then on rehearing held that quorum means majority of all members, not majority of those present.

**Senate President / Speaker election**: Internal matter subject to each House's rules. Judicial review available only for grave abuse of discretion under expanded jurisdiction in Article VIII, Section 1.

**Bicameral Conference Committee**: *Tolentino v. Secretary of Finance* (235 SCRA 630, 1994): conference committee may introduce new provisions not found in either chamber version if germane to subject.

**Electoral Tribunals**: HRET and SET are the sole judges of all contests relating to the election, returns, and qualifications of their respective members. *Lazatin v. HRET* (168 SCRA 391, 1988): jurisdiction is exclusive and final.

**Power of inquiry**: Section 21 — legislative investigations in aid of legislation. Section 22 — question hour for department heads. *Standard Chartered Bank v. Senate Committee* (G.R. No. 167173, 2007): distinctions between §21 and §22. *Neri v. Senate Committee* (G.R. No. 180643, 2008): executive privilege may protect communications but must be invoked properly.

**Appropriations**: General Appropriations Act must originate in House. *Bengzon v. Drilon* (208 SCRA 133, 1992): President cannot veto portions of appropriations law without vetoing entire item.

**PDAF/DAP**: *Belgica v. Ochoa* (710 SCRA 1, 2013): PDAF (pork barrel) declared unconstitutional for violating separation of powers and non-delegation. *Araullo v. Aquino* (728 SCRA 1, 2014): DAP partially unconstitutional.

---

### Article VII — Executive Department

**Qualifications, term, and succession**: See references/election-law.md.

**Commander-in-chief powers**: Section 18. Martial law, suspension of the privilege of the writ, calling-out power are three distinct powers with distinct constitutional standards and review mechanisms. Load references/political-law.md for detailed analysis.

**Appointment power**: Section 16. Regular appointments vs. ad interim appointments. Midnight appointments prohibition. *De Castro v. JBC* (615 SCRA 666, 2010): prohibition on midnight appointments does not apply to judiciary.

**Veto power**: Section 27. Item veto for appropriations, revenue, tariff bills. *Bengzon v. Drilon* supra.

**Treaty-making**: Section 21. Concurrence of two-thirds of Senate required. *Bayan v. Zamora* (342 SCRA 449, 2000): VFA is a treaty, upheld. *Saguisag v. Ochoa* (779 SCRA 241, 2016): EDCA is an executive agreement not requiring Senate concurrence.

---

### Article VIII — Judicial Department

**Expanded judicial review**: Section 1, second paragraph. Courts may exercise judicial power to determine "whether or not there has been grave abuse of discretion amounting to lack or excess of jurisdiction on the part of any branch or instrumentality of the Government." This is the operative basis for the Court's post-1987 activism. Load references/political-law.md for justiciability analysis.

**Supreme Court composition and quorum**: 15 members. En banc: majority for constitutional questions.

**Judicial and Bar Council**: Section 8. Appointments to judiciary coursed through JBC. *Chavez v. JBC* (G.R. No. 202242, 2013): Congress has only one representative to JBC.

---

### Article IX — Constitutional Commissions

COMELEC, COA, CSC are constitutionally independent. COMELEC has exclusive original jurisdiction over election contests for elective regional, provincial, and city officials; appellate jurisdiction over municipal and barangay. Load references/election-law.md.

---

### Article X — Local Government

Constitutional basis for the Local Government Code. Autonomous regions: Bangsamoro (post-BOL) and the Cordillera regime. *Province of North Cotabato v. GRP* (568 SCRA 402, 2008): associative relationship with "state within a state" character unconstitutional under 1987 Constitution. *Abas Kida v. Senate* (G.R. No. 196271, 2011): ARMM elections deferral analysis. Load references/local-government-law.md.

---

### Article XI — Accountability of Public Officers

**Impeachable officers**: President, Vice-President, Members of the Supreme Court, Members of Constitutional Commissions, and the Ombudsman.

**Grounds**: culpable violation of the Constitution, treason, bribery, graft and corruption, other high crimes, betrayal of public trust.

**Procedure**: verified complaint filed in House; Committee on Justice determination of sufficiency in form and substance; one-year bar rule; transmittal to Senate; trial in Senate; two-thirds vote of all members for conviction.

**One-year bar**: *Francisco v. House of Representatives* (415 SCRA 44, 2003): initiating means filing of verified complaint or resolution of impeachment, not referral to committee. Only one impeachment proceeding per year per officer.

**CJ Sereno ouster**: *Republic v. Sereno* (G.R. No. 237428, 2018): quo warranto as an alternative to impeachment upheld by divided Court. Extremely controversial. Three dissenting justices held this circumvented impeachment as the exclusive mode of removal.

Load references/legislative-procedure.md for full impeachment procedural analysis.

---

### Article XII — National Economy and Patrimony

**Filipino-first policy**: 60-40 ownership requirement for utilization of natural resources, public utilities, and mass media. *Roy III v. Herbosa* (G.R. No. 207246, 2016): "beneficial ownership" test for determining Filipino ownership.

---

### Article XVII — Amendments and Revisions

**People's initiative**: Section 2. *Santiago v. COMELEC* (270 SCRA 106, 1997): R.A. 6735 inadequate to implement people's initiative for constitutional amendments. *Lambino v. COMELEC* (505 SCRA 160, 2006): upheld *Santiago*; distinguished amendment from revision; proposed shift to parliamentary system was a revision requiring constituent assembly or constitutional convention, not people's initiative.

---

## Constitutional Interpretation Framework

Apply methods in this sequence, resolving conflicts by the nature of the dispute:

**Textualism**: Start with the plain meaning of the constitutional text. If unambiguous, this controls.

**Structural interpretation**: Infer meaning from the Constitution's overall design and the relationships between articles.

**Historical/intent-based**: Resort to Con-Com records, sponsorship speeches, and interpellations when text is ambiguous. *Civil Liberties Union v. Executive Secretary* (194 SCRA 317, 1991): framers' intent used to interpret prohibition on incompatible offices.

**Harmonization**: Provisions must be read to give effect to all, avoiding interpretations that render other provisions surplusage.

**Practical construction**: Long-continued governmental practice may evidence constitutional interpretation, but cannot override text.

**Contemporary doctrine**: Where the Supreme Court has settled an interpretive question, that doctrine controls absent an en banc reversal.

**Never**: Choose one interpretive method as universally controlling. The appropriate method depends on the nature of the provision and the dispute.
## Anti-Hallucination Guardrails — Constitutional Law

Never invent Constitutional Commission (Con-Com) deliberation quotes or paraphrase framers' intent without identified source material — fabricated framers' intent is a serious citation error. Never apply the 1935 or 1973 Constitution to post-1987 disputes without first determining whether the 1987 provision is materially different. Never treat Art. XII's ownership restriction as uniformly 60-40 across all sectors — natural resources, public utilities (as narrowed by *Gamboa* and *Roy III*), mass media (100% Filipino), and educational institutions have different rules. Never cite *Republic v. Sereno* (2018) as settled precedent without noting its 8-6 margin and the strong dissent holding quo warranto cannot circumvent impeachment — this remains contested doctrine warranting a reduced confidence score. Never state that treaty obligations automatically override inconsistent statutes in domestic courts — *Ichong v. Hernandez* (1957) held a later statute controls over an earlier treaty domestically; the hierarchy stated in SKILL.md is the constitutional ideal, not the settled domestic rule.
