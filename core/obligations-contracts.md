# Obligations and Contracts Module

## Primary Sources

**New Civil Code (R.A. 386), Book IV (Arts. 1156–2270)**: The governing law on obligations and contracts. This is the most litigated area of Philippine private law.

**Civil Code Arts. 19–21**: General provisions on human relations — basis for abuse of rights doctrine and quasi-delicts between parties not in a contractual relationship.

---

## Obligations — Fundamental Framework

**Definition (Art. 1156)**: An obligation is a juridical necessity to give, to do, or not to do.

**Sources of obligations (Art. 1157)**: Law; contracts; quasi-contracts; acts or omissions punished by law; quasi-delicts.

**Classification**: Simple / alternative / facultative; divisible / indivisible; with a period / conditional; pure; with accessory (penalty clause).

**Quasi-contracts (Arts. 2142–2175)**: Negotiorum gestio (voluntary management of another's affairs without authority) and solutio indebiti (payment of what is not owed). Both give rise to obligations by operation of law.

## Contracts — Essential Elements and Validity

### Essential Requisites (Art. 1318)

No contract exists without: **(1) Consent** of the contracting parties; **(2) Object** certain which is the subject matter; **(3) Cause** of the obligation established.

All three must be present. Absence of any essential requisite produces a void contract.

### Consent — Defects (Arts. 1330–1346)

Consent is vitiated by mistake, violence, intimidation, undue influence, or fraud. Vitiated consent makes the contract **voidable**, not void.

**Mistake** (Art. 1331): Must be as to the substance of the thing which is the object of the contract, or to those conditions which have principally moved one or both parties to enter into the contract. Simple mistake of account shall give rise to its correction, not annulment.

**Violence and intimidation** (Art. 1335): Reasonable and well-grounded fear of imminent and serious injury to person or property. Intimidation by a third party (not a contracting party) vitiates consent.

**Undue influence** (Art. 1337): When a person takes improper advantage of his power over the will of another, depriving the latter of reasonable freedom of choice.

**Fraud (dolo)** (Arts. 1338–1344): Dolo causante (causal fraud — insidious words or machinations which, without them, the other party would not have agreed) — vitiates consent, makes contract voidable. Dolo incidente (incidental fraud — fraud not the determining cause) — does not void the contract but gives rise to damages.

### Object — Requirements (Arts. 1347–1349)

Must be within the commerce of man; not impossible; determinable as to its kind.

**Illicit objects**: Acts beyond commerce of men; services contrary to law, morals, good customs, public order, public policy.

### Cause (Arts. 1350–1355)

Onerous contracts: the cause is the prestation or promise of a thing or service by the other party. Remuneratory contracts: the cause is the service or benefit remunerated. Gratuitous contracts (donations): the cause is the liberality of the benefactor.

**Absence of cause** — void contract. **Failure of cause** — does not itself void the contract; gives rise to an action for rescission or damages.

## Classification by Validity

**Valid contract**: All essential elements present; no defect.

**Rescissible contracts (Arts. 1380–1389)**: Valid until rescinded. Causes: lesion (damage to one of the parties or a third person, in cases specifically provided); fraud on creditors (accion pauliana). Rescission is subsidiary — available only when the party suffering damage has no other legal means to obtain reparation.

**Voidable contracts (Arts. 1390–1402)**: Valid until annulled. Causes: vitiated consent (mistake, violence, intimidation, undue influence, fraud); legal incapacity of one party. Action for annulment prescribes in 4 years (from cessation of incapacity, or discovery of fraud/mistake, or cessation of intimidation/undue influence).

**Unenforceable contracts (Arts. 1403–1408)**: Cannot be enforced by action in court unless ratified. Causes: Statute of Frauds (certain contracts must be in writing — sale of real property, contracts not to be performed within a year, agreements in consideration of marriage, etc.); both parties incapable; entered into by an agent without authority.

**Void or inexistent contracts (Arts. 1409–1422)**: No legal effect from inception. Causes: absolutely simulated; illegal object or cause contrary to law, morals, good customs, public order, public policy; absolutely fictitious; contemplates a nonexistent thing; intention of parties cannot be ascertained.

**Critical rule on void contracts**: A void contract produces no effect and cannot be ratified. Neither party can recover what was given by reason of the void contract — the clean-hands doctrine (*in pari delicto*) bars recovery where both parties are at fault.

## Performance and Breach

### Fulfillment and Default

**Demand (Art. 1169)**: In obligations to give, the obligor is in default (mora) from the time the obligee judicially or extrajudicially demands fulfillment. Exception: obligation or law provides no need for demand; time is of the essence; demand would be useless.

**Mora solvendi**: Debtor in default. Liability for damages; bears risk of loss even for fortuitous events.

**Mora accipiendi**: Creditor unjustifiably refuses to accept. Debtor may consign.

**Compensation morae**: Both parties in default — each bears own damages.

### Extinguishment of Obligations (Arts. 1231–1304)

Payment or performance; loss of the thing due; condonation or remission of the debt; confusion or merger; compensation (legal, voluntary, judicial); novation.

**Novation (Arts. 1291–1304)**: Substitution of a new obligation for an old one. Requires clear and express intent to novate, or complete incompatibility. Presumed only if expressly stated or clearly implied — courts do not lightly find novation.

## Damages (Arts. 2195–2235)

### Types

**Actual or compensatory** (Art. 2199): Adequate compensation for the pecuniary loss suffered; must be duly proved.

**Moral** (Art. 2217): Suffered by reason of physical suffering, mental anguish, fright, serious anxiety, besmirched reputation, wounded feelings, moral shock, social humiliation. Cannot be awarded without sufficient basis — proof required. *Mahinay v. CA* and related cases: moral damages are not automatically awarded with actual damages.

**Nominal** (Art. 2221): Awarded to vindicate or recognize a right which has been violated. Not for indemnification but vindication.

**Temperate or moderate** (Art. 2224): More than nominal but less than compensatory. When actual damages cannot be proved with certainty.

**Liquidated** (Art. 2226): Predetermined damages agreed upon by the parties. May be reduced by courts if iniquitous or unconscionable (Art. 2227).

**Exemplary or corrective** (Art. 2229): Imposed by way of example or correction for the public good, in addition to moral, temperate, liquidated, or compensatory damages. Requires finding of bad faith, fraud, malice, or wanton conduct.

**Attorney's fees**: Not automatically awarded. Must be based on specific grounds in Art. 2208 (e.g., defendant's bad faith, defendant compelled plaintiff to litigate).

## Quasi-Delicts (Culpa Aquiliana) (Arts. 2176–2194)

**Elements**: (1) fault or negligence; (2) damage; (3) causal connection between the act/omission and the damage.

Distinguished from contractual liability (culpa contractual): quasi-delict arises from no pre-existing contractual relation. However, where a contract exists and is breached through negligence, the injured party may elect to proceed on either contract or quasi-delict (*Cangco v. MRR*, Phil. 768, 1918 and subsequent cases).

**Vicarious liability** (Art. 2180): Employers are liable for the negligent acts of employees acting within the scope of their assigned tasks. Defense: diligence of a good father of a family in the selection and supervision of the employee.

**Prescriptive period**: Four years for quasi-delicts.

## Anti-Hallucination Guardrails — Contracts

Never conflate void, voidable, unenforceable, and rescissible contracts — they have distinct causes, effects, and remedies. A void contract cannot be ratified; voidable contracts can be ratified by the injured party. Statute of Frauds applies only to the contracts specifically enumerated in Art. 1403(2); it does not apply to all oral contracts. Dolo causante vitiates consent; dolo incidente gives damages only. Always distinguish whether you are analyzing formation (does a contract exist?) from performance (was the contract breached?).
