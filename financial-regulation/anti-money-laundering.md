# Financial Regulation — Anti-Money Laundering Law

## Primary Sources
**R.A. 9160 (Anti-Money Laundering Act of 2001, "AMLA")** as amended by **R.A. 9194** (2003), **R.A. 10167** (2012), **R.A. 10365** (2013), **R.A. 10927** (2017 — extending AMLA to casinos), and **R.A. 11521** (2021 — FATF gray-listing response amendments). **AMLC Regulatory Issuances (ARI)** and **Implementing Rules and Regulations (IRR)** supplement the statute. *Partial coverage exists in `commercial-law/banking-finance.md` (bank AMLA compliance obligations, §157–163) — that module covers the banking-institution compliance layer only; this module covers the full AMLA doctrine including predicate offenses, AMLC authority, freeze orders, civil forfeiture, and prosecution.*

---

## Policy Objective and FATF Framework
AMLA implements the Philippines' obligations under the **Financial Action Task Force (FATF) Recommendations** — the international anti-money laundering and counter-terrorism financing (AML/CFT) standards. The Philippines was placed on the FATF **Gray List** (Jurisdictions under Increased Monitoring) in June 2021 and exited it in February 2023 following R.A. 11521 amendments and demonstrable enforcement progress. AMLA is interpreted in light of FATF guidance to the extent not inconsistent with the Philippine Constitution.

## Money Laundering Defined (§4)
The crime of money laundering is committed by any person who:
1. Transacts, acquires, receives, possesses, uses, converts, or transmits a **monetary instrument or property** knowing that it represents, involves, or relates to the **proceeds of any unlawful activity**; or
2. Attempts or conspires to commit the foregoing; or
3. Converts, transfers, or moves such monetary instrument or property to conceal or disguise its nature, source, location, disposition, or movement; or
4. Acquires or uses such property knowing it to be proceeds of an unlawful activity.

**Knowledge may be inferred** from circumstances — deliberate ignorance or "willful blindness" suffices; the defendant need not have actual subjective awareness that the predicate offense was committed.

## Predicate Offenses (§3(i))
Money laundering requires an underlying predicate (unlawful) offense. The AMLA's predicate-offense list includes:
- **Kidnapping for ransom** (RPC Art. 267)
- **Drug trafficking offenses** (R.A. 9165 — Comprehensive Dangerous Drugs Act)
- **Graft and corrupt practices** (R.A. 3019)
- **Plunder** (R.A. 7080)
- **Robbery and extortion** (RPC Arts. 294–295, 299–302)
- **Jueteng and masiao** (P.D. 1602)
- **Piracy** (R.A. 7659)
- **Qualified theft** (RPC Art. 310)
- **Swindling** (estafa, RPC Art. 315)
- **Smuggling** (Tariff and Customs Code)
- **Electronic commerce violations** (R.A. 8792)
- **Hijacking / destructive arson / murder** (RPC)
- **Fraudulent practices under the SRC** (R.A. 8799)
- **Terrorism and terrorism financing** (R.A. 9372; R.A. 11479 — Human Security Act)
- **Trafficking in persons** (R.A. 9208)
- **Child pornography** (R.A. 9775)
- **Bribery** (RPC Arts. 210–212)
- **Malversation of public funds** (RPC Art. 217)
- **Tax evasion** — added by R.A. 10365 (2013 amendments)
- **Illegal gambling** (P.D. 1602)
- **Carnapping** (R.A. 6539)
- **Illegal logging** (P.D. 705)
- **Illegal fishing** (R.A. 8550)
- **Environmental crimes** (as enumerated by the AMLC Regulatory Issuances)

*Critical note*: Tax evasion as a predicate offense was added in 2013. Prior to the R.A. 10365 amendment, a money laundering charge could not be based on proceeds of tax evasion — this matters for historical cases and pre-2013 transactions.

## Covered Institutions (§3(a), as amended by R.A. 10927 and R.A. 11521)

**Banks and Bank-like Entities**: All banks, offshore banking units (OBUs), quasi-banks, trust entities, non-stock savings and loan associations, pawnshops, and other similar financial institutions.

**Insurance Sector**: Insurance companies, reinsurance companies, insurance agents, pre-need companies, health maintenance organizations, and mutual benefit associations.

**Securities Sector**: Securities dealers, brokers, salesmen, investment houses, investment agents, custodians, clearing agencies, exchanges, corporations generating investments from the public, venture capital companies, government securities dealers and brokers, and transfer companies.

**Other Institutions** (expanded under R.A. 10365 and R.A. 11521): Foreign exchange dealers/money changers; remittance/transfer companies; financing companies and lending companies; real estate developers and brokers (in high-value transactions); dealers in precious metals/stones; jewelers; casinos (including internet- and ship-based casinos, under R.A. 10927); offshore gaming operators; lawyers/accountants as **DNFBPs** (Designated Non-Financial Businesses and Professions) for qualifying transactions (certain real estate transactions, company management, trust management).

*Note on DNFBPs and lawyers*: Lawyers acting as DNFBPs (e.g., holding client funds in trust in real estate transactions) have AMLA compliance obligations. This interacts with CPRA Canon VI (confidentiality): the AMLA disclosure obligation for reportable transactions overrides attorney-client confidentiality in the specific transactions enumerated — see `legal-ethics/professional-responsibility.md`, Rule 6.03(5).

## Covered Transactions vs. Suspicious Transactions

**Covered Transaction (§3(b))**: A single transaction in cash or other equivalent monetary instrument involving an amount in excess of **₱500,000.00** (five hundred thousand pesos) or its equivalent in foreign currency, whether done in a single transaction or in multiple transactions that appear to be linked within one banking day. Covered institutions must report CTRs to the AMLC within **5 banking days** of the transaction.

**Suspicious Transaction (§3(b-1))**: A transaction regardless of amount, where any of the following circumstances exist:
1. There is no underlying legal or trade obligation, purpose, or economic justification.
2. The client is not properly identified.
3. The amount is not commensurate with the business or financial capacity of the client.
4. The transaction is structured to avoid AMLA reporting (structuring/smurfing).
5. Any circumstance suggesting the funds constitute proceeds of a predicate offense.

STRs (Suspicious Transaction Reports) must be filed within **5 banking days** of determination of suspicion. The decision to file an STR is that of the covered institution's compliance officer — the AMLC does not need to instruct the covered institution to file.

**Structuring** (§3(b-1)(iv)): The deliberate breaking up of transactions to fall below the ₱500,000 threshold to avoid CTR filing is itself a suspicious transaction and is independently criminalized under AMLA.

## AMLC (Anti-Money Laundering Council)

### Composition
The AMLC is an inter-agency body composed of:
- **Governor of the Bangko Sentral ng Pilipinas** (Chair)
- **Commissioner of the Insurance Commission** (Member)
- **Chairman of the Securities and Exchange Commission** (Member)

The AMLC has its own Secretariat headed by an Executive Director. Policy authority rests with the three-member Council; operational authority is exercised through the Secretariat.

### Powers and Functions

**Inquiry into bank deposits without court order** (§11, as amended): In cases involving predicate offenses listed in §3(i), the AMLC may, by majority vote, inquire into or examine deposits, placements, trust accounts, assets, and records maintained by a covered institution *without a court order* where the predicate offense is: kidnapping for ransom; drug trafficking; hijacking; destructive arson; murder; violations of firearms laws; or terrorism. For other predicate offenses, a court order from the Court of Appeals (not the RTC) is required.

**Freeze Orders (§10, as amended)**: Upon a verified ex parte petition by the AMLC to the **Court of Appeals** — the CA (not the RTC) has exclusive original jurisdiction. The CA may issue a freeze order without notice to the respondent (ex parte) if the AMLC establishes probable cause that the monetary instrument or property is related to the proceeds of a predicate offense. A freeze order is effective for **20 days** initially and may be extended by the CA upon application. After the initial 20-day period, the AMLC must file a civil forfeiture petition or secure a 6-month extension (maximum) from the CA or the frozen assets are released.

**Civil Forfeiture (§12)**: The AMLC may file a petition for civil forfeiture — the **in rem** proceeding against the property itself, not against a person — before the **Regional Trial Court**. Forfeiture does not require a prior criminal conviction; it proceeds on a preponderance-of-evidence standard showing the property is proceeds of a predicate offense. A criminal conviction strengthens the forfeiture case but is not a prerequisite.

**Financial Intelligence (§7(5))**: The AMLC monitors and analyzes suspicious transactions reported by covered institutions; maintains a financial intelligence database; may refer cases to the Department of Justice (DOJ) or Office of the Ombudsman for criminal prosecution; may enter into cooperative agreements with foreign jurisdictions.

## Safe Harbor and Duty Not to Tip Off

**Safe harbor (§30)**: A covered institution, its directors, officers, and employees that report covered or suspicious transactions in good faith are not subject to civil, criminal, or administrative liability for the disclosure, notwithstanding any law to the contrary (including bank secrecy under R.A. 1405). The safe harbor is conditional on good faith — it does not protect a covered institution that files a false or fabricated STR.

**No tipping off (§9(c))**: A covered institution and its personnel are prohibited from communicating to any person, including the account holder, that an STR or CTR has been filed, or that a AMLC investigation has been initiated. Violation of the no-tipping-off prohibition is a criminal offense under AMLA.

## Criminal Penalties (§14, as amended)
| Offense | Imprisonment | Fine |
|---|---|---|
| Money laundering (basic) | 7–14 years | Not less than ₱3 million nor more than twice the value of the monetary instrument |
| Failure to report CTR/STR (covered institution) | 6 months–4 years | Not less than ₱100,000 nor more than ₱500,000 per transaction |
| No tipping off violation | 3–8 years | |
| False reporting | 6 months–4 years + fine | |

Offenses under AMLA are not bailable if the penalty exceeds 6 years imprisonment (under Philippine bail law as to capital offenses) — but money laundering as charged is generally bailable under the ordinary rules.

**Legal persons**: Covered institutions themselves may be criminally liable under AMLA for failure to comply; corporate officers responsible for the compliance failure share personal liability.

## Customer Due Diligence and KYC
Covered institutions must establish and maintain a CDD program including:
1. **Customer identification** — verify identity at account opening and for large transactions; obtain beneficial ownership information.
2. **Enhanced due diligence (EDD)** for high-risk customers: Politically Exposed Persons (PEPs), customers from high-risk jurisdictions, unusual transaction profiles.
3. **Simplified due diligence** for low-risk customers (e.g., government entities, listed companies) — reduced verification measures.
4. **Ongoing monitoring** — keep CDD information current; monitor transactions against expected profile.
5. **Record-keeping** — all CDD records and transaction documents must be retained for **5 years** from the date of the transaction or from the end of the account relationship, whichever is later.

## Relationship to Bank Secrecy Laws
R.A. 1405 (Secrecy of Bank Deposits): The AMLA creates a specific exception — AMLC may inquire into deposits without court order for enumerated predicate offenses (see §11 above). For non-enumerated predicate offenses, a CA order is required. Bank secrecy does not bar AMLA reporting obligations — the safe harbor provision (§30) expressly overrides secrecy requirements for AMLA disclosures. Full treatment of R.A. 1405 and its other exceptions (depositor consent, court order in specified cases, impeachment, anti-graft) is in `commercial-law/banking-finance.md`.

## Anti-Hallucination Guardrails — Anti-Money Laundering
Tax evasion became a predicate offense only in **2013 (R.A. 10365)** — do not apply it to pre-2013 transactions. The freeze order petition goes to the **Court of Appeals**, not the RTC — the CA has exclusive original jurisdiction over freeze order petitions; never cite the RTC for this. Civil forfeiture is an **in rem** proceeding before the **RTC** — it does not require a prior criminal conviction; do not state that conviction is required before forfeiture can proceed. The threshold for a covered transaction is **₱500,000 or more** (single-day aggregate for linked transactions) — never state a different threshold or omit the linked-transaction rule. The safe harbor **requires good faith** — it does not protect false or fabricated reports. Do not state that the AMLC can freeze assets directly without a court order — the AMLC must petition the CA for a freeze order; the 20-day ex parte freeze is a CA order, not a unilateral AMLC action. Casinos became covered institutions only under **R.A. 10927 (2017)** — do not apply AMLA casino obligations to pre-2017 facts.
