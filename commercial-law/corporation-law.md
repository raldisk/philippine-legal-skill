# Corporation Law

## Primary Sources
**Revised Corporation Code (R.A. 11232, eff. Feb 23 2019)** — supersedes B.P. 68 except for pending B.P. 68-era disputes. **Securities Regulation Code (R.A. 8799)** — public companies, proxy, tender offers. **SEC Memorandum Circulars** — quasi-legislative. **Corporation Code IRR** — SEC issuance.

---

## Nature and Classification
**Defined (§2 RCC)**: artificial being, created by law, right of succession, powers expressly authorized or incidental. Core attributes: juridical personality, limited liability, transferable shares, perpetual or fixed term succession.

**Stock vs. non-stock**: Stock corp — capital divided into shares; may declare dividends. Non-stock — no shares, no dividends; organized for charitable, religious, educational, professional, civic, or similar purposes; governed by members, not stockholders.

**Close corporation**: AOI restricts share transfers; maximum 20 stockholders; no public offering; no exchange listing. Stockholders may directly manage without formal board if AOI so provides. Stockholders' action in lieu of meeting valid if unanimous written consent.

**One Person Corporation (OPC)**: Single stockholder only; must be a natural person, trust, or estate — NOT another corporation. Sole stockholder = sole director = president. Must file annual report to SEC. Cannot be converted into a close corporation. Banks, quasi-banks, pre-need companies, trust companies, insurance companies, public companies, and natural persons licensed to practice a profession CANNOT form OPC.

**Foreign corporations**: Must obtain SEC license to do business in the Philippines. "Doing business" defined under R.A. 7042 (FIA); isolated transactions do not constitute doing business. Representative offices and branches: representative offices — no income-generating activities; branches — may earn income, taxed on Philippine-sourced income. A foreign corporation doing business without license cannot sue (exception: enforce an isolated transaction or defend a suit).

**Government-owned or controlled corporations (GOCCs)**: May be organized under RCC or by special charter. If under special charter, Governance Commission for GOCCs (GCG) has jurisdiction.

## Incorporation
**Incorporators**: Minimum 2, maximum 15 natural persons — except OPC (1) and educational institutions governed by special law. All must be of legal age; majority must be residents of the Philippines. Corporations, associations, and partnerships CANNOT be incorporators under RCC (changed from B.P. 68).

**Articles of Incorporation (AOI)**: Must state: corporate name; purpose(s); principal place of business; term (may be perpetual); authorized capital stock; names, nationalities, and residences of incorporators; names, nationalities, and residences of initial directors/trustees; initial capital contribution.

**Minimum paid-in capital**: ₱5,000 for most corporations; higher requirements under special laws (banks, insurance, financing companies, etc.). No par value shares may be issued at any price fixed by the board — no stated par value in AOI but stated capital must be stated.

**Certificate of Incorporation**: SEC issues upon compliance; corporate existence begins on the date of issuance. **Nunc pro tunc** effect: once issued, the certificate is deemed evidence of organization from the time all requirements are met.

**De facto corporation**: Corporation operating under colorable compliance with incorporation requirements (defective but not fraudulent), relying on good faith. Its existence cannot be collaterally attacked — only the State can question it via quo warranto.

**Corporation by estoppel**: All persons who assume to act as a corporation knowing it has no authority are solidarily liable as general partners. Third parties who dealt with the ostensible corporation as if it were one are estopped from denying corporate status.

## Corporate Powers
**Express + implied powers**: Enumerated in RCC §36 + AOI purposes. All acts reasonably necessary to carry out express powers are implied.

**Ultra vires**: Acts beyond express and implied powers. Distinguished from illegal acts — ultra vires acts may be ratified by all stockholders; illegal acts cannot. *Montelibano v. Bacolod-Murcia Milling* (GR L-15092): corporation estopped if it has received the benefit. Courts generally uphold UVA to protect innocent third parties.

**Acts requiring board approval only**: Ordinary contracts, operational decisions, cash dividends, employment of officers.

**Acts requiring 2/3 stockholder approval**: Amend AOI; increase/decrease authorized capital stock; sell/dispose all or substantially all assets not in ordinary course; merger or consolidation; invest in another corporation/purpose; declare stock dividends; extend/shorten corporate term; dissolve voluntarily.

## Corporate Meetings
**Annual stockholders' meeting (§49 RCC)**: Required; date fixed in by-laws; purpose: elect directors, consider annual reports. Notice: 21 days for regular; 1 week for special (or as by-laws provide).

**Special stockholders' meeting**: For extraordinary matters; notice must state purpose.

**Quorum**: Majority of outstanding capital stock for most matters; 2/3 for fundamental corporate changes. By-laws may require higher quorum.

**Board meetings**: Majority of number of directors constitutes quorum; decisions require majority vote of quorum.

**Written assent / action without meeting**: Valid if all directors consent in writing; minutes must be filed with corporate records.

**Proxy**: Stockholder may appoint proxy in writing, signed, filed with corporate secretary before meeting. Proxy valid for the meeting specified and any adjournment — not beyond 5 years unless coupled with interest.

## Board of Directors
**Composition**: Minimum 5, maximum 15 directors (stock corps). Minimum 5 trustees (non-stock). Elected annually by holders of voting shares. Independent directors mandatory for publicly-held corps and SRC-covered companies — minimum 2 or 20% of board, whichever is higher.

**Term**: 1-year term; hold-over doctrine — directors hold office until successors are elected and qualified even if term has expired. Board must fill vacancies by majority vote if less than quorum lost; stockholder election required if quorum is lost.

**Business Judgment Rule (BJR)**: Courts defer to good-faith, informed business decisions made within authority. BJR does not protect fraud, bad faith, gross negligence, or self-dealing transactions not properly approved.

**Fiduciary duties**: (1) Obedience — act within authority; (2) Diligence — ordinarily prudent person in similar position standard; (3) Loyalty — act in best interest of corporation; disclose and avoid conflicts of interest; no self-dealing except by approval of disinterested directors/stockholders.

**Doctrine of Corporate Opportunity**: Director/officer discovering a business opportunity belonging to the corporation (within its line of business, which it has interest or expectancy in) must first offer it to the corporation before taking it personally. Taking such opportunity without disclosure gives the corporation the right to claim it.

**Liability of directors**: Generally no personal liability for corporate acts. Exception: vote for acts in violation of RCC or existing laws → solidarily liable to the corporation for resulting damages. Specific RCC violations: issuance of watered stock; payment of illegal dividends; acquiring own shares in violation of RCC; abandoning the corporation; entering into grossly disadvantageous contracts.

## Capital Structure and Stockholder Rights
**Authorized vs. outstanding**: Authorized = maximum shares AOI allows. Subscribed = shares subscribed whether or not fully paid. Outstanding = issued and fully paid + subscribed but unpaid. Treasury shares = repurchased by corp; not cancelled; no voting rights; no dividends; counted as authorized but unissued for quorum purposes.

**Classes of shares**: Common (full voting, dividend rights); Preferred (priority in dividends or assets; may be non-voting); Redeemable (corporation or holder may redeem on terms fixed at issue); Founder's shares (first refusal on management positions for limited period — not exceeding 5 years, SEC subject). Par value and no-par value shares both recognized.

**Watered stock**: Shares issued for grossly overvalued consideration (inflated property, services, promissory notes). Directors voting for issuance: solidarily liable to the corporation and creditors for deficiency.

**Stockholder rights**: (1) Vote on matters requiring stockholder action; (2) Dividends when declared; (3) Inspect books and records during reasonable business hours; (4) Pre-emptive right — pro-rata participation in new shares to maintain proportionate interest; may be denied in AOI or for shares issued in consideration for previously issued corporate securities, shares subject to employee stock options, and other exceptions; (5) Appraisal right — demand fair value of shares upon dissenting from fundamental changes (amendment of AOI restricting rights; sale/disposition of all or substantially all corporate assets; merger or consolidation); (6) Transfer shares freely unless restricted in AOI for close corps; (7) Derivative suit.

**Derivative suit**: Filed by stockholder on behalf of the corporation to enforce corporate rights which the corporation itself refuses to enforce. Requirements under Interim Rules (A.M. No. 01-2-04-SC): (1) stockholder at time of wrong; (2) exhaustion of intra-corporate remedies; (3) suit not collusive; (4) no appraisal right available for the wrong. Corporation is an indispensable party (nominal defendant).

**Piercing the corporate veil**: Disregard separate personality when used to defeat public convenience, justify wrong, protect fraud, or defend crime; or when fiction is used as alter ego/conduit/instrument. *PNB v. Ritratto Group* (GR 134767): three-element test — (1) complete domination/control; (2) control used to commit fraud/wrong; (3) control and wrong proximately caused injury. Mere ownership of shares, even 100%, does not pierce. Parent-subsidiary relationship alone insufficient.

**Trust fund doctrine**: Subscribed capital stock is trust fund for creditors. Cannot be returned to stockholders or distributed as profits unless creditors are paid or capital surplus available. Distribution of assets without clearing creditors imposes liability on directors. *Ong Yong v. Tiu* (GR 144476): Trust fund doctrine prohibits distribution of corporate assets in preference to creditors.

## Dividends
**Cash dividends**: Declared by board alone when unrestricted retained earnings available. No stockholder approval required.

**Stock dividends**: Require 2/3 vote of outstanding capital stock; issued from unappropriated retained earnings or stock dividend appropriation.

**Property dividends**: Rare; board declares; must be from unrestricted retained earnings.

**Unrestricted retained earnings (URE)**: Corporation cannot retain earnings > 100% of paid-in capital unless justified by: definite corporate expansion plans; debt covenant; legal restriction; or other SEC-approved circumstances. Failure: improperly accumulated earnings tax under NIRC.

## Dissolution and Liquidation
**Voluntary**: (1) Expiration of term; (2) Affirmative vote of 2/3 outstanding capital stock + board resolution; (3) Short-form for corps without operations or assets after 3 years of non-operation.

**Involuntary (SEC-initiated)**: Non-use of franchise; 5-year continuous inoperation; fraud; RCC violations; trust fund doctrine violations; failure to file annual reports for 3 consecutive years; no legal officer.

**3-year winding up period**: Dissolved corporation continues as juridical person for 3 years to wind up affairs; after 3 years, trustees or receivers may continue for the limited purpose of completing winding up.

**Liquidation priority**: (1) Government taxes and fees; (2) Secured creditors; (3) Unsecured creditors; (4) Preferred stockholders; (5) Common stockholders.

---

## Mergers and Consolidations
**Merger**: Absorbed corporation ceases to exist; surviving corporation acquires ALL rights, obligations, and liabilities by operation of law. No deed of assignment needed for individual assets.

**Consolidation**: All constituent corps dissolve; new consolidated corporation emerges.

**Requirements**: (1) Plan of merger/consolidation; (2) Approval by 2/3 vote of outstanding capital stock of each constituent corp + board approval; (3) Filing with SEC; (4) SEC approval (public companies: also PSE/SRC clearance). Becomes effective upon SEC approval.

**Creditor protection**: Dissenting creditors may enforce claims against surviving/consolidated corp. Claims of each constituent corporation's creditors are preserved.

## Corporate Rehabilitation
**Financial Rehabilitation and Insolvency Act (FRIA, R.A. 10142)**: Governs rehabilitation and insolvency of juridical persons. Court-supervised rehabilitation; pre-negotiated; out-of-court or informal restructuring. Rehabilitation Receiver appointed to rehabilitate debtor corporation. Automatic stay upon filing of petition.

## Anti-Hallucination Guardrails — Corporation Law
Never cite B.P. 68 as governing for acts after Feb 23 2019 — R.A. 11232 controls. Minimum incorporators under RCC is 2 (not 5); B.P. 68 required 5. OPC may only be formed by a natural person, trust, or estate — not another corporation; banks and other regulated entities cannot form OPC. BJR does not shield fraud, bad faith, or gross negligence. Stock dividends require 2/3 stockholder approval; cash dividends require board approval only. Piercing requires all three PNB elements — never pierce merely because of 100% ownership or parent-subsidiary relationship. Pre-emptive right may be restricted or denied in AOI — always check. Appraisal right is the remedy for dissenting stockholders; derivative suit is for corporate wrongs; these are distinct. Merger operates by operation of law — no separate assignment of individual assets is needed. De facto corporation cannot be collaterally attacked; only quo warranto can challenge it. Three-year winding-up period is for completing dissolution, not for ordinary business operations.

## Corporate Governance — Publicly Listed Companies
**SEC Memorandum Circular No. 19 (2016) — Code of Corporate Governance for PLCs**: Applies to all corporations covered by SRC; OTC-listed and exchange-listed. Mandatory compliance; annual compliance report to SEC.

**Board responsibilities**: Strategic direction; risk oversight; internal audit; external auditor appointment; related-party transaction (RPT) approval. Board must meet at least once quarterly.

**Independent directors**: Must be free of business or personal relationship with the company; no stockholding beyond de minimis threshold; no current or prior employment in the company or its subsidiaries within 3 years. Maximum term: 9 consecutive years; thereafter must have a 2-year cooling-off period.

**Board committees (mandatory for PLCs)**: Audit committee (majority independent; chaired by independent director); Corporate Governance committee; Board Risk Oversight committee; Related Party Transaction committee (for covered companies).

**Related Party Transactions**: RPTs must be disclosed; material RPTs require board/stockholder approval; independent directors must evaluate arm's-length nature. SEC MC No. 10 (2019) — requires RPT policy for PLCs.

**Audit committee**: Oversees financial reporting; external auditor independence; internal audit function; risk management. External auditor rotation: SEC requires mandatory audit firm rotation for PLCs (verify current period under SEC circulars).

## Foreign Investment Restrictions
**FIA (R.A. 7042 as amended by R.A. 11647)**: Foreign equity limits apply to nationalized and partially nationalized industries. **Foreign Investment Negative List (FINL)**: Lists areas of investment restricted to Filipino nationals (List A: constitutional/statutory restrictions; List B: defense, health, small enterprises).

**Constitutional limits**: No foreign equity in mass media (except recording); advertising (30% max); practice of professions (regulated by professional laws); retail trade (subject to R.A. 11595); exploration of natural resources (60% Filipino minimum); public utilities (60% Filipino minimum under R.A. 11659 amended definition).

**Grandfather Rule vs. Control Test**: Grandfather rule: trace nationality through chain of ownership to determine Filipino equity percentage. Control test: if 60% of the outstanding capital stock is owned by Filipinos, the corporation is considered Filipino regardless of board composition. *Gamboa v. Teves* (GR 176579, 2012): "capital" in public utilities means voting stock, not all classes of shares — Filipinos must own at least 60% of voting shares.

## Quo Warranto
**State action**: Only the Solicitor General or a person with direct interest may bring quo warranto against a corporation. Questions: Was the corporation validly formed? Does the corporation exercise a franchise it was not granted?

**Distinction**: Quo warranto questions de jure existence; collateral attack questions de facto existence — latter not allowed.

---

## SEC Jurisdiction
**SEC (R.A. 8799, §5)**: Intra-corporate controversies involving corporations, partnerships, and associations registered with or supervised by SEC transferred to Regional Trial Courts (designated as commercial courts) under R.A. 8799 §5.2 and SC A.M. No. 01-2-04.

**Intra-corporate disputes**: Between/among stockholders; between stockholders and corporation; between corporation and officer; election contests; inspection right enforcement; dividends; dissolution. Jurisdiction: designated commercial courts (formerly SEC).

**SEC retains jurisdiction**: Registration/revocation of registration; enforcement of SRC; investigation of securities violations; licensing of securities intermediaries.

---

## Rehabilitation and Insolvency
**FRIA (R.A. 10142)**: Governs rehabilitation and insolvency of juridical persons. Court-supervised rehabilitation; pre-negotiated rehabilitation; out-of-court or informal restructuring agreement (OCRA). Upon filing, automatic stay halts all enforcement actions against the debtor.

**Rehabilitation Receiver**: Appointed by court; takes custody of management; must be independent; acts as officer of the court. Board retains residual oversight but cannot act contrary to receiver's powers.

**Liquidation (FRIA §§90–132)**: Filed by debtor or creditors. Liquidator appointed; assets marshalled; claims adjudicated; priority: secured creditors (to extent of security) → labor claims → government taxes → unsecured creditors → stockholders.

---

## Examination of Corporate Books
**Right to inspect (§73 RCC)**: Stockholder or member may personally or through a representative examine records during reasonable hours on business days. Records covered: articles, by-laws, minutes, stock and transfer books, financial statements.

**Limitations**: Must be for legitimate purpose; demand must be in writing with stated purpose. Corporation may refuse if purpose is unlawful, harmful, or for a competitor's benefit — refusal must state reason. *Gokongwei v. SEC* (GR L-45911): inspection right is a fundamental stockholder right; restrictions must be narrowly construed.

**Penalty for refusal**: Officer/agent refusing valid inspection: administrative sanctions under SEC; possible criminal liability. Mandamus lies to compel inspection.

---

## Election Contests
**Jurisdiction**: Designated commercial courts (RTC). Rules of Procedure on Corporate Rehabilitation (A.M. No. 08-8-7-SC) and Interim Rules on Intra-corporate Controversies govern.

**Election inspector**: SEC does not directly supervise corporate elections unless voluntarily requested. Election protests filed with the commercial court which may appoint election inspector or set aside election results.

**Hold-over directors**: Continue in office until successors elected and qualified. Courts may appoint interim directors when no quorum exists to hold elections (*Valle Verde Country Club v. Africa*, GR 151969).
