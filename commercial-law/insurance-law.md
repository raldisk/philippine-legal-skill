# Insurance Law

## Primary Sources
**Insurance Code (P.D. 612 as amended by R.A. 10607, 2013)** — governs insurance contracts, insurance companies, HMOs, pre-need companies. **Insurance Commission (IC)** — regulatory authority; issues circulars; supervises insurers.

*Note (C-01 resolution)*: This file covers Insurance Code doctrine as part of the commercial law domain. For further expansion into life/non-life/mutual benefit sub-domains, a dedicated `insurance-law/` folder may be instantiated in a future phase.

## Insurance Contract — Elements
**Defined (§2[1])**: Agreement whereby one undertakes for a consideration to indemnify another against loss, damage, or liability from an unknown or contingent event.

**Essential elements**:
1. **Insurable interest** — must exist; without it, contract is a wager and void.
2. **Contingent risk** — must be uncertain/unknown, not a certainty.
3. **Premium** — consideration; non-payment avoids the contract unless credit extended, binding receipt issued, or condition waived.
4. **Meeting of minds** — offer and acceptance on coverage terms.

**Insurable interest — life**: Unlimited in own life; also in life of spouse/child; anyone on whom insured depends for education/support; anyone under legal obligation to insured; anyone on whose life an estate or interest depends.

**Insurable interest — property**: Legal or equitable interest in property; must stand to suffer direct pecuniary loss from its destruction.

---

## Concealment, Misrepresentation, Warranty
**Concealment (§§26–27)**: Neglect to communicate material information. Entitles insurer to rescind. Materiality test: would the insurer have issued the policy on same terms had it known?

**Misrepresentation (§§45–46)**: False/misleading material statement. If material and fraudulent → insurer may rescind. **Incontestability clause (life insurance)**: After 2 years from policy issuance, life insurance policy becomes incontestable except for non-payment of premium and violation of policy conditions relating to military/naval service in time of war.

**Warranty (§67)**: Statement in policy that some fact is true or certain acts will/will not be done. Breach of warranty voids the policy — no materiality requirement (stricter than misrepresentation).

---

## Classes of Insurance
**Non-life (property and casualty)**: Fire, marine, casualty, surety, fidelity, motor vehicle, CTPL. **Indemnity principle** applies strictly — insured cannot recover more than actual loss.

**Life insurance**: Not a contract of indemnity — face amount payable regardless of actual economic loss. Proceeds exempt from creditors of the beneficiary (§53).

**Compulsory third-party liability (CTPL)**: Mandatory for all motor vehicles. Insurer directly liable to injured third party regardless of insured's insolvency.

---

## Loss and Claims
**Subrogation (§235)**: Insurer, upon payment of loss, subrogated to insured's rights against the wrongdoer. Right is lost if insured releases the wrongdoer after payment by insurer. *Manila Mahogany v. CA*: unconditional release by insured defeats insurer's subrogation.

**Double insurance**: Same insured, same interest, same risk, several insurers. Total premiums must not exceed insurable interest. Each insurer liable only pro-rata.

**Over-insurance**: Insured may not recover more than actual loss from all insurers combined (indemnity contracts only; not applicable to life insurance).

---

## Regulation
**Capitalization**: R.A. 10607 raised capitalization requirements. Thresholds periodically updated by IC circulars — verify current amounts.

**Solvency reserves**: Insurers must maintain prescribed reserve funds. IC may rehabilitate or liquidate insolvent insurers.

**IC jurisdiction**: IC has primary jurisdiction over insurance matters. Courts retain jurisdiction over contract interpretation and fundamental rights issues.

## Anti-Hallucination Guardrails — Insurance Law
Incontestability clause applies only to life insurance after 2 years — non-life policies remain contestable on material misrepresentation throughout. Life insurance is NOT an indemnity contract — the face amount is payable regardless of economic loss. Insurable interest for life insurance must exist at contract inception; for property insurance, at time of loss. Warranty breach requires no showing of materiality — distinguished from misrepresentation. Subrogation is lost if insured releases wrongdoer after insurer has paid. Never cite specific peso capitalization thresholds from memory — IC circulars update these; direct to current IC issuances.

## The Insurance Contract — Formation and Interpretation
**Offer and acceptance**: Application by insured = offer; issuance of policy = acceptance. **Binding receipt**: Issued pending underwriting; provides temporary coverage. **Cover note (§52 IC)**: Temporary insurance pending issuance of policy; not to exceed 60 days; may not be renewed.

**Interpretation**: In case of ambiguity, construed against insurer (contra proferentem); insured must prove loss within coverage; insurer must prove exclusion applies. *Fieldmen's Insurance v. Vda. de Songco* (GR L-24833): exclusion clauses strictly construed against insurer.

**Reformation**: If policy does not reflect the parties' true agreement, reformation is available under NCC.

## Insurable Interest — Detailed Rules
**Property insurance — timing**: Insurable interest must exist both at time of contract AND at time of loss. Loss of interest (e.g., sale of property) between policy and loss: no recovery. *Cha v. Court of Appeals* (GR 124520): A bank with mortgage has insurable interest in the mortgaged property; mortgagor and mortgagee may both insure independently.

**Life insurance — timing**: Insurable interest must exist only at inception (time of contract); need not exist at time of death. This is why a creditor can validly insure a debtor's life even if debt is later paid.

**Insurable interest in life — extent**: (1) Unlimited in own life; (2) Unlimited in spouse's life; (3) Unlimited in child's life; (4) Anyone on whom insured is dependent for education or support; (5) Anyone under legal obligation to insured; (6) Anyone on whose life any estate or business interest depends.

## Concealment, Misrepresentation, and Warranty
**Concealment (§§26–27 IC)**: Neglect to communicate material information. Even innocent or negligent concealment entitles insurer to rescind. **Test of materiality**: Would the insurer have refused the policy or imposed different terms had it known? Objective test — not what insurer would subjectively have done.

**Oral misrepresentation**: Misrepresentation need not be in writing to vitiate the policy.

**Misrepresentation in life insurance — incontestability**: Policy incontestable after 2 years from date of issue or last reinstatement, except for: (1) non-payment of premium; (2) violation of conditions relating to military or naval service in time of war. Incontestability bars insurer from questioning truthfulness of statements after 2 years — not from denying coverage on other grounds.

**Warranty (§§67–71 IC)**: Express warranty = statement in policy that a particular fact is true; strictly interpreted; any breach voids policy regardless of materiality (distinguished from misrepresentation). Implied warranty in marine insurance: seaworthiness; legality of voyage.

---

## Loss, Notice, and Claims
**Notice of loss**: Policy must specify deadline; IC §§88–91: notice as soon as practicable; proof of loss within time stated (usually 60–90 days). Failure to give timely notice/proof: insurer may deny claim unless waiver or estoppel applies.

**Proof of loss**: Documents establishing fact and amount of loss; preliminary and final proofs required by policy.

**Denial and payment deadline**: Insurer must pay or deny within the period prescribed by IC and policy (usually 30 days from receipt of proof); failure to pay within period → entitles insured to interest at legal rate + attorney's fees. *White Gold Marine Services v. Pioneer Insurance* (GR 154514): Insurer who delays without valid ground is liable for damages and attorney's fees.

---

## Subrogation and Double Insurance
**Subrogation (§236 IC)**: Insurer who pays indemnity is subrogated to the rights of the insured against the wrongdoer. Insured cannot impair insurer's subrogation right after payment. Release by insured → insured must reimburse insurer to extent of subrogation right destroyed.

**Exceptions to subrogation**: Life insurance (no indemnity principle); insurer was negligent; insured released wrongdoer before insurer paid (insurer not yet subrogated).

**Double insurance (§95 IC)**: Same risk, same interest, same insured, multiple insurers. Not prohibited (unlike overinsurance which is). Each insurer liable only for its proportion of the loss (contribution principle). Insured must disclose existence of other policies.

**Overinsurance**: Insured recovers only actual loss even if total face amounts of all policies exceed loss — indemnity principle caps recovery. Insured cannot profit from loss.

## Non-Life Insurance — Selected Topics
**Fire insurance**: Valued or open policy; valued = face amount is agreed value of property at issue; open = actual value at time of loss determined at loss. Friendly fire vs. hostile fire: friendly fire (fire in its intended place) not covered; hostile fire (out of intended place) is covered.

**Marine insurance**: Governed by IC §§99–166; covers hull, cargo, freight, profits, liability. Implied warranties: seaworthiness at inception; proper handling; no deviation from agreed route; legality of voyage.

**Motor vehicle — CTPL (R.A. 10607)**: Compulsory coverage; insurer directly liable to victim regardless of insured's insolvency; no-fault clause for death/bodily injury up to prescribed limit; claims processed through direct settlement.

**Surety bonds**: IC §§175–183; surety is direct party, not merely guarantor; entitled to all defenses of principal debtor except insolvency; surety's liability co-extensive with principal, not accessory in the strict civil law sense.

---

## Life Insurance
**Designation and change of beneficiary**: Insured may change revocable beneficiary at any time without consent. Irrevocable beneficiary: cannot be changed without beneficiary's consent; insured cannot encumber or assign the policy without consent.

**Proceeds exempt from execution (§53 IC)**: Life insurance proceeds payable to a named beneficiary are exempt from creditors of the insured. Exception: proceeds payable to the insured's estate — subject to estate's creditors.

**Suicide (§§180–181 IC)**: Suicide during first 2 years: insurer not liable unless policy provides otherwise. After 2 years: death benefit payable even if death by suicide.

**Policy loans**: Life policies with cash value; insured may borrow up to cash surrender value; unpaid loan reduces proceeds.

---

## HMOs and Pre-Need Plans
**HMOs**: Regulated by IC (post-Philhealth Law amendment); governed by IC §§187-188 and DOH regulations. HMO plans are not insurance per se but are regulated similarly under IC oversight.

**Pre-need plans**: Formerly SEC-regulated; transferred to IC. Plans for education, pension, memorial. Subject to trust fund requirements and IC licensing.

---

## IC Jurisdiction and Remedies
**IC (§§439–441 IC)**: IC has primary regulatory jurisdiction over insurance companies, pre-need companies, HMOs. Complaints for unfair claims practices, policy violations, and licensing filed with IC. IC decisions appealable to CA via Rule 43.

**Rehabilitation and liquidation**: IC may place insolvent insurer under conservatorship or rehabilitation; upon confirmed insolvency, liquidation under IC supervision with court participation.

## Anti-Hallucination Guardrails — Insurance Law
Incontestability (2-year rule) applies only to life insurance; non-life policies remain contestable throughout. Life insurance is NOT an indemnity contract — face amount payable regardless of economic loss. Insurable interest for life must exist at inception; for property, at both inception and loss. Warranty breach requires no materiality showing — breach alone voids (stricter than misrepresentation). Subrogation is lost if insured releases wrongdoer after insurer pays. Double insurance does not void the policies — contribution applies. Life insurance proceeds to named beneficiary are exempt from insured's creditors. Cover note period is 60 days maximum — not renewable. Never state specific capitalization thresholds — IC circulars update these.

## Reinsurance
**Nature**: Contract between insurer (cedant) and reinsurer; insurer cedes portion of risk. Reinsurance does not create privity between insured and reinsurer — insured's claim is solely against original insurer.

**Proportional reinsurance**: Reinsurer takes fixed percentage of risk and premium.

**Non-proportional reinsurance**: Reinsurer pays when loss exceeds a specified retention (excess of loss); reinsurer not involved in proportionate basis.

**Cut-through clause**: Provision allowing insured to claim directly against reinsurer; creates privity; must be expressly agreed.

---

## Unfair Claims Practices (§247 IC)
**Prohibited acts**: Knowingly misrepresenting pertinent facts; failing to acknowledge communications promptly; failing to adopt reasonable claims processing standards; not attempting in good faith to effect prompt settlement of claims where liability is clear; compelling insureds to institute litigation by unreasonably low offers.

**IC enforcement**: IC may revoke license, impose fines, or order restitution for unfair claims practices. IC administrative proceedings are independent of civil claims.

---

## Variable Insurance and Investment-Linked Products
**Variable life insurance and annuities (VUL)**: Have both insurance and investment components. Investment component: sub-accounts linked to separate fund portfolios; NAV-based. IC regulates; separate accounts not part of insurer's general fund; protected from insurer creditors.

**Disclosure requirements**: Insurer must disclose charges (cost of insurance, expense charges, surrender charges); investment risks clearly stated; investment component not guaranteed; illustration based on assumed growth rates (not guaranteed).

---

## Assessment Life and Mutual Benefit Associations (§§390–422 IC)
**Assessment associations**: Collect assessments from members to pay death benefits; no level premium; not required to maintain reserves like commercial insurers. Must be nonprofit. Regulated by IC but under different rules from commercial insurers.

**Fraternal benefit societies**: Operate on lodge system with ritualistic form; provide insurance to members and dependents; IC-regulated.

---

## Group Insurance
**Group life and health**: Issued to employer/association covering employees/members; individual members receive certificates. Coverage terms set by master policy; individual certificate is evidence of coverage. Conversion right: separated employees may convert group coverage to individual policy within specified period (usually 31 days) without evidence of insurability.

**Group health — PhilHealth coordination**: Private health insurance coordinates with PhilHealth; co-payment arrangements common; IC circulars govern minimum benefit requirements.

---

## Annuities
**Nature (§§178–179 IC)**: Contract in which insurer undertakes to pay periodic payments during a specified period (e.g., life of annuitant, fixed term, or joint lives). Not a life insurance contract strictly — no death benefit; income replacement function.

**Types**: Life annuity (payments cease at death); joint and survivor (continues to survivor); certain-and-continuous (guaranteed minimum period regardless of death). Lump-sum vs. periodic premium annuities.

**Tax treatment**: Annuity payments may have tax implications under NIRC; the exclusion ratio applies to amounts representing return of premium (not taxable); remainder is ordinary income.

**Variable annuities**: Investment risk borne by annuitant; payments vary with sub-account performance; IC-regulated; disclosure requirements same as VUL.

---

## Premium Payment and Reinstatement
**Premium — condition precedent**: Non-payment of premium renders policy void from its inception or terminates existing coverage depending on terms. Exception: credit extended; binding receipt; or estoppel (insurer accepted late payments as pattern of conduct).

**Grace period (§§77, 230 IC)**: Life insurance must include grace period of at least 30 days for premium payment; policy does not lapse during grace period; if insured dies during grace period, death benefit reduced by unpaid premium.

**Reinstatement (§§230 IC)**: Lapsed life policy may be reinstated within 2 years of lapse upon: payment of all overdue premiums + interest; satisfactory evidence of insurability; no outstanding policy loans in excess of cash value. Reinstatement restarts the 2-year incontestability period.
