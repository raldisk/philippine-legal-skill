# Partnership Law

## Primary Sources
**New Civil Code Arts. 1767–1867** — general partnerships (Title IX, Book IV). **Arts. 1843–1867** — limited partnerships. SEC registration required when capital > ₱3,000; non-registration does not void the partnership as to personal property.

---

## Nature and Formation
**Defined (Art. 1767)**: Two or more persons bind themselves to contribute money, property, or industry to a common fund with intent to divide profits. Partnership has juridical personality separate from partners.

**Essential elements**: (1) valid contract; (2) mutual contribution (money, property, or industry); (3) intent to divide profits; (4) lawful object.

**Partnership vs. co-ownership**: Co-ownership arises by law, inheritance, or acquisition without profit motive. Partnership requires intent to engage in business for profit. *Heirs of Jose Lim v. Lim*: intent is the key distinguishing element.

**Form requirements**: No special form generally. **Exception**: real property contributed → must be in public instrument (Art. 1771); failure makes the partnership void as to that real property but valid as to personal property contributions. For SEC: partnerships with capital > ₱3,000 must register.

---

## Classes
| Class | Features |
|---|---|
| General partnership | All partners unlimited personal liability |
| Limited partnership | ≥1 general partner (unlimited) + ≥1 limited partner (limited to contribution); name must include "Ltd." or "Limited"; SEC certificate required (Art. 1844) |
| Universal — all present property (Art. 1778) | Partners contribute all property currently owned |
| Universal — profits (Art. 1780) | Partners contribute profits only; present property remains personal |
| Particular (Art. 1783) | Definite things, specific undertaking, profession/vocation |

---

## Capital and Property
**Partnership capital**: Aggregate contributions. **Partnership property**: all property brought in or acquired in the course of business (Art. 1811) — distinct from partner's individual property.

**Partner's interest**: Share in profits and surplus — personal property even if partnership owns real estate.

---

## Rights and Obligations
**Profit/loss sharing (Art. 1797)**: Per agreement; otherwise profits per contribution ratio; losses same as profits. Industrial partner shares in profits but exempt from losses unless agreed otherwise.

**Management (Art. 1800)**: Each partner is an agent of the partnership absent stipulation. Acts of administration: any partner may act; acts of strict dominion (alienation, encumbrance): require all partners' consent.

**Duty of loyalty**: No competition with the partnership without consent; profits from competing business go to the common fund.

**General partner liability**: Solidarily liable with the partnership for all partnership obligations after partnership assets exhausted.

**Limited partner**: Liable only to the extent of contribution, provided no participation in management. Management → loss of limited liability.

---

## Dissolution and Winding Up
**Dissolution**: Change in association; does not terminate the partnership — winding up follows.

**Causes**: Expiration; accomplishment of purpose; withdrawal (at will if indefinite term); death/insolvency/civil interdiction/insanity of a partner; loss of specific thing promised as contribution; illegality; court decree.

**Priority on liquidation**: (1) outside creditors; (2) partner creditors (for debts owed by partnership to them); (3) capital contributions; (4) profits.

---

## Anti-Hallucination Guardrails — Partnership Law
Minimum partners is 2 (not 5). Art. 1771 public instrument requirement applies to the real property contribution specifically — the partnership can still exist as to personal property contributions even if void as to real property. Industrial partner exemption from losses is default — it may be contracted away. A limited partner who participates in management loses limited liability — this is a strict rule. Dissolution ≠ termination; winding up must occur. Never apply corporate veil-piercing doctrine directly to partnerships without noting that the relevant NCC provision governs partner liability, not RCC §22.
---

## Partnership Obligations to Third Parties
**Partner as agent (Art. 1818)**: Every partner is an agent of the partnership for carrying on its business. Acts within ordinary course bind the partnership. Acts outside ordinary course require consent of all partners.

**Admission of liability**: Admission by a partner concerning partnership affairs, made in the ordinary course of business, is evidence against the partnership.

**Partner's liability**: Partners are jointly liable for partnership contracts; jointly and severally liable for wrongful acts or breach of trust by a partner acting in ordinary course.

**Incoming partner**: Admits to existing partnership obligations but liable only to extent of share in assets — not personally liable for pre-admission debts unless the settlement agreement provides otherwise.

**Retiring partner**: Remains liable for pre-retirement obligations unless creditors agree to release. Must give adequate notice of retirement to avoid continued liability for new obligations.

---

## Dissolution — Grounds and Effects
**Dissolution by act of partners**: Any partner may dissolve at will if term is indefinite; dissolution must not be in breach of agreement. Dissolution in breach: breaching partner liable for damages; other partners may continue the business and claim damages.

**Dissolution by operation of law**: Death of partner; insolvency of partner or partnership; supervening illegality of business.

**Dissolution by court decree (Art. 1831)**: Partner is lunatic/unsound mind; partner incapable of performing duties; willful/persistent breach of partnership agreement; business can only be carried on at a loss; other equitable grounds.

**Effect on authority**: After dissolution, partners may bind the partnership only to wind up (not new business) except where: partner had no notice of dissolution and third party dealt in good faith; or the other party extended credit before dissolution without notice.

**Winding up — who may wind up**: Partners who have not wrongfully dissolved; legal representative of last surviving partner; court-appointed receiver.

---

## Liability of Partners in Detail
**General partner — solidary liability**: After exhaustion of partnership assets, creditors may proceed against individual general partners solidarily. *Muñasque v. CA* (GR L-39520): partnership creditors need not sue partners first; may go directly against general partners.

**Industrial partner (Art. 1797)**: Contributes industry (services); shares in profits; exempted from losses by default unless expressly agreed. Cannot engage in business for self without consent — profits inure to partnership.

**Managing partner (Art. 1800)**: If designated in articles, authority continues until legally removed; cannot revoke authority without just cause. If designated post-formation, revocable by majority vote.

---

## Limited Partnership — Technical Rules
**Certificate requirements (Art. 1844)**: Name; character of business; location; partner names/addresses; LP contributions; additional contributions on conditions; profit shares; right to substitute assignee; admission of partners; priority of partners; return of contributions rules; remaining rights on dissolution; duration.

**Strict rules for limited partners**: Must contribute actual capital (not services). Name must not appear in firm name (except if same as general partner's name or if LP knew name was used and did not object). Taking part in management forfeits limited liability.

**Assignment of LP interest**: LP may assign economic interest; assignee does not become a substituted LP without consent of all partners; assignee gets only economic rights (profits/distributions).

**Dissolution of LP**: On death, retirement, insanity, or insolvency of a general partner, unless remaining general partners or all partners agree to continue.

---

## Partnership vs. Joint Venture
**Joint venture**: Combination of property, capital, or skill for a single business transaction or series of related transactions; not intended to be a continuing partnership. *Aurbach v. Sanitary Wares* (GR 75875): joint ventures are governed by the law on partnerships. JVs for construction, mining, and infrastructure projects are common in Philippine practice.

**JV vs. partnership**: JV = limited purpose; partnership = general ongoing enterprise. Both create fiduciary duties among parties.

---

## Taxation of Partnerships
**General professional partnerships**: Not taxed as entity; income flows through to individual partners, each pays individual income tax.

**Business/commercial partnerships**: Taxed as corporations for NIRC purposes. Distributable shares of partners subject to final withholding tax.

**Joint venture for construction**: Exempt from corporate income tax if registered with PCAB and constituted for a specific project per NIRC §22(B).

---

## Anti-Hallucination Guardrails — Partnership Law
Minimum partners: 2 (not 5). Art. 1771 public instrument requirement applies to real property contribution specifically — partnership remains valid as to personal property contributions. Industrial partner exemption from losses is default — may be contracted away. Limited partner who participates in management loses limited liability — strict rule without equitable exception. Dissolution ≠ termination; winding up required. Partners are jointly liable for contracts but jointly AND severally for torts/breach of trust — distinguish. General professional partnerships flow through to partners; business partnerships taxed as corporations under NIRC. Never apply corporate veil-piercing doctrine directly to partnerships without citing the applicable NCC provision.

## Partnership Accounting and Financial Obligations
**Capital accounts**: Each partner maintains a capital account reflecting initial contribution, additional contributions, share of profits, and withdrawals. Drawings reduce capital; losses reduce capital; profits increase capital.

**Right to accounting (Art. 1809)**: Any partner has the right to a formal accounting of partnership affairs whenever: wrongful exclusion from partnership business; rights exist under partnership agreement; circumstances render it just and reasonable; dissolution.

**Advances beyond contribution (Art. 1793)**: Partner who makes advances beyond agreed contribution is entitled to interest from time of advance; also entitled to interest on capital from time it should have been paid.

**Losses exceeding capital**: If partnership losses exceed capital, partners must contribute proportionally unless limited by agreement; general partners bear losses ultimately.

---

## Registration and Notice
**SEC registration of general partnerships**: Required when capital > ₱3,000. Non-registration does not void the partnership inter se or as to third parties who dealt with the partnership; it merely subjects the partners to regulatory sanctions.

**Certificate of limited partnership (Art. 1844)**: Filed with SEC; contains all required information. Amendments required when any stated fact changes materially. Failure to amend in circumstances that misled a person who suffered damages → partners liable.

**Constructive notice**: Upon filing of certificate (LP) or articles, third parties charged with notice of partnership existence and terms.

---

## Partnership Name and Liability to Third Parties
**Firm name**: May or may not include partner names. General partnership may use any firm name. Limited partnership must include "Ltd." or "Limited" in name. Use of limited partner's name in firm name → limited partner loses limited liability for those who extend credit relying on that name.

**Liability of non-partner using firm name**: Person who uses a firm name without being a partner, or who permits use of their name, is liable to third parties who rely on that name. *Trading under a phantom partner's name creates full liability*.

**Dormant partners**: Contribute capital but take no active part in management; not known as partners publicly. Liable to the partnership and among partners but NOT liable to third parties who did not know of their existence — exception: if they are later disclosed, liability attaches from that point.

---

## Disputes and Remedies
**Arbitration**: Partnership agreement commonly includes arbitration; governed by R.A. 9285 (ADR Act). Intra-partnership disputes are arbitrable; court may stay action pending arbitration.

**Dissolution by court decree (Art. 1831)**: (a) Partner is declared insane or of unsound mind by competent court; (b) incapable of performing partnership duties; (c) guilty of conduct prejudicially affecting business; (d) wilful/persistent breach of partnership agreement; (e) continued partnership business operates only at a loss; (f) other equitable grounds.

**Receiver and winding up**: Court may appoint receiver on dissolution; receiver winds up affairs under court supervision; priority of claims: (1) outside creditors; (2) partner loans; (3) capital; (4) profits.

---

## Partnership in Special Contexts
**Law firm partnerships**: Professional partnerships governed by NCC; subject to professional regulations (CPE, ethical standards). Profits shared per agreement; liability for malpractice — individual partner liable plus partnership. Law firm may not incorporate in the Philippines; must be partnership or sole proprietorship.

**CPA partnerships**: Subject to BOA regulations; must comply with RA 9298 (Philippine Accountancy Act); name must include only CPA partners. 

**Joint venture for construction (PCAB)**: Construction joint ventures registered with PCAB for specific projects; exempted from corporate income tax if constituted for the project only (NIRC §22(B)).

**Contractual JV vs. corporate JV**: Contractual — no new juridical entity; parties are co-venturers bound by partnership rules; corporate — incorporates a JV company under RCC; separate entity, separate liability.

**Mining partnership**: Special rules under R.A. 7942 (Mining Act); may be organized for mining operations; subject to mining permit requirements.

---

## Prescription of Partnership Claims
**Claims against partners**: Prescriptive period for contractual claims against the partnership: 10 years (written contract); 6 years (oral). Claims among partners for accounting: 4 years from date of dissolution or discovery of wrong. Claims against limited partners: same as general contract claims; partner contributions — LP's obligation to contribute runs under partnership agreement terms.

**Laches**: Equity defense available where partner delayed unreasonably in asserting rights; courts balance equities in intra-partnership disputes.

**Prescription — NCC vs. special law**: Where a special statute governs a particular partnership type (mining, construction), that statute's prescriptive period controls over NCC general rules.

---

## Key Cases — Partnership
**Aurbach v. Sanitary Wares Mfg.** (GR 75875): Joint ventures governed by partnership law; co-venturer who breaks the agreement may be expelled; voting rights and management participation are contractual.

**Heirs of Tan Eng Kee v. CA** (GR 126881): Profit-sharing alone does not establish partnership; must show intent to form partnership, mutual contribution, and intent to divide profits as partners (not as employee-employer).

**Muñasque v. CA** (GR L-39520): Partnership creditor may proceed directly against general partners without first exhausting partnership assets — partners are solidarily liable as among themselves to third-party creditors.

**Lim Tong Lim v. Philippine Fishing Gear** (GR 136448): Partners who act as such are liable even if they deny the partnership; conduct creates the legal relationship despite absence of formal agreement.

**Commissioner of Internal Revenue v. Suter** (GR L-25532): A limited partnership which is in reality a joint venture does not qualify for the professional partnership tax treatment; its income taxed as corporation. Highlights the importance of verifying whether a partnership is truly a commercial partnership subject to corporate income tax or a professional partnership that flows through.
**Key tax rule for partnerships**: Under NIRC §22(B), a joint venture or consortium formed for construction projects or engaging in petroleum, coal, geothermal operations under consortium service contracts with the government, that do not operate as a corporation, shall not be taxable as a corporation — income taxed to co-venturers individually. All other joint ventures treated as corporations for income tax purposes.
