# ICC / Rome Statute Module

## Philippines and the ICC — Chronology

**Accession**: The Philippines signed the Rome Statute on December 28, 2000. The Senate concurred via Senate Resolution No. 546 on August 30, 2011. The Philippines deposited its instrument of ratification on August 30, 2011, making it the 117th State Party. The Rome Statute entered into force for the Philippines on September 1, 2011.

**Withdrawal**: On March 17, 2018, President Duterte announced the Philippines' withdrawal from the Rome Statute. The formal notification of withdrawal was filed with the UN Secretary-General. Under Article 127 of the Rome Statute, withdrawal takes effect one year after the notification is received — the withdrawal became effective March 17, 2019.

**Current status as of knowledge cutoff**: The Philippines is no longer a State Party to the Rome Statute as of March 17, 2019. However, withdrawal does not extinguish jurisdiction over crimes allegedly committed while the Philippines was a State Party.

## Jurisdictional Framework — Three Temporal Periods

Analyze every ICC issue by identifying which temporal period the alleged conduct falls into.

### Period 1: Pre-Ratification (Before September 1, 2011)

**ICC jurisdiction**: None as a matter of Article 11 temporal jurisdiction, absent a Security Council referral (which would invoke a different jurisdictional basis). The ICC has jurisdiction only over crimes committed after the Rome Statute entered into force for that State.

**Conclusion**: ICC lacks temporal jurisdiction over crimes allegedly committed before September 1, 2011, unless referred by the Security Council. Confidence: [95].

### Period 2: During Membership (September 1, 2011 — March 17, 2019)

**ICC jurisdiction**: Full jurisdiction applies. The Philippines was a State Party. Territorial and personal jurisdiction over crimes committed on Philippine territory or by Philippine nationals.

**Conduct at issue**: The anti-drug campaign ("War on Drugs") substantially occurred during this period. The OTP's preliminary examination was opened in February 2018. Formal investigation authorized.

**Complementarity**: The ICC's jurisdiction is complementary to national jurisdiction. If the Philippines is genuinely investigating and prosecuting the same persons for the same conduct, the ICC must defer. However, "inability or unwillingness" to genuinely prosecute activates ICC admissibility. The ICC must assess: (a) are national proceedings shielding perpetrators? (b) are national proceedings unduly delayed? (c) is the national justice system unable to proceed?

**Current status of proceedings**: The ICC Pre-Trial Chamber authorized an investigation on September 15, 2021. The Philippines has challenged admissibility on the ground that it is conducting its own investigations. The ICC's assessment of these domestic proceedings for complementarity purposes is an ongoing matter. **Verify current status against ICC public records.**

**Conclusion**: ICC has jurisdiction over Period 2 conduct. Withdrawal does not retroactively remove this jurisdiction. Confidence: [95].

### Period 3: Post-Withdrawal (After March 17, 2019)

**ICC jurisdiction**: No jurisdiction for new crimes committed after withdrawal, absent Security Council referral.

**Continued obligation to cooperate**: Under **Article 127(2)** of the Rome Statute, withdrawal does not affect cooperation obligations arising from investigations or proceedings that commenced before the withdrawal became effective. The scope of this continuing obligation after effective withdrawal is a contested legal question.

**Conclusion**: ICC lacks jurisdiction over new post-withdrawal crimes. Confidence: [90]. Continuing obligations remain subject to interpretation. Confidence: [65].

## Subject Matter Jurisdiction

**Crimes within ICC jurisdiction**: genocide, crimes against humanity, war crimes, crime of aggression (for States that have accepted jurisdiction).

**Extrajudicial killings in the drug war**: The OTP's theory is that killings in the anti-drug campaign may constitute crimes against humanity under Article 7 of the Rome Statute — specifically murder as part of a widespread or systematic attack on a civilian population, with knowledge of the attack.

**Elements of crimes against humanity**:
1. An attack — course of conduct involving multiple acts
2. Against a civilian population
3. The attack is widespread (scale) or systematic (organized, pursuant to a policy)
4. The accused's act was part of the attack
5. The accused knew the attack was being conducted against civilians

**No armed conflict requirement**: Crimes against humanity do not require armed conflict, unlike war crimes. The Philippines was not in an international armed conflict during the drug war.

## Complementarity Analysis

The ICC's complementarity principle (Article 17) is not a mechanism for States to escape accountability by initiating nominal proceedings. The Court analyzes:

**Genuine investigation**: Are the domestic proceedings directed at persons with the greatest responsibility? Do they cover substantially the same conduct as the ICC investigation? Are they conducted in good faith?

**Inability**: The state is unable when its judicial system is substantially collapsed or unavailable.

**Unwillingness**: The state is unwilling when proceedings are designed to shield, proceedings are unduly delayed, or proceedings are not conducted independently and impartially.

**Philippine domestic proceedings**: The DOJ and NBI investigations launched as a response to ICC scrutiny will be evaluated by the ICC against this standard. Nominal domestic proceedings designed primarily to trigger complementarity without genuine accountability do not satisfy Article 17.

## Sovereignty Arguments vs. Jurisdictional Arguments

**Critical distinction**: Sovereignty objections are political arguments. They do not constitute valid legal defenses before the ICC.

**Political objection**: "The ICC has no right to examine our internal affairs; the drug war is a domestic law enforcement matter."
**Legal status of this objection**: Not a valid legal defense. States voluntarily accede to the Rome Statute. Accession is an exercise of sovereignty, not a limitation on it.

**Jurisdictional objection**: "The ICC lacks temporal jurisdiction because the conduct occurred before ratification."
**Legal status**: A valid legal argument subject to factual determination.

**Procedural objection**: "We are genuinely investigating; complementarity must apply."
**Legal status**: A valid procedural argument subject to the Court's assessment of genuineness.

Never conflate these three categories. Each has a different legal weight and a different mode of resolution.

## Withdrawal — Legal Effect Under Article 127

Article 127 permits withdrawal. It takes effect one year after notification to the UN Secretary-General. Crucially: withdrawal "shall not affect any cooperation with the Court in connection with criminal investigations and proceedings in relation to which the withdrawing State had a duty to cooperate and which were commenced prior to the date on which the withdrawal became effective."

**This means**: All cooperation obligations arising from the pre-March 2019 investigation period continue notwithstanding withdrawal. The Philippines cannot use withdrawal as a shield against cooperation requests predating the withdrawal's effectiveness.

## ICC Procedural Posture — Analysis Checklist

For any ICC-related legal question, proceed through this sequence:

Step 1 — Temporal jurisdiction: Identify the date of the alleged conduct. Apply the three-period analysis above.

Step 2 — Subject matter jurisdiction: Identify the crime alleged. Map to Rome Statute definitions. Apply Elements of Crimes.

Step 3 — Personal jurisdiction: Is the accused a national of a State Party, or did the conduct occur on the territory of a State Party, or was there a Security Council referral?

Step 4 — Complementarity: Are there genuine domestic proceedings? Apply the Article 17 inability/unwillingness test.

Step 5 — Admissibility: Is the case of sufficient gravity? The ICC focuses on those bearing greatest responsibility.

Step 6 — Distinguish political objections from legal defenses: Identify which category each argument falls into. Do not conflate.

Step 7 — Assign confidence score: Account for ongoing proceedings and evolving ICC rulings.

## Interaction with Philippine Constitutional Law

**Treaty vs. withdrawal**: The Senate concurred in ratification under Article VII, §21. The President withdrew without Senate concurrence. Whether presidential withdrawal requires Senate concurrence is an unsettled question in Philippine constitutional law. The withdrawal has taken legal effect internationally; the domestic constitutional question regarding the proper procedure remains.

**Constitutional challenge to withdrawal**: A petition challenging the withdrawal as requiring Senate concurrence or as unconstitutionally impeding the Philippines' international obligations could theoretically reach the Supreme Court. As of knowledge cutoff, no definitive ruling exists on this specific question.

**Incorporation doctrine (Article II, §2)**: The Philippines has adopted generally accepted principles of international law as part of the law of the land. The Rome Statute, as a ratified treaty, was part of Philippine law. Post-withdrawal, the Statute's provisions are no longer automatically operative domestically, but customary international law obligations may persist.
## Anti-Hallucination Guardrails — ICC / Rome Statute

Never cite Article 70 of the Rome Statute for post-withdrawal cooperation obligations — Article 70 governs offenses against the administration of justice; the correct provision for post-withdrawal cooperation is **Article 127(2)**. Never state that the ICC lacks jurisdiction over pre-withdrawal conduct — withdrawal takes effect prospectively only; Period 2 conduct (September 1, 2011 to March 17, 2019) remains within ICC jurisdiction. Never treat China's rejection of the South China Sea Arbitration Award as affecting the legal validity of the Award under UNCLOS — China's non-participation and non-compliance are political facts that do not nullify the tribunal's legal determination. Never state definitively that the Philippines' withdrawal extinguishes all ICC jurisdiction — it does not for Period 2 conduct and does not for cooperation obligations arising from pre-withdrawal requests. The ICC proceedings are ongoing and the status of admissibility challenges evolves — always note that current ICC procedural posture must be verified against current ICC records.
