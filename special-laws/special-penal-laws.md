# Special Penal Laws Module

## Overview and Analytical Framework

Special penal laws are statutes outside the Revised Penal Code that define and penalize specific offenses. The RPC applies suppletorily unless the special law expressly provides otherwise or suppletory application is inconsistent with the law's nature (*People v. Simon*, G.R. No. 93028, 1994). Most special penal law offenses are *mala prohibita* — criminal intent is not an element, and proof of the act itself (with knowledge, where required) is sufficient. Load `criminal-law.md` for the mala in se / mala prohibita distinction and for RPC defenses applicable suppletorily.

## R.A. 9165 — Comprehensive Dangerous Drugs Act of 2002

**Key offenses**:

**Section 5 — Sale, trading, administration, dispensation, delivery, distribution and transportation**: The most severely penalized offense. Elements: (1) identity of the buyer and seller, and consideration; (2) delivery of the thing sold and payment therefor. Penalty: life imprisonment to death (R.A. 9346 abolished death; life imprisonment is the effective maximum) and fine of ₱500,000 to ₱10,000,000.

**Section 11 — Possession of dangerous drugs**: Penalty depends on quantity. Possession of 10 grams or more of opium, morphine, heroin, cocaine, marijuana resin or resin oil, shabu, or analogues — life imprisonment. Possession of below threshold quantities — imprisonment ranges from 12 years and one day to 20 years and fine. Possession of equipment, instrument, apparatus, and other paraphernalia (Section 12) is a separate offense.

**Section 15 — Use of dangerous drugs**: Person apprehended or arrested found to be positive for drug use after confirmatory test. First offense — minimum of six months rehabilitation; second offense — imprisonment of six years and one day to 12 years and fine.

### Chain of Custody (Section 21, as amended by R.A. 10640)

The chain of custody rule is the most litigated evidentiary issue in drug cases. **The four links**:

1. Seizure and marking — seized items must be immediately inventoried and photographed after seizure at the place of arrest or, when practicable, at the nearest police station or nearest office of the apprehending officer
2. Turnover to the investigating officer
3. Turnover to the forensic chemist for examination
4. Submission to the court

**Required witnesses during inventory and photography**: (1) the accused or the accused's representative or counsel; (2) an elected public official; (3) a representative of the National Prosecution Service (NPS) **or** a media representative. All three witnesses are required under the current amendment. Prior to R.A. 10640's amendment, a DOJ representative was required in lieu of NPS — verify which version applies based on the date of the offense.

**Saving clause**: Non-compliance with Section 21 does not automatically render the seizure void if: (a) there is a justifiable ground for non-compliance; and (b) the integrity and evidentiary value of the seized items were properly preserved. The prosecution bears the burden of explaining the justifiable grounds for non-compliance and proving preservation of integrity. *People v. Lim* (G.R. No. 231989, 2018) and subsequent cases: the SC has progressively tightened enforcement of the chain of custody rule and narrowed the saving clause — recent decisions require a more exacting justification.

**Planting of evidence and instigation**: Defenses available if there is proof that law enforcement planted the drugs or induced the accused to commit the offense. Instigation (entrapment by inducing an innocent person) differs from entrapment (catching a person already engaged in criminal activity through a buy-bust) — instigation bars conviction.

## R.A. 9262 — Anti-Violence Against Women and Their Children Act of 2004

**Coverage**: The law protects women and their children from violence committed by the following offenders: (a) husband or ex-husband; (b) man with whom the woman has or had a sexual or dating relationship; (c) man with whom the woman has a common child.

**Acts of violence covered**:
- **Physical abuse**: Bodily or physical harm
- **Sexual abuse**: Rape, sexual harassment, treating the woman or child as sex objects, denying reproductive rights
- **Psychological abuse**: Emotional anguish, public humiliation, stalking, repeated verbal abuse, social isolation, controlling behavior
- **Economic abuse**: Withdrawing financial support, controlling the woman's money, destroying property, threatening to deprive support

**Elements of the offense**: (1) the offender is a man; (2) the offended party is his wife, former wife, girlfriend, ex-girlfriend, or a woman with whom he has a common child; (3) the offender causes physical, sexual, psychological, or economic abuse on the offended party or her child.

**Protection orders**:
- **Barangay Protection Order (BPO)**: Issued by the Punong Barangay; effective for 15 days; enjoins commission of acts of violence.
- **Temporary Protection Order (TPO)**: Issued by the court ex parte within 24 hours of application; effective for 30 days.
- **Permanent Protection Order (PPO)**: Issued by the court after notice and hearing; effective until revoked by court.

Violation of a protection order is a separate criminal offense under Section 9.

**Prescriptive period**: Dependent on the penalty of the underlying act. The 2004 IRR provisions should be consulted for the applicable prescriptive period for specific forms of abuse.

*Note*: R.A. 9262 has been the subject of constitutional challenge on equal protection grounds. The SC has upheld its constitutionality — the classification (protecting women and children) rests on substantial distinction (*Garcia v. Drilon*, G.R. No. 179267, 2013).

## R.A. 3019 — Anti-Graft and Corrupt Practices Act (1960, as amended)

**The most commonly charged graft offenses (Section 3)**:

**Section 3(b)**: Directly or indirectly requesting or receiving any gift, present, share, percentage, or benefit for himself or any other person in connection with any contract or transaction between the government and any other party, wherein the public officer in his official capacity has to intervene, under the law, in his official capacity.

**Section 3(c)**: Directly or indirectly requesting or receiving any gift, present, or other pecuniary or material benefit for himself or for another, from any person for whom the public officer, in any manner or capacity, has secured or obtained, or will secure or obtain, any government permit or license, in consideration for the help given or to be given.

**Section 3(e)** — the most commonly charged provision: Causing any undue injury to any party, including the Government, or giving any private party any unwarranted benefits, advantage, or preference in the discharge of his official administrative or judicial functions through manifest partiality, evident bad faith, or gross inexcusable negligence. Elements: (1) the accused is a public officer; (2) acting in his official capacity in administrative, judicial, or official functions; (3) through manifest partiality, evident bad faith, or gross inexcusable negligence; (4) causing undue injury to any party **or** giving unwarranted benefits to a private party. Undue injury and unwarranted benefits are alternative, not conjunctive, elements.

**Section 3(g)**: Entering, on behalf of the Government, into any contract or transaction manifestly and grossly disadvantageous to the same, whether or not the public officer profited or will profit thereby.

**Sandiganbayan jurisdiction**: R.A. 10660 (2015): The Sandiganbayan has exclusive original jurisdiction over graft cases involving public officers with Salary Grade 27 and above. For public officers below SG 27, the Regional Trial Court has jurisdiction, unless the offense was committed in relation to the office and the accused holds a position equivalent to SG 27.

**Prescriptive period under R.A. 3019**: Section 11 prescribes that offenses shall prescribe in 15 years. However, *People v. Sandiganbayan* and related cases apply *Act No. 3326* (prescriptive periods for violations of special laws) to determine when the period starts — the clock starts from the date of discovery by the offended party or by the State if the offense is not immediately discoverable.

## R.A. 7080 — Plunder Law (1991, as amended by R.A. 7659)

**Definition of plunder**: A public officer who, by himself or in connivance with members of his family, relatives, business associates, subordinates, or other persons, amasses, accumulates, or acquires ill-gotten wealth through a combination or series of overt or criminal acts in the aggregate amount or total value of at least ₱50,000,000 through any of the enumerated means (misappropriation, conversion, misuse or malversation, receiving pecuniary benefit in connection with a government contract, illegal or fraudulent conveyances, by taking advantage of official position, by obtaining from banks loans or financial accommodations, by obtaining interests in any business enterprise).

**Key constitutional challenge** — *Estrada v. Sandiganbayan* (G.R. No. 148560, November 19, 2001): The SC upheld the constitutionality of the Plunder Law. Clarified: "combination" means at least two different categories of acts; "series" means at least two acts in the same category; "pattern" does not require all acts be previously proven. The main plunderer must be shown to have the requisite criminal intent; co-conspirators need not each be shown to have acted with the same deliberateness.

**Penalty**: Reclusion perpetua and perpetual absolute disqualification from public office. The death penalty provision was effectively superseded by R.A. 9346.

**Prescriptive period**: 20 years under Section 6 of R.A. 7080.

## B.P. Blg. 22 — Bouncing Checks Law (1979)

**Elements**: (1) making, drawing, or issuing a check; (2) knowledge at the time of issue that the drawer does not have sufficient funds in or credit with the bank to cover the check in full; (3) dishonor of the check upon presentment; (4) failure to pay the amount due or to make arrangements for payment within five banking days after receiving notice of dishonor.

**Prima facie presumption**: Knowledge of insufficient funds is presumed from the dishonor of the check. The drawer must rebut this presumption by showing: (a) absence of the element of knowledge; or (b) payment of the full amount within five banking days of notice of dishonor.

**Notice of dishonor**: The notice must be received by the accused. Actual receipt is required — constructive notice (e.g., notice to a lawyer absent specific authorization) is insufficient.

**Prescriptive period**: Four years under *Act No. 3326* (violations of special laws without a prescribed period): four years from commission. But see *People v. Sandiganbayan* for when the period starts if there is a continuing offense.

**Penalty policy** — *Administrative Circular No. 13-2001* (SC, December 2001): Courts should preferentially impose fine over imprisonment in B.P. 22 cases, taking into account the policy that criminal punishment for B.P. 22 is intended as a deterrent, not punitive incarceration. Imprisonment is not foreclosed; the Circular is a policy direction, not a mandatory rule prohibiting imprisonment. Estafa arising from the same check-issuing may be charged separately and is governed by RPC penalty rules.

## R.A. 10175 — Cybercrime Prevention Act of 2012

**Key offenses** (Section 4):

**Cybercrime offenses against the confidentiality, integrity, and availability of computer data and systems**: Illegal access (Section 4(a)(1)); illegal interception (Section 4(a)(2)); data interference (Section 4(a)(3)); system interference (Section 4(a)(4)); misuse of devices (Section 4(a)(5)); cyber-squatting (Section 4(a)(6)).

**Computer-related offenses**: Computer-related forgery (Section 4(b)(1)); computer-related fraud (Section 4(b)(2)); computer-related identity theft (Section 4(b)(3)).

**Content-related offenses**: Cybersex (Section 4(c)(1)); child pornography (Section 4(c)(2)); unsolicited commercial communications or spam (Section 4(c)(3)); libel (Section 4(c)(4)).

**Cyber-libel (Section 4(c)(4))**: Libel as defined in Art. 355 RPC committed through a computer system or any other similar means. The penalty is one degree higher than ordinary libel. *Prescriptive period*: *Disini v. Secretary of Justice* (G.R. No. 203335, February 18, 2014) upheld cyber-libel as constitutional but struck down the provision extending liability to those who receive or react to online defamatory material. The prescriptive period for cyber-libel has generated jurisprudential dispute — the SC has addressed this in *Ressa v. People* (verify for current ruling on the 12-year period under *Act No. 3326* vs. the 1-year period under Art. 90 RPC for ordinary libel).

**Constitutional challenges** — *Disini v. Secretary of Justice*: The SC upheld most of R.A. 10175, struck down the provision on unsolicited commercial communications as overbroad, narrowed the take-down provision, and limited the one-hour cybercrime response team power. Always verify current scope of upheld provisions.

**Jurisdiction**: The cybercrime court designated under R.A. 10175 handles these cases. The Act has extraterritorial application (Section 21) — applies even when the offense was committed outside the Philippines if the offender or victim is Philippine resident or citizen, or if the computer data or system is accessed from the Philippines.

## R.A. 11479 — Anti-Terrorism Act of 2020

**Replaces**: Human Security Act of 2007 (R.A. 9372).

**Definition of terrorism (Section 4)**: Engaging in acts intended to cause death or serious bodily injury to any person, or endangering a person's life, or creating serious risk to public safety, property, or critical infrastructure, for the purpose of: intimidating the general public; creating widespread fear; coercing the government to do or abstain from an act; disrupting critical infrastructure; destabilizing fundamental political, economic, social structures; creating a public emergency; or seriously undermining public safety. Advocacy, protest, dissent, stoppage of work, industrial or mass action, and similar exercises of civil and political rights are expressly excluded from the definition.

**Key Constitutional Challenge** — *Calleja v. Executive Secretary* (G.R. No. 252578, December 7, 2021): The SC upheld the constitutionality of R.A. 11479 with modifications. Struck down as unconstitutional: Section 4's use of the phrase "similar manner" as a qualifying element (too vague); the provision on foreign terrorist fighters (Section 10) to the extent it might criminalize humanitarian assistance; certain provisions giving the Anti-Terrorism Council (ATC) designation power without judicial imprimatur in specified contexts. The precise scope of the *Calleja* ruling must be consulted for current analysis; the decision is lengthy and nuanced.

**Detention without judicial warrant (Section 29)**: Allows detention of suspects for up to 14 days (extendable by 10 days) without judicial warrant. The provision was upheld in *Calleja* but with the caveat that the right to counsel, habeas corpus, and judicial review are not suspended.

**Designation by ATC**: The ATC may designate a person or organization as a terrorist, which carries consequences including asset freezing under AMLA. The *Calleja* decision's treatment of this power is central to current analysis.

## R.A. 7610 — Special Protection of Children Against Abuse, Exploitation and Discrimination Act (1992)

**Scope**: Protects children below 18 years of age or those over 18 but unable to fully take care of themselves against abuse, exploitation, cruelty, discrimination, and other conditions prejudicial to their development.

**Child abuse (Section 10)**: Maltreatment of a child — physical, psychological, or other forms of abuse; cruelty; exploitation; neglect; commission of sexual offenses against children.

**Child prostitution and sexual abuse (Section 5)**: Children, whether male or female, who are exploited in prostitution or subjected to other sexual abuse.

**Relationship to RPC**: *Malto v. People* (G.R. No. 164733, 2007) and related cases: R.A. 7610 may apply to sexual offenses against children even when the RPC's rape provisions might also apply — the specific law governs; prosecutors must carefully select the applicable charge.

## R.A. 8049 — Anti-Hazing Law (1995, as amended by R.A. 11053, 2018)

**Definition of hazing**: An initiation rite or practice as a prerequisite for admission into membership in a fraternity, sorority, or organization that places the recruit, neophyte, or applicant in some embarrassing or humiliating situations or that may also involve physical or psychological suffering or injury.

**Key amendment (R.A. 11053, 2018)**: Expanded coverage to schools, armed forces, PNP, and similar organizations; increased penalties; made organizers, facilitators, and officers presumptively liable regardless of presence during the hazing; made school authorities liable if hazing occurred in school premises or sponsored by the school.

**Presumption of liability**: Officers and members who are present during hazing are presumed to have participated in it. The burden shifts to them to prove they made efforts to prevent the hazing.

## Anti-Hallucination Guardrails — Special Penal Laws

Never state that the death penalty is currently imposed in the Philippines — R.A. 9346 abolished it in 2006; verify whether any subsequent restoration legislation has been enacted before addressing the current penalty regime. For R.A. 9165 drug cases, the saving clause for chain-of-custody non-compliance is not a blanket excuse — recent SC decisions have tightened the standards; never present the saving clause as easily satisfied. The elements of Section 3(e) of R.A. 3019 require either undue injury **or** unwarranted benefits — these are disjunctive; do not require both. For B.P. 22, the five-banking-day period runs from *receipt* of notice of dishonor, not from dishonor itself — actual receipt is required. R.A. 11479 (Anti-Terrorism Act) was upheld in *Calleja* with specific provisions struck down as unconstitutional — never apply it without consulting the *Calleja* decision for current scope; certain provisions are no longer operative. The prescriptive period for cyber-libel is disputed — present both the 1-year view (Art. 90 RPC suppletorily applied) and the 12-year view (*Act No. 3326* for special law offenses), note the pending or decided SC ruling, and assign a confidence score to the current doctrine. Never fabricate specific case numbers for drug cases, graft cases, or cybercrime cases — these are numerous and easily confused; cite landmark doctrinal cases only when you can confirm them, and flag others as unconfirmed requiring verification against SC E-Library.
