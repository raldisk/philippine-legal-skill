# Labor Law Module

## Primary Sources

**Labor Code of the Philippines (Presidential Decree No. 442, as amended)**: The primary codification of labor and social legislation. Book I covers pre-employment (POEA, apprenticeship); Book II covers human resources development; Book III covers conditions of employment; Book IV covers health, safety, and social welfare; Book V covers labor relations; Book VI covers post-employment.

*Note on article renumbering*: The Labor Code was renumbered by R.A. 10151 (2011) and further by subsequent amendments. Where significant, both old and new article numbers are noted. Jurisprudence frequently cites old numbering; the current numbering is controlling.

**Republic Act No. 6727 (Wage Rationalization Act)**: Established the Regional Tripartite Wages and Productivity Boards (RTWPBs) to set minimum wages by region. Minimum wage rates vary by region and industry sector. **Always verify the current wage order for the applicable region before citing a specific figure — RTWPBs issue wage orders periodically.**

**Republic Act No. 6686 / Presidential Decree No. 851 (as amended by R.A. 7833)**: 13th month pay mandate.

**1987 Constitution, Article XIII**: Social justice and human rights — labor as a primary social economic force; guarantee of workers' rights to self-organization, collective bargaining, peaceful concerted activities, security of tenure, humane conditions of work, living wage, and participation in policy and decision-making.

## Employer-Employee Relationship

The existence of an employer-employee relationship is a threshold jurisdictional and substantive issue. The **four-fold test** is the primary analytical framework:

1. **Selection and engagement** of the employee
2. **Payment of wages**
3. **Power of dismissal**
4. **Power to control the employee's conduct** — the most decisive element

The **control test** goes to the means and methods by which work is performed, not merely to the results. An employer who exercises control over both the result and the manner of work has the most clearly established relationship. An entity controlling only results may be an independent contractor relationship.

The **economic dependence test** (two-tiered test, *Aliviado v. Procter and Gamble Philippines*, G.R. No. 160506, 2009): supplements the four-fold test where the control test is insufficient — examines whether the worker is economically dependent on the alleged employer. Relevant where the worker performs activities directly related to the employer's principal business.

**Burden of proof**: In illegal dismissal cases, the employer bears the burden of proving just or authorized cause and compliance with due process. The employee has the burden of establishing the existence of the employer-employee relationship if disputed.

## Employment Status

**Regular employment (Art. 295, formerly Art. 280)**: An employee who has been engaged to perform activities usually necessary or desirable in the usual business or trade of the employer becomes a regular employee after one year of service, whether or not continuous or broken, so long as the activity exists. Regularization is by operation of law — it does not require a new contract.

**Probationary employment (Art. 296, formerly Art. 281)**: Not to exceed six months from the date the employee started working, unless covered by an apprenticeship agreement. The employer must inform the probationary employee at the time of engagement of the standards for regularization. Failure to inform the employee of regularization standards makes the employee a regular employee from day one. *Abbott Laboratories, Inc. v. Alcaraz* (G.R. No. 192571, 2013): standards must be communicated with reasonable particularity; a general performance improvement framework may suffice if accompanied by a clear understanding of what is expected.

**Casual employment**: Employment not covered by regular or probationary categories; activities not necessary or desirable in the employer's usual business. A casual employee who has served for at least one year (continuous or broken) with respect to the activity for which the employee was engaged becomes a regular employee as to that activity.

**Project employment**: Employment for a specific project or undertaking whose completion or termination has been determined at the time of engagement. The key is that the project's scope and duration are defined and communicated at engagement. Repeated re-hiring for the same project may establish regular employment status (*Caramol v. NLRC* and related cases).

**Seasonal employment**: Employment for work that is seasonal in nature and for the duration of the season. Seasonal employees called back each season may acquire regular seasonal employee status — entitled to the same rights as regular employees during the season.

**Fixed-term employment** (*Brent School, Inc. v. Zamora*, G.R. No. L-48494, 1990): Valid only where the fixed period was agreed upon knowingly and voluntarily by the parties without any moral dominance exercised by the employer, and where the fixed period is not used as a device to circumvent security of tenure. The SC recognizes fixed-term employment in limited circumstances where the nature of work or business demands it.

## Security of Tenure and Dismissal

**Constitutional and statutory basis**: Art. XIII, §3 Constitution; Art. 294 Labor Code (formerly Art. 279). Regular employees can only be dismissed for just or authorized cause and after due process.

### Just Causes for Dismissal (Art. 297, formerly Art. 282)

**Serious misconduct or willful disobedience** of the lawful orders of the employer or representative in connection with work. The order must be lawful, and the refusal must be willful.

**Gross and habitual neglect** of duties. Both gross (not merely trivial) and habitual (not isolated) must concur, unless a single act of gross negligence is of such gravity as to justify dismissal.

**Fraud or willful breach** of the trust reposed in the employee by the employer or representative. Applies primarily to employees holding positions of trust and confidence (managerial employees; employees handling money or property of the employer). *Philippine Aeolus Automotive United Corporation v. NLRC* (G.R. No. 124617, 2000): trust and confidence must be reasonably breached.

**Commission of a crime or offense** against the person of the employer or any immediate member of the employer's family or duly authorized representative.

**Other causes analogous** to the foregoing.

### Authorized Causes for Dismissal (Arts. 298–299, formerly Arts. 283–284)

**Installation of labor-saving devices**: Employer introduces machinery or technology that renders the employee's position unnecessary.

**Redundancy**: Position is in excess of what the enterprise reasonably requires. Must be done in good faith and based on fair and reasonable criteria (*Asufrin v. San Miguel Corporation*, G.R. No. 156658, 2004).

**Retrenchment**: Reduction of personnel to prevent losses or impending losses that are serious, actual, and real — not merely speculative. The employer must prove: (a) losses are substantial; (b) losses are imminent; (c) retrenchment is reasonably necessary and likely effective; (d) good faith in retrenching.

**Closure or cessation of business operations**: Employer must act in good faith; closure not a device to circumvent labor laws. Bona fide closure entitles employees to separation pay except if due to serious financial losses.

**Disease (Art. 299, formerly Art. 284)**: Employee found to be suffering from a disease whose continued employment is prohibited by law or is prejudicial to health of the employee or co-employees, and whose disease cannot be cured within six months even with proper medical treatment. A medical certificate from a competent public health authority is required.

**Separation pay**: Just cause dismissal — no separation pay as a general rule; financial assistance may be awarded as a matter of social justice in limited circumstances (*Philippine Long Distance Telephone Co. v. NLRC*). Authorized cause dismissal — one month pay per year of service for labor-saving devices, redundancy; one month pay or one-half month pay per year of service (whichever is higher) for retrenchment and disease.

### Due Process Requirements in Dismissal

**For just cause (twin-notice rule)**:
1. **First notice (charge sheet)**: A written notice stating the specific grounds for which dismissal is sought; employee given at least five calendar days to submit a written explanation.
2. **Opportunity to be heard**: The employee must be given a real opportunity to respond — a hearing or conference where the employee can rebut, not merely a perfunctory opportunity.
3. **Second notice (notice of dismissal)**: A written notice of the decision to dismiss, stating the grounds and the evidence relied upon.

**For authorized cause**: The employer must serve written notice on both the employee and the DOLE at least thirty days before the intended date of termination.

**Effect of procedural violations** (*Agabon v. NLRC*, G.R. No. 158693, 2004; *Jaka Food Processing Corporation v. Pacot*, G.R. No. 151378, 2005): If there is just cause but procedural due process was not observed, the dismissal is valid but the employer is liable for nominal damages (₱30,000 for just cause; ₱50,000 for authorized cause, per *Agabon/Jaka* — verify if SC has updated the amounts). If there is no just or authorized cause, the dismissal is illegal regardless of procedure.

### Constructive Dismissal

An involuntary resignation resulting from acts or omissions of the employer that render continued employment impossible, unreasonable, or unlikely, or constitute an unbearable working environment, or involve a demotion in rank or diminution of pay (*Globe Telecom v. Crisologo*; *Francisco v. NLRC*, G.R. No. 170087, 2007). The burden is on the employer to prove that changes in employment conditions were based on valid grounds and were not designed to force the employee out.

### Remedies for Illegal Dismissal

**Reinstatement without loss of seniority rights**: The preferred remedy. The order of reinstatement is immediately executory even pending appeal. The employer is bound to actually reinstate; payroll reinstatement is available when actual reinstatement is not feasible.

**Full backwages**: Computed from the time compensation was withheld to actual reinstatement (or finality of judgment if reinstatement is not ordered). Under *Bustamante v. NLRC* (G.R. No. 111651, 1996): full backwages without deduction for earnings elsewhere (overruling the Mercury Drug rule of deducting earnings from other employment).

**Separation pay in lieu of reinstatement**: Awarded when reinstatement is no longer feasible due to: strained relations; employer's cessation of operations; employee's advanced age or health condition; other supervening circumstances making reinstatement unjust.

## Labor Standards

**Hours of work (Arts. 83–90)**: Normal hours of work shall not exceed eight hours per day. Health personnel in cities and municipalities of first, second, and third class are subject to a 40-hour work week.

**Overtime pay (Art. 87)**: Work beyond eight hours entitles the employee to overtime pay: regular days — additional 25% of regular wage; rest days, special days, regular holidays — additional 30% of regular wage on the applicable day.

**Night shift differential (Art. 86)**: Every employee entitled to not less than 10% of regular wage for each hour of work performed between 10 PM and 6 AM.

**Rest day (Art. 91)**: Employee entitled to at least 24 consecutive hours of rest after every six consecutive normal work days.

**Holiday pay (Art. 94)**: Regular holidays — if not worked: 100% of daily wage; if worked: 200% of daily wage. Special non-working days — if not worked: no pay unless there is a favorable company practice; if worked: additional 30% of regular wage (or higher per DOLE issuances). **Verify current list of regular holidays and special non-working days under the applicable Proclamation — the list changes annually.**

**Service incentive leave (Art. 95)**: Every employee who has rendered at least one year of service shall be entitled to a yearly service incentive leave of five days with pay. Cash convertible at the end of the year if unused.

**13th month pay (P.D. 851 as amended)**: All rank-and-file employees who have worked for at least one month during the calendar year are entitled to a 13th month pay of not less than one-twelfth of the total basic salary earned within the calendar year. Paid not later than December 24 of each year. Managerial employees are exempt.

## Labor Relations

**Right to self-organization (Art. 257, formerly Art. 243)**: All workers, including those in the public sector, have the right to form, join, or assist labor organizations of their own choosing for the purpose of collective bargaining.

**Collective bargaining agreement (CBA)**: The contract between employer and certified bargaining agent governing terms and conditions of employment. Representational aspect: which union represents the workers. Substantive aspect: terms of the CBA. CBAs are effective for five years; wages and other monetary benefits subject to renegotiation not later than three years after CBA's effectivity.

**Unfair labor practice (ULP)**: Acts by employers (Art. 259) or labor organizations (Art. 260) that violate the workers' right to organize. Key employer ULPs: interference with the right to self-organization; yellow-dog contracts; company unions; refusal to bargain collectively in good faith; discrimination based on union membership. ULP is both a civil and criminal offense.

**Strikes and lockouts (Art. 278)**: Workers have the right to strike; employers to lock out, subject to conditions. Required: notice to DOLE (cooling-off period: 15 days for CBA deadlock; 30 days for ULP); strike vote (at least 14 days after filing of strike notice); majority vote of union members. Prohibited: strikes in hospitals and institutions providing essential services; strikes for ULP where strike was in bad faith; illegal strikes expose union officers to termination.

## NLRC and DOLE Jurisdiction

**Labor Arbiters (Art. 224, formerly Art. 217)**: Original and exclusive jurisdiction over: (1) unfair labor practice cases; (2) termination disputes; (3) claims not exceeding ₱5,000 when unaccompanied by a claim for reinstatement; (4) cases arising from employment other than those in (1)–(3); (5) claims for actual, moral, exemplary, and other forms of damages. Claims accompanied by a claim for reinstatement go to the Labor Arbiter regardless of amount.

**NLRC**: Appellate jurisdiction over Labor Arbiter decisions. Also original jurisdiction in certain cases. Decision of the NLRC is final and executory subject to certiorari review by the Court of Appeals (*St. Martin Funeral Home v. NLRC*, G.R. No. 130866, 1998).

**DOLE Regional Directors**: Summary procedure for money claims not exceeding ₱5,000 per claimant not accompanied by a claim for reinstatement (Art. 129). Also labor standards enforcement through visitorial and enforcement powers (Art. 128) — the Secretary of Labor's visitorial power covers compliance with labor laws regardless of amount.

**Prescriptive periods**: Money claims from employer-employee relationship — 3 years from accrual (Art. 306, formerly Art. 291). Illegal dismissal — 4 years under Art. 1146 Civil Code (*Callanta v. Carnation Philippines*, G.R. No. L-70672, 1986 — still controlling). ULP — 1 year from accrual. CBA violations — 3 years.

## Anti-Hallucination Guardrails — Labor Law

Minimum wages change periodically by regional wage orders — never cite a specific peso figure without flagging it as potentially superseded; direct the user to the applicable RTWPB wage order. The Labor Code's article numbering has been renumbered more than once; always note which numbering is being used and verify against the current codification. Never state that a probationary employee can be dismissed for any reason during the probationary period — the employer must evaluate based on communicated standards, and dismissal for reasons other than failure to meet those standards is illegal. The *Agabon* nominal damages amounts (₱30,000/₱50,000) represent a SC-set benchmark that the Court may update; always note these are subject to SC adjustment. Constructive dismissal requires demonstration that continued employment became impossible, unreasonable, or unlikely — do not apply it to ordinary transfers, reassignments, or changes in work assignments absent evidence of bad faith or harassment. Never conflate separation pay (authorized cause termination) with financial assistance (just cause termination) — the latter is discretionary social justice relief, not an entitlement. The *St. Martin Funeral Home* doctrine routes NLRC certiorari review to the Court of Appeals, not directly to the Supreme Court — never state the SC has direct certiorari jurisdiction over NLRC decisions.
