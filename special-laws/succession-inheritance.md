# Succession and Inheritance Module

## Primary Sources

**New Civil Code (R.A. 386), Book III (Arts. 774–1105)**: The governing law on succession. Book III spans Arts. 774–1105.

**Family Code (E.O. 209)**: Modifies the successional rights of illegitimate children and spouses in conjunction with NCC Book III.

**1987 Constitution, Art. XV, §3**: The State shall defend the right of children to assistance, including proper care and nutrition, and special protection from all forms of neglect, abuse, cruelty, exploitation, and other conditions prejudicial to their development.

**R.A. 11642 (2022)**: Affects adoptee's successional rights — adopted child inherits as legitimate child.

---

## Fundamental Concepts

**Succession defined (Art. 774)**: A mode of acquisition by virtue of which the property, rights, and obligations to the extent of the value of the inheritance of a person are transmitted through his death to another, or others, either by his will or by operation of law.

**Decedent**: The person whose property is transmitted through succession.

**Estate**: The mass of property, rights, and obligations that a decedent leaves behind.

**Heir**: A person called to the succession either by will or by law to receive the entire or aliquot portion of the inheritance.

**Legatee / devisee**: Legatee receives personal property by will; devisee receives real property by will. Heirs receive aliquot shares; legatees and devisees receive specific things.

**Kinds of succession (Art. 778)**:
- Testamentary — by will
- Legal or intestate — by operation of law in the absence of a will
- Mixed — partly testamentary, partly intestate

## Testamentary Succession

### Wills — General Requirements

A will is an act whereby a person is permitted to control the disposition of his estate in accordance with law, to take effect after his death. Two forms: notarial (ordinary) will and holographic will.

**Notarial will requisites (Art. 805)**: In writing; in a language known to the testator; signed at the end by the testator or by the testator's name written by someone else in his presence and under his express direction; attested and subscribed by three or more credible witnesses in the presence of the testator and of one another; each page signed on the left margin by the testator and the witnesses; pages numbered correlatively in letters placed on the upper part; attestation clause stating the number of pages; acknowledged before a notary public by the testator and the witnesses.

**Holographic will requisites (Art. 810)**: Entirely written, dated, and signed by the hand of the testator himself. No witnesses required. Subject to strict authenticity scrutiny in probate.

**Testamentary capacity (Art. 796–799)**: Persons of either sex not below 18 years of age, and of sound mind at the time of execution. Sound mind means the testator knows the nature of the estate, the proper objects of bounty, and the character of the testamentary act.

### Probate — Mandatory Requirement

No will shall pass real or personal property unless it is proved and allowed in the proper court (Art. 838). Probate is mandatory and cannot be dispensed with. The court in probate determines only the extrinsic validity of the will (due execution), not the intrinsic validity of the provisions — the latter is determined in the separate settlement of estate proceedings. *Acain v. IAC* and related cases: probate jurisdiction is limited to extrinsic validity.

### Institution of Heirs

A testator may designate one or more persons to succeed to the whole or an aliquot part of the estate. The institution must not impair the legitime of compulsory heirs.

### Substitution

The testator may designate a second heir to take the inheritance in default of the first heir. Simple substitution, brief substitution, reciprocal substitution, and fideicommissary substitution (Arts. 857–870).

### Conditional and Modal Institutions

A condition suspends the heir's right until fulfilled. A modal institution imposes a charge or obligation on the heir. Impossible conditions in a will are deemed not written; they do not annul the institution.

## Compulsory Heirs and the Legitime

### Who Are Compulsory Heirs (Art. 887)

Legitimate children and descendants; in default, legitimate parents and ascendants; surviving spouse; illegitimate children.

### The Legitime

The legitime is the portion of the testator's property that cannot be freely disposed of because the law has reserved it for the compulsory heirs.

**Legitime of legitimate children (Art. 888)**: One-half of the hereditary estate of the father or mother. Each legitimate child gets an equal share of the legitime.

**Legitime of legitimate parents (Art. 889)**: One-half of the hereditary estate when there are no legitimate children or their descendants.

**Legitime of surviving spouse**:
- With legitimate children: one-fourth of the hereditary estate (Art. 892, ¶1)
- With one legitimate child: one-fourth (Art. 892, ¶2)
- With legitimate parents and no legitimate children: one-fourth (Art. 893)
- Alone (no legitimate children, no legitimate parents): the surviving spouse is entitled to the entire estate (Art. 995 NCC, intestate succession)

**Legitime of illegitimate children (Art. 895)**: One-half of the legitime of each legitimate child. Each illegitimate child receives half of what each legitimate child receives from the legitime portion.

**Summary formula — when legitimate children and illegitimate children and surviving spouse all survive**:
- Legitime = ½ of estate
- Free portion = ½ of estate
- Surviving spouse's share from legitime = ¼ of estate
- Illegitimate children's total legitime = ½ of legitimate children's total legitime
- The computation requires proportional reduction if the estate is insufficient

### Impairment of Legitime

If the testamentary dispositions impair the legitime, they shall be reduced on petition of any compulsory heir in the order established in Art. 950–954 (reduction of legacies and devises). Inofficious testamentary dispositions are reduced to the extent of the impairment.

## Intestate Succession

Intestate succession occurs when: the decedent left no will; the will was declared void; the will did not dispose of the entire estate; the heir renounced or was incapacitated (Art. 960).

### Order of Intestate Succession (Arts. 978–1014)

The NCC establishes a hierarchy of heirs. Later groups are excluded by earlier groups, subject to the right of representation.

**First order — Legitimate children and descendants**: Legitimate children inherit in equal shares. Legitimate grandchildren take by right of representation if their parent predeceased the decedent.

**Second order — Legitimate parents and ascendants**: Take only if no legitimate children/descendants survive.

**Illegitimate children**: Concur with legitimate children and parents. They do not exclude and are not excluded by the surviving spouse. Illegitimate children receive half the share of each legitimate child.

**Surviving spouse**: Concurs with legitimate children (gets share equal to one legitimate child), with illegitimate children (gets equal to one legitimate child's share), or with legitimate parents (gets one-half). If alone, the spouse inherits the entire estate.

**Collateral relatives**: Brothers, sisters, nephews, nieces — succeed only in the absence of descendants, ascendants, and surviving spouse. Half-blood collaterals receive half the share of full-blood collaterals (Art. 1006).

**The State**: In default of all other heirs, the State inherits (Art. 1011).

### Right of Representation (Arts. 970–977)

The right by which the children or descendants of a person who predeceased or is incapacitated to inherit take the place and degree and the rights of the person they represent. Representation operates in the descending line always; in the collateral line, only in favor of children of brothers and sisters, whether full or half-blood.

Representation does not apply in testamentary succession to the person designated as heir (unless the testator intended substitution), but operates in intestacy.

## Disinheritance

A compulsory heir may be deprived of the legitime only through disinheritance, based on specific grounds in the NCC (Arts. 919–921, 1032).

**Grounds for disinheritance of children (Art. 919)**: Conviction of a crime with civil interdiction; maltreatment; leading a dishonorable or disgraceful life; conviction of adultery or concubinage with the parent's spouse; abandonment; false accusation of a crime; causing the testator to make or change a will through fraud/violence; refusal to support the parent without justifiable cause.

**Grounds for disinheritance of parents (Art. 920)**: Abandonment; attempt against the life of the testator; conviction of an offense carrying civil interdiction; leading a life of continued dissipation; attempt to corrupt or prostitute; conviction of adultery or concubinage.

**Grounds for disinheritance of spouse (Art. 921)**: Adultery/concubinage; attempt against life; false accusation of a crime; causing the testator to make or change will by fraud/violence; abandonment without justifiable cause.

**Reconciliation (Art. 922)**: Subsequent reconciliation between the testator and the disinherited heir deprives the testator of the right of disinheritance and renders ineffectual any disinheritance already made.

## Collation and Partition

**Collation (Art. 1061)**: Every compulsory heir who succeeds with other compulsory heirs must bring into the mass of the estate any property or rights which he may have received from the decedent during the lifetime of the latter, by way of donation or any gratuitous title, in order that it may be computed in the determination of the legitime of each heir, and in the account of the partition.

**Partition**: Division of the estate among the heirs. May be extrajudicial (if no will and no debt) or judicial. *Extrajudicial settlement* under Rule 74 of the Rules of Court: heirs may divide the estate among themselves by executing a public instrument, provided no creditor objects and the estate has no debts.

## Special Rules — Successional Rights of Specific Persons

**Adopted children (post-R.A. 11642)**: Inherit as legitimate children of the adopting parents. Legal ties with biological parents are severed for succession purposes except when adopted by a relative or stepparent.

**Illegitimate children**: Cannot inherit ab intestato from the legitimate children and relatives of the father or mother, nor shall such children or relatives inherit from the illegitimate child (Art. 992). This is the **Iron Curtain Rule** — the barrier between the legitimate and illegitimate families in intestate succession. Heavily criticized but remains controlling law. Confidence: [95] that Art. 992 remains in force.

**Surviving spouse and legal separation**: The offending spouse in a final decree of legal separation loses the right to inherit from the innocent spouse by intestacy (Art. 63(4), Family Code).

**Unworthiness (Art. 1032)**: Persons disqualified from succeeding: those convicted of an attempt against the life of the decedent, the decedent's spouse, descendants, or ascendants; those convicted of calumny against the decedent; adults who failed to report the violent death of the decedent to the authorities; those who by fraud, violence, intimidation, or undue influence cause the decedent to make a will or change one.

## Key Jurisprudence — Succession

*Acain v. IAC* (G.R. No. 72706, 1987): Probate court jurisdiction limited to extrinsic validity; intrinsic validity is for the estate court.

*Heirs of Malabanan v. Republic* (G.R. No. 179987, 2009): Land registration and acquisitive prescription interact with succession — heirs step into the shoes of their predecessor in possession claims.

*Natcher v. Court of Appeals* (G.R. No. 133000, 2001): A Regional Trial Court sitting as a probate court has limited jurisdiction; it cannot decide questions of title to property claimed by third persons outside the estate.

*In re: Estate of Hilario M. Ruiz* (G.R. No. 118671, 1996): Executor's authority over estate property; fiduciary obligations.

*Viuda de Villanueva v. Juico* and related collation cases: Collation applies only to compulsory heirs who inherit; voluntary heirs are not subject to collation unless the will expressly requires it.

**Iron Curtain Rule cases**: Art. 992 NCC has been consistently upheld by the SC. Illegitimate relatives cannot inherit from legitimate relatives and vice versa in intestate succession. No en banc decision has yet overruled this; legislative reform proposals exist but have not been enacted as of knowledge cutoff.

## Anti-Hallucination Guardrails — Succession

Never invent NCC article numbers. The NCC succession provisions run Arts. 774–1105; cite the specific article and verify against the text. The legitime fractions are fixed by statute — do not approximate. The Iron Curtain Rule (Art. 992) remains in force; do not apply the rule selectively or assume it has been judicially abrogated. Always distinguish testamentary from intestate succession — the rules differ substantially.
