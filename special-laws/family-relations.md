# Family Relations Module

## Primary Sources

**Executive Order No. 209 (Family Code of the Philippines)**: Took effect August 3, 1988. Superseded the New Civil Code (R.A. 386) provisions on marriage, legal separation, property relations, parental authority, and support, except where the Family Code expressly retains NCC provisions or where the NCC fills gaps.

**New Civil Code (R.A. 386), Books I and II**: Residual source for family relations not governed by the Family Code. NCC provisions on persons and family still apply where not modified.

**1987 Constitution, Article XV**: The State recognizes the Filipino family as the foundation of the nation. Marriage is an inviolable social institution, the foundation of the family, and shall be protected by the State. No law shall be passed allowing divorce.

**Anti-Violence Against Women and Their Children Act (R.A. 9262)**: Criminal and civil remedies for VAWC within family relations.

**Anti-Child Abuse Law (R.A. 7610)**: Protection of children within family context.

**Republic Act 11642 (Domestic Administrative Adoption and Alternative Child Care Act, 2022)**: Transferred adoption proceedings from courts to the National Authority for Child Care (NACC). Domestic adoption is now administrative, not judicial. Inter-country adoption remains judicial.

## Marriage

### Essential Requisites (Family Code, Art. 2)

Legal capacity of contracting parties (male and female, at least 18 years old); consent freely given in the presence of the solemnizing officer.

### Formal Requisites (Art. 3)

Authority of the solemnizing officer; valid marriage license (except in cases exempt by law); marriage ceremony — personal appearance of parties before the solemnizing officer and their personal declaration that they take each other as husband and wife in the presence of at least two witnesses.

### Absence of Formal Requisite vs. Absence of Essential Requisite

Under **Article 4 of the Family Code**, the absence of a formal requisite does not void or voidable the marriage — rather, the party responsible shall be civilly, criminally, and administratively liable. The one critical exception is the **marriage license** (Art. 3[2]): absence of a marriage license renders the marriage **void** under Art. 35(3), not merely voidable. The absence of other formal requisites (e.g., authority of the solemnizing officer, presence of witnesses) creates liability only and does not affect the validity of the marriage. *Cosca v. Palaypayon* and related cases: the distinction between essential and formal requisites has significant practical consequences.

### Void Marriages (Art. 35–38, 44)

Contracted by a party below 18 years old; solemnized by person without authority; without a marriage license (except exempt cases); bigamous or polygamous marriages; mistake in identity; subsequent marriages void under Art. 53 (failure to comply with judgment of annulment partition requirements); incestuous marriages (Art. 37); marriages void by reason of public policy (Art. 38).

**Psychological incapacity (Art. 36)**: A marriage contracted by a party who was psychologically incapacitated to comply with the essential marital obligations at the time of the celebration shall be void. *Santos v. Court of Appeals* (G.R. No. 112019, 1995): psychological incapacity must be characterized by gravity, juridical antecedence, and incurability. *Republic v. Court of Appeals and Molina* (G.R. No. 108763, 1997): established the Molina guidelines — eight operational criteria for Art. 36 petitions. *Tan-Andal v. Andal* (G.R. No. 196359, 2021): **landmark** — SC En Banc modified *Molina*; psychological incapacity is a legal, not a medical, concept; expert testimony is helpful but not indispensable; incurability means relative incurability in the context of the marriage, not absolute medical incurability; gravity means the incapacity renders the party genuinely unable to fulfill essential marital obligations. *Tan-Andal* is the current controlling doctrine, superseding the stricter *Molina* framework.

### Voidable Marriages (Art. 45)

Party was 18–21 and married without parental consent; either party was of unsound mind; consent obtained by fraud (enumerated in Art. 46); consent obtained by force, intimidation, or undue influence; physical incapability to consummate; party afflicted with sexually transmissible disease found to be serious and incurable.

### Legal Separation (Arts. 55–67)

Does not dissolve the marriage bond — parties remain married. Grounds: repeated physical violence; moral pressure; attempt to corrupt or induce to prostitution; final judgment of imprisonment over 6 years; drug addiction or habitual alcoholism; lesbianism or homosexuality; contracting a subsequent bigamous marriage; sexual infidelity; attempt to take the life of the spouse; abandonment over 1 year without justifiable cause.

**Effects**: Separation of property; the offending spouse is disqualified from inheriting by intestacy from the innocent spouse. The marriage bond is not severed; neither party may remarry.

### Declaration of Nullity vs. Annulment vs. Legal Separation

| Remedy | Effect on Bond | Grounds | Who May File |
|---|---|---|---|
| Declaration of nullity (void marriage) | Bond never existed | Art. 35–38, 44 grounds | Either party; Solicitor General has interest |
| Annulment (voidable marriage) | Bond severed from decree | Art. 45 grounds | Specific parties per Art. 47 |
| Legal separation | Bond intact | Art. 55 grounds | Either spouse |

### No-Fault Divorce — Constitutional Status

The 1987 Constitution, Art. XV explicitly protects marriage as an inviolable social institution and prohibits divorce legislation. Absolute divorce between Filipino citizens on Philippine soil remains constitutionally unavailable. *Divorce obtained abroad by a foreign spouse*: Art. 26(2) Family Code — where a marriage between a Filipino citizen and a foreigner is validly celebrated and a divorce is thereafter validly obtained abroad by the alien spouse capacitating him or her to remarry, the Filipino spouse shall have capacity to remarry under Philippine law. *Republic v. Manalo* (G.R. No. 221029, 2018): Art. 26(2) applies even when the divorce was initiated by the Filipino spouse, not just the foreign spouse.

## Property Relations Between Spouses

### Default Regime (Art. 75)

If no marriage settlement is executed before the marriage: **Absolute Community of Property (ACP)** for marriages after August 3, 1988 (effectivity of Family Code). Under ACP, all property owned before and acquired after the marriage becomes community property, with exceptions (Art. 92): property acquired by gratuitous title (donation or inheritance) unless donor/testator provides otherwise; personal property for exclusive use; property acquired before marriage by either spouse who has legitimate descendants from a prior union.

### Conjugal Partnership of Gains (CPG)

Applicable to marriages before August 3, 1988 (under NCC) and to post-Family Code marriages where parties elect CPG in their marriage settlement. Under CPG, each spouse retains ownership of property brought into the marriage; only fruits and income during the marriage are conjugal.

### Separation of Property

May be agreed upon in a marriage settlement before marriage, or judicially decreed during marriage on specific grounds.

## Parental Authority

**Joint parental authority** (Art. 211): Father and mother shall jointly exercise parental authority over their unemancipated children. In case of disagreement, the father's decision shall prevail unless there is a judicial order to the contrary. The mother's authority is preferred for children under seven years old.

**Tender-age presumption** (Art. 213): Children under seven years of age shall not be separated from the mother, unless the court finds compelling reasons to order otherwise. *Briones v. Miguel* (G.R. No. 156343, 2004): the tender-age presumption in favor of the mother is rebuttable by compelling reasons.

**Illegitimate children** (Art. 176, as amended by R.A. 9255): Illegitimate children are under the sole parental authority of the mother, except when the father recognizes the child, in which case shared custody may be arranged. *De Asis v. Court of Appeals* (G.R. No. 127578, 1999): support obligation of father for illegitimate child.

**Emancipation**: A child is emancipated upon reaching the age of majority (18 years old under R.A. 6809). Parental authority terminates upon emancipation.

## Support

**Obligatory support** (Art. 195): Spouses are obliged to support each other; legitimate ascendants and descendants; parents and illegitimate children and legitimate/illegitimate children of the latter; legitimate brothers and sisters.

**Scope** (Art. 194): Support comprises everything indispensable for sustenance, dwelling, clothing, medical attendance, education, and transportation, in keeping with the financial capacity of the family.

**Amount** (Art. 201): Proportionate to the resources of the giver and the necessities of the recipient. May be increased or reduced accordingly.

## Paternity and Filiation

**Legitimate children** (Art. 164): Conceived or born during a valid marriage or within 300 days after its dissolution if the mother has not remarried.

**Proof of filiation** (Art. 172): Record of birth in the civil register; final judgment; admission in a public document or private handwritten instrument signed by the parent. In the absence of these: open and continuous possession of legitimate child status; other means allowed by law and Rules of Court.

**Impugning legitimacy** (Art. 166): Grounds: physical impossibility for husband to have sexual access to wife; biological or scientific reasons; wife gave birth 300+ days after separation or annulment decree. Action must be filed within one year from knowledge of birth (Art. 170).

**Illegitimate children's rights**: Right to use father's surname if recognized under R.A. 9255; right to support; reduced hereditary share (Art. 176) — half of that of a legitimate child.

---

## Adoption

Post-R.A. 11642 (2022): Domestic adoption is now an **administrative proceeding** before the National Authority for Child Care. Court involvement removed for domestic adoption. Inter-country adoption retains judicial involvement under R.A. 8043.

**Effects of adoption**: Adopted child has same rights as legitimate child of adopting parents. Severing of legal ties with biological parents, except when adopted by a relative or stepparent.

## Key Jurisprudence — Family Relations

*Tan-Andal v. Andal* (G.R. No. 196359, 2021, En Banc): Modified *Molina* on psychological incapacity. Current controlling doctrine. Psychological incapacity is legal, not medical; relative incurability in the marriage context suffices; expert testimony helpful but not mandatory.

*Republic v. Manalo* (G.R. No. 221029, 2018, En Banc): Art. 26(2) applies even when divorce abroad was initiated by the Filipino spouse.

*Santos v. CA* (G.R. No. 112019, 1995): Established gravity, juridical antecedence, incurability as elements of psychological incapacity — partially superseded by *Tan-Andal* on the incurability standard.

*Briones v. Miguel* (G.R. No. 156343, 2004): Tender-age presumption for children under 7 favors mother; rebuttable by compelling reasons.

*Republic v. CA and Molina* (G.R. No. 108763, 1997): Eight Molina guidelines — effectively modified by *Tan-Andal* but some guidelines remain useful as analytical tools, not mandatory requirements.
## Anti-Hallucination Guardrails — Family Relations

Never state that absence of a formal requisite makes a marriage voidable — Art. 4 FC makes the responsible party *liable* (civilly, criminally, and administratively), not the marriage voidable; only the absence of a marriage license (Art. 35[3]) voids the marriage among formal requisite defects. Never apply the *Molina* guidelines as mandatory requirements after *Tan-Andal v. Andal* (2021) — *Tan-Andal* specifically modified *Molina*; always note that the eight-guideline checklist from *Molina* is no longer the controlling test. Never state that Art. 26(2) applies only when the foreign spouse initiates the divorce — *Republic v. Manalo* (2018) extended it to divorces initiated by the Filipino spouse. Never invent specific case numbers for family law cases — the volume of FC cases at trial court level makes fabrication easy and verification hard; cite only landmark SC decisions with confirmed GR numbers. Never state that psychological incapacity under Art. 36 requires a medical diagnosis — *Tan-Andal* explicitly rejected the medical-diagnosis requirement.
