# Legal Ethics — Professional Responsibility (CPRA Canons I–VI)

## Primary Sources
**A.M. No. 22-09-01-SC — Code of Professional Responsibility and Accountability (CPRA), effective April 11, 2023.** Supersedes the 1988 Code of Professional Responsibility (1988 CPR) in its entirety. All citations in this module use **Canon** and **Section** references under the CPRA. Where a 1988 CPR rule is referenced for comparative purposes, it is explicitly labeled as such.

*Source note*: The CPRA was promulgated by the Supreme Court en banc. It applies to all lawyers admitted to practice in the Philippines, including government lawyers, in-house counsel, and lawyers in the judiciary in their capacity as members of the Bar.

---

## Structure of the CPRA
The CPRA has **nine Canons** organized around duties owed to different institutions: the legal profession (independence and dignity), the courts (fairness), clients (fidelity, competence, confidentiality), adverse parties and society (propriety), and the overall system of justice. The present module covers Canons I–VI. Canon VII (Accountability/Discipline), Canon VIII (Fairness to Other Lawyers), and Canon IX (Dignity) are addressed in `legal-ethics/disciplinary-proceedings.md`, which also covers IBP discipline procedure, penalties, and notarial practice.

## Canon I — Independence
**Rule 1.01 — Independence of the legal profession**: The legal profession is independent and must be kept free from undue influence, pressure, intimidation, or interference by any person, branch of government, or institution, including clients.

**Rule 1.02 — Independence of the individual lawyer**: A lawyer must maintain personal independence when advising clients — free from fear of reprisal from clients, third parties, or the State. A lawyer's judgment on legal strategy, ethics, and the merits of a position must be the lawyer's own.

**Rule 1.03 — Prohibition on interference with independence**: Neither the government nor any person may dictate a lawyer's advice to a client. Pressure to give a particular opinion, recommendation, or legal strategy, if yielded to, compromises the lawyer's independence and may itself constitute a violation of the CPRA.

**Independence and government lawyers**: Government lawyers owe primary loyalty to the Constitution, the law, and the public interest — not to the appointing authority. A government lawyer may not follow instructions from a superior to take an unlawful position or abandon a legally meritorious one. *Caveat*: A government lawyer defending the government must still ensure the government's actual legal position is honestly presented; the obligation of candor to the court is the same for government as for private practitioners.

## Canon II — Propriety
**Rule 2.01 — Dignified and respectful conduct**: A lawyer must, in all professional dealings, act with propriety — conducting themselves in a manner that maintains the respect due to courts, other counsel, parties, and the legal profession itself.

**Rule 2.02 — Decorum in and out of court**: Proper behavior is required not only within court proceedings but in all professional dealings, including media appearances, social media, and public communications. A lawyer who makes intemperate, scandalous, or disrespectful statements about courts, judges, or proceedings — even on social media — violates Canon II.

**Rule 2.03 — No solicitation; no ambulance-chasing**: Direct solicitation of clients, directly or through agents ("runners"), is prohibited. This includes: in-person or phone solicitation of prospective clients who did not invite the contact; pay-per-referral agreements with non-lawyers; and advertisements that are false, misleading, or create unjustified expectations. Permissible advertising is truthful, not comparative, not self-laudatory, and does not guarantee outcomes.

**Rule 2.04 — No false or misleading firm names or professional titles**: A firm name may not imply a partnership where none exists, may not include the name of a deceased or retired partner where continued use would mislead, and may not include the name of a sitting judge or government official.

**Rule 2.05 — Prohibition on multiplex legal services**: A lawyer may not simultaneously practice law and render or direct professional services outside the law (e.g., as a medical practitioner, real estate broker) in a manner that tends to mislead the public about the nature of the services.

**Rule 2.06 — Unlawful discriminatory conduct**: A lawyer may not, in professional dealings, discriminate on grounds of sex, gender, gender identity, sexual orientation, ethnicity, religion, physical disability, or other prohibited grounds.

## Canon III — Fidelity (The Lawyer-Client Relationship)

### Formation of the Lawyer-Client Relationship
An attorney-client relationship is formed when: (1) a lawyer voluntarily agrees to represent a client; or (2) an implied agreement arises from the client's reasonable expectation of professional assistance based on the lawyer's conduct, even in the absence of a formal engagement. A single consultation, the receipt of confidential information, or a referral intake may suffice to establish implied formation.

The relationship may also arise by court appointment (e.g., counsel de oficio for indigent accused in criminal cases; PAO attorney; SC-appointed amicus curiae).

### Duties Within the Relationship

**Rule 3.01 — Loyalty**: A lawyer owes undivided loyalty to a client — the client's legitimate interests must be advanced as far as law and ethics permit, not merely as a professional courtesy.

**Rule 3.02 — No conflict of interest**: A lawyer shall not represent conflicting interests unless all affected clients give informed written consent after full disclosure. The test for conflict: whether the attorney's representation of one client will require advocacy adversarial to, or at the expense of, another client (current or former). Two sub-categories:

*Direct conflict* — simultaneous representation of parties with directly opposed interests in the same proceeding (categorically prohibited, not curable by consent in contentious matters).

*Indirect/potential conflict* — prior representation in a related or substantially related matter where confidences gained from one client could be used against the interests of a current or former client. Requires full disclosure and informed consent; if consent cannot be obtained, the attorney must withdraw.

**Rule 3.03 — No representation against a former client in the same or a substantially related matter**: The duty of loyalty survives the termination of the engagement. A lawyer who has represented a client in a transaction may not later represent the adverse party in litigation over that same transaction, even after the relationship has ended, without the former client's informed written consent.

**Rule 3.04 — No counsel shopping by the lawyer**: A lawyer may not advise or assist a client in employing multiple attorneys to obtain conflicting opinions for the purpose of selecting the one favorable to the client's desired position.

**Rule 3.05 — Prohibition on acquiring interest adverse to the client**: During the engagement, a lawyer may not purchase or acquire any property interest adverse to the client's interest that is the subject of the representation, or become a creditor of the client in a matter arising out of or in connection with the representation.

**Rule 3.06 — Lawyer's fees**: Fees must be fair and reasonable. Factors: time and effort, novelty and difficulty, skill required, importance of the matter, customary local fees, counsel's professional standing, the results obtained, and whether the engagement precludes other employment. Contingency fees in criminal cases and family law cases (involving the validity of marriage) are prohibited. Contingency fees in civil and commercial matters are allowed subject to the reasonableness standard.

**Rule 3.07 — Retaining lien**: A lawyer may retain possession of a client's documents, funds, and papers until the lawyer's fees are paid, subject to limitations: the lien does not apply to documents the client needs urgently for health, safety, or imminent legal action, and does not allow a lawyer to convert or misappropriate retained funds.

**Rule 3.08 — No unauthorized media disclosure of client matters**: A lawyer who knows a case is being tried in the media must not make extrajudicial statements that have a substantial likelihood of prejudicing the proceeding. This applies to lawyers on both sides.

**Rule 3.09 — Client's right to discharge the lawyer**: A client may terminate the relationship at any time with or without cause. The lawyer's right to fees earned is preserved (quantum meruit); the client must pay reasonable compensation for services rendered before withdrawal.

**Rule 3.10 — Lawyer's right to withdraw**: A lawyer may withdraw for cause (e.g., client's persistent refusal to follow legitimate legal advice, inability to pay fees, discovery that the client has lied to the lawyer, conflict of interest subsequently discovered). Mandatory withdrawal in cases where continued representation would violate the CPRA or the law. A withdrawing lawyer must give adequate notice, assist the client in finding new counsel, surrender all papers and funds due to the client, and may not prejudice the client's interest.

## Canon IV — Competence and Diligence

**Rule 4.01 — Duty of competence**: A lawyer must have and apply the legal knowledge, skill, thoroughness, and preparation reasonably necessary for the representation. Competence is not limited to knowing the substantive law — it includes procedural law, rules of court, and the evidentiary and practical requirements of the specific matter.

**Rule 4.02 — Duty of diligence**: A lawyer must pursue a client's matter with promptness and dedication. Unjustified delay, failure to file pleadings on time, failure to appear, and neglect of correspondence are each independent violations of the duty of diligence.

**Rule 4.03 — Continuing legal education**: Competence is a continuing obligation — a lawyer is expected to update knowledge of law and practice through continuing legal education (CLE), study of legislative and jurisprudential developments, and professional development. MCLE (Mandatory Continuing Legal Education) compliance is a separate regulatory requirement; non-compliance suspends the right to practice.

**Rule 4.04 — Supervision of subordinates**: A senior lawyer (supervising partner, senior associate, or lead counsel) is responsible for ensuring that subordinate lawyers and non-lawyer staff within the lawyer's direction act consistently with the CPRA. A supervising lawyer who orders, ratifies, or fails to prevent a subordinate's violation when in a position to do so shares responsibility.

**Rule 4.05 — Responsibility of subordinate lawyer**: A subordinate lawyer who knowingly carries out an instruction that violates the CPRA is not excused by the fact that the instruction came from a superior. A subordinate is excused from responsibility only if the instruction involved a reasonably arguable point of professional judgment, the instruction came from the supervising lawyer, and the subordinate complied in good faith without independent reason to doubt the instruction's propriety.

## Canon V — Equality

**Rule 5.01 — Non-discrimination in access to legal services**: A lawyer may not refuse to accept a client or provide a lower quality of service on grounds of sex, gender, sexual orientation, gender identity, race, ethnicity, religion, national origin, disability, economic status, or political affiliation, in circumstances where acceptance would not cause a conflict of interest or compromise the lawyer's independence.

**Rule 5.02 — Pro bono obligation**: The practice of law is a privilege burdened with public duties. Every lawyer is expected to render a minimum amount of pro bono legal service annually — the IBP sets the minimum; failure to do so may be cited in disciplinary proceedings in appropriate cases.

**Rule 5.03 — Equality before tribunals**: A lawyer must treat all parties, witnesses, opposing counsel, court personnel, and judicial officers with equal respect, without regard to the same protected characteristics enumerated in Rule 5.01.

**Rule 5.04 — Legal aid**: A lawyer shall support, and where practicable participate in, legal aid programs for indigent litigants and marginalized groups.

## Canon VI — Confidentiality

**Rule 6.01 — General duty**: A lawyer must maintain the confidentiality of all information relating to the representation of a client — whether or not the information is privileged in the strict evidentiary sense. This covers information conveyed by the client, information obtained in the course of the representation, and even the existence of the attorney-client relationship itself, if the client so directs.

**Scope compared to attorney-client privilege**: The *duty of confidentiality* under Canon VI is broader than the *attorney-client privilege* (an evidentiary doctrine). Privilege governs what can be compelled in court proceedings. Confidentiality governs what the lawyer may voluntarily disclose in any context — including to the press, to third parties, and in informal settings. A lawyer who discloses client information not under legal compulsion violates Canon VI even if the information is not strictly privileged.

**Rule 6.02 — Duration**: The duty of confidentiality is not extinguished by the termination of the engagement, the death of the client, or the lawyer's retirement from practice. It survives indefinitely as to information obtained during the representation.

**Rule 6.03 — Exceptions (permitted disclosure)**: A lawyer may — and in some circumstances must — disclose confidential information:
1. With the client's **informed consent**.
2. To prevent a client from committing a crime or fraud that is **reasonably certain to result in substantial bodily harm** or **substantial financial harm to a third party**, where disclosure is necessary to prevent the harm and less restrictive means (e.g., persuading the client to desist) have been exhausted.
3. To defend the lawyer against charges by the client (fee disputes, disciplinary complaints, malpractice claims) — only to the extent necessary.
4. When required by **court order** — but the lawyer must first assert the privilege on behalf of the client and exhaust all legal remedies before complying.
5. To comply with other law or rules — e.g., AMLA reporting obligations for lawyers as DNFBPs (Designated Non-Financial Businesses and Professions) in qualifying transactions.

**Rule 6.04 — Prohibition on using client information against the client**: A lawyer may not use confidential information acquired during the representation to the disadvantage of the client — whether for the lawyer's own benefit, or for the benefit of a third party — unless the client consents.

**Rule 6.05 — Former clients**: The duty under Rule 6.04 continues to protect former clients. A lawyer may not use information obtained from a former client in representing a new client in a matter adverse to the former client.

## Landmark Cases — Professional Responsibility
**Atty. Florido v. Florido**, A.C. No. 5624 (Feb. 17, 2020): Restatement of the rule that the duty of candor to the court overrides the duty of zealous advocacy — a lawyer who knowingly makes false statements of fact to the court violates both Canon III and the pre-CPRA Canon 10.01, and may be suspended regardless of the client's instruction.

**Nakpil v. Valdes**, A.C. No. 2040 (Mar. 4, 1998): Extended the duty of loyalty and confidentiality to the period *before* formal engagement — information disclosed by a prospective client in the initial consultation is protected even if the lawyer is not subsequently retained.

**Small v. Banares**, A.C. No. 7860 (Jan. 15, 2014): Articulated the "continuous relationship" doctrine — the prohibition on representing conflicting interests applies throughout the duration of the attorney-client relationship, not only during active litigation phases.

*Note on case citations pre-CPRA*: Because the CPRA took effect in April 2023, many landmark cases decided under the 1988 CPR remain authoritative for the underlying doctrinal propositions (confidentiality, loyalty, conflict of interest, diligence) because the CPRA codifies and develops rather than repudiates those principles. Pre-CPRA cases must be cited with awareness that the rule number has changed; the underlying obligation generally survives.

## Anti-Hallucination Guardrails — Professional Responsibility
The **CPRA (2023)** supersedes the 1988 Code of Professional Responsibility in its entirety — never cite 1988 CPR Canon or Rule numbers as currently applicable; cite the CPRA Canon/Section and note, where relevant, that the principle also existed under the 1988 CPR. Contingency fees are permitted in civil and commercial cases but are **prohibited in criminal cases and family law matters involving the validity of marriage**; do not state contingency fees are categorically prohibited. The duty of confidentiality is **broader** than the attorney-client evidentiary privilege — they are not the same thing; never equate them or use them interchangeably. A lawyer who complies with a court order to produce privileged documents is **not in violation** of Canon VI — but must first assert the privilege and exhaust remedies before complying. The formation of an attorney-client relationship does **not** require a signed engagement letter or payment of a retainer — implied formation from the client's reasonable reliance on the lawyer's conduct suffices. Do not state that a government lawyer's loyalty runs primarily to the appointing authority — it runs to the Constitution, law, and public interest.
