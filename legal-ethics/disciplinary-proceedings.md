## Canon VII — Accountability

### Duty of Candor to the Court (Rule 7.01–7.05)

**Rule 7.01 — No false statements to the court**: A lawyer must not make any false statement of fact or law to a tribunal — whether in oral argument, in pleadings, or in documentary submissions. This is among the most absolute duties in the CPRA; no instruction from a client, no interest in winning, and no economic pressure justifies a knowingly false statement to the court.

**Rule 7.02 — Duty to disclose controlling adverse authority**: If a lawyer knows of a directly controlling legal authority from the jurisdiction that is adverse to the lawyer's position — a Supreme Court ruling that squarely decides against the client — and opposing counsel has not cited it, the lawyer **must** disclose it to the tribunal. This duty overrides the adversarial instinct; withholding controlling authority is itself a misrepresentation. (This duty applies only to *controlling* authority; adverse authority from other jurisdictions need not be volunteered but may not be affirmatively misrepresented.)

**Rule 7.03 — No frivolous claims**: A lawyer shall not file a claim, assert a defense, or take any other action in a proceeding that is not warranted by existing law or a non-frivolous argument for modifying, extending, or reversing existing law. Filing a complaint purely for harassment, delay, or increasing litigation costs — without a colorable legal basis — is sanctionable under the CPRA and independently under the Rules of Court (Rule 7, §3 on certifications against forum shopping; Rule 65 anti-abuse provisions).

**Rule 7.04 — Truthfulness in ex parte proceedings**: In ex parte applications (temporary restraining orders, petitions for the issuance of writs where the other side is not yet heard), the duty of candor is heightened because there is no opposing counsel to catch misrepresentations. A lawyer presenting ex parte matters must make full and frank disclosure of all material facts, including those adverse to the client's position, that would be relevant to the tribunal's decision.

**Rule 7.05 — No false evidence**: A lawyer shall not offer evidence that the lawyer knows to be false. If a lawyer comes to know that evidence already offered is false, the lawyer must take reasonable remedial measures — including, if necessary, disclosing it to the tribunal — unless the client's constitutional right against self-incrimination would be violated by the disclosure. In civil matters, no such privilege applies and the lawyer must correct the false submission.

## Canon VIII — Fairness

### Duties to Opposing Counsel, Parties, and Witnesses

**Rule 8.01 — No harassment or oppression**: A lawyer shall not harass witnesses, parties, or opposing counsel through the use of abusive, threatening, or disrespectful language; by filing groundless grievances; or by engaging in any conduct intended to intimidate rather than persuade.

**Rule 8.02 — No deceptive tactics**: A lawyer shall not knowingly make false statements to opposing counsel or engage in deception in the course of negotiations, discovery, or correspondence.

**Rule 8.03 — No interference with opposing counsel's clients**: A lawyer shall not communicate directly with a represented adverse party about the subject matter of the representation without the consent of opposing counsel. The prohibition applies regardless of whether the adverse party initiated contact.

**Rule 8.04 — Fairness in discovery**: A lawyer shall not abuse discovery processes to delay, harass, or impose unnecessary burdens on the opposing party. This includes: withholding discoverable materials not protected by privilege; stonewalling depositions; and making objections not grounded in law.

**Rule 8.05 — No improper inducements to witnesses**: A lawyer shall not offer or provide monetary payments to a witness beyond lawful witness fees and reasonable expenses, and shall not advise a witness not to testify truthfully.

## Canon IX — Dignity

**Rule 9.01 — Upholding the dignity of the legal profession**: A lawyer must conduct themselves in a manner that upholds the honor and dignity of the legal profession both inside and outside professional practice. Conduct unbecoming a lawyer — even if committed purely in a personal capacity — may be the basis for disciplinary action where it reflects adversely on the lawyer's fitness to practice.

**Rule 9.02 — Duties to the integrated bar**: A lawyer must pay IBP dues, comply with MCLE requirements, and support the IBP's activities as an institution that promotes the rule of law. Non-payment of IBP dues is independently sanctionable and is one of the enumerated grounds for the lifting of the privilege to practice.

**Rule 9.03 — Respect for the judiciary**: Criticism of judicial decisions, while protected as free speech, must be temperate, fair, and offered through appropriate channels (motions for reconsideration, appeals, scholarly commentary). Scandalous, disrespectful, or malicious attacks on judges or on the judicial process as an institution — whether in court pleadings, in the media, or on social media — violate Canon IX and may be punished as contempt of court in addition to constituting a CPRA violation.

## IBP Disciplinary Proceedings (Rule 139-B, Rules of Court)

### Jurisdiction
The **Supreme Court** has exclusive jurisdiction over the admission to and disbarment from the practice of law in the Philippines. The IBP, acting through its Commission on Bar Discipline (CBD), hears complaints against lawyers at the first level and recommends sanctions; final action rests with the Supreme Court.

### Who May File
Any person, including courts, may file a verified complaint against a lawyer. Courts may initiate proceedings motu proprio. The IBP may initiate proceedings based on its own knowledge or information. The complainant need not be the client; victims of a lawyer's misconduct in any capacity may file.

### Procedure
1. **Verified complaint** filed with the CBD (IBP National Office) or Regional Governor, accompanied by affidavits and documentary evidence.
2. **Docketing and assignment** to an Investigating Commissioner.
3. **Service on respondent lawyer** — 15 days to file a verified answer.
4. **Hearing** — Investigating Commissioner may receive evidence, conduct clarificatory hearings, or submit the matter on the pleadings.
5. **Report and recommendation** by Investigating Commissioner — transmitted to IBP Board of Governors.
6. **IBP Board of Governors action** — may adopt, modify, or reverse the Investigating Commissioner's recommendation; issues a formal Resolution.
7. **Transmittal to the Supreme Court** — all IBP Resolutions recommending suspension or disbarment are transmitted to the SC for final action.
8. **Supreme Court resolution** — the SC exercises plenary review; it may impose, reduce, increase, or decline to impose the recommended penalty. The SC's decision is final.

### Prescriptive Period
There is **no prescriptive period** for disbarment proceedings. Disciplinary proceedings are *sui generis* — they are not civil or criminal proceedings, and limitations applicable to civil or criminal actions do not apply. A complaint may be filed against a lawyer years or decades after the misconduct, provided the facts alleged are established.

### Burden of Proof
Clear and convincing evidence. Not proof beyond reasonable doubt (criminal standard), and not a mere preponderance (civil standard). The evidence must be sufficient to generate a clear conviction, without hesitation, that the charged misconduct occurred.

## Penalties and Sanctions

| Penalty | Description | SC Discretion |
|---|---|---|
| **Disbarment** | Permanent removal from the Roll of Attorneys | SC may impose without IBP recommendation; no term |
| **Indefinite suspension** | Suspension without specified end date; readmission requires separate application | Available for serious misconduct |
| **Definite suspension** | Suspension for a specified period | Common for moderate violations |
| **Fine** | Monetary penalty | Imposed in addition to or in lieu of suspension for some violations |
| **Censure / reprimand** | Formal public condemnation | Lightest sanction; appropriate for minor violations |
| **Warning** | Caution against repetition | Not formal discipline; precautionary |

**Multiple penalties**: The SC may impose multiple penalties for multiple charges or for particularly egregious single violations (e.g., suspension plus fine plus return of misappropriated funds).

---

## Reinstatement After Disbarment
A disbarred lawyer may apply for reinstatement to the SC after sufficient time has elapsed and there is convincing evidence of rehabilitation. Factors: conduct after disbarment; sincere repentance; restitution of any funds misappropriated; good moral character; no subsequent criminal convictions. Reinstatement is not a right; the SC has absolute discretion to grant or deny.

**Relevant case** — *In re: Almacen*, 31 SCRA 562 (1970): Articulated the principle that the power to admit, suspend, and disbar lawyers is inherent in the SC's supervisory power over the Bar; it is not a power of the IBP (though the IBP assists); and it may be exercised on the SC's own initiative without a formal complaint.

## Criminal Liability of Lawyers — Overlap With CPRA

CPRA violations are not criminal acts per se — they are administrative offenses within the SC's supervisory jurisdiction. However, the same conduct may simultaneously constitute: a criminal offense (e.g., estafa, forgery, perjury, or violation of R.A. 3019 for government lawyers), a civil tort, and a CPRA violation. The three tracks are independent and may proceed concurrently. A criminal acquittal does not bar disciplinary proceedings (different burden of proof; different purpose).

## Notarial Practice (A.M. No. 02-8-13-SC, 2004 Rules on Notarial Practice)

### Notarial Commission
A lawyer in good standing may apply for appointment as a **Notary Public** before the Regional Trial Court of the city or province where the lawyer has a regular law office. The commission: valid for 2 years; renewable. Requirements: IBP good standing; office in jurisdiction; MCLE compliance; payment of bond.

### Notarial Acts
A notary public may perform:
1. **Acknowledgment** — the execution of the document before the notary by a person who is known to the notary or identified through competent evidence of identity (CII).
2. **Jurat** — the affiant/signatory swears to the truth of the document's contents before the notary.
3. **Copy certification** — the notary certifies that the attached copy is a true copy of the original.
4. **Signature witnessing** — witnessing the execution of a document.
5. **Oath and affirmation** — administering an oath.
6. **Protest** — for a negotiable instrument under the NIL.

### Competent Evidence of Identity (CII)
Current identification document issued by a government agency or foreign government bearing the photograph, signature, and identification number — e.g., passport, driver's license, PRC ID, SSS/GSIS card, UMID. Personal knowledge of the notary (without a CII) is no longer sufficient under the 2004 Rules; the CII requirement is mandatory.

### Prohibitions for Notaries Public
A notary public may **not**:
- Notarize a document when the affiant/signatory is not personally present.
- Notarize a document the notary prepared and in which the notary has a direct financial or beneficial interest.
- Notarize their own signature.
- Perform a notarial act on a document that is incomplete or blank.
- Use a fictitious or expired commission.

**Remote notarization**: As of the current framework, remote or electronic notarization is governed by specific Supreme Court rules (A.M. No. 20-07-04-SC on the conduct of notarial acts during the pandemic, and subsequent guidelines). Lawyers practicing notarial services must track current SC circulars on this evolving area.

### Sanctions for Notarial Violations
Revocation of the notarial commission; disqualification from being a commissioned notary public; criminal liability (falsification of public documents if an unauthorized act is intentional); and CPRA disciplinary action if the notarial violation reflects on the lawyer's fitness to practice.

## Anti-Hallucination Guardrails — Disciplinary Proceedings and Notarial Practice
There is **no prescriptive period** for disbarment proceedings — do not analogize to civil or criminal statutes of limitation. The **burden of proof** is clear and convincing evidence, not proof beyond reasonable doubt and not mere preponderance — never state one or the other standard applies. The **SC, not the IBP**, has final disciplinary jurisdiction; the IBP's resolution is a recommendation, not a final order. A criminal acquittal **does not bar** a disbarment proceeding based on the same facts — different purposes and different burdens. Notarization without personal appearance is **categorically prohibited** under the 2004 Rules — do not present personal appearance as optional or curative. A notary public who notarizes their own signature is in violation even if they have a valid commission — never state a lawyer may self-notarize. Do not conflate the CPRA Canon IX duty to respect the judiciary with a prohibition on all criticism of courts — temperate criticism through proper channels (motions, appeals, scholarly writing) is permitted; scandalous and malicious attacks are not.
